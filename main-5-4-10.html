    Subsection 3.2.8 Summary & Exercises

    Summary:
    • The method of undetermined coefficients is used to solve non-homogeneous linear differential equations.
    • The general solution to a non-homogeneous equation is the sum of the general solution to the corresponding homogeneous equation and a particular solution.
    • The method involves guessing the form of the particular solution based on the form of the non-homogeneous term and solving for the coefficients.
    • The method is applicable when the non-homogeneous term can be expressed as a linear combination of known functions.
    The method of undetermined coefficients involves the following steps:
    1. Verify that the method is applicable to the given differential equation.
    2. Find the general solution to the corresponding homogeneous equation.
    3. Guess the form of the particular solution based on the form of the non-homogeneous term.
    4. Solve for the coefficients of the particular solution.
    5. Combine the general solution of the homogeneous equation with the particular solution to obtain the complete solution.

    3.

    Describe the difference between a homogeneous and a non-homogeneous differential equation.
    Solution.
    A differential equation is termed "homogeneous" if all the terms of the equation are either derivatives of the unknown function or multiples of the unknown function itself, and the equation can be set equal to zero. In other words, there are no terms in the equation that are solely functions of the independent variable or constants. For example, the equation \(y'' + 2y' + y = 0 \) is homogeneous because all its terms involve \(y \) or its derivatives and the equation is set equal to zero. A differential equation is termed "non-homogeneous" if it contains terms that are solely functions of the independent variable or constants which are not multiples of the unknown function or its derivatives. For example, the equation \(y'' + 2y' + y = e^x \) is non-homogeneous because of the term \(e^x \) on the right side, which is not a derivative or multiple of \(y \text{.}\) The distinction between homogeneous and non-homogeneous differential equations is crucial as the methods used to solve them can differ.

    Notice that the DE in Question \ref{Q3} is actually a first-order linear differential equation, so we can use another method of solution to solve it (and we should get the same answer!). Solve the same IVP using an integrating factor and compareyour answers.
    Answer.
    \(\ds x = 3t^2 - 12t + 24 - 9e^{-\frac{1}{2}t} \)
    Solution.
    \\ First we put DE in standard form for a first-order linear DE \(\ds \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = f(x), \) so we can identif \(\ds P(x). \) In this case \(\ds x \) is the dependent variable (i.e., it behaves lik \(\ds y \) ) an \(\ds t \) is the independent variable (i.e., it behaves lik \(\ds x \) ), so the standard form we seek i \(\ds \frac{dx}{dt} + P(t)x = f(t), \) so we can identif \(\ds P(t). \)
    \begin{align*} 2x' + x \amp = 3t^2 \\ x' + \frac{1}{2}x \amp = \frac{3}{2}t^2 \end{align*}
    Thus \(\ds P(t) = \frac{1}{2}. \) Then we find the integrating facto \(\ds z. \)
    \begin{align*} z \amp = e^{\mbox{an antiderivative o }} \\ \amp = e^{\mbox{an antiderivative o }} \\ \amp = e^{\frac{1}{2}t} \end{align*}
    Now we multiply both sides of the DE by the integrating factor.
    \begin{align*} e^{\frac{1}{2}t}\cdot x' + e^{\frac{1}{2}t}\cdot \frac{1}{2}x \amp = e^{\frac{1}{2}t}\cdot \frac{3}{2}t^2 \nonumber \\ e^{\frac{1}{2}t}\cdot x' + \frac{1}{2}e^{\frac{1}{2}t}\cdot x \amp = \frac{3}{2}t^2e^{\frac{1}{2}t} \nonumber \\ \underbrace{e^{\frac{1}{2}t}}_{f}\cdot \underbrace{x'}_{g'} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}e^{\frac{1}{2}t}}_{f'}\cdot \underbrace{x}_{g} \amp = \frac{3}{2}t^2e^{\frac{1}{2}t} \label{eq20} \end{align*}
    Now we recall the product rule for derivatives: \(\ds \frac{d}{dt}\Big( f\cdot g \Big) = f\cdot g' + f' \cdot g. \) We note that the two terms on the left hand side of equation (\ref{eq20}) are the result of taking the derivative of the product. Hence we can undo the product rule as follows: \(\ds\frac{d}{dt}\Big(e^{\frac{1}{2}t}\cdot x \Big) = \frac{3}{2}t^2e^{\frac{1}{2}t}.\) Now we can integrate both sides of the equation and then solve for the dependent variable. On the left hand side we will need to integration by parts. We begin by choosin \(\ds u \) an \(\ds dv, \) and then we can find th \(\ds du, \) the derivative o \(\ds u \) , an \(\ds v \) , the antiderivative o \(\ds dv, \) as follows.
    \begin{align*} u \amp = \frac{3}{2}t^2 \amp dv \amp = e^{\frac{1}{2}t}dt \\ \Rightarrow du \amp = 3tdt \amp \Rightarrow v \amp = 2e^{\frac{1}{2}t} \end{align*}
    Hence we have
    \begin{align*} \int \frac{d}{dt}\Big(e^{\frac{1}{2}t}\cdot x \Big)dt \amp = \int\left(\frac{3}{2}t^2e^{\frac{1}{2}t}\right) dt \\ e^{\frac{1}{2}t}\cdot x \amp = \int \underbrace{\frac{3}{2}t^2}_{u}\underbrace{e^{\frac{1}{2}t} dt}_{dv} \\ \amp = \int u dv \\ \amp = uv-\int v du \\ \amp = \frac{3}{2}t^2\cdot 2e^{\frac{1}{2}t} - \int 2e^{\frac{1}{2}t}\cdot 3tdt \\ \amp = 3t^2e^{\frac{1}{2}t} - \int 6te^{\frac{1}{2}t}dt \end{align*}
    The new integral is an improvement, but will require another integration by parts. Again, we choose \(\ds u \) an \(\ds dv \) as follows.
    \begin{align*} u \amp = 6t \amp dv \amp = e^{\frac{1}{2}t}dt \\ \Rightarrow du \amp = 6dt \amp \Rightarrow v \amp = 2e^{\frac{1}{2}t} \end{align*}
    Hence we continue solving our DE as follows:
    \begin{align*} e^{\frac{1}{2}t}\cdot x \amp = 3t^2e^{\frac{1}{2}t} - \int \underbrace{6t}_{u}\underbrace{e^{\frac{1}{2}t}dt}_{dv} \\ \amp = 3t^2e^{\frac{1}{2}t} - \int u dv \\ \amp = 3t^2e^{\frac{1}{2}t} - \left[ uv - \int v du \right] \\ \amp = 3t^2e^{\frac{1}{2}t} - \left[ 6t\cdot 2e^{\frac{1}{2}t} - \int 2e^{\frac{1}{2}t}\cdot 6dt \right] \\ \amp = 3t^2e^{\frac{1}{2}t} - 12te^{\frac{1}{2}t} + 12\int e^{\frac{1}{2}t}dt \\ \amp = 3t^2e^{\frac{1}{2}t} - 12te^{\frac{1}{2}t} + 24e^{\frac{1}{2}t} + C \\ \amp \\ x \amp = \frac{3t^2e^{\frac{1}{2}t}}{e^{\frac{1}{2}t}} - \frac{12te^{\frac{1}{2}t}}{e^{\frac{1}{2}t}} + \frac{24e^{\frac{1}{2}t}}{e^{\frac{1}{2}t}} + \frac{C}{e^{\frac{1}{2}t}} \\ \amp = 3t^2 - 12t + 24 + Ce^{-\frac{1}{2}t} \end{align*}
    We now take the general solution and apply the initial conditio \(\ds x(0) = 15 \) so that we can solve for the particular solution.
    \begin{align*} 15 \amp = x(0) \\ \amp = 3(0)^2 - 12(0) + 24 + Ce^{-\frac{1}{2}\cdot 0} \\ \amp = 24+C \\ -9 \amp = C \end{align*}
    Hence, the solution to this IVP i \(\ds x = 3t^2 - 12t + 24 - 9e^{-\frac{1}{2}t} \) (which is the same answer we got using the method of undetermined coefficients).
