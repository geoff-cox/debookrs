    Subsection The General Integrating Factor Formula

    We are now ready to determine the general formula for \(\mu\) given any first-order linear differential equation. Before we can find \(\mu\text{,}\) we must first put our equation in standard form, given by
    \begin{equation} \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)\ y = Q(x)\text{.}\tag{20} \end{equation}
    Now, we want the function, \(\mu\text{,}\) we multiply onto (20) to write it in the form
    \begin{equation} \frac{d}{dx} \left[ \mu y \right] = \mu Q(x).\tag{21} \end{equation}

    Details 14. Why All 1st-Order Linear Equations have a Standard Form.

    All first-order linear differential equations contain at most, \(y'\) terms, \(y\) terms, and constant terms, allowing you to organize such an equation as
    \begin{equation*} A(x)y' + B(x)y = C(x)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Dividing both sides by the leading coefficient, \(A(x)\text{,}\) and renaming the \(y\) and constant coefficients as \(P\) and \(Q\text{,}\) gives the standard form
    \begin{equation*} y' + \us{P(x)}{\ub{\frac{B(x)}{A(x)}}}y = \us{Q(x)}{\ub{\frac{C(x)}{A(x)}}} \quad \implies \quad y' + P(x)y = Q(x)\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Subsubsection Deriving the Formula

    As with the motivating example, we start by multiplying both sides of the differential equation by an unknown function \(\mu\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation} \mu \frac{dy}{dx} + \mu P(x) y = \mu Q(x).\tag{22} \end{equation}
    The left side can only turn into \(\frac{d}{dx} \left[ \mu y \right]\text{,}\) if it is also equal to this derivative’s expanded product rule form. In particular, we need the following to be true:
    \begin{equation*} \ob{ \mu \frac{dy}{dx} + \us{\Large 🚩}{\ul{\mu P(x)}}\ y }^{\large\text{left-side of }\href{main-6-3-4-4.html#if-mu-standard-form}{(22)}} = \ob{ \mu \frac{dy}{dx} + \us{\Large 🚩}{\ul{\frac{d\mu}{dx}}}\ y }^{\large\text{expanded }\frac{d}{dx} \left[ \mu y \right]} \text{.} \end{equation*}
    As you can see, the two sides are identical, except for the flagged 🚩 expressions. This means, (22) can only be written as (21) if \(\mu\) also satisfies
    📌 Note. If you are not sure where equation (23) comes from, we encourage you to have another look at A Motivating Example and how we get equation (18).
    \begin{equation} \frac{d\mu}{dx} = \mu P(x).\tag{23} \end{equation}
    To find such a function, \(\mu\text{,}\) we solve equation (23) using separation of variables.
    Separate:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\mu}\ d\mu = P(x)\ dx \end{equation*}
    Integrate:
    \begin{align*} \int \frac{1}{\mu}\ d\mu \amp = \int P(x)\ dx \\ \ln|\mu| \amp = \int P(x) dx \end{align*}
    📌 Note. The absolute value is dropped since the sign of the integrating factor is taken to be positive by convention.
    Solve for \(\mu(x)\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \mu(x) = e\vphantom{\sum}^{{\Large\int} P(x)\ dx}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Integrating Factor: General Formula.

    The integrating factor for a first-order linear differential equation in standard form
    \begin{equation*} y' + P(x)y = Q(x) \end{equation*}
    is given by \(\ \ds\mu(x) = e\vphantom{\sum}^{{\Large\int} P(x)\ dx} \text{.}\)
    Now that we have derived the general formula, let’s apply it to a few examples.

    Subsubsection Examples of Integrating Factors

    Example 15. Basic Integrating Factor.

    Find the integrating factor \(\mu(x)\) for the differential equation:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx} + 4y = x. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    The equation is in standard form with \(P(x) = 4\text{.}\) Using the formula:
    \begin{equation*} \mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) dx} = e^{\int 4 dx} = e^{4x}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus, the integrating factor is \(e^{4x}\text{.}\)

    Example 16. Integrating Factor with a Log Function.

    Determine the integrating factor \(\mu(x)\) for the differential equation:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{2}{x} y = x^3, \quad x > 0. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    The equation is in standard form with \(P(x) = \frac{2}{x}\text{.}\) Using the formula:
    \begin{equation*} \mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) dx} = e^{\int \frac{2}{x} dx}. \end{equation*}
    Evaluating the integral:
    \begin{equation*} \mu(x) = e^{2 \ln x} = x^2. \end{equation*}
    Thus, the integrating factor is \(x^2\text{.}\)

    Example 17. Integrating Factor with a Trigonometric Function.

    Compute the integrating factor \(\mu(x)\) for the differential equation:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx} + \tan x \cdot y = \cos x. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    The equation is in standard form with \(P(x) = \tan x\text{.}\) Using the formula:
    \begin{equation*} \mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) dx} = e^{\int \tan x dx}. \end{equation*}
    Evaluating the integral:
    \begin{equation*} \mu(x) = e^{\ln | \sec x |} = \sec x. \end{equation*}
    Thus, the integrating factor is \(\sec x\text{.}\)
    In this section, we derived the general formula for the integrating factor and demonstrated how to compute it with a few examples. The key takeaway is that the integrating factor always takes the form
    \begin{equation*} \mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) dx}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and once we have it, we can multiply it onto our equation to rewrite the left-hand side as a single derivative. In the next section, we will apply this approach step-by-step to solve first-order linear differential equations.

    Exercises Check your Understanding

    1. Answer-the-Following.

    Complete the questions on each tab to check your understanding.
    (a) Effect of Multiplying by the Integrating Factor.
      What happens when we multiply a first-order linear equation by its integrating factor?
    • The left-hand side becomes a single derivative of a product.
    • Correct! Multiplication by \(\mu(x)\) allows us to rewrite the left-hand side as \(\frac{d}{dx}[\mu(x)y]\text{.}\)
    • The equation transforms into a homogeneous equation.
    • Incorrect. The equation remains nonhomogeneous unless \(Q(x) = 0\text{.}\)
    • The integrating factor cancels out completely.
    • Incorrect. The integrating factor is essential to rewriting the equation.
    • The equation always becomes separable.
    • Incorrect. The equation is rewritten into an integrable form, but it is not necessarily separable.
    (b) Standard Form Requirement.
      Why must a first-order linear differential equation be written in the form \(y' + P(x)y = Q(x)\) before using the integrating factor formula?
    • Because the formula was derived assuming this form.
    • Correct! The integrating factor formula is based on the standard form of a first-order linear equation and to compute it, \(P(x)\) is needed.
    • Because it ensures that the equation is linear.
    • No. You can ensure linearity without writing the equation in this form.
    • Because it guarantees the equation has a unique solution.
    • Incorrect. Writing the equation in this form does not determine uniqueness.
    • Because this is the standard form.
    • Incorrect. It is true that this is the standard form, but there is a better answer choice for this question.
    (c) Describing the Integrating Factor.
      Which of the following is the closest description of an integrating factor?
    • A particular solution to the differential equation.
    • Incorrect. The integrating factor is not a solution to the differential equation, but a function that helps solve it.
    • A function that is multiplied onto both sides of an equation.
    • Correct! The integrating factor is a function that you multiply both sides of a differential equation by.
    • A number that converts the equation into a different form.
    • Incorrect. In general, the integrating factor is a function, not a number.
    • The anti-derivative of \(P(x)\) (from the standard form).
    • Incorrect. The integrating factor is \(e\) raised to the power of an anti-derivative of \(P(x)\text{,}\) but it is not the anti-derivative of \(P(x)\text{,}\) itself.
    (d) Selecting the Integrating Factor.
      What is the integrating factor for the equation
      \begin{equation*} y' + 3y = e^x\text{?} \end{equation*}
    • \(\quad\ds\mu = e^{e^{3x}}\)
    • Incorrect. This is not the correct expression for the integrating factor.
    • \(\quad\mu = e^{3x}\)
    • Correct! The integrating factor is \(e^{3x}\text{.}\)
    • \(\quad\ds\mu = e^{x^2/2}\)
    • Incorrect. The correct integrating factor should involve the integral of the coefficient of \(y\text{,}\) which is \(3\text{,}\) not \(x\text{.}\)
    • \(\quad\ds\mu = e^{x}\)
    • Incorrect. This is not the correct form for the integrating factor.
