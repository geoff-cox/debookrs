    Subsubsection IF Stuff

    The integrating factor method relies on rewriting a differential equation so that the left-hand side becomes the “derivative of a product”. To do this, we need to recognize when an expression “can be written as a product rule in reverse”.
    In this subsection, we will:
    ✅ Review the product rule from calculus.
    ✅ Practice reversing the product rule on different expressions.
    ✅ Apply this idea to transform differential equations into a solvable form.
    Rewriting a differential equation so that one side is a “completed” derivative is a crucial step in the integrating factor method. The examples in this section show that when an equation already has the structure of a product rule, it can be solved easily. However, most first-order linear equations are missing a necessary function to complete the derivative.

    🗝️ Key Takeaways...

    • Completing the square introduces a missing term to rewrite a quadratic equation in a solvable form.
    • Completing the derivative introduces a missing factor (the integrating factor) to rewrite a differential equation in a solvable form.
    • The integrating factor method works by modifying the equation to fit a product rule structure, allowing direct integration.
    In the next section, we will introduce the concept of an integrating factor, which allows us to modify a first-order linear equation so that it always fits the product rule structure.

    🗝️ Key Takeaways...

    • The product rule is not only used to compute derivatives but also to combine multiple functions into a single derivative.
    • “Reversing” the product rule within a differential equation is a key step in the integrating factor method.
    • Once we rewrite one side of a differential equation as a single derivative, it can be solved by direct integration.
    \begin{equation} \ub{\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x) y = Q(x)}_{\text{Given Equation}} \quad \os{\text{Integrating}}{\us{\text{Factor Method}}{\longrightarrow}} \quad \ub{\frac{d}{dx}[ \mu(x) y ] = \mu(x) Q(x)}_{\text{Completed Derivative Equation}}\text{.}\tag{27} \end{equation}
    While this integrating factor is specific to (13), the general integrating factor is found in a similar manner, which we will now show.
    Consider the first-order linear equation:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx} + 2y = 5\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The left-hand side almost resembles the result of the product rule, but something is missing. If we attempt to rewrite it in the form \(\frac{d}{dx} [f(x) y]\text{,}\) we quickly run into a problem. Labeling \(y\) as \(g\) and \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) as \(g'\text{,}\) we see that no corresponding \(f\) satisfies:
    \begin{align*} \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\large f}}{ \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\uparrow}}{\ul{\ \vphantom{\frac11} ? \ }}}\ \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\large g'}}{ \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\uparrow}}{\ul{\frac{dy}{dx}}}} + \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\large f^{'}}}{ \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\uparrow}}{\ul{\ \vphantom{\frac11} 2 }}}\ \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\large g}}{ \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\uparrow}}{\ul{\vphantom{\frac11} y\ }}} =\amp\ 5 \end{align*}
    The left-hand side lacks the function \(f\) that satisfies both parts of the product rule. To resolve this, let’s create our own \(f\) function by multiplying both sides by it, like so
    \begin{align*} \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\large f}}{ \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\uparrow}}{\ul{\vphantom{\frac11} \boxed{\phantom{\large|} \color{BurntOrange}{f} \phantom{\large|}}}}}\ \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\large g'}}{ \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\uparrow}}{\ul{\frac{dy}{dx}}}} + \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\large f^{'}}}{ \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\uparrow}}{\ul{\ \vphantom{\frac11} 2\ \boxed{\phantom{\large|} \color{BurntOrange}{f} \phantom{\large|}} }}}\ \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\large g}}{ \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\uparrow}}{\ul{\vphantom{\frac11} y\ }}} =\amp\ 5\ \boxed{\phantom{\large|} \color{BurntOrange}{f} \phantom{\large|}} \end{align*}
    For this to be a valid product rule, the derivative of \(f\) must be in the \(f'\) position. Can you think of a function has a derivative that is \(2\) times itself? There is only one, and that function is \(e^{2x}\text{,}\) since
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx} \big[ {\color{BurntOrange}\us{\text{itself}}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{e^{2x}}}}} \big] = 2{\color{BurntOrange}\us{\text{itself}}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{e^{2x}}}}} \end{equation*}
    Multiplying both sides of the equation by \(e^{2x}\) gives us
    \begin{equation*} e^{2x} \frac{dy}{dx} + 2e^{2x} y = 5e^{2x}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    but this is the same equation as in Example 9, which can be written as
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx} \left[ e^{2x} y \right] = 5e^{2x} \end{equation*}
    and solved using direct integration.
    This example demonstrates that multiplying by the right function can complete a missing derivative, allowing us to solve the equation. This function is called the integrating factor, and in the next section, we will formally define it and derive a general formula.
    ---------------------------------------------------------
    Now, recall that in order for this to be a valid product rule, we must have
    \begin{equation*} \mu^{\prime}= 2\mu \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{d}{dx} [\mu] = 2\mu\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(\mu\) is the unknown function we multiply the equation by. Notice that this is a differential equation! In particular, it is separable and we can find \(\mu\) with the separation of variables method, as follows
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx} [\mu(x) y] = \mu(x) \frac{dy}{dx} + \mu'(x) y. \end{equation*}
    Comparing this with the left-hand side of our given equation,
    \begin{equation*} \mu(x) \frac{dy}{dx} + \mu(x) \cdot 2y = \mu(x) \cdot 5, \end{equation*}
    we see that for these to match, \(\mu(x)\) must satisfy the equation:
    \begin{equation*} \mu'(x) = 2\mu(x). \end{equation*}
    This is a separable differential equation, which we solve as follows:
    Separate Variables:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d\mu}{\mu} = 2 dx. \end{equation*}
    Integrate Both Sides:
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{d\mu}{\mu} = \int 2dx. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \ln|\mu| = 2x + C. \end{equation*}
    Solve for \(\mu(x)\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \mu(x) = e^{2x + C} = e^C e^{2x}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(e^C\) is an arbitrary constant, we can choose \(C = 0\) (since any constant multiple of an integrating factor still works), giving us:
    \begin{equation} \mu(x) = e^{2x}.\tag{28} \end{equation}
    This function is called the integrating factor. It allows us to transform our equation into a perfect derivative, which can then be solved by direct integration.
    Every first-order linear differential equation has the form
    \begin{equation*} y' + P(x)y = Q(x)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and the left side is always one piece away from a complete product rule. To find this piece, we multiply both sides by \(\mu\text{,}\) and label the pieces of the product rule, as before,
    \begin{align*} \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\large \mu}}{ \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\uparrow}}{\ul{\vphantom{\frac11} \mu }}}\ \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\large y'}}{ \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\uparrow}}{\ul{\vphantom{\frac11} y'}}} + \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\large \mu^{'}}}{ \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\uparrow}}{\ul{\ \vphantom{\frac11} P(x)\ \mu }}}\ \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\large y}}{ \underset{\color{BurntOrange}{\uparrow}}{\ul{\vphantom{\frac11} y\ }}} =\amp\ Q(x)\ \mu \end{align*}
    We reason that in order for this to be a valid product rule, we must have
    \begin{equation} \mu^{\prime} = P(x)\ \mu\text{.}\tag{29} \end{equation}
    which is a differential equation that can be solved by separation of variables to find the integrating factor, \(\mu\text{,}\) as
    \begin{equation} \mu = e\vphantom{\large|}^{\textstyle\int P(x)\ dx}\text{.}\tag{30} \end{equation}
