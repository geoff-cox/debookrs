    Section 8.2 Thinking in Steps: How Euler‚Äôs Method Works One Step at a Time

    This shift towards numerical approximation is not just a workaround but a powerful approach that expands our capacity to solve differential equations arising in complex systems where exact solutions are not readily derivable. Euler‚Äôs method, as we will see, is the entry point into this expansive field of computational mathematics, demonstrating how straightforward concepts can approximate solutions to complex problems.
    üîó

    Iterative Methods‚ÄîThinking in Steps.

    Most numerical methods are iterative, which means they don‚Äôt try to solve the whole problem at once. Instead, they repeat the same small task again and again.
    üîó
    That task is simple: use the information you already have to generate new information. Do it once, you get the next step. Do it a hundred times, you build a whole solution.
    üîó
    So, the key to understanding an iterative method is by learning its task.
    üîó

    Checkpoint 107. üìñ‚ùì What does ‚Äúiterative‚Äù mean?

    Euler‚Äôs method (and most numerical methods) are described as iterative. What does that mean in practice?
    üîó
    • It repeats a single simple step many times to build up a solution.
    • Correct ‚Äî iterative methods loop the same calculation to generate new information.
    • It always produces the exact solution in one step.
    • No ‚Äî that‚Äôs the opposite of iterative thinking. Iteration builds up an approximate solution step by step.
    • It skips steps and jumps straight to the final answer.
    • No ‚Äî iterative methods never ‚Äúskip.‚Äù They accumulate results step by step.
    • It uses only algebra, not calculus, to solve equations.
    • Not quite ‚Äî Euler‚Äôs method uses the slope from calculus, but applies it in a repetitive way.
    üîó
    üîó

    Euler‚Äôs Method Task.

    For Euler‚Äôs method, the repeated task can be summed up like this:
    üîó
    ‚Äúfrom a known point, move to a nearby point along the direction of the slope.‚Äù
    üîó
    Think of following a hiking trail using only a compass‚Äîyou don‚Äôt know exactly where the trail ends, but if you keep stepping in the right direction, you‚Äôll trace the path. In Euler‚Äôs method, that ‚Äúdirection‚Äù is the slope from the differential equation.
    üîó
    More precisely, if \((t_{\text{cur}}, y_{\text{cur}})\) is a point on the solution and you know the slope there, you can predict where the next point \((t_{\text{new}}, y_{\text{new}})\) will be.
    üîó
    To understand this ‚Äúone step,‚Äù we‚Äôll answer two guiding questions:
    üîó
    1. What does it mean to move in the direction of a slope?
      üîó
    2. Given a point, slope, and step size, what is the formula for the next point?
      üîó
    üîó

    Moving in the Direction of a Slope.

    First question: ‚ÄúWhat does it mean to move in the direction of a slope?‚Äù Suppose you‚Äôre at the point \((1,1)\) and the slope there is \(\frac{3}{2}\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Slope is ‚Äúrise over run‚Äù: for every unit you move horizontally (‚Äúrun‚Äù), you move a certain amount vertically (‚Äúrise‚Äù). From \((1,1)\text{,}\) a slope of \(\frac{3}{2}\) means that for every 2 units of ‚Äúrun,‚Äù you rise 3 units, like so:
    \begin{equation*} (1 + \text{run}, 1 + \text{rise}) = (1 + 2, 1 + 3) = (3,4)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    üîó
    So moving from \((1,1)\) in a direction with slope \(\frac{3}{2}\text{,}\) lands you at \((3,4)\text{.}\) See Figure¬†109.(a).
    üîó

    üìù 108. ‚ÄúRise‚Äù Clarification.

    ‚ÄúRise‚Äù doesn‚Äôt always mean ‚Äúup‚Äù‚Äîif the slope is negative, ‚Äúrise‚Äù is actually a fall. We‚Äôll keep calling it ‚Äúrise‚Äù for simplicity, but remember it might point downward.
    üîó
    üîó
    Of course, there‚Äôs nothing special about using a ‚Äúrun‚Äù of 2. We could have used \(4\) (rise would then be 6) or even \(\pi\) (rise would be \(\frac{3}{2}\pi\)). All of the following fractions
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \left(\frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}}\right)\quad\quad \frac32,\ \frac{6}{4},\ \frac{3\pi}{2\pi},\ \frac{\frac32}{1},\ \ldots, \end{equation*}
    describe the same slope, which can be visualized in Figure¬†109.
    üîó
    In Euler‚Äôs method, we intentionally choose a small run called the step size, \(h\text{.}\) Therefore, for a small run, \(h\text{,}\) you get the corresponding rise by:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \text{slope} = \frac{\text{rise}}{h} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{rise} = \text{slope} \times h \end{equation*}
    So given a direction (slope) to move and the step size (\(h\)) to take, this formula gives you the rise. Next, we‚Äôll see how this simple relationship is the core of Euler‚Äôs one-step move.
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \text{rise} = \text{slope} \cdot h. \end{equation*}

    Checkpoint 110. üìñ‚ùì Calculating the Rise.

    Suppose the slope at a point is \(m = \frac{3}{2}\) and you take a step size of \(h = 0.2\text{.}\) What is the ‚Äúrise‚Äù over that step?
    üîó
    • \(0.3\)
    • Yes ‚Äî rise = slope √ó step size = \(\frac{3}{2} \times 0.2 = 0.3\text{.}\)
    • \(1.5\)
    • That‚Äôs the slope itself, not the rise for the small step.
    • \(0.2\)
    • That‚Äôs the step size (run), not the rise.
    • \(3.2\)
    • Double-check the multiplication ‚Äî slope √ó step size is much smaller.
    üîó
    üîó

    The Movement Rule.

    Now that we‚Äôve decided how to take a step, we can write it as a formula.
    üîó
    From basic geometry, a new point is just the old point plus the changes:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} (t_{\text{new}}, y_{\text{new}}) = (t_{\text{cur}} + \text{run}, \ y_{\text{cur}} + \text{rise})\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Euler‚Äôs method sets the ‚Äúrun‚Äù to the step size \(h\) and the ‚Äúrise‚Äù to \(h \times \text{slope}\text{.}\) Substituting those gives the update rule:
    üîó
    \begin{equation} (t_{\text{new}}, y_{\text{new}}) = (t_{\text{cur}} + h,\ y_{\text{cur}} + h \times \text{slope})\text{.}\tag{0} \end{equation}
    This single equation captures the whole one-step task: start at the current point, find the slope, take a step of size \(h\) in that direction, and you land at the next point. Euler‚Äôs method is simply this step repeated many times.
    üîó

    Checkpoint 111. üìñ‚ùì Understanding the Movement Formula.

    What does the term ‚Äú\(h \times \text{slope}\)‚Äù represent in Euler‚Äôs movement rule?
    üîó
    • The change in \(y\) over the chosen step.
    • Correct ‚Äî the slope tells the direction, and \(h\) scales it into a small ‚Äúrise.‚Äù
    • The next \(t\) value.
    • No ‚Äî the \(t\) update is handled separately as \(t_{\text{cur}} + h\text{.}\)
    • The slope of the entire solution curve.
    • Careful ‚Äî this is the slope at a single point, not the whole curve.
    • The error made by Euler‚Äôs method in each step.
    • Not true ‚Äî \(h \times \text{slope}\) is the predicted rise, not the error.
    üîó
    üîó

    Checkpoint 112. üìñ‚ùì Big Picture of Euler‚Äôs Step.

    Which statement best describes what Euler‚Äôs method does one step at a time?
    üîó
    • From a current point, it uses the slope to predict the next point.
    • Correct ‚Äî Euler‚Äôs method is nothing more than this step, done over and over.
    • It finds one term of the exact general solution.
    • No ‚Äî finding terms of the solution would be an analytic method.
    • It moves to a nearby point on the solution curve.
    • No ‚Äî if you knew where the solution curve was, you would already have the solution to the equation.
    • It approximates the roots of the characteristic equation.
    • No ‚Äî Euler‚Äôs approximates points of the solution, not the roots of the characteristic equation.
