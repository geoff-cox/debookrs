    2. Multiple-Choice.

    (a) Choosing the Right Switch.
    Suppose a function \(f(t)\) is ON for \(t \ge 6\text{.}\) Which expression correctly switches it ON at that point?
    üîó
    • \(1 - u_6(t)\)
      üîó
    • This turns OFF at \(t = 6\text{,}\) which is the opposite of what we want.
    • \(u_6(t)\)
      üîó
    • That‚Äôs right, \(u_6(t)\) flips ON at \(t = 6\) and stays ON from there.
    • \(u(t - 6)\)
      üîó
    • Close! This is also correct, but we prefer to write it as \(u_6(t)\) for clarity in this context.
    • \(u_0(t) - u_6(t)\)
      üîó
    • This turns ON from \(t = 0\) to \(t = 6\text{,}\) then OFF. Not what we want here.
    üîó
    (b) Unit Step Behavior.
    What is the only possible set of values that any unit step function \(u_c(t)\) can take?
    üîó
    • {1 only}
      üîó
    • Unit step functions are OFF before \(t = c\text{,}\) so they can also be 0.
    • {0, 1}
      üîó
    • Correct! Unit step functions are always either 0 (OFF) or 1 (ON).
    • {any real number}
      üîó
    • No, unit step functions are discrete switches, not continuous functions.
    • {any nonnegative number}
      üîó
    • Even though the values are nonnegative, only 0 and 1 are ever used.
    üîó
    (c) Rewriting an ON Interval.
    Which expression represents a function that is ON from \(t = 1\) to \(t = 4\) and OFF otherwise?
    üîó
    • \(u_1(t) - u_4(t)\)
      üîó
    • This expression turns ON at \(t = 1\) and back OFF at \(t = 4\text{.}\)
    • \(u_1(t)\)
      üîó
    • This stays ON after \(t = 1\text{,}\) but never switches OFF at \(t = 4\text{.}\)
    • \(1 - u_4(t)\)
      üîó
    • This is ON before \(t = 4\text{,}\) but never turns ON at \(t = 1\text{.}\)
    • \(u_4(t) - u_1(t)\)
      üîó
    • That would actually be negative during the interval from \(t = 1\) to \(t = 4\text{,}\) not what we want.
    üîó
    (d) Multiplying by a Step Function.
    What is the effect of multiplying a function \(f(t)\) by \(u_6(t)\text{?}\)
    üîó
    • It shifts \(f(t)\) 6 units to the left.
      üîó
    • Shifting the input would require \(f(t + 6)\text{,}\) not multiplication by \(u_6(t)\text{.}\)
    • It keeps \(f(t)\) OFF before \(t = 6\) and turns it ON at \(t = 6\text{.}\)
      üîó
    • Correct. \(u_6(t)\) acts as a switch that activates \(f(t)\) at \(t = 6\text{.}\)
    • It subtracts 6 from the output of \(f(t)\text{.}\)
      üîó
    • Step functions don‚Äôt affect the output directly, they control when the function is active.
    • It delays \(f(t)\) by 6 units.
      üîó
    • Delaying would require rewriting the input as \(f(t - 6)\text{,}\) not just multiplying by \(u_6(t)\text{.}\)
    üîó
    (e) Equivalent to Piecewise Form.
    Which step-function expression is equivalent to the piecewise function
    \begin{equation*} f(t) = \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} 3, \amp 0 \le t \lt 2 \\ 0, \amp \text{otherwise} \end{array} \right. \end{equation*}
    üîó
    • \(3 \cdot (u_0(t) - u_2(t))\)
      üîó
    • This turns 3 ON at \(t = 0\) and OFF again at \(t = 2\text{.}\)
    • \(3 \cdot u_2(t)\)
      üîó
    • This turns ON at \(t = 2\text{,}\) not \(t = 0\text{.}\)
    • \(3 \cdot (1 - u_2(t))\)
      üîó
    • This would be ON before \(t = 2\text{,}\) but it wouldn‚Äôt stop at \(t = 0\text{,}\) it‚Äôs ON too early.
    • \(3 \cdot u_0(t)\)
      üîó
    • This is ON at \(t = 0\text{,}\) but it stays ON forever, it doesn‚Äôt turn OFF at \(t = 2\text{.}\)
    üîó
    (f) Reading a Step Expression.
    Which interval does \(5 \cdot (1 - u_6(t))\) activate over?
    üîó
    • \(t \lt 6\)
      üîó
    • \(1 - u_6(t)\) is ON before \(t = 6\) and OFF afterward.
    • \(t \gt 6\)
      üîó
    • That would be true of \(u_6(t)\text{,}\) not its reversal.
    • \(t = 6\) only
      üîó
    • This is a step function, not a spike, it applies to intervals, not points.
    • \(t \ge 6\)
      üîó
    • Again, that‚Äôs when \(u_6(t)\) turns ON, not when its complement is active.

    (g) Laplace of a Shifted Step.
    What is \(\lap{u_3(t)}\text{?}\)
    üîó
    • \(\ds \dfrac{e^{-3s}}{s}\)
      üîó
    • This is the standard formula for the Laplace transform of \(u_c(t)\text{.}\)
    • \(\ds \dfrac{1}{s + 3}\)
      üîó
    • This is the transform of \(e^{-3t}\text{,}\) not a step function.
    • \(\ds \dfrac{1}{s} - e^{-3s}\)
      üîó
    • This isn‚Äôt a correct transformation of a step function, it doesn‚Äôt match the form.
    • \(\ds \dfrac{s}{e^{3s}}\)
      üîó
    • This inverts the formula incorrectly, look carefully at the units and exponents.

    (h) Using the Shift Rule.
    Suppose \(\lap{f(t)} = F(s)\text{.}\) What is \(\lap{f(t)\cdot u_4(t)}\text{?}\)
    üîó
    • \(e^{-4s} \cdot \lap{f(t+4)}\)
      üîó
    • This is the shift rule in action: delay the function, then shift its input left.
    • \(e^{4s} \cdot F(s)\)
      üîó
    • The exponent should be negative, delaying adds \(e^{-cs}\text{.}\)
    • \(\lap{f(t - 4)}\)
      üîó
    • This shifts the function right but doesn‚Äôt match the form used in the shift rule.
    • \(\lap{f(t)} \cdot u_4(t)\)
      üîó
    • The Laplace transform applies to the whole product, you can‚Äôt apply it to one piece separately.
    üîó
    (i) Shifting Inside the Transform.
    Which of the following is equal to \(\lap{(t^2)\cdot u_2(t)}\text{?}\)
    üîó
    • \(e^{-2s} \cdot \lap{(t + 2)^2}\)
      üîó
    • This follows directly from the shift rule, replace \(t\) with \(t + 2\text{.}\)
    • \(e^{-2s} \cdot \lap{t^2}\)
      üîó
    • We must shift the input, \(t^2\) becomes \((t + 2)^2\text{.}\)
    • \(\lap{t^2} \cdot u_2(t)\)
      üîó
    • You can‚Äôt break apart the transform like this, it applies to the whole product.
    • \(e^{-2s} \cdot (t^2 + 4t + 4)\)
      üîó
    • That‚Äôs the shifted polynomial, but we want the Laplace transform of that expression, not the polynomial itself.
    üîó

    • It is active only for \(t \ge 1\text{.}\)
      üîó
    • No, \(u_0(t)\) activates the function at \(t = 0\text{.}\)
    • It is always equal to \(2t\text{.}\)
      üîó
    • Only the first term is \(2t\text{,}\) and it gets overridden by the later terms.
    • The function turns off completely at \(t = 1\text{.}\)
      üîó
    • It doesn‚Äôt turn OFF at \(t = 1\text{,}\) it switches to a new expression.

    \(\begin{array}{l} y'' + y = g(t) \\ y(0) = 0,\ y'(0) = 0 \end{array} \quad\) where \(\quad g(t) = \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} 2t, \amp 0 \le t \lt 1 \\ 3, \amp 1 \le t \lt 4 \\ 0, \amp t \ge 4 \end{array} \right.\)
    Solution.
    Step 1 ‚Äî Into the Laplace Domain.
    Recall that our first step is to apply the Laplace transform of both sides:
    \begin{equation*} \lap{y'' + y} = \lap{g(t)} \end{equation*}
    üîó
    It‚Äôs clear how to handle the left-side, but the right-side requires the transform of a piecewise function. Before we can use the rules we derived in this chapter, we first need to write \(g(t)\) in unit step form.
    üîó
    Apply the Laplace transform to both sides of the differential equation:
    \begin{equation*} y'' + y = \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} 0, \amp t \lt 1 \\ 3t, \amp 1 \le t \lt 2 \\ 0, \amp t \ge 2 \end{array} \right., \qquad y(0) = 0,\quad y'(0) = 0 \end{equation*}
    üîó
    The forcing function is active only from \(t = 1\) to \(t = 2\text{,}\) so we rewrite it using a unit step window:
    \begin{equation*} g(t) = 3t \cdot \left(u_1(t) - u_2(t)\right). \end{equation*}
    üîó
    Apply the Laplace transform to both sides of the DE:
    \begin{equation*} \lap{y'' + y} = \lap{3t \cdot (u_1(t) - u_2(t))}. \end{equation*}
    On the left-hand side, using \(y(0) = y'(0) = 0\text{,}\) we have:
    \begin{equation*} s^2 Y(s) + Y(s). \end{equation*}
    üîó
    On the right-hand side, distribute the Laplace transform:
    \begin{equation*} \lap{3t \cdot (u_1(t) - u_2(t))} = 3\lap{t \cdot u_1(t)} - 3\lap{t \cdot u_2(t)}. \end{equation*}
    Use the shift rule for each term:
    \begin{gather*} \lap{t \cdot u_1(t)} = e^{-s} \lap{t + 1} = e^{-s} \cdot \lap{t + 1} \\ \lap{t \cdot u_2(t)} = e^{-2s} \lap{t + 2} = e^{-2s} \cdot \lap{t + 2} \end{gather*}
    Now apply linearity and known transforms:
    \begin{gather*} \lap{t + c} = \lap{t} + c\lap{1} = \dfrac{1}{s^2} + \dfrac{c}{s} \end{gather*}
    So:
    \begin{align*} \lap{3t \cdot (u_1 - u_2)} \amp = 3e^{-s}\left(\dfrac{1}{s^2} + \dfrac{1}{s}\right) - 3e^{-2s}\left(\dfrac{1}{s^2} + \dfrac{2}{s}\right) \end{align*}
    üîó
    Combining both sides, we arrive at the Laplace equation:
    \begin{equation*} (s^2 + 1)Y(s) = 3e^{-s}\left(\dfrac{1}{s^2} + \dfrac{1}{s}\right) - 3e^{-2s}\left(\dfrac{1}{s^2} + \dfrac{2}{s}\right). \end{equation*}
    This completes Step 1.
    üîó
    Now that we‚Äôve built the Laplace equation, the remaining steps are familiar. We‚Äôll solve for \(Y(s)\text{,}\) simplify, and invert. But there‚Äôs one twist: the presence of \(e^{-cs}\) terms means we‚Äôll need special care when taking the inverse Laplace transform. Let‚Äôs explore that next.
    üîó
    üîó
    Step 2 ‚Äî Solving in the Laplace Domain.
    üîó
    Step 3 ‚Äî Leaving the Laplace Domain.
    Once we‚Äôve solved for \(Y(s)\) in the Laplace domain, the final challenge is to take the inverse Laplace transform. When the forcing function was continuous, this step relied on partial fractions and matching terms from the Laplace table. That still works here, but now we‚Äôll also see terms involving exponentials like \(e^{-cs}F(s)\text{.}\)
    üîó
    These exponentials come directly from transforming unit step expressions. To move back to the \(t\)-domain, we need a new tool: a rule that undoes the exponential shift.

    6.

    Preparation ‚Äî Forcing Function in Step Form.
    We begin by rewriting \(g(t)\) with unit step functions. Since it equals \(3\) on \([0,1)\text{,}\) \(0\) on \([1,4)\text{,}\) and \(1\) after \(t=4\text{:}\)
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} g(t) = 3(u_0(t) - u_1(t)) + (1 - 3)(u_4(t)) \end{equation*}
    which simplifies to:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} g(t) = 3u_0(t) - 3u_1(t) + u_4(t). \end{equation*}
    üîó
    Step 1 ‚Äî Into the Laplace Domain.
    Take the Laplace transform of both sides:
    \begin{equation*} \lap{y''} + 2\lap{y'} = \lap{3u_0(t) - 3u_1(t) + u_4(t)}. \end{equation*}
    Using the initial conditions \(y(0)=0\) and \(y'(0)=0\text{,}\) the left side becomes:
    \begin{equation*} s^2 Y(s) + 2s Y(s). \end{equation*}
    Apply L\(_9\) to each step term:
    \begin{equation*} \lap{3u_0(t)} = \dfrac{3}{s}, \quad \lap{-3u_1(t)} = -\dfrac{3e^{-s}}{s}, \quad \lap{u_4(t)} = \dfrac{e^{-4s}}{s}. \end{equation*}
    This gives the Laplace-domain equation:
    \begin{equation*} (s^2 + 2s)Y(s) = \dfrac{3}{s} - \dfrac{3e^{-s}}{s} + \dfrac{e^{-4s}}{s}. \end{equation*}
    üîó
    üîó
    Step 2 ‚Äî Solve for \(Y(s)\).
    Factor out \(Y(s)\) and divide through by \((s^2 + 2s)\text{:}\)
    üîó
    \begin{gather*} Y(s) = \dfrac{1}{s(s + 2)}\left( 3 - 3e^{-s} + e^{-4s} \right). \end{gather*}
    To prepare for inversion, write:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} Y(s) = F(s)\big( 3 - 3e^{-s} + e^{-4s} \big), \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} F(s) = \dfrac{1}{s(s + 2)}. \end{equation*}
    We‚Äôll find \(f(t) = \ilap{F(s)}\) so we can invert \(Y(s)\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Solution. Partial Fraction Decomposition of \(F(s)\)
    Decompose:
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{1}{s(s + 2)} = \dfrac{A}{s} + \dfrac{B}{s + 2}. \end{equation*}
    Multiply through:
    \begin{equation*} 1 = A(s + 2) + B(s). \end{equation*}
    Sub in convenient \(s\)-values:
    • At \(s = 0\text{:}\) \(1 = 2A\) ‚Üí \(A = \dfrac{1}{2}\)
      üîó
    • At \(s = -2\text{:}\) \(1 = -2B\) ‚Üí \(B = -\dfrac{1}{2}\)
      üîó
    Thus:
    \begin{equation*} F(s) = \dfrac{1}{2}\dfrac{1}{s} - \dfrac{1}{2}\dfrac{1}{s+2}. \end{equation*}
    üîó
    Take the inverse Laplace:
    \begin{equation*} f(t) = \ilap{F(s)} = \dfrac{1}{2} - \dfrac{1}{2}e^{-2t}. \end{equation*}
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó
    Step 3 ‚Äî Back to the Time Domain.
    Each exponential factor in \(Y(s)\) corresponds to a shifted version of \(f(t)\) multiplied by the appropriate step function using L\(_11\).
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \ilap{Y(s)} = 3f(t) - 3f(t-1)u_1(t) + f(t-4)u_4(t). \end{equation*}
    Substitute \(f(t) = \dfrac{1}{2} - \dfrac{1}{2}e^{-2t}\) into each term:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} y(t) = 3\left(\dfrac{1}{2} - \dfrac{1}{2}e^{-2t}\right) - 3\left(\dfrac{1}{2} - \dfrac{1}{2}e^{-2(t-1)}\right)u_1(t) + \left(\dfrac{1}{2} - \dfrac{1}{2}e^{-2(t-4)}\right)u_4(t). \end{equation*}
    This simplifies to the complete solution for all \(t \ge 0\text{.}\) It clearly shows how each piece of the forcing term triggers its own shifted response in the solution.
