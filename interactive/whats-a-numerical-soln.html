    Section What is a Numerical Solution?

    Most of what we‚Äôve done, so far, has been focused on finding exact solutions to differential equations, like \(y(t) = e^{-3t} \sin(2t)\text{.}\) This kind of solution is called an analytic solution, or sometimes a closed-form solution. It is valuable because it expresses \(y(t)\) as a formula-like structure that you can plug in any value of \(t\) and instantly get the exact \(y\)-value.
    üîó
    However, many differential equations, such as
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} y' = e^{-y^2}, \quad y(0) = 0. \end{equation*}
    simply don‚Äôt have a tidy closed-form solution. In those cases, we switch tools. Instead of searching for an exact formula, we use a numerical method. A numerical method doesn‚Äôt hand you \(y(t)\) as a formula‚Äîit builds an approximation, one step at a time, starting from what you know and using the differential equation to predict what happens next. The result is a numerical solution.
    üîó

    Checkpoint 99. üìñ‚ùìAnalytic Solution, AKA ‚Ä¶.

    What is another name for an analytic solution?
    • Closed-form solution
    • Yes , a closed-form solution is another name for an analytic solution.
    • Approximate solution
    • No , an approximate solution refers to a numerical solution.
    • Continuous solution
    • Not all analytic solutions are continuous , and not all continuous solutions are analytic.
    • Systematic solution
    • While you may solve analytically using a systematic process, this is not a name for an analytic solution.
    üîó

    Analytic vs. Numerical Solutions.

    To see the difference between analytic and numerical solutions, consider the initial value problem:
    üîó
    \begin{equation} y' = y, \quad y(0) = 1, \quad 0 \le t \le 2\tag{25} \end{equation}
    Analytically, this equation has the elegant solution:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} y(t) = e^t\text{.} \end{equation*}
    With this formula, you can calculate the exact value at any time. For example, \(t = 0.65\) gives
    \begin{equation*} y(0.65) = e^{0.65} \approx 1.915540829...\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Checkpoint 100. üìñ‚ùìRecognizing a Numerical Solution.

    When you say you‚Äôve found a ‚Äúnumerical solution‚Äù to an initial value problem, what do you actually have?
    • A list of values approximating \(y(t)\) at specific times
    • Correct‚Äînumerical solutions are tables of approximate values, not formulas.
    • A formula expressing \(y(t)\) using functions like \(\sin t\) or \(e^t\)
    • No, that describes an analytic solution.
    • An exact solution valid for all \(t\)
    • No, numerical solutions are approximations.

    üîó
    The analytic solution gives a smooth curve for every \(t\text{.}\) The numerical solution gives dots‚Äîa sequence of approximations. Connect those dots and you get a picture of the solution‚Äôs shape, even though no formula was found.

    üìù 101. Precision vs Practicality.

    Numerical solutions are approximations. They carry small errors, but in exchange, they let us handle equations that analytic methods can‚Äôt touch. This trade-off between perfect precision and practical usefulness is at the heart of numerical methods.

    Why Do We Need Numerical Methods?

    At first glance, analytic solutions might seem ‚Äúbetter‚Äù than numerical ones. But there are important reasons why numerical methods aren‚Äôt just useful‚Äîthey‚Äôre essential:
    üîó
    1. Many equations simply don‚Äôt have a closed-form solution.
      üîó
    2. Even when they do, the formula might be so complicated it‚Äôs impractical.
      üîó
    3. Numerical methods are easier to tweak if the model or data changes.
      üîó
