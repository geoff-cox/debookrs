    Section Laplace Transform Rules for Step Functions

    Up to this point, you‚Äôve seen how unit step functions can ‚Äúswitch‚Äù pieces of a function ON and OFF at the right times. But to actually use these functions in solving differential equations, we need to understand how they behave when we take their Laplace transform.
    üîó
    This section develops the Laplace transform rules for unit step functions:
    üîó
    • \(\lap{u_c(t)}\) ‚Äì how to transform a single step switch.
      üîó
    • \(\lap{f(t) u_c(t)}\) ‚Äì how to transform a function that is switched ON at \(t=c\text{.}\)
      üîó
    • \(\lap{f(t-c) u_c(t)}\) ‚Äì how to transform a shifted function that starts at \(t=c\text{.}\)
      üîó
    These rules are the backbone for handling piecewise functions with Laplace methods.
    üîó

    Checkpoint 203. üìñ‚ùì How Do Inputs Shift?

      The function \(f(t + 2)\) is the same as \(f(t)\) shifted left by 2 units.
      üîó

    • True.

    • Replacing \(t\) with \(t + c\) shifts the graph to the left by \(c\) units. This is the opposite direction compared to \(f(t - c)\text{.}\)

    • False.

    • Replacing \(t\) with \(t + c\) shifts the graph to the left by \(c\) units. This is the opposite direction compared to \(f(t - c)\text{.}\)
    üîó

    Subsection Transform of \(u_c(t)\)

    We begin with the Laplace transform of a single shifted unit step switch, \(u_c(t)\text{.}\) Finding its transform lays the groundwork for the more general rules that follow.
    üîó
    The Laplace transform of a shifted unit step function is as follows:
    üîó

    üìú Laplace Transform of \(u_c(t)\).

    \begin{gather*} \text{L}_9:\quad\lap{u_c(t)} = \frac{e^{-cs}}{s}, \qquad s \gt 0. \end{gather*}

    üí° Derivation of \(\lap{u_c(t)}\).

    Start with the definition of the Laplace transform:
    \begin{equation*} \lap{u_c(t)} = \int_{0}^{\infty} u_c(t) \, e^{-st} \, dt. \end{equation*}
    üîó
    Recall that:
    \begin{equation*} u_c(t) = \begin{cases} 0, \amp t \lt c \\ 1, \amp t \ge c \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    which means the integrand is zero until \(t=c\) and equals \(e^{-st}\) afterward.
    üîó
    That trims our integral down since:
    \begin{equation*} \lap{u_c(t)} = \os{\large t\ \lt\ c}{\overline{\int_0^{c} (0)\ e^{-st}\ dt}} + \os{\large t\ \ge\ c}{\overline{\int_c^{\infty} (1)\ e^{-st}\ dt}} = \int_c^{\infty} e^{-st}\ dt \end{equation*}
    üîó
    Evaluate the improper integral as a limit:
    \begin{align*} \lap{u_c(t)} \amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \int_{c}^{b} e^{-st}\ dt\\ \amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[ \frac{-1}{s} e^{-st} \right]_{c}^{b} = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left( \frac{-1}{s}\big(e^{-sb} - e^{-sc}\big) \right) \end{align*}
    üîó
    As long as \(s \gt 0\text{,}\) the term \(e^{-sb}\) goes to zero as \(b \to \infty\text{,}\) leaving:
    \begin{equation*} \lap{u_c(t)} = \frac{e^{-cs}}{s}. \end{equation*}
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó
    This result shows that the shifted unit step function introduces an exponential factor \(e^{-cs}\) in the Laplace domain. You will use it to transform terms of the form \(k u_{c}(t)\text{,}\) where \(k\) is a constant. For example,
    \begin{equation*} \lap{6 u_3(t)} = 6\lap{u_3(t)} = \frac{6e^{-3s}}{s} \end{equation*}
    üîó

    Checkpoint 204. üìñ‚ùìFinding the Laplace Transform of \(u_c(t)\).

    What is the Laplace transform of \(u_5(t)\text{?}\)
    üîó
    • \(\ds \frac{e^{-5}}{s}\)
      üîó
    • Close, but \(s\) is a variable in the Laplace domain, it doesn‚Äôt get replaced by a number here.
    • \(\ds \frac{e^{-5s}}{s}\)
      üîó
    • That‚Äôs the formula: \(\lap{u_c(t)} = \frac{e^{-cs}}{s}\text{,}\) and here \(c = 5\text{.}\)
    • \(\ds \frac{1}{s + 5}\)
      üîó
    • That‚Äôs the transform of \(e^{-5t}\text{,}\) not a step function. The exponential in \(u_c(t)\) appears in the numerator, not the exponent of the input.
    • \(\ds \frac{s}{e^{-5s}}\)
      üîó
    • That‚Äôs an incorrect inversion of the formula. The exponential should be in the numerator, and \(s\) in the denominator.
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection Transform of \(f(t) u_c(t)\)

    The next rule provides a formula for the product of a step function and a function of \(t\) and will be the primary tool used when forward transforming a piecewise function.
    üîó

    üìú Laplace Transform of \(f(t) u_c(t)\).

    \begin{gather*} \text{L}_{10}:\quad\lap{f(t) u_c(t)} = e^{-cs} \lap{f(t + c)} \end{gather*}

    üí° Derivation of \(\lap{f(t)\ u_c(t)}\).

    Start with the Laplace transform definition:
    \begin{equation*} \lap{f(t) u_c(t)} = \int_{0}^{\infty} f(t) u_c(t) e^{-st}\ dt. \end{equation*}
    üîó
    Since \(u_c(t) = 0\) for \(t \lt c\text{,}\) the integral simplifies to:
    \begin{equation*} \lap{f(t)\cdot u_c(t)} = \int_c^\infty f(t)\ e^{-st}\ dt. \end{equation*}
    üîó
    This new integral looks a lot like the Laplace transform of \(f(t)\text{,}\) but it starts at \(t = c\) instead of \(t = 0\text{.}\) Luckily, we can shift the graph of \(f(t)\ e^{-st}\) to the left \(c\) units to force the integral start back at \(t = 0\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Note: Shifting a function \(c\) units to the left means adding \(c\) to all of the \(t\) inputs:
    \begin{equation*} \text{before shift:}\quad f(t)\ e^{-st}\qquad\text{after shift:}\quad f(t + c)\ e^{-s(t + c)} \end{equation*}
    üîó
    Using this idea, leads to the desired transform:
    üîó
    \begin{align*} \int_{c}^{\infty} f(t)\ e^{-st}\ dt \amp \os{\large\DLBb \text{A}}{=} \int_{0}^{\infty} f(t + c)\ e^{-s(t + c)}\ dt\\ \amp \os{\large\DLBb \text{B}}{=} \int_0^\infty f(t + c) \left(e^{-st} \cdot e^{-sc}\right)\ dt\\ \amp \os{\large\DLBb \text{C}}{=} e^{-cs} \int_0^\infty f(t + c) e^{-st}\ dt\\ \amp = e^{-cs} \lap{f(t + c)} \end{align*}

    \(\DLBb \text{A}, \text{B}, \text{C}\) Details.

    \(\DLBb \text{A}\ \): Shifting \(f(t)e^{-st}\) to the left \(c\) units makes it
    \begin{equation*} f(t + c)\ e^{-s(t + c)} \end{equation*}
    and starts the integral at \(t=0\text{.}\)
    üîó
    \(\DLBb \text{B}\ \): Exponential Property \(e^{-s(t + c)} = e^{-st} e^{-sc}\)
    üîó
    \(\DLBb \text{C}\ \): Factor out the constant, \(e^{-sc}\text{,}\) as we are integrating in \(t\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó
    Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} \lap{f(t) \, u_c(t)} = e^{-cs} \lap{f(t + c)}. \end{equation*}
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó
    Let‚Äôs try this on a few examples to see how the rule works in practice.
    üîó

    üåå Example 205. Transform of \((2 - t)\cdot u_3(t)\).

    Compute the Laplace transform of \((2 - t) u_3(t)\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Solution. \(\rightarrow \lap{(2-t)\cdot u_3(t)}\)
    For this problem, we have \(c=3\text{.}\) So the rule becomes:
    \begin{equation*} \lap{f(t)\cdot u_3(t)} = e^{-3s} \lap{f(t + 3)}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where, in our case, \(f(t) = 2-t\text{,}\) so
    \begin{equation*} f(t+3) = 2-(t+3) = -t-1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    üîó
    Plugging \(f(t)\) and \(f(t+3)\) into the rule shows us that
    \begin{equation*} \lap{(2-t)\cdot u_3(t)} = e^{-3s} \lap{-t-1}. \qquad \text{(‚≠ê)} \end{equation*}
    üîó
    So all we have left to do is compute \(\lap{-t-1}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \lap{-t-1} = -\lap{t}-\lap{1} = -\frac1{s^2}-\frac1{s} \end{equation*}
    and plug it into (‚≠ê), to complete the transform:
    \begin{equation*} \lap{(2-t)\, u_3(t)} = -e^{-2s}\left(\frac{1 + s}{s^2}\right). \end{equation*}
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection Transform of \(f(t-c) u_c(t)\)

    Finally, we cover the last rule that will be particularly useful for inverting expressions in the Laplace domain (i.e., step 3) that are associated with step functions.
    üîó

    üìú Laplace Transform of \(f(t-c) u_c(t)\).

    \begin{gather*} \text{L}_{11}:\quad\lap{f(t-c) u_c(t)} = e^{-cs} F(s), \quad\text{where}\quad F(s) = \lap{f(t)} \end{gather*}

    Derivation of \(\lap{f(t-c) u_c(t)}\).

    In terms of the definition of the Laplace transform, we want to show:
    \begin{align*} \os{\ds\lap{f(t-c) u_c(t)}}{\os{\ds\downarrow}{\overline{\int_{0}^{\infty} f(t-c) u_c(t) e^{-st}\ dt}}} \amp = e^{-cs} \os{\ds \lap{f(t)}}{\os{\ds\downarrow}{\overline{\int_{0}^{\infty} f(t) e^{-st}\ dt}}} \end{align*}
    üîó
    To show this, we will start with the integral on the left and show it leads to the integral on the right.
    üîó
    \begin{align*} \int_{0}^{\infty} f(t-c) u_c(t) e^{-st}\ dt \amp \os{\large\DLBb \text{A}}{=} \int_{c}^{\infty} f(t-c) e^{-st}\ dt\\ \amp \os{\large\DLBb \text{B}}{=} \int_{0}^{\infty} f(t)\ e^{-s(t+c)}\ dt \\ \amp \os{\large\DLBb \text{C}}{=} e^{-cs} \int_0^\infty f(t) e^{-st} \, du \end{align*}

    \(\DLBb \text{A}, \text{B}, \text{C}\) Details.

    \(\DLBb\text{A}\ \): \(u_c(t)=0\) on \([0,c)\) makes the lower limit of the integral \(c\text{.}\)
    üîó
    \(\DLBb\text{B}\ \): Shifting \(f(t-c)e^{-st}\) to the left \(c\) units makes it \(f(t)\ e^{-s(t + c)}\) and starts the integral at \(t=0\text{.}\)
    üîó
    \(\DLBb\text{C}\ \): Use the exponential property \(e^{-s(t + c)} = e^{-st} e^{-sc}\) and factor out the constant, \(e^{-sc}\text{,}\) as we are integrating in \(t\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó
    Thus, we have shown
    \begin{equation*} \lap{f(t-c) u_c(t)} = e^{-cs} F(s) \end{equation*}
    where \(F(s) = \lap{f(t)}\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó
    Although you can use this for forward transforms, its main utility is for backward transforms since it only requires you to match terms that look like
    \begin{equation*} e^{-cs}F(s) \end{equation*}
    where \(F(s)\) is one of the common Laplace transforms from the same table. We will explore later when we invert terms in the Laplace domain containing \(e^{-cs}\text{.}\)
    üîó
