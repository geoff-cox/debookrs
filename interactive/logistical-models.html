    Subsection The Logistic Model

    One of the most famous autonomous differential equations is the logistic model, which describes population growth when there are limited resources. The equation is:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dP}{dt} = rP\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right), \end{equation*}
    where \(P\) is the population at time \(t\text{,}\) \(r\) is the growth rate, and \(K\) is the carrying capacity, the maximum population that the environment can sustain.
    üîó
    This equation has two equilibrium points:
    üîó
    • \(P = 0\) (extinction),
      üîó
    • \(P = K\) (population stabilizes at carrying capacity).
      üîó
    The phase line for this model shows:
    üîó
    • At \(P = 0\text{,}\) the equilibrium is unstable, any small population increase leads to growth.
      üîó
    • At \(P = K\text{,}\) the equilibrium is stable, populations near this value settle toward it.
      üîó
    • When \(0 < P < K\text{,}\) populations increase.
      üîó
    • When \(P > K\text{,}\) populations decrease.
      üîó
    This model captures how populations grow rapidly when small, slow down as they approach the carrying capacity, and eventually level off.
    üîó

    üåå Example 96. Logistic Model Analysis.

    Consider the logistic equation
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dP}{dt} = rP\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    • Identify the equilibrium points.
      üîó
    • Sketch the phase line and classify the stability of each equilibrium.
      üîó
    • Explain how this model describes long-term population behavior.
      üîó
    üîó
    Solution.
    The equilibrium points are \(P = 0\) and \(P = K\text{.}\)
    üîó
    On the phase line:
    üîó
    • \(P = 0\) is a source (unstable).
      üîó
    • \(P = K\) is a sink (stable).
      üîó
    Populations grow when between \(0\) and \(K\text{,}\) and shrink when above \(K\text{.}\) The model predicts eventual stabilization at the carrying capacity.
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó
    The logistic model is sometimes written in a slightly different form:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dP}{dt} = kP\left(1 - \frac{P}{N}\right), \end{equation*}
    where \(N\) is the carrying capacity and \(k\) is the growth-rate constant. This form emphasizes that growth is proportional to both the current population and the remaining resource capacity \((1 - P/N)\text{.}\)
    üîó
    This is still a first-order autonomous differential equation, but unlike the exponential model, it is nonlinear since the right-hand side is not a linear function of \(P\text{.}\)
    üîó
    The analysis is the same: the equilibria are \(P = 0\) and \(P = N\text{.}\) The phase line tells us their stability, just like before.
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection A Modified Logistic Model

    Let‚Äôs now consider a variation on the logistic model that includes an important ecological detail, what happens to very small populations. The standard logistic model assumes that populations always grow when small, but in reality, sparse populations can have difficulty finding mates, making growth negative at low levels.
    üîó
    As an example, consider pine squirrels in the Rocky Mountains. These animals are territorial, and both overcrowding and underpopulation can limit their growth:
    üîó
    • If the population is too large, territorial conflict slows growth or even causes decline.
      üîó
    • If the population is too small, individuals may not find mates, also resulting in decline.
      üîó
    • If the population is exactly zero, it stays at zero forever.
      üîó
    Let‚Äôs model this situation. We define:
    üîó
    • \(t\) = time,
      üîó
    • \(S(t)\) = squirrel population at time \(t\text{,}\)
      üîó
    • \(k\) = growth-rate coefficient,
      üîó
    • \(N\) = carrying capacity (too large),
      üîó
    • \(M\) = sparsity threshold (too small).
      üîó
    We want the growth rate \(g(S)\) to satisfy the following:
    üîó
    • \(g(S) < 0\) if \(S < M\) or \(S > N\text{,}\)
      üîó
    • \(g(S) > 0\) if \(M < S < N\text{,}\)
      üîó
    • \(g(0) = 0\text{.}\)
      üîó
    A function with this behavior might look like:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dS}{dt} = kS\left(1 - \frac{S}{N}\right)\left(\frac{S}{M} - 1\right). \end{equation*}
    This is the modified logistic model. It adjusts the standard model to account for low-population risks.
    üîó
    Let‚Äôs analyze it. The equilibrium points occur where the right-hand side equals zero:
    üîó
    • \(S = 0\) (extinction),
      üîó
    • \(S = M\) (sparsity threshold),
      üîó
    • \(S = N\) (carrying capacity).
      üîó
    The phase line tells us:
    üîó
    • \(S = 0\) is a stable equilibrium (a sink).
      üîó
    • \(S = M\) is an unstable equilibrium (a source).
      üîó
    • \(S = N\) is a stable equilibrium (a sink).
      üîó
    This model better captures real-world behavior by showing that small populations might go extinct if they fall below the sparsity threshold.
    üîó
