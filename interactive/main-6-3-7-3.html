    What classification of DEs be solved by applying an integrating factor?
    Answer.

    3. Select-the-Best-Answer.

      In solving the equation \(\ds y' + P(x)y = Q(x)\text{,}\) what form does the general solution take after direct integration?
    • \(\ds y(x) = ∫Q(x)dx\text{.}\)
    • Incorrect. This is not the correct form. You need to account for the integrating factor as well.
    • \(\ds y(x) = e^{∫P(x) dx} ∫e^{-∫P(x)dx} Q(x) dx\text{.}\)
    • Correct! This is the general solution after applying the integrating factor and performing direct integration.
    • \(\ds y(x) = ∫e^{P(x)} Q(x) dx\text{.}\)
    • Incorrect. The correct general solution includes the integrating factor in both the exponent and the integral.
    • \(\ds y(x) = Ce^{Q(x)}\text{.}\)
    • Incorrect. This form does not represent the general solution to this type of equation.

    8.

    Answer
    (a) Role of the Integrating Factor.
      Why is an integrating factor used in solving differential equations?
    • To modify the equation so that one side becomes a perfect derivative.
    • Correct! The integrating factor enables us to rewrite the equation in a form that allows direct integration.
    • To eliminate the derivative term completely.
    • Incorrect. The integrating factor does not eliminate derivatives but reorganizes the equation.
    • To factor the differential equation into linear terms.
    • Incorrect. The integrating factor does not factor the equation in the algebraic sense.
    • To make the equation homogeneous.
    • Incorrect. The integrating factor does not necessarily make an equation homogeneous.
    (b) Forming a Single Derivative.
      What condition must hold for the left-hand side of the differential equation to become a single derivative?
    • The function \(\mu(x)\) must satisfy the equation \(\frac{d\mu}{dx} = 2\mu\text{.}\)
    • Correct! This condition ensures that multiplying by \(\mu(x)\) allows the product rule to be reversed.
    • The function \(\mu(x)\) must be a constant.
    • Incorrect. If \(\mu(x)\) were constant, it would not allow the left-hand side to transform into a derivative.
    • The equation must be rewritten in homogeneous form first.
    • Incorrect. The integrating factor method applies to nonhomogeneous equations as well.
    • The right-hand side must be equal to zero.
    • Incorrect. The right-hand side does not need to be zero for the integrating factor method to work.
    (c) Final Step in Solving.
      After rewriting the equation as \(\frac{d}{dx} [e^{2x} y] = 5e^{2x}\text{,}\) what is the next step?
    • Apply the product rule to expand the left-hand side.
    • Incorrect. The product rule was already reversed to reach this form; expanding it again would undo progress.
    • Integrate both sides to solve for \(y\text{.}\)
    • Correct! Now that the left-hand side is a derivative, we integrate both sides to solve for \(y\text{.}\)
    • Multiply by another integrating factor.
    • Incorrect. A second integrating factor is unnecessary.
    • Differentiate both sides to simplify the expression.
    • Incorrect. Further differentiation is not required at this stage.
    (d) Effect of Multiplying by the Integrating Factor.
      What happens when a first-order linear equation is multiplied by its integrating factor?
    • The left-hand side becomes a single derivative of a product.
    • Correct! The integrating factor allows the left-hand side to be rewritten as \(\frac{d}{dx} [\mu(x) y]\text{.}\)
    • The equation transforms into a separable equation.
    • Incorrect. The equation is rewritten for integration but does not necessarily become separable.
    • The integrating factor cancels out completely.
    • Incorrect. The integrating factor is essential to rewriting the equation.
    • The equation always becomes homogeneous.
    • Incorrect. The integrating factor method applies to both homogeneous and nonhomogeneous equations.
    (e) Effect of Multiplying by \(\mu(x)\).
      What is the purpose of multiplying both sides of the equation by \(\mu(x)\text{?}\)
    • To rewrite the left-hand side as a single derivative \(\frac{d}{dx}[\mu(x)y]\text{.}\)
    • Correct! The IF method works by transforming the equation into a form that can be directly integrated.
    • To eliminate the derivative term entirely.
    • Incorrect. The derivative remains but is rewritten as a total derivative.
    • To make the equation separable.
    • Incorrect. The IF method does not require separability.
    • To make \(Q(x)\) a constant function.
    • Incorrect. \(Q(x)\) is still a function of \(x\) after multiplication.
    (f) True-or-False.
      The differential equation, below, is in standard form.
      \begin{equation*} xy'+2xy=x^2 \end{equation*}

    • True.

    • This differential equation is not in the form \(y'+P(x)y=Q(x)\text{.}\)

    • False.

    • This differential equation is not in the form \(y'+P(x)y=Q(x)\text{.}\)
    Hint.
    Check to see that the differential equation is in the form \(y'+P(x)y=Q(x)\text{.}\)
    (g) Key Steps in the Solution Process.
      Which of the following is the correct sequence of steps when solving a first-order linear differential equation using the integrating factor method?
    • Rewrite the equation in standard form, compute the integrating factor, multiply the equation by the integrating factor, integrate both sides, and solve for \(y\text{.}\)
    • Correct! These are the essential steps in the integrating factor method.
    • Find the integrating factor, multiply by it, take the second derivative, and solve for \(y\text{.}\)
    • Incorrect. The second derivative is not needed in this method.
    • Integrate both sides first, find the integrating factor, then rewrite the equation in standard form.
    • Incorrect. The integrating factor is computed before integration.
    • Rewrite the equation in standard form, take the square root of both sides, and integrate.
    • Incorrect. The square root step is not part of the integrating factor method.
    (h) Choosing the Correct Integrating Factor.
      If a first-order linear equation is written as \(y' + 3y = x\text{,}\) what is the appropriate integrating factor?
    • \(e^{\int 3dx} = e^{3x}\)
    • Correct! The integrating factor is found using \(P(x) = 3\text{.}\)
    • \(e^{\int xdx} = e^{x^2/2}\)
    • Incorrect. The integrating factor should be based on \(P(x)\text{,}\) not \(Q(x)\text{.}\)
    • \(e^{-3x}\)
    • Incorrect. The exponent should be positive, following \(\mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) dx}\text{.}\)
    • \(3e^x\)
    • Incorrect. The integrating factor should be based on the integral of \(P(x)\text{,}\) not an arbitrary function.
    (i) Final Step in Solving the Equation.
      Once the equation is rewritten in the form \(\frac{d}{dx} [\mu(x)y] = \mu(x)Q(x)\text{,}\) what is the next step?
    • Differentiate both sides again.
    • Incorrect. Further differentiation is not needed at this stage.
    • Multiply both sides by \(\mu(x)\) again.
    • Incorrect. The equation is already multiplied by the integrating factor.
    • Integrate both sides to find \(y\text{.}\)
    • Correct! At this stage, integration is the key step to solving for \(y\text{.}\)
    • Factor the equation to solve for \(y\text{.}\)
    • Incorrect. The equation is already structured for integration, not factoring.

    3.
    \(\ds \frac{dy}{dx} - y = e^{3x} \)
    Answer.
    \(\ds y = \frac{1}{2}e^{3x} + Ce^x \)
    Solution.
    First we note that this DE is already in standard form for a first-order linear DE, \(\ds \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = f(x), \) with \(\ds P(x) = -1. \) Then we find the integrating factor \(\ds z. \)
    \begin{align*} z \amp = e^{\Big(\mbox{an antiderivative of }\Big)} \\ \amp = e^{\Big(\mbox{an antiderivative of }\Big)} \\ \amp = e^{-x} \end{align*}
    Now we multiply both sides of the DE by the integrating factor.
    \begin{align*} e^{-x}\cdot\frac{dy}{dx} - e^{-x}y \amp = e^{-x}e^{3x} \nonumber \\ e^{-x}\cdot\frac{dy}{dx} - e^{-x}y \amp = e^{2x}\nonumber \\ \underbrace{e^{-x}}_{f}\cdot\underbrace{\frac{dy}{dx}}_{g'} + \underbrace{- e^{-x}}_{f'}\cdot\underbrace{y}_{g} \amp = e^{2x} \label{eq1} \end{align*}
    Now we recall the product rule for derivatives: \(\ds \frac{d}{dx}\Big( f\cdot g \Big) = f\cdot g' + f' \cdot g. \) We note that the two terms on the left hand side of equation (\ref{eq1}) are the result of taking the derivative of the product. Hence we can undo the product rule as follows: \(\ds\frac{d}{dx}\Big(e^{-x}\cdot y \Big) = e^{2x}.\) Now we can integrate both sides of the equation and then solve for the dependent variable.
    \begin{align*} \int \frac{d}{dx}\Big(e^{-x}\cdot y \Big)dx \amp = \int e^{2x} dx \\ e^{-x}\cdot y \amp = \frac{1}{2}e^{2x} + C \\ y \amp = \frac{1}{2}\frac{e^{2x}}{e^{-x}} + \frac{C}{e^{-x}} \\ \amp = \frac{1}{2}e^{2x}e^x + Ce^x \\ \amp = \frac{1}{2}e^{3x} + Ce^x \end{align*}
    4.
    \(\ds \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{y}{x} + 2x + 1 \) , where \(\ds x>0 \)
    Answer.
    \(\ds y = 2x^2 + x\ln x + Cx \)
    Solution.
    First we put DE in standard form for a first-order linear DE \(\ds \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = f(x), \) so we can identify \(\ds P(x). \)
    \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} \amp = \frac{y}{x} + 2x + 1 \\ \frac{dy}{dx} - \frac{y}{x} \amp = 2x + 1 \\ \frac{dy}{dx} + \left(- \frac{1}{x}\right)y \amp = 2x + 1 \end{align*}
    Thus, \(\ds P(x) = - \frac{1}{x}. \) Then we find the integrating factor \(\ds z. \)
    \begin{align*} z \amp = e^{\Big(\mbox{an antiderivative of }\Big)} \\ \amp = e^{\mbox{\Big(an antiderivative of }\Big)} \\ \amp = e^{-\ln|x|} \\ \amp = e^{\ln (|x|^{-1})} \\ \amp = |x|^{-1} \\ \amp = \frac{1}{|x|} \\ \amp = \frac{1}{x} \mbox{ (since )} \end{align*}
    Now we multiply both sides of the DE by the integrating factor.
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{x}\cdot\frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{1}{x}\cdot \left(- \frac{1}{x}\right)y \amp = \frac{1}{x}(2x + 1) \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{x}\cdot\frac{dy}{dx} + \left(-\frac{1}{x^2}\right)\cdot y \amp = 2 + \frac{1}{x} \nonumber \\ \underbrace{\frac{1}{x}}_{f}\cdot\underbrace{\frac{dy}{dx}}_{g'} + \underbrace{\left(-\frac{1}{x^2}\right)}_{f'}\cdot \underbrace{y}_{g} \amp = 2 + \frac{1}{x} \label{eq2} \end{align*}
    Now we recall the product rule for derivatives: \(\ds \frac{d}{dx}\Big( f\cdot g \Big) = f\cdot g' + f' \cdot g. \) We note that the two terms on the left hand side of equation (\ref{eq2}) are the result of taking the derivative of the product. Hence we can undo the product rule as follows: \(\ds\frac{d}{dx}\Big(\frac{1}{x}\cdot y \Big) = 2 + \frac{1}{x}.\) Now we can integrate both sides of the equation and then solve for the dependent variable.
    \begin{align*} \int \frac{d}{dx}\Big(\frac{1}{x}\cdot y \Big)dx \amp = \int \left(2 + \frac{1}{x} \right)dx \\ \frac{1}{x}\cdot y \amp = 2x + \ln|x| + C \\ \frac{1}{x}\cdot y \amp = 2x + \ln(x) + C \mbox{ (since } \\ y \amp = x \cdot (2x + \ln(x) + C) \\ \amp = 2x^2 + x\ln x + Cx \end{align*}
    5.
    \(\ds \frac{dr}{d\theta} + r\tan \theta = \sec \theta, \) where \(\ds -\frac{\pi}{2} \le \theta \le \frac{\pi}{2} \)
    Answer.
    \(\ds r = \sin \theta + C\cos \theta \)
    Solution.
    First we put DE in standard form for a first-order linear DE \(\ds \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = f(x), \) so we can identify \(\ds P(x). \) Notice that for this DE \(\ds \theta \) is the independent variable (i.e., it is like the \(\ds x \) ) and \(\ds r \) is the dependent variable (i.e., it is like the \(\ds y \) ). Hence the standard form we will aim for is \(\ds \frac{dr}{d\theta} + P(\theta)r = f(\theta), \) and we want to identify \(\ds P(\theta). \)
    \begin{align*} \frac{dr}{d\theta} + r\tan \theta \amp = \sec \theta \\ \frac{dr}{d\theta} + \tan \theta\cdot r \amp = \sec \theta \end{align*}
    Thus, \(\ds P(\theta) = \tan \theta. \) Then we find the integrating factor \(\ds z. \)
    \begin{align*} z \amp = e^{\Big(\mbox{an antiderivative of }\Big)} \\ \amp = e^{\Big(\mbox{an antiderivative of }\Big)} \\ \amp = e^{-\ln|\cos \theta|} \\ \amp = e^{\ln (|\cos \theta|^{-1})} \\ \amp = |\cos \theta|^{-1} \\ \amp = \frac{1}{|cos \theta|} \\ \amp = \frac{1}{\cos \theta} \mbox{ (since )} \\ \amp = \sec \theta \end{align*}
    Now we multiply both sides of the DE by the integrating factor.
    \begin{align*} \sec\theta \cdot\frac{dr}{d\theta} + \sec \theta\cdot \tan \theta\cdot r \amp = \sec\theta\cdot \sec \theta \nonumber \\ \sec\theta \cdot\frac{dr}{d\theta} + \sec \theta \tan \theta\cdot r \amp = \sec^2\theta \nonumber \\ \underbrace{\sec\theta}_{f} \cdot\underbrace{\frac{dr}{d\theta}}_{g'} + \underbrace{\sec \theta \tan \theta}_{f'}\cdot \underbrace{r}_{g} \amp = \sec^2\theta \label{eq3} \end{align*}
    Now we recall the product rule for derivatives: \(\ds \frac{d}{dx}\Big( f\cdot g \Big) = f\cdot g' + f' \cdot g. \) We note that the two terms on the left hand side of equation (\ref{eq3}) are the result of taking the derivative of the product. Hence we can undo the product rule as follows: \(\ds\frac{d}{dx}\Big(\sec\theta\cdot r \Big) = \sec^2\theta.\) Now we can integrate both sides of the equation and then solve for the dependent variable.
    \begin{align*} \int \frac{d}{dx}\Big(\sec\theta\cdot r \Big)d\theta \amp = \int(\sec^2\theta) d\theta \\ \sec\theta\cdot r \amp = \tan \theta + C \\ r \amp = \frac{\tan \theta}{\sec\theta} + \frac{C}{\sec \theta} \\ \amp = \tan\theta \cdot \cos \theta + C\cos\theta \\ \amp = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}\cdot \cos\theta + C\cos\theta \\ \amp = \sin \theta + C\cos \theta \end{align*}
    6.
    \(\ds y\frac{dx}{dy} + 2x = 5y^3 \)
    Answer.
    \(\ds x = y^3 + \frac{C}{y^2} \)
    Solution.
    First we put DE in standard form for a first-order linear DE \(\ds \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = f(x), \) so we can identify \(\ds P(x). \) ...EXCEPT for this DE, the roles of \(\ds x \) and \(\ds y \) are switched. Notice that here \(\ds y \) is the independent variable and \(\ds x \) is the dependent variable. So we will aim for the standard for \(\ds \frac{dx}{dy} + P(y)x = f(y), \) and we want to identify \(\ds P(y). \)
    \begin{align*} y\frac{dx}{dy} + 2x \amp = 5y^3 \\ \frac{dx}{dy} + \frac{2}{y}x \amp = 5y^2 \end{align*}
    Thus, \(\ds P(y) = \frac{2}{y}. \) Then we find the integrating factor \(\ds z. \)
    \begin{align*} z \amp = e^{\Big(\mbox{an antiderivative of }\Big)} \\ \amp = e^{\Big(\mbox{an antiderivative of }\Big)} \\ \amp = e^{2\ln|y|} \\ \amp = e^{\ln(|y|^2)} \\ \amp = |y|^2 \\ \amp = y^2 \end{align*}
    Now we multiply both sides of the DE by the integrating factor.
    \begin{align*} y^2\cdot \frac{dx}{dy} + y^2\cdot \frac{2}{y}x \amp = y^2\cdot 5y^2 \nonumber \\ y^2\cdot \frac{dx}{dy} + 2y\cdot x \amp = 5y^4 \nonumber \\ \underbrace{y^2}_{f} \cdot\underbrace{\frac{dx}{dy}}_{g'} + \underbrace{2y}_{f'}\cdot \underbrace{x}_{g} \amp = 5y^4 \label{eq4} \end{align*}
    Now we recall the product rule for derivatives: \(\ds \frac{d}{dy}\Big( f\cdot g \Big) = f\cdot g' + f' \cdot g. \) We note that the two terms on the left hand side of equation (\ref{eq4}) are the result of taking the derivative of the product. Hence we can undo the product rule as follows: \(\ds\frac{d}{dy}\Big(y^2\cdot x \Big) = 5y^4.\) Now we can integrate both sides of the equation and then solve for the dependent variable.
    \begin{align*} \int \frac{d}{dy}\Big(y^2\cdot x \Big)dy \amp = \int(5y^4) dy \\ y^2\cdot x \amp = y^5 + C \\ x \amp = \frac{y^5}{y^2} + \frac{C}{y^2} \\ \amp = y^3 + \frac{C}{y^2} \end{align*}
