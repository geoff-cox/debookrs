    1.
    Use the definition of Laplace Transform (above) to find the Laplace Transform of \(\ds h(t) = 7t. \)
    Answer.
    \(\ds H(s) = \frac{7}{s^2} \)
    Solution.
    \\ In order to evaluate this integral, we will need to compute an integration by parts. When that time comes, we will choose \(\ds u = t \) an \(\ds dv = e^{-st}. \) Then we take the derivative o \(\ds u \) to fin \(\ds du \) and the antiderivative o \(\ds dv \) to fin \(\ds v \) , as follows.
    \begin{align*} u \amp = t \amp dv \amp= e^{-st}dt \\ \Rightarrow du \amp = dt\hspace{1cm}\mbox{} \amp \Rightarrow v \amp= -\frac{1}{s}e^{-st} \end{align*}
    Note also that we will use L’Hospital’s Rule when we have a limit of for \(\ds \frac{\infty}{\infty}. \)
    \begin{align*} H(s) \amp = \lap\{ h(t) \} \\ \amp = \int_0^{\infty}e^{-st}h(t) dt \\ \amp = \int_0^{\infty}e^{-st}\cdot 7t dt \\ \amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot 7t dt \\ \amp = 7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \int_0^b te^{-st} dt \\ \amp = 7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[t\cdot\left(-\frac{1}{s}e^{-st}\right)\Bigg|_0^b - \int_0^b -\frac{1}{s}e^{-st} dt\right] \\ \amp = 7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{t}{se^{st}}\Bigg|_0^b + \frac{1}{s}\int_0^b e^{-st} dt\right] \\ \amp = 7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{t}{se^{st}} + \frac{1}{s} \cdot -\frac{1}{s}e^{-st} \right]_0^b \\ \amp = 7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{t}{se^{st}} - \frac{1}{s^2 e^{st}} \right]_0^b \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} \amp = 7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\left( -\frac{b}{se^{sb}} - \frac{1}{s^2 e^{sb}} \right) - \left( -\frac{0}{se^{s\cdot 0}} - \frac{1}{s^2 e^{s\cdot 0}} \right) \right] \\ \amp = 7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\left( -\frac{b}{se^{sb}} - \frac{1}{s^2 e^{sb}} \right) - \left( 0 - \frac{1}{s^2} \right) \right] \\ \amp = 7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{b}{se^{sb}} - \frac{1}{s^2 e^{sb}}+ \frac{1}{s^2} \right] \\ \amp = 7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{b}{se^{sb}} \right] - 7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\frac{1}{s^2 e^{sb}} \right] + 7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\frac{1}{s^2} \right] \\ \amp = -7\cdot \frac{1}{s} \cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\frac{b}{e^{sb}} \right] - 7\cdot \frac{1}{s^2}\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\frac{1}{e^{sb}} \right] + 7\cdot \frac{1}{s^2} \\ \amp = -\frac{7}{s} \cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\frac{1}{se^{sb}} \right] - \frac{7}{s^2}\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\frac{1}{e^{sb}} \right] + \frac{7}{s^2} \mbox{ (using L'H Rule)} \\ \amp = -\frac{7}{s^2} \cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\frac{1}{e^{sb}} \right] - \frac{7}{s^2}\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\frac{1}{e^{sb}} \right] + \frac{7}{s^2} \\ \amp = -\frac{7}{s^2} \cdot 0 - \frac{7}{s^2}\cdot 0 + \frac{7}{s^2} \\ \amp = \frac{7}{s^2} \end{align*}

    2.
    Use the definition of Laplace Transform (above) to find the Laplace Transform of \(\ds h(t) = e^{at}\) where \(a\) is some constant.
    Answer.
    \(\ds W(s) = \frac{1}{s-a} \)
    Solution.
    \\
    \begin{align*} W(s) \amp = \lap\{ w(t) \} \\ \amp = \int_0^{\infty}e^{-st}w(t) dt \\ \amp = \int_0^{\infty}e^{-st}\cdot e^{at} dt \\ \amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot e^{at} dt \\ \amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \int_0^b e^{-st + at}dt \\ \amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \int_0^b e^{(-s + a)t}dt \\ \amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[ \frac{1}{-s+a} \cdot e^{(-s + a)t} \right]_0^b \\ \amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[ \left(\frac{1}{-s+a} \cdot e^{(-s + a)\cdot b}\right) - \left(\frac{1}{-s+a} \cdot e^{(-s + a)\cdot 0}\right) \right] \\ \amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[ \left(\frac{1}{-s+a} \cdot e^{(-s + a)\cdot b}\right) - \left(\frac{1}{-s+a} \cdot 1\right) \right] \\ \amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[ \frac{1}{-s+a} \cdot e^{(-s + a)\cdot b} - \frac{1}{-s+a} \right] \\ \amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[ \frac{1}{-s+a} \cdot e^{(-s + a)\cdot b} \right] - \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[ \frac{1}{-s+a} \right] \\ \amp = \frac{1}{-s+a} \cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[ e^{(-s + a)\cdot b} \right] - \frac{1}{-s+a} \\ \amp = \frac{1}{-s+a} \cdot 0 - \frac{1}{-s+a} \mbox{ (assumin is negative)} \\ \amp = - \frac{1}{-s+a} \\ \amp = \frac{1}{s-a} \end{align*}

    3.
    Use the definition of Laplace Transform (above) to find the Laplace Transform of \(\ds y(t) = 7t + e^{5t}\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    \(\ds Y(s) = \frac{7}{s^2} + \frac{1}{s-5} \)
    Solution.
    \\ We will use the results from the previous two questions.
    \begin{align*} Y(s) \amp = \lap\{ y(t) \} \\ \amp = \int_0^{\infty}e^{-st}y(t) dt \\ \amp = \int_0^{\infty}e^{-st}\cdot (7t + e^{5t}) dt \\ \amp = \int_0^{\infty}(e^{-st}\cdot 7t + e^{-st}\cdot e^{5t}) dt \\ \amp = \int_0^{\infty}e^{-st}\cdot 7t dt + \int_0^{\infty}(e^{-st}\cdot e^{5t} dt \\ \amp = \lap\{ 7t \} + \lap\{ e^{5t} \} \\ \amp= \frac{7}{s^2} + \frac{1}{s-5} \end{align*}
    Notice that what we showed in the middle of all this is that \(\ds \lap\{ 7t + e^{5t} \} = \lap\{ 7t \} + \lap\{ e^{5t} \}. \) This is an example of the linearity property of Laplace Transforms.

    4.
    Use the definition of Laplace Transform (above) to find the Laplace Transform of the function \(\ds d(t) = \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} 0, \amp t \lt 0\\ 7, \amp 0 \le t \lt 3\\ 0, \amp t \ge 3\\ \end{array} \right.\)
    Answer.
    \(\ds D(s) = \frac{7}{s} - \frac{7}{s}e^{-3s} \)
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} D(s) \amp = \lap\{ d(t) \} \\ \amp = \int_0^{\infty}e^{-st}d(t) dt \\ \amp = \int_0^3 e^{-st}\cdot 7 dt + \int_3^{\infty} e^{-st}\cdot 0 dt \\ \amp = 7\int_0^3 e^{-st}dt + 0 \\ \amp = 7 \cdot \left[\frac{1}{-s}e^{-st}\right]_0^3 \\ \amp = 7 \cdot \left[\left(\frac{1}{-s}e^{-s\cdot 3}\right) - \left(\frac{1}{-s}e^{-s\cdot 0}\right)\right] \\ \amp = 7 \cdot \left[\left(\frac{1}{-s}e^{-s\cdot 3}\right) - \left(\frac{1}{-s}\cdot 1\right)\right] \\ \amp = 7 \cdot \left[-\frac{1}{s}e^{-3s} +\frac{1}{s}\cdot 1\right] \\ \amp = 7 \cdot \left[-\frac{1}{s}e^{-3s} +\frac{1}{s}\right] \\ \amp = \frac{7}{s} - \frac{7}{s}e^{-3s} \end{align*}

    8.
    \(\ds f(t) = e^{-2t}\sin(2t) + t^2 e^{3t} \)
    Answer.
    \(\ds F(s) = \frac{2}{(s+2)^2 + 4} + \frac{2}{(s-3)^3} \)
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} F(s) \amp = \lap\{ f(t) \} \\ \amp = \lap\{ e^{-2t}\sin(2t) + t^2 e^{3t} \} \\ \amp = \lap\{ e^{-2t}\sin(2t)\} + \lap\{t^2 e^{3t} \} \mbox{ (by L9)} \\ \amp = \frac{2}{\big(s- (-2) \big)^2 + 2^2} + \frac{2!}{(s-3)^{2+1}} \mbox{ (by L7 and L6)} \\ \amp = \frac{2}{(s+2)^2 + 4} + \frac{2}{(s-3)^3} \end{align*}

    9.
    \(\ds q(t) = 8t\cos(6t) + e^{3t}\sin(4t) \)
    Answer.
    \(\ds Q(s) = -\frac{8(36 - s^2)}{(s^2 + 36)^2} + \frac{4}{(s-3)^2 + 16} \)
    Solution.
    For this solution, we will need to use property L14 wit \(\ds f(t) = \cos(6t). \) We will need to know the Laplace transform for this function, so let’s do that now.
    \begin{align*} F(s) \amp = \lap\{ f(t) \} \\ \amp = \lap\{ \cos(6t) \} \\ \amp = \frac{s}{s^2 + 6^2} \mbox{ (by L5)} \\ \amp = \frac{s}{s^2 + 36} \end{align*}
    Then we have the following. Note that when we use the quotient rule to take the derivative o \(\ds F(s) \) .
    \begin{align*} Q(s) \amp = \lap\{ q(t) \} \\ \amp = \lap\{ 8t\cos(6t) + e^{3t}\sin(4t) \} \\ \amp = 8\lap\{ t\cos(6t) \} + \lap\{ e^{3t}\sin(4t) \} \mbox{ (by L9)} \\ \amp = 8\lap\{ t\cos(6t) \} + \frac{4}{(s-3)^2 + 4^2} \mbox{ (by L7)} \\ \amp = 8\lap\{ t\cos(6t) \} + \frac{4}{(s-3)^2 + 16} \\ \amp = 8\lap\{ t\cdot f(t) \} + \frac{4}{(s-3)^2 + 16} \\ \amp = 8\cdot (-1)^1 \cdot \frac{d}{ds}F(s) + \frac{4}{(s-3)^2 + 16} \mbox{ (by L14)} \\ \amp = -8 \cdot \frac{d}{ds}\left( \frac{s}{s^2 + 36} \right) + \frac{4}{(s-3)^2 + 16} \\ \amp = -8 \cdot \frac{(s^2 + 36)\cdot 1 - s\cdot (2s + 0)}{(s^2 + 36)^2} + \frac{4}{(s-3)^2 + 16} \\ \amp = -8 \cdot \frac{s^2 + 36 - 2s^2}{(s^2 + 36)^2} + \frac{4}{(s-3)^2 + 16} \\ \amp = -\frac{8(36 - s^2)}{(s^2 + 36)^2} + \frac{4}{(s-3)^2 + 16} \end{align*}

    10.
    \(\ds p(t) = t^2 \sin(3t) \)
    Answer.
    \(\ds P(s) = \frac{18\left[s^2 - 3\right]}{(s^2 + 9)^3} \)
    Solution.
    For this solution, we will need to use property L14 wit \(\ds f(t) = \sin(3t). \) We will need to know the Laplace transform for this function, so let’s do that now.
    \begin{align*} F(s) \amp = \lap\{ f(t) \} \\ \amp = \lap\{ \sin(3t) \} \\ \amp = \frac{3}{s^2 + 3^2} \mbox{ (by L5)} \\ \amp = \frac{3}{s^2 + 9} \end{align*}
    In using L14, we will also need the second derivative o \(\ds F(s), \) so we work to compute that now. Note that we will use the chain rule when we take the derivative o \(\ds (s^2 + 9)^2 \) .
    \begin{align*} F'(s) \amp = \frac{d}{ds}\left( \frac{3}{s^2 + 9} \right) \\ \amp = \frac{(s^2 + 9) \cdot 0 - 3 \cdot (2s+0)}{(s^2 + 9)^2} \\ \amp = \frac{-6s}{(s^2 + 9)^2} \\ F''(s) \amp = \frac{(s^2 + 9)^2 \cdot (-6) - (-6s) \cdot 2(s^2 + 9)(2s)}{(s^2 + 9)^4} \\ \amp = \frac{-6(s^2 + 9)^2 + 24s^2(s^2 +9)}{(s^2 + 9)^4} \\ \amp = \frac{6(s^2 + 9)\left[-(s^2 + 9) + 4s^2\right]}{(s^2 + 9)^4} \\ \amp = \frac{6\left[-s^2 - 9 + 4s^2\right]}{(s^2 + 9)^3} \\ \amp = \frac{6\left[3s^2 - 9\right]}{(s^2 + 9)^3} \\ \amp = \frac{18\left[s^2 - 3\right]}{(s^2 + 9)^3} \end{align*}
    Then we have the following.
    \begin{align*} P(s) \amp = \lap\{ t^2 \sin(3t) \} \\ \amp = \lap\{ t^2 f(t) \} \\ \amp = (-1)^2 \cdot F''(s) \mbox{ (by L14)} \\ \amp = 1 \cdot \frac{18\left[s^2 - 3\right]}{(s^2 + 9)^3} \\ \amp = \frac{18\left[s^2 - 3\right]}{(s^2 + 9)^3} \end{align*}
