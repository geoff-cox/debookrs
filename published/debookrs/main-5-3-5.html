    Subsection 3.1.3 Characteristic Equation

    The previous section showed us that solutions to LHCC equations involve \(e^{rx}\) terms. We will now build on this idea by showing that every LHCC equation is associated with another equation that allows us to find the specific \(r\)-values that give us our solutions. Let’s explore this by looking at a few concrete examples, starting the equation
    \begin{equation*} y' - 5y = 0\text{,} \end{equation*}
    Now, imagine we wanted verify that \(y = e^{rx}\) is a solution to this equation. To do this, we plug it into the equation and see if it leads to \(0=0\text{.}\) In this case, we get
    \begin{align*} \left[e^{rx}\right]' - 5 \left[e^{rx}\right] \amp= 0 \\ r e^{rx} - 5 e^{rx} \amp= 0 \\ \us{\text{must be 0}}{\ub{(r - 5) e^{rx}}} \amp= 0 \text{,} \end{align*}
    which shows that the left-side must also be \(0\text{,}\) but this only happens for specific \(r\)-values. To find them, we note that \(e^{rx}\) is never zero, and solve
    \begin{align*} (r - 5) e^{rx} =\amp \ 0 \qquad (\text{divide by } e^{rx})\\ r - 5 =\amp \ 0 \quad \rightarrow \quad r = 5\text{,} \end{align*}
    showing that \(e^{5x}\) is a solution.
    Now, let’s consider a second order equation, say
    \begin{equation*} y'' - 5y' + 6y = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Plugging \(y = e^{rx}\) into this equation, we have
    \begin{align*} \left[e^{rx}\right]'' - 5 \left[e^{rx}\right]' + 6 \left[e^{rx}\right] \amp= 0 \\ r^2 e^{rx} - 5 r e^{rx} + 6 e^{rx} \amp= 0 \\ (r^2 - 5r + 6) e^{rx} \amp= 0 \end{align*}
    Again, \(e^{rx}\) is never zero, so we must have \(r^2 - 5r + 6 = 0\text{.}\) Solving this quadratic equation gives \(r = 2\) and \(r = 3\text{,}\) yielding the solutions \(e^{2x}\) and \(e^{3x}\text{.}\)
    Regardless of the order, every LHCC equation can be approached in a similar way. To help us with the general equation, observe how each derivative of \(e^{rx}\) adds a multiple of \(r\) (by the chain rule). In particular,
    \begin{equation*} y = e^{rx} \ \ \os{\text{chain rule}}{\rightarrow} \ \ y' = re^{rx} \ \ \os{\text{chain rule}}{\rightarrow} \ \ y'' = r^2e^{rx} \quad \os{\text{chain rule}}{\rightarrow \, \dots \, \rightarrow} \quad y^{(n)} = r^ne^{rx}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We are now ready to plug \(y = e^{rx}\) into the general LHCC equation:
    \begin{align*} a_n\ \left[e^{rx}\right]^{(n)} + \cdots + a_2\ \left[e^{rx}\right]'' + a_1\ \left[e^{rx}\right]' + a_0\ e^{rx} \amp= 0 \\ a_n\ r^n e^{rx} + \cdots + a_2\ r^2 e^{rx} + a_1\ r e^{rx} + a_0\ e^{rx} \amp= 0 \\ \left(a_n\ r^n + \cdots + a_2\ r^2 + a_1\ r + a_0\right)e^{rx} \amp= 0 \text{.} \end{align*}
    Following the same pattern as before, we get the \(r\)-values we need by solving
    \begin{equation*} a_n\ r^n + \cdots + a_2\ r^2 + a_1\ r + a_0 = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This polynomial equation that gives us the \(r\)-values we need to write down our solutions is referred to as the characteristic equation.

    Definition 3.5. LHCC Characteristic Equation.

    The characteristic equation of a linear homogeneous differential equations with constant coefficients is given by the \(n\)-th degree polynomial equation
    \begin{equation} a_n\ r^n + \cdots + a_2\ r^2 + a_1\ r + a_0 = 0 \text{.}\tag{5} \end{equation}

    Example 3.6.

    Give the characteristic equation of the differential equation
    \begin{equation*} y'' + 5y' - y = 0. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} r^2 + 5r - 1 = 0 \end{equation*}

    Reading Questions Check your Understanding

    1. Verifying \(y = e^{rx}\) as a solution.

      True or False: To verify that \(y = e^{rx}\) is a solution to \(y' - 5y = 0\text{,}\) we substitute it into the differential equation and simplify.
    • True
    • Correct! Substituting \(y = e^{rx}\) into the differential equation helps verify the solution.
    • False
    • Incorrect. This is the correct method to verify the solution.

    2. Characteristic equation for first-order LHCC.

      What is the characteristic equation for \(y' - 5y = 0\text{?}\)
    • \(r - 5 = 0\)
    • Correct! The characteristic equation is \(r - 5 = 0\text{.}\)
    • \(r + 5 = 0\)
    • Incorrect. Check the sign of the coefficient of \(y\text{.}\)
    • \(r^2 - 5 = 0\)
    • Incorrect. The characteristic equation for a first-order LHCC is linear, not quadratic.
    • \(5r - 1 = 0\)
    • Incorrect. Make sure to use the correct coefficients from the original equation.

    3. Characteristic equation for second-order LHCC.

      What is the characteristic equation for \(y'' - 5y' + 6y = 0\text{?}\)
    • \(r^2 - 5r + 6 = 0\)
    • Correct! The characteristic equation is \(r^2 - 5r + 6 = 0\text{.}\)
    • \(r^2 - 6r + 5 = 0\)
    • Incorrect. Ensure the correct coefficients are used.
    • \(r^2 + 5r - 6 = 0\)
    • Incorrect. Check the signs of the coefficients.
    • \(r^2 - 5r = 0\)
    • Incorrect. Make sure to include all terms.

    4. Roots of the characteristic equation.

      What are the roots of the characteristic equation \(r^2 - 5r + 6 = 0\text{?}\)
    • \(r = 2\) and \(r = 3\)
    • Correct! The roots are \(r = 2\) and \(r = 3\text{.}\)
    • \(r = -2\) and \(r = -3\)
    • Incorrect. Check the signs of the roots.
    • \(r = 1\) and \(r = 6\)
    • Incorrect. Ensure you solve the quadratic equation correctly.
    • \(r = 5\) and \(r = 1\)
    • Incorrect. Revisit the quadratic formula to solve for the roots.

    5. General solution for second-order LHCC.

      What is the general solution for \(y'' - 5y' + 6y = 0\text{?}\)
    • \(y = C_1 e^{2x} + C_2 e^{3x}\)
    • Correct! The general solution is \(y = C_1 e^{2x} + C_2 e^{3x}\text{.}\)
    • \(y = C_1 e^{-2x} + C_2 e^{-3x}\)
    • Incorrect. Check the signs of the exponents.
    • \(y = C_1 e^{5x} + C_2 e^{x}\)
    • Incorrect. Make sure to use the correct roots.
    • \(y = C_1 e^{x} + C_2 e^{-x}\)
    • Incorrect. Revisit the roots of the characteristic equation.

    6. Understanding the characteristic equation.

      True or False: The characteristic equation is a polynomial equation whose roots give the \(r\)-values for the solutions of the LHCC equation.
    • True
    • Correct! The roots of the characteristic equation provide the \(r\)-values for the solutions.
    • False
    • Incorrect. This statement is true.

    7. General form of the characteristic equation.

      What is the general form of the characteristic equation for an \(n\)-th order LHCC equation?
    • \(a_n r + a_{n-1} r^{n-1} + \cdots + a_1 r + a_0 = 0\)
    • Incorrect. Ensure the highest power of \(r\) is correct.
    • \(a_n r^n + a_{n-1} r^{n-1} + \cdots + a_1 r + a_0 = 0\)
    • Correct! The characteristic equation is a polynomial equation of degree \(n\text{.}\)
    • \(a_n r^n + a_{n-1} r^{n-1} + \cdots + a_0 = 0\)
    • Incorrect. Ensure all terms involving \(r\) are included.
    • \(a_n r^n + a_{n-1} r^{n-2} + \cdots + a_1 r + a_0 = 0\)
    • Incorrect. Ensure the powers of \(r\) decrease correctly.

    8. Match the Differential Equation to its Characteristic Equation.

      Match each differential equation on the left to its characteristic equation on the right.
    • \(y' + 3y = 0 \)
    • \(r + 3 = 0 \)
    • \(y'' + 4y' + 4y = 0 \)
    • \(r^2 + 4r + 4 = 0 \)
    • \(y'' - y = 0 \)
    • \(r^2 - 1 = 0 \)
    • \(y' - 2y = 0 \)
    • \(r - 2 = 0 \)
    • \(y'' + y = 0 \)
    • \(r^2 + 1 = 0 \)

    9. Characteristic equation for third-order LHCC.

      What is the characteristic equation for \(y''' - 2y'' + y' - y = 0\text{?}\)
    • \(r^3 - 2r^2 + r - 1 = 0\)
    • Correct! The characteristic equation is \(r^3 - 2r^2 + r - 1 = 0\text{.}\)
    • \(r^3 + 2r^2 - r + 1 = 0\)
    • Incorrect. Check the signs of the coefficients.
    • \(r^3 - r^2 + r - 2 = 0\)
    • Incorrect. Ensure all terms are included correctly.
    • \(r^3 - r^2 + r = 0\)
    • Incorrect. Make sure to include all terms.

    10. Solving the characteristic equation.

      If the characteristic equation for an LHCC equation is \(r^2 - 4r + 4 = 0\text{,}\) what are the roots?
    • \(r = 2\) (double root)
    • Correct! The root \(r = 2\) has a multiplicity of 2.
    • \(r = 4\)
    • Incorrect. Check the quadratic equation carefully.
    • \(r = -2\) and \(r = 2\)
    • Incorrect. Ensure you solve the quadratic equation correctly.
    • \(r = 2 \pm 2i\)
    • Incorrect. These are not the correct roots for this equation.
