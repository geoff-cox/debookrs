<section label="selecting-the-particular-soln.ptx">
	<title> Selecting the Particular Solution Form </title>
	
	<introduction>
		<p>
			In the previous section, we saw that the general solution to a linear nonhomogeneous constant coefficient (LNCC) equation has two parts: the <em>homogeneous solution</em> <m>y_h</m> and the <em>particular solution</em> <m>y_p</m>. The homogeneous solution is found using the characteristic equation, while the particular solution is constructed by (1) selecting a form similar to the forcing function, <m>f(x)</m>, and (2) determining the coefficients of that form.
		</p>

		<p>
			This section focuses on the first step: selecting a form for <m>y_p</m>. The key idea is to choose a form that resembles the forcing function <m>f(x)</m>, so that when it is substituted into the differential equation, all the resulting terms simplify to <m>f(x)</m>.
		</p>
	</introduction>

	<paragraphs label="selecting-basic-forms-for-yp">
		<title> Selecting Basic Forms </title>

		<p>
			The first step in choosing <m>y_p</m> is recognizing the structure of <m>f(x)</m>. Since <m>y_p</m> and its derivatives must eventually simplify to match <m>f(x)</m>, <m>y_p</m> should have a similar form. If <m>f(x)</m> is a polynomial, exponential, sine, or cosine function, then <m>y_p</m> should be too.
		</p>

		<p>
			<xref ref="yp-forms-table"/> provides a guide for selecting an initial form of <m>y_p</m> based on the type of <m>f(x)</m>. In each case, <m>A, B, C, \dots</m> represent the undetermined coefficients we'll solve for later.
		</p>

		<table xml:id="yp-forms-table">
			<title> 
				<m>y_p</m> forms based on <m>f(x)</m>, where <m>a, b, c, d, \alpha, \beta</m> are known constants. <m>A, B, C, D</m> must be determined. Degree 4+ polynomials follow a similar pattern.
			</title>
			<tabular top="medium" left="medium" right="medium" bottom="minor">
				<col halign="center" top="none" right="minor"/>
				<col halign="center" right="minor"/>
				<col halign="center"/>
				<row left="none" bottom="medium">
				<cell right="medium"/>
				<cell><term>Forcing Function Type, </term><m>f(x)</m></cell>
				<cell><term>Particular Solution Form, </term><m>y_p</m></cell>
				</row>
				<row>
				<cell>1</cell>
				<cell><m>a</m> (constant)</cell>
				<cell><m>A</m></cell>
				</row>
				<row>
				<cell>2</cell>
				<cell><m>ax + b</m></cell>
				<cell><m>Ax + B</m></cell>

				<row>
				<cell>3</cell>
				<cell><m>ax^2 + bx + c</m></cell>
				<cell><m>Ax^2 + Bx + C</m></cell>
				</row>
				<row>
				<cell>4</cell>
				<cell><m>ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d</m></cell>
				<cell><m>Ax^3 + Bx^2 + Cx + D</m></cell>
				</row>
				<row>
				<cell>5</cell>

				</row>
				<row bottom="medium">
				<cell>6</cell>
				<cell><m>a \sin(\beta x) + b \cos(\beta x)</m></cell>
				<cell><m>A \sin(\beta x) + B \cos(\beta x)</m></cell>
				</row>
			</tabular>
		</table>

		<p>
			One important note: even if a term is missing in <m>f(x)</m>, you should still include it in <m>y_p</m> if it's part of the general form. For example:
		</p>

		<sidebyside widths="5% 95%" valign="top">
			<p>ðŸ”¸</p>
			<p>
				If <m>f(x) = x^2 + 1</m>, the <m>x</m> term is missing, but <m>y_p</m> must still include it:
				<me> y_p = Ax^2 + Bx + C </me>.
			</p>
		</sidebyside>

		<sidebyside widths="5% 95%" valign="top">
			<p>ðŸ”¸</p>
			<p>
				If <m>f(x) = \cos(3x)</m>, then <m>y_p = A\cos(3x) + B\sin(3x)</m>. Even though <m>\sin(3x)</m> isn't in <m>f(x)</m>, it's needed because its derivative produces cosine terms.
			</p>
		</sidebyside>

		<example>
			<title> Selecting Basic Forms of <m>y_p</m> </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
				Use <xref ref="yp-forms-table"/> to determine the appropriate form of <m>y_p</m> in each case.
				</p>

				<sidebyside widths="34% 64%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
					<p><m>\ds y'' + y' + 5y = 3e^{6x}</m></p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								Since <m>f(x)</m> is exponential, we use <xref ref="yp-forms-table-row-5" text="title"/>:
								<me> y_p = Ae^{6x} </me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<sidebyside widths="34% 64%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
					<p><m>\ds y'' + 3y' - y = 2\sin(2x)</m></p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								This matches <xref ref="yp-forms-table-row-6" text="title"/>. Even though <m>\cos(2x)</m> doesn't appear in <m>f(x)</m>, we include it:
								<me> y_p = A\sin(2x) + B\cos(2x) </me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<sidebyside widths="34% 64%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
					<p><m>\ds \omega''' + \omega' = 5\theta^2 + \theta + 1</m></p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								<m>f(x)</m> is a degree 2 polynomial, so from <xref ref="yp-forms-table-row-3" text="title"/>:
								<me> \omega_p = A\theta^2 + B\theta + C </me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<sidebyside widths="34% 64%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
					<p><m>\ds 2\frac{dy}{dx} + 5y + 3 = 0</m></p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								Move the constant to the right side: <m>2y' + 5y = -3</m>. Now <m>f(x)</m> is constant (<xref ref="yp-forms-table-row-1" text="title"/>):
								<me> y_p = A </me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<sidebyside widths="34% 64%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
					<p><m>\ds y'' -3y' + 0.5y = 14x^4</m></p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								4th degree polynomials are not represented in the table, but follow a similar pattern:
								<me> y_p = Ax^4 + Bx^3 + Cx^2 + Dx + E </me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>
			</statement>
		</example>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs label="yp-method-limits">
		<title> When This Method Works (and When It Doesn't) </title>
		<p>
			The Method of Undetermined Coefficients only works when <m>f(x)</m> belongs to a specific family of functions. In particular, this approach applies when <m>f(x)</m> can be written as a sum or product of:
		</p>
		
		<ul marker="square">
			<li><p>Polynomials, like <m>f(x) = 2x^2 + x - 4</m></p></li>
			<li><p>Exponentials, like <m>f(x) = 5 e^{2x}</m></p></li>
			<li><p>Sine or cosine functions, like <m>f(x) = \sin(\pi x) - 3\cos(\pi x)</m></p></li>
			<li><p>Any combination of the types above (via addition or multiplication)</p></li>
		</ul>
			
		<p>
			Functions such as <m>\ln x</m>, <m>\tan x</m>, or <m>1/x</m> fall outside this category. For those, we'll need a different method entirely. But for the allowed types, this method offers a fast and reliable way to select <m>y_p</m>.
		</p>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs label="adding-yp-forms">
		<title> Combining Basic Forms via Addition </title>

		<p>
			If <m>f(x)</m> is a sum of different function types, say a polynomial plus an exponential, then <m>y_p</m> should be a sum of the corresponding matching forms.
		</p>

		<example>
			<title> Initial form of <m>y_p</m> </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Use <xref ref="yp-forms-table"/> to identify the appropriate form of <m>y_p</m> in each case.
				</p>

				<sidebyside widths="38% 60%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
					<p><m>\ds y'' + 2y' + 3y = 2e^{w} - 1</m></p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								This matches is exponential plus constant, so:
								<me> y_p = Ae^{w} + B </me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<sidebyside widths="38% 60%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
					<p><m>\ds u'' - u = 3\sin(2\theta) + \cos(2\theta)</m></p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								Both terms have the same argument, <m>2\theta</m>, the form is the standard <xref ref="yp-forms-table-row-6" text="title"/>
								<me> y_p = A\sin(2\theta) + B\cos(2\theta) </me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<sidebyside widths="38% 60%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
					<p><m>\ds \omega''' + \omega' = 5e^{3w} - 2\sin(4w)</m></p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								This combines exponential and sine, so we need:
								<me> y_p = Ae^{3w} + B\sin(4w) + C\cos(4w) </me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<sidebyside widths="38% 60%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
					<p><m>\ds P' + P = \cos(-3t) - \cos(5t)</m></p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								Cosines with different arguments requires the sum of two forms:
								<md>
									<mrow> y_p = A\sin(3t) \amp + B\cos(3t) </mrow>
									<mrow> \amp + C\sin(5t) + D\cos(5t) </mrow>
								</md>
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>
			</statement>
		</example>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs label="product-forms-simplifying">
  		<title> Combining Basic Forms via Multiplication </title>

		<p>
			If the forcing function <m>f(x)</m> is a product of basic function typesâ€”such as a polynomial times an exponentialâ€”then the particular solution <m>y_p</m> must also be a product of their matching forms. For example, the differential equation
		</p>

		<me>
			y'' + y' + 5y = 10x^2 e^{6x}
		</me>,

		<p>
			has a forcing function that is a quadratic times an exponential, so we start with:
			<me> y_p = (Ax^2 + Bx + C)(D e^{6x}) </me>.
		</p>

		<p>
			However, be careful: multiplying by <m>D</m> just makes new coefficients:
			<me>
				y_p = (AD x^2 + BD x + CD) e^{6x}
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			Since each pair can be merged as single coefficient, we just relabel them:
			<me>
				y_p = (Ax^2 + Bx + C) e^{6x}
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			We'll use this simplification trick often. It keeps the form of <m>y_p</m> clean and will make solving for <m>A</m>, <m>B</m> and <m>C</m> much easier later.
		</p>

		<example><title> Initial form of <m>y_p</m> - Products of Forms </title>

			<statement>
				<p>
					For each of the following forcing functions, select an appropriate initial form of <m>y_p</m>. Simplify by combining or eliminating any redundant constants.
				</p>

			<sidebyside widths="34% 64%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
				<p><m>\ds f(x) = e^{-x}\cos(x)</m></p>
				<p>
					<solution>
						<p>
							This is an exponential times a cosine:
							<me>
								y_p = Ae^{-x}(B\sin x + C\cos x)
							</me>.
							However, distributing <m>A</m> shows that it is redundant and we relabel as:
							<me>
								y_p = e^{-x}(A\sin x + B\cos x)
							</me>.
						</p>
					</solution>
				</p>
			</sidebyside>

			<sidebyside widths="34% 64%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
				<p><m>\ds f(x) = e^{6x}e^{-2x}</m></p>
				<p>
					<solution>
						<p>
							The forcing function simplifies: <m>e^{6x}e^{-2x} = e^{4x}</m>, so:
							<me> y_p = Ae^{4x} </me>
						</p>
					</solution>
				</p>
			</sidebyside>

			<sidebyside widths="34% 64%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
				<p><m>\ds f(t) = t^{3}\cos t</m></p>
				<p>
					<solution>
						<p>
							This is a 3rd degree polynomial times a cosine. So, initially we set:
							<me>
								y_p = (At^3 + Bt^2 + Ct + D)(E\sin t + F\cos t)
							</me>.
						</p>

						<p>
							Multiplying the polynomial onto sine and cosine, gives us
							<md>
								<mrow>	y_p = (At^3	\amp + Bt^2 + Ct + D)E\sin t </mrow>
								<mrow>				\amp + (At^3 + Bt^2 + Ct + D)F\cos t </mrow>
							</md>.
						</p>

						<p>
							Distributing <m>E</m> and <m>F</m> shows that they are redundant, so we relabel as follows:
							<md>
								<mrow>	y_p = (At^3	\amp + Bt^2 + Ct + D)\sin t </mrow>
								<mrow>				\amp + (Et^3 + Ft^2 + Gt + H)\cos t</mrow>.
							</md>
						</p>
				</solution>
				</p>
			</sidebyside>

			<sidebyside widths="34% 64%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
				<p><m>\ds f(x) = x^2\cos x + x \sin x</m></p>
				<p>
					<solution>
						<p>
							Here, both terms involve powers of <m>x</m> times trig functions. But we don't need separate guessesâ€”one form covers both:
							<md>
								<mrow> y_p = (Ax^2 + Bx	\amp + C)\cos x </mrow>
								<mrow>					\amp + (Dx^2 + Ex + F)\sin x </mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
						<p>
							This form includes the <m>x^2\cos x</m> term and also handles <m>x\sin x</m> automatically.
						</p>
					</solution>
				</p>
			</sidebyside>
			</statement>
		</example>

		<p>
			With these examples and the table as a guide, you're now equipped to handle both additive and multiplicative combinations in <m>f(x)</m>. Next, we'll deal with one last wrinkle: what to do if <m>y_p</m> accidentally overlaps with the homogeneous solution <m>y_h</m>.
		</p>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs label="modifying-yp">
		<title> Modifying Particular Solutions </title>
		<p>
			We now know how to choose a form for <m>y_p</m> based on the structure of <m>f(x)</m>. But there's one final complication: If a term in <m>y_p</m> a term in the homogeneous solution <m>y_h</m>, then that term cannot contribute to generating <m>f(x)</m>. Instead, it will give zero, since that is what terms in <m>y_h</m> do when substituted into the differential equation.
		</p>

		<p>
			To fix this, we multiply the overlapping part of <m>y_p</m> by <m>x</m>. If there's still overlap, we multiply by <m>x</m> againâ€”repeating until <m>y_p</m> shares no like-terms with <m>y_h</m>.
		</p>

		<p>
			For example, consider:
			<me> y'' - 4y' + 3y = e^{3x} </me>

			The homogeneous solution and the initial selection for the particular solution are:
			<me>
				y_h = c_1 e^x + {\DLO c_2 e^{3x}}, \quad y_p = {\DLO A e^{3x}}
			</me>.
		</p>

		<p>
			Notice that <m>y_h</m> and  in <m>y_p</m> contain the like terms: <m>c_2 e^{3x}</m> and <m>A e^{3x}</m>. This means that plugging <m>y_p</m> into the left side of the equation will simplify to <m>0</m>, not <m>e^{3x}</m>, as required. 
		</p>

		<p>
			So we modify <m>y_p</m> by multiplying the conflicting term by <m>x</m>:
			<me> y_p = A x e^{3x} </me>
		</p>

		<p>
			Now, <m>y_p</m> has no terms in common with <m>y_h</m>, ensuring that they are independent.
		</p>

		<assemblage xml:id="modifying-yp">
			<title> Modification Rule for the Particular Solution </title>
			<p>
				If any term in your guess for <m>y_p</m> also appears in <m>y_h</m>, multiply that term by <m>x</m>. Repeat as needed until all overlapping terms are eliminated.
			</p>
		</assemblage>

		<p>
			Let's work through some examples where modification is required, and see how this rule plays out.
		</p>

		<example><title> Modifying the Initial Form of <m>y_p</m> </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					For each LNCC equation below:
					<ol marker="a.">
						<li>Find the homogeneous solution <m>y_h</m>.</li>
						<li>Select the initial form of <m>y_p</m>.</li>
						<li>Modify <m>y_p</m> to ensure it has no overlap with <m>y_h</m>.</li>
					</ol>
				</p>

					<sidebyside widths="36% 62%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
						<p><m>\ds y'' - 2y' + y = e^x</m></p>
						<p>
							<solution>
								<p>
									<ol marker="a.">
										<li>
											<li>
												<p>
													The characteristic equation is
													<me>r^2 - 2r + 1 = 0</me>,
													which has a repeated root: <m>r=1</m>. So,
													<me> y_h = c_1 e^x + c_2 x e^x </me>.
												</p>
											</li>
										</li>
										<li>
											<p>
												The natural guess for <m>y_p</m> to match <m>e^x</m> is
												<me> 
													y_p = A e^x
												</me>.
											</p>
										</li>
										<li>
											<p>
												Since <m>e^x</m> is in <m>y_h</m>, we multiply <m>y_p</m> by <m>x</m>:
              									<me> y_p = A x e^x </me>
											</p>
											<p>
												But, <m>x e^x</m> is still in <m>y_h</m>, so we repeat:
												<me>
													y_p = A x^2 e^x
												</me>.
											</p>
										</li>
									</ol>
								</p>
							</solution>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>

					<sidebyside widths="36% 62%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
						<p><m>\ds y''' + 4y' = \cos(2x) - \sin(x)</m></p>
						<p>
							<solution>
								<ol marker="a.">
									<li>
										<p>
											Since the characteristic equation is
											<me>
												r^3 + 4r = 0
											</me>,
											the roots are: <m>r = 0, \pm 2i</m> and
											<me>
												y_h = c_1 + c_2 \cos(2x) + c_3 \sin(2x)
											</me>.
										</p>
									</li>
									<li>
										<p>
											The guess for <m>y_p</m> is 
											<md> 
												<mrow> y_p	\amp = \ob{A \cos(2x) + B \sin(2x)}^{\large\text{already in } y_h}</mrow>
												<mrow>		\amp \hphantom{=\ A \cos(2x)} + D \cos(x) + E \sin(x)</mrow>
											</md>.
										</p>
									</li>
									<li>
										<p>
											Only the <m>\cos(2x)</m> and <m>\sin(2x)</m> terms conflict. Modify them:
											<md> 
												<mrow> y_p = Ax\cos(2x)	\amp + Bx\sin(2x) </mrow>
												<mrow>					\amp + D \cos(x) + E \sin(x)</mrow>
											</md>.
										</p>
									</li>
								</ol>
							</solution>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>

					<sidebyside widths="36% 62%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
						<p><m>\ds y'' + y = x e^x</m></p>
						<p>
							<solution>
								<p>
									<ol marker="a.">
										<li>
											<p>
												The characteristic equation
												<me>
													r^2 + 1 = 0
												</me>,
												has roots: <m>r = \pm i</m>, leading to
												<me>
													y_h = c_1 \cos(x) + c_2 \sin(x)
												</me>.
											</p>
										</li>
										<li>
											<p>
												The initial guess for <m>y_p</m> is
												<me>
													y_p = (Ax + B)\ e^x
												</me>.
											</p>
										</li>
										<li>
											<p>
												No modification is needed since <m>e^x</m> doesn't appear in <m>y_h</m>.
											</p>
										</li>
									</ol>
								</p>
							</solution>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>

					<sidebyside widths="36% 62%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
						<p><m>\ds y'' - 6y' + 9y = x^2 e^{3x}</m></p>
						<p>
							<solution>
								<p>
									<ol marker="a.">
										<li>
											<p>
												The characteristic equation
												<me>
													r^2 - 6r + 9 = 0
												</me>,
												has the repeated solution <m>r = 3</m>, so
												<me>
													y_h = (c_1 + c_2 x) e^{3x}
												</me>.
											</p>
										</li>
										<li>
											<p>
												The initial guess for <m>y_p</m> is:
												<me>
													y_p = (Ax^2 + Bx + C) e^{3x}
												</me>.
											</p>
										</li>
										<li>
											<p>
												Multiplying out <m>y_h</m> and <m>y_p</m> helps show that <m>e^{3x}</m> and <m>x e^{3x}</m> are conflicting terms. Multiplying all terms by <m>x^2</m> gives the correct form:
												<me>
													y_p = (Ax^4 + Bx^3 + Cx^2) e^{3x}
												</me>.
											</p>
										</li>
									</ol>
								</p>
							</solution>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
			</statement>
		</example>

		</p>
