<section label="uc-method">
	<title> Undetermined Coefficients Method </title>
	
	<introduction>
		<p>
			In the previous section, we established that the general solution to a linear nonhomogeneous constant coefficient (LNCC) equation consists of two parts: the <em>homogeneous solution</em> <m>y_h</m> and the <em>particular solution</em> <m>y_p</m>. While <m>y_h</m> is found by solving the characteristic equation of the corresponding homogeneous equation, finding <m>y_p</m> requires a different approach.
		</p>

		<p>
			The <em>Method of Undetermined Coefficients</em> is a systematic technique for determining <m>y_p</m> when the nonhomogeneous term, <m>f(x)</m>, has a specific form. Instead of solving for <m>y_p</m> directly, we first assume its general structure based on the form of <m>f(x)</m>, then determine the unknown coefficients by substituting <m>y_p</m> into the differential equation and solving for them.
		</p>

		<p>
			One limitation of this method is that it only works when <m>f(x)</m> is:
		</p>

		<ul marker="square">
			<li><p>A Polynomial, such as <m>f(x) = 2x^2 + x - 4</m></p></li>
			<li><p>An Exponential function, such as <m>f(x) = 5 e^{2 x}</m></p></li>
			<li><p>Sine or Cosine functions, such as <m>f(x) = \sin(\pi x) - 3\cos(\pi x)</m></p></li>
			<li><p>Any sum or product of these function types</p></li>
		</ul>

		<p>
			Nevertheless, when applicable, it provides a straightforward and efficient way to determine the general solution.
		</p>

		<p>
			In this section, we will develop the <em>Method of Undetermined Coefficients</em> by first identifying how to select an appropriate form for <m>y_p</m>, then learning how to modify <m>y_p</m> when it conflicts with <m>y_h</m>. Finally, we will apply the full method to solve LNCC equations step by step.
		</p>
	</introduction>

	<paragraphs label="determining-coefficients">
		<title> Determining the Coefficients </title>

		<p>
			Now that we can select an appropriate form for the particular solution <m>y_p</m> and modify it when necessary, we can proceed to determine the coefficients of <m>y_p</m>. This process involves substituting <m>y_p</m> into the LNCC equation, simplifying the resulting expression, and matching coefficients to solve for the unknown constants.
		</p>

		<p>
			To illustrate this process, consider the following differential equation:

			<me>y'' - 2y' + y = 2x^2 + 3x</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			The characteristic equation is
			<me>
				r^2 - 2r + 1 = 0
			</me>,
			which has the roots <m>r_1 = 1</m> and <m>r_2 = 1</m>, so
			<me>
				y_h = c_1 e^x + c_2 x e^x
			</me>.
		</p>

		<p>
			Based on <m>2x^2 + 3x</m>, we guess the initial form of <m>y_p</m> as
			<me>
				y_p = Ax^2 + Bx + C
			</me>.
			Since <m>y_p</m> has no terms in common with <m>y_h</m>, no adjustment is needed.
		</p>

		<p>
			Substituting <m>y_p</m> and its derivatives,
			<me>
				y_p' = 2Ax + B,\quad y_p'' = 2A
			</me>,
			into the equation and collecting like-terms gives
			<md>
				<mrow> (2A) - 2(2Ax + B) + (Ax^2 + Bx + C) = 2x^2 + 3x </mrow>
				<mrow> 2A - 4Ax - 2B + Ax^2 + Bx + C = 2x^2 + 3x </mrow>
				<mrow> \us{\ds\text{1️⃣}}{\ul{A}}x^2 + \us{\ds\text{2️⃣}}{\ul{(-4A + B)}}x + \us{\ds\text{3️⃣}}{\ul{(2A - 2B + C)}} = \us{\ds\text{1️⃣}}{\ul{2}}x^2 + \us{\ds\text{2️⃣}}{\ul{3}}x + \us{\ds\text{3️⃣}}{\ul{0}} </mrow>
			</md>.
		</p>
		
		<p>
			Matching the underlined coefficients leads to the following system of equations and solution for <m>A</m>, <m>B</m>, and <m>C</m>:
		</p>

		<sidebyside widths="10% 40% 10% 15% 10%">
			<p>
				<md>
					<mrow>	\amp\ \text{1️⃣}	</mrow>
					<mrow>	\amp\ \text{2️⃣}	</mrow>
					<mrow>	\amp\ \text{3️⃣}	</mrow>
				</md>
			</p>
			<p>
				<md>
					<mrow> A			\amp = 2	</mrow>
					<mrow> -4A + B		\amp = 3	</mrow>
					<mrow> 2A - 2B + C	\amp = 0	</mrow>
				</md>
			</p>
			<p>
				<md>
					<mrow>	\rightarrow	</mrow>
					<mrow>	\rightarrow	</mrow>
					<mrow>	\rightarrow	</mrow>
				</md>
			</p>
			<p>
				<md>
					<mrow>	\amp\ A = 2  </mrow>
					<mrow>	\amp\ B = 11 </mrow>
					<mrow>	\amp\ C = 18  </mrow>
				</md>
			</p>
			<p/>
		</sidebyside>

		<p>
			Therefore, <m>\quad\ds y_p = 2x^2 + 11x + 18</m>.
		</p>

		<p>
			This gives us the particular solution <m>y_p</m>. The general solution is now just one step away:
			<me> 
				y = y_h + y_p = c_1 e^x + c_2 x e^x + 2x^2 + 11x + 18
			</me>.
		</p>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs label="uc-method-steps">
		<title> Undetermined Coefficient Method Steps </title>

		<p>
			In the previous subsections, we determined how to select an appropriate form for the particular solution, <m>y_p</m>, and modify it when necessary to ensure independence from the homogeneous solution, <m>y_h</m>. Now, we complete the process by solving for the unknown coefficients in <m>y_p</m> to construct the full general solution.
		</p>

		<p>
			This process, known as the <em>Method of Undetermined Coefficients</em>, consists of systematically substituting <m>y_p</m> into the differential equation, simplifying the terms, and solving for the unknown constants. The steps are outlined below.
		</p>

		<exploration xml:id="uc-method"><title> Undetermined Coefficients </title>
			<p>
				For the linear nonhomogeneous constant coefficient equation,
			</p>
			<men xml:id="uc-method-lncc-de">
				a_n y^{(n)} + a_{n-1} y^{(n-1)} + \dots + a_1 y' + a_0 y = f(x),
			</men>
			<p>
				the general solution is given by <m>y = y_h + y_p</m>, where <m>y_h</m> is the homogeneous solution obtained from
			</p>
			<men xml:id="uc-method-lhcc-de">
				a_n y^{(n)} + a_{n-1} y^{(n-1)} + \dots + a_1 y' + a_0 y = 0,
			</men>
			<p>
				and <m>y_p</m> is the particular solution that we now determine using the following steps:
			</p>
			<dl width="narrow">
				<li xml:id="uc-step-1"><title> Solve for <m>y_h</m> </title>
					<p>
						Find the homogeneous solution <m>y_h</m> by solving the characteristic equation associated with <xref ref="uc-method-lhcc-de"/>.
					</p>
				</li>
				<li xml:id="uc-step-2"><title> Select <m>y_p</m> </title>
					<p>
						Use <xref ref="yp-forms-table"/> to determine an appropriate initial form for <m>y_p</m> based on the structure of <m>f(x)</m>.
					</p>
				</li>
				<li xml:id="uc-step-3"><title> Modify <m>y_p</m> if Necessary </title>
					<p>
						Ensure <m>y_p</m> does not contain any terms already present in <m>y_h</m>. If such terms exist, multiply the affected terms in <m>y_p</m> by <m>x</m> repeatedly until all overlap is removed.
					</p>
				</li>
				<li xml:id="uc-step-4"><title> Determine the Coefficients </title>
					<p>
						Substitute <m>y_p</m> into <xref ref="uc-method-lncc-de"/>, compute all derivatives, and simplify. Then, collect like terms and match coefficients on both sides to create a system of equations for the unknown coefficients.
					</p>
				</li>
				<li xml:id="uc-step-5"><title> Construct the General Solution </title>
					<p>
						Solve for the unknown coefficients, substitute them back into <m>y_p</m>, and express the final solution as <m>y = y_h + y_p</m>.
					</p>
				</li>
			</dl>
		</exploration>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs label="uc-method-examples">
		<title> Worked Examples </title>
		
		<p>
			To illustrate the complete process, let's go through a full example step by step.
		</p>

		<example><title> General Solution of an LNCC Equation </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					For each of the following linear nonhomogeneous differential equations, find the general solution using the <xref ref="uc-method" text="custom">method of undetermined coefficients</xref>.
					<ol marker="A.">
						<li>
							<p>
								<m>\ds y'' + 3y' - 28y = 14t - 1 + e^{4t}</m>
								<solution>
									<p>
										<term> <xref ref="uc-step-1" text="title"/>. </term>
										The characteristic equation is
										<me>
											r^2 + 3r - 28 = 0
										</me>,
										which has the roots <m>r_1 = 4</m> and <m>r_2 = -7</m>, so
										<me>
											y_h = c_1 e^{4t} + c_2 e^{-7t}
										</me>.
									</p>

									<p>
										<term> <xref ref="uc-step-2" text="title"/>. </term>
										Since the constant term as a 1st degree polynomial plus an exponential, the initial <m>y_p</m> is
										<me>
											y_p = At + B + Ce^{4t}
										</me>.
									</p>

									<p>
										<term> <xref ref="uc-step-3" text="title"/>. </term>
										Since <m>y_p</m> and <m>y_h</m> share an <m>e^{4t}</m> term, we multiply this term in <m>y_p</m> by <m>t</m> to get
									</p>

									<me>
										y_p = At + B + C t e^{4t}
									</me>.

									<p>
										<term> <xref ref="uc-step-4" text="title"/>. </term>
										Substituting <m>y_p</m> and its derivatives,
										<me>
											y_p' = A + C e^{4t} + 4C t e^{4t},\quad y_p'' = 8C e^{4t} + 16C t e^{4t}
										</me>,
										into the equation and collecting like-terms gives
										<md>
											<mrow>
												8C e^{4t} + 16C t e^{4t} \amp + 3(A + C e^{4t} + 4C t e^{4t})
											</mrow>
											<mrow>
												\amp - 28(At + B + C t e^{4t})z = 14t - 1 + e^{4t}
											</mrow>
											<mrow>
												8C e^{4t} + 16C t e^{4t} \amp + 3A + 3C e^{4t} + 12C t e^{4t}
											</mrow>
											<mrow>
												\amp - 28At - 28B - 28C t e^{4t} = 14t - 1 + e^{4t}
											</mrow>
											<mrow>
												\us{\ds\text{1️⃣}}{\ul{11C}} e^{4t} - \us{\ds\text{2️⃣}}{\ul{28A}} t \amp + \us{\ds\text{3️⃣}}{\ul{3A - 28B}} = \us{\ds\text{2️⃣}}{\ul{14}} t + \us{\ds\text{3️⃣}}{\ul{-1}} + \us{\ds\text{1️⃣}}{\ul{1}} e^{4t}
											</mrow>
										</md>.
									</p>

									<p>
										Matching the underlined coefficients leads to the following system of equations and solution for <m>A</m>, <m>B</m>, and <m>C</m>:
									</p>

									<sidebyside widths="10% 40% 10% 30% 10%">
										<p>
											<md>
												<mrow>	\amp\ \text{1️⃣}	</mrow>
												<mrow>	\amp\ \text{2️⃣}	</mrow>
												<mrow>	\amp\ \text{3️⃣}	</mrow>
											</md>
										</p>
										<p>
											<md>
												<mrow> 11C		\amp = 1	</mrow>
												<mrow> -28A		\amp = 14	</mrow>
												<mrow> 3A - 28B	\amp = -1	</mrow>
											</md>
										</p>
										<p>
											<md>
												<mrow>	\rightarrow	</mrow>
												<mrow>	\rightarrow	</mrow>
												<mrow>	\rightarrow	</mrow>
											</md>
										</p>
										<p>
											<md>
												<mrow>	\amp\ C = 1/11  </mrow>
												<mrow>	\amp\ A = -1/2  </mrow>
												<mrow>	\amp\ B = -1/56  </mrow>
											</md>
										</p>
										<p/>
									</sidebyside>

									<p>
										Therefore, <m>\quad\ds y_p = -\frac{1}{2} t - \frac{1}{56} + \frac{1}{11} t e^{4t}</m>.
									</p>

									<p>
										<term> <xref ref="uc-step-5" text="title"/>. </term>
										With <m>y_h</m> and <m>y_p</m> in hand, we have
										<me> 
											y = y_h + y_p = c_1 e^{4t} + c_2 e^{-7t} - \frac{1}{2} t - \frac{1}{56} + \frac{1}{11} t e^{4t}
										</me>.
									</p>
								</solution>
							</p>
						</li>
						<li>
							<p>
								<m>\ds y'' - 3y' + 2y = 5e^{2x}</m>
								<solution>
									<p>
										<term> <xref ref="uc-step-1" text="title"/>. </term>
										The characteristic equation is:
									</p>
									<me>
										r^2 - 3r + 2 = 0.
									</me>
									<p>
										Factoring gives <m>(r - 1)(r - 2) = 0</m>, so the homogeneous solution is:
									</p>
									<me>
										y_h = c_1 e^x + c_2 e^{2x}.
									</me>

									<p>
										<term> <xref ref="uc-step-2" text="title"/>. </term>
										Since <m>f(x) = 5e^{2x}</m>, we initially choose:
									</p>
									<me>
										y_p = A e^{2x}.
									</me>

									<p>
										<term> <xref ref="uc-step-3" text="title"/>. </term>
										However, <m>e^{2x}</m> appears in <m>y_h</m>, so we modify <m>y_p</m> by multiplying by <m>x</m>:
									</p>
									<me>
										y_p = A x e^{2x}.
									</me>

									<p>
										<term> <xref ref="uc-step-4" text="title"/>. </term>
										Compute derivatives and substitute into the differential equation:
									</p>
									<me>
										y_p' = A e^{2x} + 2Ax e^{2x},
									</me>
									<me>
										y_p'' = 2A e^{2x} + 2A e^{2x} + 4Ax e^{2x}.
									</me>
									<p>
										Substituting into the equation and simplifying, we solve for <m>A</m> and find <m>A = 5/2</m>. Thus, the particular solution is:
									</p>
									<me>
										y_p = \frac{5}{2} x e^{2x}.
									</me>

									<p>
										<term> <xref ref="uc-step-5" text="title"/>. </term>
										The general solution is:
									</p>
									<me>
										y = c_1 e^x + c_2 e^{2x} + \frac{5}{2} x e^{2x}.
									</me>
								</solution>
							</p>
						</li>
					</ol>
				</p>
			</statement>
		</example>

		<p>
			These examples illustrate how each step in the method leads systematically to the final general solution. With practice, recognizing the necessary modifications and solving for coefficients will become second nature.
		</p>
	</paragraphs>
