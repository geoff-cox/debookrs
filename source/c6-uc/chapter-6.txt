<section label="lncc-equation">
  <title> The LNCC Equation </title>

	<introduction>
		<p>
			You already know that a linear homogeneous constant coefficient (LHCC) equation has the form:
		</p>

		<me>
			a_n y^{(n)} + a_{n-1} y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_1 y' + a_0 y = 0
		</me>

		<p>
			All the terms are on the left, and the right-hand side is zero. This equation is called <em>homogeneous</em> because the solution only includes terms that cancel each other out.
		</p>

		<p>
			A linear nonhomogeneous constant coefficient (LNCC) equation looks almost the same, except the right-hand side is now a function:
		</p>

		<me>
			a_n y^{(n)} + a_{n-1} y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_1 y' + a_0 y = f(x)
		</me>

		<p>
			The function <m>f(x)</m> is called the <term>forcing function</term> because it introduces an external input that <q>forces</q> the system to respond in a particular way.
		</p>

		<task label="lncc-equation-chkpt-1">
			<title> üìñ‚ùì Homogeneous vs Nonhomogeneous </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Which structure or property of a differential equation separates homogeneous from nonhomogeneous equations?
				</p>
			</statement>
			<choices randomize="yes">
				<choice correct="yes">
					<statement>
						<p>
							The presence of a non-zero forcing function.
						</p>
					</statement>
					<feedback>
						<p>
							Correct! A non-zero constant term on the right-hand side of the equation indicates a nonhomogeneous equation.
						</p>
					</feedback>
				</choice>
				<choice>
					<statement>
						<p>
							The dependent variable.
						</p>
					</statement>
					<feedback>
						<p>
							Incorrect. The dependent variable is present in both homogeneous and nonhomogeneous equations.
						</p>
					</feedback>
				</choice>
				<choice>
					<statement>
						<p>
							The order of the equation.
						</p>
					</statement>
					<feedback>
						<p>
							Incorrect. Both homogeneous and nonhomogeneous equations can have the same order.
						</p>
					</feedback>
				</choice>
				<choice>
					<statement>
						<p>
							The linearity of the equation.
						</p>
					</statement>

						</p>
					</feedback>
				</choice>
			</choices>
		</task>
	</introduction>

  	<paragraphs label="guessing-solution">
		<title> Guessing a Solution that Accounts for the Forcing Function </title>

		<p>
			<em>Note: For brevity, we will refer to the left side of equation <xref ref="lncc-example-eqn"/> as üçÑ. Also, when we say <q>plug <m>y</m> into üçÑ</q>, we mean calculate and substitute <m>y''</m>, <m>y'</m>, <m>y</m> into <m>y'' - 4y' + 3y</m>, then simplify. </em>
		</p>

		<p>
			Imagine you had to make your best guess as to which function, <m>y</m>, satisfies the equation
		</p>

		<men xml:id="lncc-example-eqn">
			\ub{y'' - 4y' + 3y}_{\Large üçÑ} = 9x + 6
		</men>.

		<p>
			Remember, after differentiating your guess and plugging it into üçÑ, all terms must simplify to <m>9x + 6</m>. That is, 
		</p>

		<me>
			(\text{guess})'' - 4(\text{guess})' + 3(\text{guess})\quad\rightarrow\quad\text{simplifies to}\quad 9x + 6
		</me>.

		<p>
			Logically, for the sum of three terms to simplify to <m>9x + 6</m>, they must share a similar structure as <m>9x + 6</m> (i.e., a degree <m>1</m> polynomial).<fn>or contain terms that completely cancel out, but we will ignore these for now.</fn>
		</p>

		<task label="lncc-equation-chkpt-2">
			<title> üìñ‚ùì Select the most appropriate solution </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Based on this logic, which function is the only possible candidate for the solution to the LNCC equation?
				</p>
				<me>
					y'' - 2y' + y = x^3 + x
				</me>.
				<p>
					<em>Hint: Think about the type of functions you need to add together to get <m>x^3 + x</m>.</em>
				</p>
			</statement>
			<choices randomize="yes">
				<choice correct="no">
					<statement>
						<p>
							<m>\ds\quad e^{3x}</m>
						</p>
					</statement>
					<feedback>
						<p>
							Incorrect. This function does not resemble the forcing function.
						</p>
					</feedback>
				</choice>
				<choice correct="no">
					<statement>
						<p>
							<m>\ds\quad 3x^2 + 1 </m>
						</p>
					</statement>
					<feedback>
						<p>
							Incorrect. Plugging <m>3x^2 + 1</m> into <m>y'' - 2y' + y</m> will not give you an <m>x^3</m> term you need to match the forcing function, <m>x^3 + x</m>.
						</p>
					</feedback>
				</choice>
				<choice correct="yes">
					<statement>

							<m>\ds\quad x^3 + 6x^2 + 18x + 24</m>
						</p>
					</statement>
					<feedback>
						<p>
							Correct! This 3rd degree polynomial has the same polynomial structure as the forcing function, <m>x^3 + x</m>.
						</p>
					</feedback>
				</choice>
				<choice correct="no">
					<statement>
						<p>
							<m>\ds\quad x^4 + x^2</m>
						</p>
					</statement>
					<feedback>
						<p>
							Incorrect. Plugging <m>x^4 + x^2</m> into <m>y'' - 2y' + y</m> will leave you with an <m>x^4</m> term that does not exist in the forcing function, <m>x^3 + x</m>.
						</p>
					</feedback>
				</choice>
			</choices>
		</task>

		<p>
			In this context, a <q>similar function-type</q> to <m>9x + 6</m> means our guess should also be a degree <m>1</m> polynomial. Since we don't know which one, we use the general degree <m>1</m> form:
			<me> y_p = Ax + B </me>
		</p>

		<p>
			Plugging this <m>y_p</m> into <xref ref="lncc-example-eqn" text="custom">üçÑ</xref> and taking some derivatives, we get 
		</p>

		<md>
			<mrow> y'' - 4y' + 3y	\amp = (Ax + B)'' - 4(Ax + B)' + 3(Ax + B) </mrow>
			<mrow>					\amp = 0 - 4A + 3Ax + 3B </mrow>
			<mrow>					\amp = 3Ax + 3B - 4A  </mrow>
		</md>

		<p>
			This is not <m>9x + 6</m>, but maybe we can find <m>A</m> and <m>B</m> values that get us there. Setting the expression equal to <m>9x + 6</m>, we have
		</p>

		<me>
			{\DLO 3A}x + {\DLBa 3B - 4A} = {\DLO 9}x + {\DLBa 6}
		</me>

		<p>
			Matching the <m>{\DLO x\ \text{coefficients}}</m>  and <m>{\DLBa \text{constants}}</m>, gives us the system:
		</p>

		<md>
			<mrow> 3A \amp = 9 \quad \Rightarrow \quad A = 3 </mrow>
			<mrow> -4A + 3B \amp = 6 \quad \Rightarrow \quad -12 + 3B = 6 \Rightarrow B = 6 </mrow>
		</md>

		<p>
			Substituting <m>A=3</m> and <m>B=6</m> into <m>y_p</m> completes the solution:
			<me> y_p = 3x + 6 </me>
		</p>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs xml:id="particular-check">
		<title> Confirming our Guess </title>
		<p>
			Let's double-check that the function, <m>y_p</m>, we found really satisfies the equation.
		</p>

		<md>
			<mrow>
				(3x+6)'' - 4(3x+6)' + 3(3x+6)
					\amp = 9x + 6
			</mrow>
			<mrow>
				0 - 12 + 9x + 18
					\amp = 9x + 6
			</mrow>
			<mrow>
				{\color{RoyalBlue}\text{all terms simplify to }\ \rightarrow}\quad 9x + 6
					\amp = 9x + 6\quad {\color{RoyalBlue}\leftarrow\ \text{forcing function}}
			</mrow>
		</md>

		<p>
			This confirms <m>y_p</m> as a solution. In fact, it is the most important solution because it accounts for the specific forcing function <m>9x + 6</m> and must be included in every solution to equation <xref ref="lncc-example-eqn"/>. Such solutions are called <term>particular solutions</term>, but they are only part of the whole story.
		</p>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs xml:id="hidden-solns">
		<title> The Hidden Parts of Nonhomogeneous Solutions </title>
		<p>
			We established that the particular solution, <m>y_p = 3x + 6</m>, is what you plug into <xref ref="lncc-example-eqn" text="custom">üçÑ</xref> to get <m>9x + 6</m>. However, there could be additional terms you can plug into <xref ref="lncc-example-eqn" text="custom">üçÑ</xref> that cancel out after taking derivatives and simplifying. Specifically,
		</p>

		<me>
			y = \ub{3x + 6}_{\large y_p} + 
			\left[
			\begin{array}{c}
				\text{terms that simplify to}\ 0 \\
				\text{when plugged into üçÑ}
			\end{array}
			\right]
		</me>
		
		<p>
			We already know how to find these functions since they are just the solutions to the homogeneous version of the same equation:
		</p>

		<me>
		y'' - 4y' + 3y = 0
		</me>

		<p>
			This LHCC equation is solved using its characteristic equation:
		</p>

		<md>
			<mrow> r^2 - 4r + 3 = 0	\amp\quad\Rightarrow\quad (r - 1)(r - 3) = 0 </mrow>
			<mrow> 					\amp\quad\Rightarrow\quad r = 1,\ 3 </mrow>
		</md>

		<p>
			This homogeneous solution, we'll call <m>y_h</m>, is given by
		</p>

		<me>
			y_h = c_1 e^{x} + c_2 e^{3x}
		</me>

		<p>
			and is the other half of LNCC solution. They provide the terms that cancel out when plugged into <xref ref="lncc-example-eqn" text="custom">üçÑ</xref> and have no impact on the forcing function at all.
		</p>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs xml:id="building-the-soln">
		<title> Combining the Parts Into a General Solution </title>

		<p>
			To summarize, the general solution of equation
			<me>
				y'' - 4y' + 3y = 9x + 6
			</me>
			consists of two parts:
			<ul marker="square">
				<li>
					The homogeneous part: <m>\quad y_h = c_1 e^{x} + c_2 e^{3x}\quad</m> (canceling terms), and 
				</li>
				<li>
					The particular part: <m>\quad y_p = 3x + 6\quad</m> (account for <m>9x + 6</m>)
				</li>
			</ul>
			and we write it as the sum of these parts:
		</p>

		<me>
			y = y_h + y_p = c_1 e^{x} + c_2 e^{3x} + 3x + 6
		</me>.

		<p>
			Finally, let's see how the different parts of the general solution behave when plugged into the left side of the equation. First, let's plug in <m>y_h + y_p</m>
		</p>

		<md>
			<mrow> (y_h + y_p)'' - 4(y_h + y_p)' + 3(y_h + y_p) \amp = 9x + 6 </mrow>
		</md>
		
		<p>
			and regroup the <m>y_h</m> and <m>y_p</m> terms:
		</p>

		<md>
			<mrow>  \ub{y_h'' - 4y_h' + 3y_h}_{\ds\text{1Ô∏è‚É£}} + \ub{y_p'' - 4y_p' + 3y_p}_{\ds\text{2Ô∏è‚É£}} \amp = 9x + 6 </mrow>
		</md>

		<p>
			Now, we will now show that the homogeneous terms, 1Ô∏è‚É£, simplify to zero:
			<md>
				<mrow>\text{1Ô∏è‚É£}:\ y_h'' - 4y_h' + 3y_h \amp = \left(c_1e^{x} + c_2e^{3x}\right)'' - 4\left(c_1e^{x} + c_2e^{3x}\right)' + 3\left(c_1e^{x} + c_2e^{3x}\right) </mrow>
				<mrow> \amp = c_1e^{x} + 9c_2e^{3x} - 4\left(c_1e^{x} + 3c_2e^{3x}\right) + 3\left(c_1e^{x} + c_2e^{3x}\right) </mrow>
				<mrow> \amp = c_1e^{x} + 9c_2e^{3x} - 4c_1e^{x} + 12c_2e^{3x} + 3c_1e^{x} + 3c_2e^{3x} </mrow>
				<mrow> \amp = 0 \quad \text{‚úÖ}</mrow>
			</md>
			<p>
				Next, we will show that the particular terms, 2Ô∏è‚É£, simplify to <m>9x + 6</m>:
			</p>
			<md>
				<mrow>\text{2Ô∏è‚É£}:\ y_p'' - 4y_p' + 3y_p \amp = (3x + 6)'' - 4(3x + 6)' + 3(3x + 6) </mrow>
				<mrow> \amp = 0 - 12 + 9x + 18 </mrow>
				<mrow> \amp = 9x + 6 \quad \text{‚úÖ}</mrow>
			</md>
			<p>
				So, adding the results of 1Ô∏è‚É£ and 2Ô∏è‚É£, leaves us with the forcing function, <m>9x + 6</m>.
			</p>
		</p>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs xml:id="lncc-general-solution">
		<title> The General Solution to LNCC Equations  </title>

		<p>
			Let's summarize what we've learned so far:
		</p>

		<assemblage xml:id="lncc-equation-summary">
			<title> ‚ú≥Ô∏è LNCC General Solution Structure </title>
			<p>
				<p>
					A linear nonhomogeneous constant coefficient (LNCC) equation has the form:
				</p>

				<men>
					a_n y^{(n)} + a_{n-1} y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_0 y = f(x)
				</men>

				<p>
					Its general solution is built from two parts:
				</p>

				<men>
					y = y_h + y_p
				</men>

				<ul marker="square">
					<li>
						<p>
							<m>y_h</m> is the solution to the corresponding homogeneous (LHCC) equation:
							<me> a_n y^{(n)} + \cdots + a_0 y = 0 </me>
						</p>
					</li>

					<li><m>y_p</m> the particular solution that produces the forcing function <m>f(x)</m>.</li>
				</ul>
			</p>
		</assemblage>

		<task label="lncc-equation-chkpt-3">

			<statement>

						</p>
					</feedback>
				</choice>

		<p>
			Here's a quick example to help solidify this concept.
		</p>
	
		<example><title> Finding the general solution, given <m>y_p</m> </title>
			<p>
				<statement>
					<p>
						Find the general solution to the differential equation
						<me>
							y'' - 4y' - 12y = 3{e^{5t}}
						</me>
						given that the particular solution is known to be <m>y_p(t) =  -\frac{3}{7}{{e}^{5t}}</m>.
					</p>
				</statement>
			</p>
			<solution>
				<p>
					The general solution has the form:
				</p>

				<me>
					y = y_h + y_p
				</me>

				<p>
					Since <m>y_p</m> is given, we only need to solve the homogeneous equation:
				</p>

				<me>
					y'' - 4y' - 12y = 0
				</me>

				<p>
					Using the characteristic equation:
				</p>

				<md>
					<mrow> r^2 - 4r - 12 	\amp = 0											</mrow>
					<mrow> (r - 6)(r + 2) 	\amp = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad r_1 = 6, r_2 = -2	</mrow>
				</md>

				<p>
					The homogeneous solution is:
				</p>

				<me>
					y_h(t) = c_1e^{-2t} + c_2e^{6t}
				</me>

				<p>
					Therefore, the general solution is:
				</p>

				<me>
					y(t) = \ub{c_1e^{-2t} + c_2e^{6t}\vphantom{\frac{3}{7}}}_{\Large y_h}\ \ub{-\frac{3}{7}{e^{5t}}}_{\Large y_p}
				</me>
			</solution>
		</example>

		<p>
			While the structure of LNCC solutions is now clear, actually determining <m>y_p</m> requires a systematic approach. The next section introduces the Method of Undetermined Coefficients, a technique for efficiently constructing <m>y_p</m> when <m>f(x)</m> has a specific form.
		</p>
	</paragraphs>

	<objectives>
		<ul>
			<li>
				A linear differential equation is <em>nonhomogeneous</em> if the right-hand side is not zero‚Äîit includes a <term>forcing function</term>, <m>f(x)</m>.
			</li>
			<li>
				You can often guess a particular solution that matches the structural forms of the forcing function.
			</li>
			<li>
				The general solution is <m>y = y_h + y_p</m>.
			</li>
		</ul>
	</objectives>

<section label="selecting-the-particular-soln">
	<title> Selecting the Particular Solution Form </title>
	
	<introduction>
		<p>
			In the previous section, we saw that the general solution to a linear nonhomogeneous constant coefficient (LNCC) equation has two parts: the <em>homogeneous solution</em> <m>y_h</m> and the <em>particular solution</em> <m>y_p</m>. The homogeneous solution is found using the characteristic equation, while the particular solution is constructed by (1) selecting a form similar to the forcing function, <m>f(x)</m>, and (2) determining the coefficients of that form.
		</p>

		<p>
			In this section, we focus on selecting a form for <m>y_p</m>. The key idea is to match the structural form of the forcing function <m>f(x)</m>, so that when it is substituted into the differential equation, all the resulting terms simplify to <m>f(x)</m>.
		</p>
	</introduction>

	<paragraphs label="selecting-basic-forms-for-yp">
		<title> Selecting Basic Forms </title>

		<p>
			The first step in choosing <m>y_p</m> is recognizing the structure of <m>f(x)</m>. Since <m>y_p</m> and its derivatives must eventually simplify to match <m>f(x)</m>, <m>y_p</m> should have a similar form. If <m>f(x)</m> is a polynomial, exponential, sine, or cosine function, then <m>y_p</m> should be too.
		</p>

		<p>
			<xref ref="yp-forms-table"/> provides a guide for selecting an initial form of <m>y_p</m> based on the type of <m>f(x)</m>. In each case, <m>A, B, C, \dots</m> represent the undetermined coefficients we'll solve for later.
		</p>

		<table xml:id="yp-forms-table">
			<title> 
				<m>y_p</m> forms based on <m>f(x)</m>, where <m>a, b, c, d, \alpha, \beta</m> are known constants. <m>A, B, C, D</m> must be determined. Degree 4+ polynomials follow a similar pattern.
			</title>
			<tabular top="medium" left="medium" right="medium" bottom="minor">
				<col halign="center" top="none" right="minor"/>
				<col halign="center" right="minor"/>
				<col halign="center"/>
				<row left="none" bottom="medium">

				</row>
				<row>
				<cell>1</cell>
				<cell><m>a</m> (constant)</cell>
				<cell><m>A</m></cell>
				</row>
				<row>
				<cell>2</cell>
				<cell><m>ax + b</m></cell>
				<cell><m>Ax + B</m></cell>
				</row>
				<row>

				</row>
				<row>
				<cell>4</cell>

		<p>
			One important note: even if a term is missing in <m>f(x)</m>, you should still include it in <m>y_p</m> if it's part of the general form. For example:
		</p>

		<sidebyside widths="5% 95%" valign="top">
			<p>üî∏</p>
			<p>
				If <m>f(x) = x^2 + 1</m>, the <m>x</m> term is missing, but <m>y_p</m> must still include it:
				<me> y_p = Ax^2 + Bx + C </me>.
			</p>
		</sidebyside>

		<sidebyside widths="5% 95%" valign="top">
			<p>üî∏</p>
			<p>
				If <m>f(x) = \cos(3x)</m>, then <m>y_p = A\cos(3x) + B\sin(3x)</m>. Even though <m>\sin(3x)</m> isn't in <m>f(x)</m>, it's needed because its derivative produces cosine terms.
			</p>
		</sidebyside>

		<example>
			<title> Selecting Basic Forms of <m>y_p</m> </title>
			<statement>

					</p>

	<paragraphs label="yp-method-limits">
		<title> When Guessing <m>y_p</m> Works (and When It Doesn't) </title>
		<p>
			This method of guessing the particular solution based on the forcing function is very effective. However, it does have its limitations. In particular, it only applies when <m>f(x)</m> can be written as a sum or product of:
		</p>

		<ul marker="square">
			<li><p>Polynomials, like <m>f(x) = 3x^2 - 4x + 5</m></p></li>
			<li><p>Exponentials, like <m>f(x) = 6 e^{2x}</m></p></li>
			<li><p>Sine and cosine functions, like <m>f(x) = \cos(3x) + 2\sin(3x)</m></p></li>
			<li><p>Any sum or product of these function types</p></li>
		</ul>
			
		<p>
			Functions such as <m>\ln x</m>, <m>\tan x</m>, or <m>1/x</m> fall outside this category. For those, we'll need a different method entirely. But for the allowed types, this method offers a fast and reliable way to select <m>y_p</m>.
		</p>

				</choice>
				<choice correct="yes">
					<statement>Exponential functions like <m>e^{2x}</m></statement>
					<feedback>Correct! Exponential functions are also suitable.</feedback>

				</choice>
				<choice correct="no">

					<feedback>Not quite. The method does not apply to logarithmic or tangent functions.</feedback>
				</choice>

		</task>

	<paragraphs label="adding-yp-forms">
		<title> Combining Basic Forms via Addition </title>

		<p>
			If <m>f(x)</m> is a sum of different function types, say a polynomial plus an exponential, then <m>y_p</m> should be a sum of the corresponding matching forms.
		</p>

		<example>
			<title> Initial form of <m>y_p</m> </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Use <xref ref="yp-forms-table"/> to identify the appropriate form of <m>y_p</m> in each case.
				</p>

				<sidebyside widths="38% 60%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
					<p><m>\ds y'' + 2y' + 3y = 2e^{w} - 1</m></p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								This matches is exponential plus constant, so:
								<me> y_p = Ae^{w} + B </me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<sidebyside widths="38% 60%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
					<p><m>\ds u'' - u = 3\sin(2\theta) + \cos(2\theta)</m></p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								Both terms have the same argument, <m>2\theta</m>, the form is the standard <xref ref="yp-forms-table-row-6" text="title"/>
								<me> y_p = A\sin(2\theta) + B\cos(2\theta) </me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<sidebyside widths="38% 60%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
					<p><m>\ds \omega''' + \omega' = 5e^{3w} - 2\sin(4w)</m></p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								This combines exponential and sine, so we need:
								<me> y_p = Ae^{3w} + B\sin(4w) + C\cos(4w) </me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<sidebyside widths="38% 60%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
					<p><m>\ds P' + P = \cos(-3t) - \cos(5t)</m></p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								Cosines with different arguments requires the sum of two forms:
								<md>
									<mrow> y_p = A\sin(3t) \amp + B\cos(3t) </mrow>
									<mrow> \amp + C\sin(5t) + D\cos(5t) </mrow>
								</md>
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>
			</statement>
		</example>

	<paragraphs label="product-forms-simplifying">
  		<title> Combining Basic Forms via Multiplication </title>

		<p>
			If the forcing function <m>f(x)</m> is a product of basic function types‚Äîsuch as a polynomial times an exponential‚Äîthen the particular solution <m>y_p</m> must also be a product of their matching forms. For example, the differential equation
		</p>

		<me>
			y'' + y' + 5y = 10x^2 e^{6x}
		</me>,

		<p>
			has a forcing function that is a quadratic times an exponential, so we start with:
			<me> y_p = (Ax^2 + Bx + C)(D e^{6x}) </me>.
		</p>

		<p>
			However, be careful: multiplying by <m>D</m> just makes new coefficients:
			<me>
				y_p = (AD x^2 + BD x + CD) e^{6x}
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			Since each pair can be merged as single coefficient, we just relabel them:
			<me>
				y_p = (Ax^2 + Bx + C) e^{6x}
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			We'll use this simplification trick often. It keeps the form of <m>y_p</m> clean and will make solving for <m>A</m>, <m>B</m> and <m>C</m> much easier later.
		</p>

		<task label="selecting-the-particular-soln-chkpt-4">
			<title> üìñ‚ùì Product of particular solution forms </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Select the initial form of the particular solution for an LNCC equation with forcing function:
					<me>
						f(x) = x^2 e^{x}
					</me>.
				</p>
			</statement>
			<choices randomize="yes">
				<choice correct="yes">
					<statement><m>\ds\quad y_p = (Ax^2 + Bx + C)e^{x}</m></statement>
					<feedback>Correct! The forcing function is the product of a 2nd degree polynomial and an exponential function, so the particular solution should be the product the most general 2nd degree polynomial and an exponential function.</feedback>
				</choice>
				<choice>
					<statement><m>\ds\quad y_p = Ax^2e^{x}</m></statement>
					<feedback>Incorrect. Although this form perfectly matches the form of the forcing function, the solution could potentially include <m>x</m> and constant terms.</feedback>
				</choice>
				<choice>
					<statement><m>\ds\quad y_p = (x^2 + Ax + B)e^x</m></statement>
					<feedback>Incorrect. The <m>x^2</m> term also needs a coefficient.</feedback>
				</choice>
				<choice>
					<statement><m>\ds\quad y_p = (Ax^2 + Bx + C)(De^{x})</m></statement>
					<feedback>Incorrect. The extra coefficient <m>D</m> is unnecessary, as it can be absorbed into the constants <m>A, B,</m> and <m>C</m>.</feedback>
				</choice>
			</choices>
		</task>

		<example><title> Initial form of <m>y_p</m> - Products of Forms </title>

			<statement>
				<p>
					For each of the following forcing functions, select an appropriate initial form of <m>y_p</m>. Simplify by combining or eliminating any redundant constants.
				</p>

			<sidebyside widths="34% 64%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
				<p><m>\ds f(x) = e^{-x}\cos(x)</m></p>
				<p>
					<solution>
						<p>
							This is an exponential times a cosine:
							<me>
								y_p = Ae^{-x}(B\sin x + C\cos x)
							</me>.
							However, distributing <m>A</m> shows that it is redundant and we relabel as:
							<me>
								y_p = e^{-x}(A\sin x + B\cos x)
							</me>.
						</p>
					</solution>
				</p>
			</sidebyside>

			<sidebyside widths="34% 64%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
				<p><m>\ds f(x) = e^{6x}e^{-2x}</m></p>
				<p>
					<solution>
						<p>
							The forcing function simplifies: <m>e^{6x}e^{-2x} = e^{4x}</m>, so:
							<me> y_p = Ae^{4x} </me>
						</p>
					</solution>
				</p>
			</sidebyside>

			<sidebyside widths="34% 64%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
				<p><m>\ds f(t) = t^{3}\cos t</m></p>
				<p>
					<solution>
						<p>
							This is a 3rd degree polynomial times a cosine. So, initially we set:
							<me>
								y_p = (At^3 + Bt^2 + Ct + D)(E\sin t + F\cos t)
							</me>.
						</p>

						<p>
							Multiplying the polynomial onto sine and cosine, gives us
							<md>
								<mrow>	y_p = (At^3	\amp + Bt^2 + Ct + D)E\sin t </mrow>
								<mrow>				\amp + (At^3 + Bt^2 + Ct + D)F\cos t </mrow>
							</md>.
						</p>

						<p>
							Distributing <m>E</m> and <m>F</m> shows that they are redundant, so we relabel as follows:
							<md>
								<mrow>	y_p = (At^3	\amp + Bt^2 + Ct + D)\sin t </mrow>
								<mrow>				\amp + (Et^3 + Ft^2 + Gt + H)\cos t</mrow>.
							</md>
						</p>
				</solution>
				</p>
			</sidebyside>

			<sidebyside widths="34% 64%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
				<p><m>\ds f(x) = x^2\cos x + x \sin x</m></p>
				<p>
					<solution>
						<p>
							Here, both terms involve powers of <m>x</m> times trig functions. But we don't need separate guesses‚Äîone form covers both:
							<md>
								<mrow> y_p = (Ax^2 + Bx	\amp + C)\cos x </mrow>
								<mrow>					\amp + (Dx^2 + Ex + F)\sin x </mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
						<p>
							This form includes the <m>x^2\cos x</m> term and also handles <m>x\sin x</m> automatically.
						</p>
					</solution>
				</p>
			</sidebyside>
			</statement>
		</example>

		</p>

				</match>
				<match>
					<premise> <m> f(x) = e^{-12x} \cos x </m> </premise>
					<response><m> y_p = e^{-12x}(A\cos x + B\sin x) </m> </response>
				</match>
				<match>
					<response> <m> y_p = A x \sin x </m> </response>
					<response> <m> y_p = (Ax + B) \sin x </m> </response>
				</match>
			</cardsort>
		</task>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs label="modifying-yp">
		<title> Modifying Particular Solutions </title>
		<p>
			We now know how to choose a form for <m>y_p</m> based on the structure of <m>f(x)</m>, but there's one final complication: If a term in <m>y_p</m> a term in the homogeneous solution <m>y_h</m>, then that term cannot contribute to generating <m>f(x)</m>. Instead, it will give zero, since that is what terms in <m>y_h</m> do when substituted into the differential equation.
		</p>

		<p>
			To fix this, we multiply the overlapping part of <m>y_p</m> by <m>x</m>. If there's still overlap, we multiply by <m>x</m> again‚Äîrepeating until <m>y_p</m> shares no like-terms with <m>y_h</m>.
		</p>

		<p>
			For example, consider:
			<me> y'' - 4y' + 3y = e^{3x} </me>

			The homogeneous solution and the initial particular solution are:
			<me>
				y_h = c_1 e^x + {\DLO c_2 e^{3x}}, \quad y_p = {\DLO A e^{3x}}
			</me>.
		</p>

		<p>
			Notice that <m>y_h</m> and  in <m>y_p</m> contain the like terms: <m>c_2 e^{3x}</m> and <m>A e^{3x}</m>. This means that plugging <m>y_p</m> into the left side of the equation will simplify to <m>0</m>, not <m>e^{3x}</m>, as required. 
		</p>

		<p>
			So we modify <m>y_p</m> by multiplying the conflicting term by <m>x</m>:
			<me> y_p = A x e^{3x} </me>
		</p>

		<p>
			Now, <m>y_p</m> has no terms in common with <m>y_h</m>, ensuring that they are independent.
		</p>

		<assemblage xml:id="modifying-yp">
			<title> Modification Rule for the Particular Solution </title>
			<p>
				If any term in your guess for <m>y_p</m> also appears in <m>y_h</m>, multiply that term by the independent variable <m>x</m>. Repeat as needed until all overlapping terms are eliminated.
			</p>
		</assemblage>

		<task label="selecting-the-particular-soln-chkpt-6">
			<title> üìñ‚ùì Handling Like Terms </title>
			<statement>
				<p>What should you do if <m>y_p</m> contains a term also present in <m>y_h</m>?</p>
			</statement>
			<choices randomize="yes">
				<choice correct="yes">
					<statement>Multiply the term in <m>y_p</m> by the independent variable.</statement>
				</choice>
				<choice correct="no">
					<statement>Delete the term from <m>y_p</m>.</statement>
				</choice>
				<choice correct="no">
					<statement>Multiply the term in <m>y_h</m> by the independent variable.</statement>
				</choice>
				<choice correct="no">
					<statement>Use integration to eliminate the term.</statement>
				</choice>
			</choices>
		</task>

		</p>

<section label="uc-method">
	<title> Undetermined Coefficients Method </title>
	
	<introduction>
		<p>
			In the last sections, we learned how to choose a form for the particular solution <m>y_p</m> by matching it to the forcing function <m>f(x)</m>, and how to modify <m>y_p</m> when it overlaps with the homogeneous solution <m>y_h</m>.
		</p>

		<p>
			Now, we'll finish the process by finding the exact values of the unknown coefficients in <m>y_p</m>. This final step is called the <term>Method of Undetermined Coefficients</term>, and it uses substitution and simplification to solve for the unknowns.
		</p>
	</introduction>

	<paragraphs label="determining-coefficients">
		<title> Determining the Coefficients </title>
		<p>
			With <m>y_p</m> properly chosen and modified, the final step is to determine the values of the unknown coefficients. This step is purely algebraic: we plug <m>y_p</m> into the differential equation, simplify, and match coefficients to solve for the unknowns.
		</p>

		<p>
			We will walk through this process using a concrete example. To solve
			<me>y'' - 2y' + y = 2x^2 + 3x</me>
			we first find <m>y_h</m> by solving the homogeneous equation
			<me>y'' - 2y' + y = 0</me>.
		</p>

		<p>
			The characteristic equation is
			<me> r^2 - 2r + 1 = 0, </me>
			which factors as <m>(r - 1)^2 = 0</m>. This gives a repeated root <m>r = 1</m>, so
			<me> y_h = c_1 e^x + c_2 x e^x. </me>
		</p>

		<p>
			Now, pick <m>y_p</m> to match <m>f(x) = 2x^2 + 3x</m>. Since <m>f(x)</m> is a quadratic polynomial, we guess
			<me> y_p = A x^2 + B x + C. </me>
			<p>
				Since <m>y_p</m> has no terms in common with <m>y_h</m>, no modification is needed.
			</p>
		</p>

		<p>
			Substituting <m>y_p</m> and its derivatives,
			<me>
				y_p' = 2Ax + B,\quad y_p'' = 2A
			</me>,
			into the equation and collecting like-terms gives
			<md>
				<mrow> (2A) - 2(2Ax + B) + (Ax^2 + Bx + C) = 2x^2 + 3x </mrow>
				<mrow> 2A - 4Ax - 2B + Ax^2 + Bx + C = 2x^2 + 3x </mrow>
				<mrow> \us{\ds\text{1Ô∏è‚É£}}{\ul{A}}x^2 + \us{\ds\text{2Ô∏è‚É£}}{\ul{(-4A + B)}}x + \us{\ds\text{3Ô∏è‚É£}}{\ul{(2A - 2B + C)}} = \us{\ds\text{1Ô∏è‚É£}}{\ul{2}}x^2 + \us{\ds\text{2Ô∏è‚É£}}{\ul{3}}x + \us{\ds\text{3Ô∏è‚É£}}{\ul{0}} </mrow>
			</md>.
		</p>
		
		<p>
			Matching the underlined coefficients leads to the following system of equations and solution for <m>A</m>, <m>B</m>, and <m>C</m>:
		</p>

		<sidebyside widths="30% 25% 5% 16%" margins="12% 12%">
			<p>
				<md>
					<mrow>	\amp\ \text{1Ô∏è‚É£}\ (x^2\ \text{terms})	</mrow>
					<mrow>	\amp\ \text{2Ô∏è‚É£}\ (x\ \text{terms})	</mrow>
					<mrow>	\amp\ \text{3Ô∏è‚É£}\ (\text{constant terms})	</mrow>
				</md>
			</p>
			<p>
				<md>
					<mrow> A			\amp = 2	</mrow>
					<mrow> -4A + B		\amp = 3	</mrow>
					<mrow> 2A - 2B + C	\amp = 0	</mrow>
				</md>
			</p>
			<p>
				<md>
					<mrow>	\rightarrow	</mrow>
					<mrow>	\rightarrow	</mrow>
					<mrow>	\rightarrow	</mrow>
				</md>
			</p>
			<p>
				<md>
					<mrow>	\amp\ A = 2  </mrow>
					<mrow>	\amp\ B = 11 </mrow>
					<mrow>	\amp\ C = 18  </mrow>
				</md>
			</p>
		</sidebyside>

		<p>
			Therefore, <m>y_p = 2x^2 + 11x + 18</m> and we can now write the general solution as
			<me> 
				y = y_h + y_p = c_1 e^x + c_2 x e^x + 2x^2 + 11x + 18
			</me>.
		</p>

		<p>
			Assuming the forcing function is of the type found in <xref ref="yp-forms-table"/>, this process should always lead to a single solution. If not, then double check your work and make sure the form of <m>y_p</m> was selected and modified correctly.
		</p>

		<task label="uc-method-chkpt-1">
			<title> üìñ‚ùì Not A Step in Finding <m>y_p</m> </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Which of the following is NOT a step in finding the undetermined coefficients of the particular solution?
				</p>
			</statement>
			<choices randomize="yes">
				<choice correct="yes">
					<statement>Applying the initial conditions.</statement>
					<feedback>Correct! The initial conditions are used to solve for the unknown constants in the general solution, not the particular solution.</feedback>
				</choice>
				<choice>
					<statement>Plugging <m> y_p </m> into the differential equation.</statement>
					<feedback>Incorrect. This is the first step in determining the coefficients.</feedback>
				</choice>
				<choice>
					<statement>Collecting and matching like-terms.</statement>
					<feedback>Incorrect. This is a crucial step for setting up the system of equations used to find the undetermined coefficients: <m>A</m>, <m>B</m>, ...</feedback>
				</choice>
				<choice>
					<statement>Solving a system of equations.</statement>
					<feedback>Incorrect. This is the final step in finding the coefficients.</feedback>
				</choice>
			</choices>
		</task>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs label="uc-method-steps">
		<title> Undetermined Coefficient Method Steps </title>
		<p>
			Let's summarize the entire process of using the Method of Undetermined Coefficients. Your goal is to find the full general solution <m>y = y_h + y_p</m> by first solving the homogeneous equation and then selecting, modifying, and finding <m>y_p</m>.
		</p>

		<exploration xml:id="uc-method"><title> Undetermined Coefficients </title>
			<p>
				The general solution for the linear nonhomogeneous constant coefficient equation,
				<men xml:id="uc-method-lncc-de">
					a_n y^{(n)} + a_{n-1} y^{(n-1)} + \dots + a_1 y' + a_0 y = f(x)
				</men>,
				is determined from the following steps:
			</p>

			<dl width="narrow">
				<li xml:id="uc-step-1">
					<title> Step 1: Solve the homogeneous equation to get <m>y_h</m> </title>
					<p>
						Find the homogeneous solution <m>y_h</m> by solving the characteristic equation from
						<me>
							a_n y^{(n)} + a_{n-1} y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_1 y' + a_0 y = 0
						</me>.
					</p>
				</li>
				<li xml:id="uc-step-2">
					<title> Step 2: Select the form of <m>y_p</m> </title>
					<p>
						Use <xref ref="yp-forms-table"/> to select a starting form for <m>y_p</m> based on <m>f(x)</m>. The constants <m>A</m>, <m>B</m>, <m>C</m>, ... are the <em>undetermined coefficients</em>.
					</p>
				</li>				
				<li xml:id="uc-step-3">
					<title> Step 3: Modify <m>y_p</m> if it conflicts with <m>y_h</m> </title>
					<p>
						Check <m>y_p</m> for conflicting terms already in <m>y_h</m>. If present, multiply these terms by <m>x</m>. Repeat until no terms are shared.
					</p>
				</li>
				<li xml:id="uc-step-4">
					<title> Step 4: Find the Undetermined Coefficients of <m>y_p</m> </title>
					<p>
						Substitute <m>y_p</m> and its derivatives into <xref ref="uc-method-lncc-de"/>. Simplify and match coefficients of like terms to set up a system of equations. Solving this system gives the coefficients, <m>A</m>, <m>B</m>, <m>C</m>, ....
					</p>
				</li>
				<li xml:id="uc-step-5">
					<title> Step 5: Give the Solution </title>
					<p>
						Write the general solution as
						<me>
							y = y_h + y_p.
						</me>
						and apply any initial conditions, if given.
					</p>
				</li>
			</dl>
		</exploration>

		<p>
			You'll follow these same five steps for any forcing function that fits the method. Now let's apply this process to some worked examples to see how it all comes together.
		</p>

		<p>
			<task label="uc-method-chkpt-2">
				<title> üìñ‚ùì Not A Step in the Undetermined Coefficient Method </title>
				<statement>
					<p>
						What are the main steps for finding <m>y_p</m> using the method of undetermined coefficients?
					</p>
				</statement>
				<choices randomize="yes">
					<choice correct="yes">
						<statement>Select a <m>y_p</m> form ‚ûú modify it, if necessary ‚ûú find its coefficients.</statement>
						<feedback>Correct! The process involves making an educated guess, substituting it, and solving for unknown coefficients.</feedback>
					</choice>
					<choice correct="no">
						<statement>Select a <m>y_p</m> form ‚ûú integrate it ‚ûú find its coefficients.</statement>
						<feedback>Incorrect. Integration is not a part of the method of undetermined coefficients.</feedback>
					</choice>
					<choice correct="no">
						<statement>Select a <m>y_p</m> form ‚ûú find its coefficients ‚ûú differentiate it.</statement>
						<feedback>Incorrect. The focus is on selecting and solving for <m>y_p</m>, not guessing the homogeneous solution.</feedback>
					</choice>
					<choice correct="no">
						<statement>Select a <m>y_p</m> form ‚ûú differentiate it ‚ûú verify the solution.</statement>
						<feedback>Incorrect. Integration is not a step in this method; it's about solving for coefficients of <m>y_p</m>.</feedback>
					</choice>
				</choices>
			</task>

			<task label="uc-method-chkpt-3">
				<title> üìñ‚ùì Final Step of the Method </title>
				<statement>
					<p>In the final step of the method, after substituting <m>y_p</m> and simplifying, what do you do next?</p>
				</statement>
				<choices randomize="yes">
					<choice correct="no"><statement>Graph <m>y_h</m> and <m>y_p</m> to verify behavior.</statement></choice>
					<choice correct="no"><statement>Use initial conditions to determine integration constants.</statement></choice>
					<choice correct="yes"><statement>Match coefficients of like terms and solve for unknown constants in <m>y_p</m>.</statement></choice>
					<choice correct="no"><statement>Integrate <m>y_p</m> to eliminate forcing terms.</statement></choice>
				</choices>
			</task>
		</p>
	</paragraphs>

		<example>
			<title> First-Order LNCC with an Initial Condition </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Find the general solution of
					<me> y' - 3y = 9x^2 + x, \quad y(0) = 5 </me>
				</p>
			</statement>
			<solution>
				<p><xref ref="uc-step-1" text="title"/></p>
				<sbsgroup widths="40% 56%" margins="2% 2%" valign="middle">
					<sidebyside>
						<p>characteristic equation:</p>
						<p><m>\ds r - 3 = 0	\quad\Rightarrow\quad r = 3 </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
					<sidebyside>
						<p>homogeneous solution:</p>
						<p><m>\ds y_h = c_1 e^{3x} </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
				</sbsgroup>

				<p><xref ref="uc-step-2" text="title"/></p>
				<sbsgroup widths="20% 76%" margins="2% 2%" valign="middle">
					<sidebyside>
						<p>initial <m>y_p</m>:</p>
						<p><m>\ds y_p = A x^2 + B x + C </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
				</sbsgroup>

				<p><xref ref="uc-step-3" text="title"/> ‚ûú No modification needed.</p>

				<p><xref ref="uc-step-4" text="title"/></p>
				<sbsgroup widths="30% 66%" margins="2% 2%" valign="middle">
					<sidebyside>
						<p>compute <m>y_p'</m>:</p>
						<p><m>y_p' = 2A x + B </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
					<sidebyside>
						<p>substitute <m>y_p</m>, <m>y_p'</m>:</p>
						<p><m> (2A x + B) - 3 (A x^2 + B x + C) = 9x^2 + x </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
					<sidebyside>
						<p>group powers of <m>x</m>:</p>
						<p><m> (-3A)\ x^2 + (2A - 3B)\ x + (B - 3C) = 9x^2 + x </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
					<sidebyside widths="15% 24% 4% 32% 4% 17%" valign="top">
						<p>
							Match coefficients:
						</p>
						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow> -3A		\amp = 9 </mrow>
								<mrow> 2A - 3B	\amp = 1 </mrow>
								<mrow> B - 3C	\amp = 0 </mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow>	\Rightarrow	</mrow>
								<mrow>	\Rightarrow	</mrow>
								<mrow>	\Rightarrow	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow> A = -3 \amp </mrow>
								<mrow> 2(-3)-3B			\amp = -2 </mrow>
								<mrow> -\sfrac34 - 3C	\amp = 0 </mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow/>
								<mrow>	\Rightarrow	</mrow>
								<mrow>	\Rightarrow	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow/>
								<mrow> B \amp = -\sfrac{3}{4} </mrow>
								<mrow> C \amp = -\sfrac{1}{4} </mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
					<sidebyside>
						<p>particular solution:</p>
						<p><m>\ds y_p = -3 x^2 - \frac34 x - \frac14 </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
				</sbsgroup>

				<p><xref ref="uc-step-5" text="title"/></p>
				<sbsgroup widths="30% 66%" margins="2% 2%" valign="middle">
					<sidebyside>
						<p>general solution:</p>
						<p><m>\ds y = c_1 e^{3x} - 3 x^2 - \frac34 x - \frac14 </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
					<sidebyside>
						<p>apply <m>y(0) = 5</m>:</p>
						<p><m>\ds 5 = y(0) = c_1 - \frac14 \quad\Rightarrow c_1 = \frac{21}{4} </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
					<sidebyside>
						<p>final solution:</p>
						<p><m>\ds y = \frac{21}{4} e^{3x} - 3 x^2 - \frac34 x - \frac14 </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
				</sbsgroup>
			</solution>
		</example>

		<example>
			<title> Exponential Forcing Function </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Find the general solution of
					<me>
						y'' - 3y' + 2y = 5e^{2x}
					</me>
				</p>
			</statement>
			<solution>
				<p><xref ref="uc-step-1" text="title"/></p>
				<sbsgroup widths="40% 56%" margins="2% 2%" valign="middle">
					<sidebyside>
						<p>characteristic equation:</p>
						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow> r^2 - 3r + 2 = 0  \amp\quad\Rightarrow\quad (r - 1)(r - 2) = 0 </mrow>
								<mrow>					\amp\quad\Rightarrow\quad r = 1, 2 </mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
					<sidebyside>
						<p>homogeneous solution:</p>
						<p><m>\ds y_h = c_1 e^x + c_2 e^{2x} </m></p>

				<sbsgroup widths="20% 76%" margins="2% 2%" valign="middle">
					<sidebyside>
						<p>initial <m>y_p</m>:</p>
						<p><m>\ds y_p = A e^{2x} </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
				</sbsgroup>

				<p><xref ref="uc-step-3" text="title"/></p>
				<p>Since <m>Ae^{2x}</m> and <m>c_2e^{2x}</m> are like terms, we modify <m>y_p</m>:</p>
				<sbsgroup widths="20% 76%" margins="2% 2%" valign="middle">
					<sidebyside>
						<p>modified <m>y_p</m>:</p>
						<p><m>\ds y_p = Axe^{2x} </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
				</sbsgroup>

				<p><xref ref="uc-step-4" text="title"/></p>
				<sbsgroup widths="30% 66%" margins="2% 2%" valign="top">
					<sidebyside>
						<p>compute <m>y_p'</m>, <m>y_p''</m>:</p>
						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow>y_p'	\amp = A e^{2x} + 2Ax e^{2x}</mrow>

							</md>
						</p>

				<sbsgroup widths="30% 66%" margins="2% 2%" valign="top">
					<sidebyside>
						<p>group like terms:</p>
						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow>
									\ub{(2A - 3A + 2A)}_{\ds = A} e^{2x} + \ub{(4A - 6A + 2A)}_{\ds = 0} x e^{2x} = 5e^{2x}
								</mrow>

								</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>

						<p><m> A = 5 </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
					<sidebyside>

						<p><m>\ds y_p = 5 x e^{2x} </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
				</sbsgroup>

				<p><xref ref="uc-step-5" text="title"/></p>
				<sbsgroup widths="30% 66%" margins="2% 2%" valign="middle">
					<sidebyside>
						<p>general solution:</p>
						<p><m>\ds y = c_1 e^x + c_2 e^{2x} + 5 x e^{2x} </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
				</sbsgroup>
			</solution>

		<example>
			<title> Second-Order LNCC with <m>y_p</m> Modification </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Find the general solution of
					<me>
						y'' + 3y' - 28y = 14t - 1 + e^{4t}
					</me>
				</p>
			</statement>
			<solution>
				<p><xref ref="uc-step-1" text="title"/></p>
				<sbsgroup widths="30% 58%" margins="2% 10%" valign="top">
					<sidebyside>
						<p>characteristic equation:</p>
						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow> r^2 + 3r - 28 = 0	\amp\quad\Rightarrow\quad (r - 4)(r + 7) = 0 </mrow>
								<mrow>						\amp\quad\Rightarrow\quad r = 4, -7 </mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
					<sidebyside>
						<p>homogeneous solution:</p>
						<p><m>\ds y_h = c_1 e^{4t} + c_2 e^{-7t} </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
				</sbsgroup>

				<p><xref ref="uc-step-2" text="title"/></p>
				<sbsgroup widths="20% 76%" margins="2% 2%" valign="middle">
					<sidebyside>
						<p>initial <m>y_p</m>:</p>
						<p><m>\ds y_p = At + B + Ce^{4t} </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
				</sbsgroup>

				<p><xref ref="uc-step-3" text="title"/></p>
				<p>Since <m>y_p</m> and <m>y_h</m> share an <m>e^{4t}</m> term, we modify this term in <m>y_p</m>:</p>
				<sbsgroup widths="20% 76%" margins="2% 2%" valign="middle">
					<sidebyside>
						<p>modified <m>y_p</m>:</p>
						<p><m>\ds y_p = At + B + Ct\,e^{4t} </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
				</sbsgroup>

				<p><xref ref="uc-step-4" text="title"/></p>
				<sbsgroup widths="30% 66%" margins="2% 2%" valign="top">
					<sidebyside widths="26% 40%" margins="2% 30%">
						<p>compute <m>y_p'</m>, <m>y_p''</m>:</p>
						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow>
									y_p'	\amp = A + C e^{4t} + 4Ct\, e^{4t}
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									y_p'' \amp = 8C e^{4t} + 16Ct\, e^{4t}
								</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
				</sbsgroup>

				<p>substitute <m>y_p</m>, <m>y_p'</m>, <m>y_p''</m>:</p>
				<p>
					<md>
						<mrow>
							8C\ e^{4t} + 16Ct\, e^{4t} + 3(A + C e^{4t} + 4Ct\, e^{4t}) - 28(At \amp + B + Ct\, e^{4t})
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = 14t - 1 + e^{4t}
						</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>

				<p>The <m>te^{4t}</m> terms cancel, but you can group the <m>e^{4t}</m>, <m>t</m>, and constant terms:</p>
				<p>
					<md>
						<mrow>

		<example>
			<title> Forcing Function as a Product </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
				Find the general solution of
				<me> y'' - 10y' = x \sin x </me>
				</p>
			</statement>
			<solution>
				<p><xref ref="uc-step-1" text="title"/></p>
				<sbsgroup widths="40% 56%" margins="2% 2%" valign="middle">
					<sidebyside>
						<p>characteristic equation:</p>
						<p><m>\ds r^2 - 10r = 0, \ r(r - 10) = 0, \ r = 0, 10 </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
					<sidebyside>
						<p>homogeneous solution:</p>
						<p><m>\ds y_h = c_1 + c_2 e^{10x} </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
				</sbsgroup>

				<p><xref ref="uc-step-2" text="title"/></p>
				<sbsgroup widths="20% 76%" margins="2% 2%" valign="middle">
					<sidebyside>
						<p>initial <m>y_p</m>:</p>
						<p><m>\ds y_p = (A x + B) \cos x + (C x + D) \sin x </m></p>
					</sidebyside>
				</sbsgroup>

				<p><xref ref="uc-step-3" text="title"/> ‚ûú No modification needed.</p>

				<p><xref ref="uc-step-4" text="title"/></p>
				<p>
					<em>Note: This part can be a bit intense. The product rule is used multiple times and after each step, we regroup the <m>\cos x</m>, <m>\sin x</m>, <m>x\cos x</m>, and <m>x\sin x</m> terms.</em>
				</p>
				<p>compute <m>y_p'</m> and <m>y_p''</m>:</p>
				<md>
					<mrow> y_p'		\amp = A \cos x - (Ax + B) \sin x + C \sin x + (Cx + D) \cos x </mrow>
					<mrow>			\amp = (A + D)\cos x + (-B + C)\sin x + Cx\cos x - Ax\sin x </mrow>
					<mrow> y_p''	\amp = -(A + D)\sin x + (-B + C)\cos x </mrow>
					<mrow> 			\amp \hspace{4cm} + C\cos x - Cx\sin x - A\sin x - Ax\cos x </mrow>
					<mrow>			\amp = (-B + 2C)\cos x + (-2A + D)\sin x - Ax\cos x - Cx\sin x</mrow>
				</md>

				<p>substitute <m>y_p''</m>, <m>y_p'</m>:</p>
				<md>
					<mrow> ((-B \amp + 2C)\cos x + (-2A + D)\sin x - Ax\cos x - Cx\sin x)  </mrow>
					<mrow> \amp - 10((A + D)\cos x + (-B + C)\sin x + Cx\cos x - Ax\sin x) = x \sin x </mrow>
				</md>

				<p>group like-terms:</p>
				<md>
					<mrow> \amp (-B + 2C - 10A - 10D)\cos x </mrow>
					<mrow> \amp +\ (-2A + D + 10B - 10C)\sin x </mrow>
					<mrow> \amp +\ (-A + C)\ x\cos x + {\DLO (-C - A)}\ x\sin x = {\DLO 1} x \sin x </mrow>
				</md>

				<p>
					<sidebyside widths="20% 39% 4% 35%">
						<p>
							Match coefficients:
						</p>
						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow> -B + 2C - 10A - 10D	\amp = 0 </mrow>
								<mrow> -2A + D + 10B - 10C	\amp = 0 </mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
						<p/>
						<p/>
					</sidebyside>
					<sidebyside widths="40% 19% 10% 16%" valign="middle" margins="0% 13%">
						<p/>
						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow> -A + C			\amp = 0 </mrow>
								<mrow> {\DLO -C - A}	\amp = {\DLO 1} </mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow>	\Rightarrow	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow> A	\amp = \sfrac12 </mrow>
								<mrow> C	\amp = \sfrac12 </mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
				</p>

				<p>
					Substituting <m>A = \sfrac12</m> and <m>C = \sfrac12</m> into the first two equations gives:
				</p>

				<sidebyside widths="30% 4% 30% 4% 20%" valign="middle" margins="6% 6%">
					<p>
						<md>
							<mrow> -B - 10D	\amp = 4 </mrow>
							<mrow> 10B + D	\amp = 6 </mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
					<p>
						<md>
							<mrow>	\Rightarrow	</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
					<p>
						<md>
							<mrow> -10B - 100D	\amp = 40 </mrow>
							<mrow> 10B + D	\amp = 6 </mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
					<p>
						<md>
							<mrow>	\Rightarrow	</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
					<p>
						<md>
							<mrow> B	\amp = \sfrac{64}{99} </mrow>
							<mrow> D	\amp = \sfrac{46}{99} </mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<p>particular solution:</p>
				<me> y_p = \left(\frac{1}{2} + \frac{64}{99}x\right) \cos x + \left(\frac{1}{2} + \frac{46}{99}x\right) \sin x </me>
				
				<p><xref ref="uc-step-5" text="title"/></p>

				<p> General solution:</p>
				<me> y = c_1 \cos x + c_2 \sin x + \left(\frac{1}{2} + \frac{64}{99}x\right) \cos x + \left(\frac{1}{2} + \frac{46}{99}x\right) \sin x </me>
			</solution>
		</example>

		<p>
			These examples illustrate the systematic nature of this method. No matter how complex the forcing function, the steps remain consistent: identify the homogeneous solution, propose a form for the particular solution, modify it if necessary, compute derivatives, substitute into the differential equation, and match coefficients to solve for unknowns. 
		</p>
	</paragraphs>

	<objectives label="uc-method-wrapup">
		<ul>
			<li> 
				The general solution to a nonhomogeneous differential equation is <m>y = y_h + y_p</m>, where <m>y_h</m> solves the homogeneous equation and <m>y_p</m> accounts for the forcing function.
			</li>
			<li>
				The <term>Method of Undetermined Coefficients</term> provides a systematic way to find <m>y_p</m> when <m>f(x)</m> consists of polynomials, exponentials, sines, cosines, or their combinations.
			</li>
			<li>
				Choose <m>y_p</m> by matching the form of <m>f(x)</m> and modify it by multiplying by <m>x</m> when necessary to avoid overlap with <m>y_h</m>.
			</li>
			<li>
				Substitute <m>y_p</m> and its derivatives into the differential equation, simplify, and match like terms to solve for unknown coefficients.
			</li>
		</ul>
	</objectives>
