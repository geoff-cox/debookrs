	<subsection><title> From a Differential Equation to a Laplace Equation </title>

		<task> temporarily omitted </task>

		<p>
			While applying the Laplace transform directly from a table makes this step appear simple, it is important to understand the underlying mechanics of this process.
		</p>

		<p>
			Recall from earlier discussions that the essence of the Laplace transform involves multiplying the entire differential equation by the exponential function <m>e^{-st}</m> and then integrating from <m>0</m> to <m>\infty</m> with respect to <m>t</m>. Symbolically, we represent this operation as:
			<me>
				\ub{\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} y''\, dt}_{\large\lap{y''}}
				- \ub{\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} (9y)\, dt}_{\large\lap{9y}}
				= \ub{\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st}(10e^{2t})\, dt}_{\large\lap{10e^{2t}}}
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			Applying integration by parts to the integrals involving derivatives of the unknown function <m>y(t)</m> enables us to transfer these derivatives onto the <m>e^{-st}</m> terms, resulting in a power of <m>s</m> for each. After performing integration by parts twice on the first integral and computing the integral on the right-hand side, we arrive at:
			<me>
				-sy(0) - y'(0) + s^2\ub{\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st}y\, dt}_{\large\lap{y}=Y}
				- 9\ub{\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st}y\, dt}_{\large\lap{y}=Y}
				= \frac{10}{s-2}
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			Notice that the two integrals are <m>\lap{y}</m>, which can bundle up as just <m>Y</m>. Incorporating the provided initial conditions, <m>y(0) = 1</m> and <m>y'(0) = -7</m>, the equation simplifies neatly to the algebraic form:
			<me>
				s^2Y - 9Y - s + 7 = \frac{10}{s-2}
			</me>.
		</p>

		<p>
			By transforming our equation into this new form, we have temporarily hidden <m>y(t)</m> elegantly inside <m>Y</m>. Therefore, by solving for <m>Y</m>, we are effectively also solving for <m>y(t)</m> at the same time! While we will not be showing this every time we solve a different equation, it is nice to be able to appreciate the magic behind the curtain.
		</p>

		<p>
			In practice, we get the forward transforms from the <xref ref="lt-table-common-rules-merged" text="custom">table of transforms</xref>, which is a much faster process. Let's demostrate this procedure with some examples.
		</p>

		<example><title> Forward Transforming Equations </title>
			<p>
				<statement>

						<ol>

								</p>
							</li>
							<li>
								<p>
									<m>\ds y'' - 4y' + 6y = t^2, \quad y(0) = 2, \quad y'(0) = 0.</m>
								</p>
							</li>
						</ol>
					</p>
				</statement>
			</p>

		</p>

		<subsubsection><title> Using the Table Laplace Transforms for Inverses </title>

			<p>
				The beauty of the Laplace transform table is that it not only simplifies forward transformations but also neatly provides the corresponding inverse transformations. To reverse the process, you simply match your Laplace solution <m>F(s)</m>, found on the right side of the table, to its inverse <m>f(t)</m>, appearing on the left. For example, <xref ref="lt-L2" text="custom">L<m>_2</m></xref> conveniently offers both:
			</p>

			<p>
				<sidebyside widths="10% 40% 10% 40%" valign="top">
					<p>
						Foward transform:
					</p>
					<p>
						<me>
							\lap{ e^{at} } = \frac{1}{s - a}
						</me>
					</p>
					<p>
						Backward transform:
					</p>
					<p>
						<me>
							\ilap{ \frac{1}{s - a} } = e^{at}
						</me>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>
			</p>

Once you've simplified your algebraic solution <m>Y(s)</m> (step 3), finding its inverse becomes simply a matter of identifying the correct form from the table. 		<subsubsection><title> Linearity of <m>\laplacesym^{-1}</m> </title>

			<p>
				Usually, the Laplace solution you're trying to invert isn't just one simple term; it's composed of multiple pieces. Fortunately, like the forward transform, the inverse Laplace transform is also linear. This means that inverse transforms of sums and constant multiples of functions can be broken down into smaller, more manageable steps.
			</p>

			<assemblage xml:id="lt-inverse-linearity"><title> Linearity of the Inverse Laplace Transform </title>
				<p>
					Suppose <m>f(s)</m> and <m>g(s)</m> are functions in the Laplace domain, and <m>a</m> and <m>b</m> are constants. Then we have:
					<me>
						\ilap{ af(s) \pm bg(s) } = a\ilap{ f(s) } \pm b\ilap{ g(s) }
					</me>
				</p>
			</assemblage>

			<p>
				Let's walk through an example to illustrate how this linearity property streamlines the backward transformation.
			</p>

			<example><title> Inverse Transforms using Linearity </title>
				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Apply a backward transform (step 3) to revert each Laplace solution back into a function of <m>t</m>.
							<ol>
								<li>
									<p>
										<m>\ds Y(s) = \frac{12}{s} + \frac{7s}{s^2 + 25} </m>
									</p>
								</li>
								<li>
									<p>
										<m>\ds Y(s) = \frac{4}{(s - 2)^2 + 16} + \frac{2}{(s+7)^5}</m>
									</p>
								</li>
							</ol>
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>

			<p>
				By leveraging linearity, we've simplified what initially appeared as complicated inverses into straightforward, table-based solutions. As you become more comfortable with this approach, you'll find inverse transformations much quicker and easier. However, sometimes we'll still need a bit more strategy, which we'll explore in the next section.
			</p>

		</subsubsection>

		<subsubsection><title> Minor Adjustments for a Perfect Match </title>

			<p> 
				Often, the Laplace solution you need to invert won't exactly match a form from the transform table right away. However, if the denominator matches, you're usually only a few minor adjustments away from a perfect match. These small tweaks usually involve adding a missing constant or splitting the fraction to align the numerator with the forms in the Laplace transform table.
			</p>

			<p> 
				Let's illustrate this with an example. Consider the Laplace solution: 
			</p>

			<me>
				Y(s) = \frac{2s + 3}{s^2 + 4}
			</me>.
			
			<p>
				Notice that the denominator, <m>s^2 + 4</m>, matches the denominator in both <xref ref="lt-L4" text="custom">L<m>_4</m></xref> or <xref ref="lt-L5" text="custom">L<m>_5</m></xref>. However, the numerator has two distinct terms. To clearly align this with the table, we split the fraction into two simpler fractions:
			</p>

			<me>
				Y(s) = \frac{2s}{s^2 + 4} + \frac{3}{s^2 + 4}
			</me>.

			<p>
				Then factor constants and reassess how they match with the table.
			</p>

			<me>
				Y(s) 
				= 2\ub{\frac{s}{s^2 + 4}}_{\large<xref ref="lt-L5" text="custom">L_5</xref>\text{ perfect}} 
				+ 3\ub{\frac{1}{s^2 + 4}}_{\large<xref ref="lt-L4" text="custom">L_4</xref>\text{ close}}
			</me>.

			<p> 
				The first term now perfectly matches <xref ref="lt-L5" text="custom">L<m>_5</m></xref> (with <m>b=2</m>). The second term, however, needs a <m>2</m> in its numerator to match <xref ref="lt-L4" text="custom">L<m>_4</m></xref>. To make this small adjustment, we multiply by <m>\sfrac{2}{2}</m>, a form of multiplying by 1:
			</p>

			<aside><title> Multiplying by <m>\sfrac{2}{2}</m> </title>
				<p>
					Multiplying by <m>\sfrac{2}{2}</m> is just multiplying by 1, preserving the original value. It's a useful technique for adjusting numerators without altering the equation.
				</p>
			</aside>

			<md>
				<mrow>	
					Y(s) \amp = 2\frac{s}{s^2 + 4} + 3\frac{1}{s^2 + 4} \cdot \frac{2}{2}
				</mrow>
				<mrow>	
					Y(s)	
					\amp = 2\ub{\frac{s}{s^2 + 4}}_{\large<xref ref="lt-L5" text="custom">L<m>_5</m></xref>\text{ perfect}} 
					+ \frac{3}{2}\ub{\frac{2}{s^2 + 4}}_{\large<xref ref="lt-L4" text="custom">L<m>_4</m></xref>\text{ perfect}}
				</mrow>
			</md>.

			<p>
				With these minor adjustments, the inverse transform becomes straightforward:
				<md>
					<mrow>	
						\ilap{Y(s)} = 2\ilap{\frac{s}{s^2 + 4}} + \frac{3}{2}\ilap{\frac{2}{s^2 + 4}}
					</mrow>
					<mrow>	
						y(t) = 2\cos(2t) + \frac{3}{2}\sin(2t)
					</mrow>
				</md>.
			</p>

			<p>
				Mastering these small algebraic manipulations simplifies your workflow considerably. Let's examine another example to reinforce this process:
			</p>
			
			<example><title> MAdjusting Laplace Solutions (Fraction Splitting) </title>
				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Apply a backward transform (step 3) to revert the Laplace solution back into a function of <m>t</m>.
						</p>
						<p>
							<me>

							</me>
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>

			</example>

			<p>
				Another frequent adjustment is needed when dealing with forms like <xref ref="lt-L8" text="custom">L<m>_8</m></xref>. Consider the Laplace solution:
			</p>

			<me>
				Y(s) = \frac{s + c}{(s-a)^2 + b^2}
			</me>.

			<p>
				To match this form precisely, we need the numerator to have an explicit <m>(s - a)</m>. To achieve this, add and subtract <m>a</m> in the numerator:
			</p>

			<me>
				Y(s) = \frac{s - a + a + c}{(s-a)^2 + b^2}
			</me>.

			<aside><title> Adding and Subtracting <m>a</m></title>
				<p> 
					Adding and subtracting the same number does not change the value of the expression, but it is a useful trick for adjusting numerators into recognizable forms.
				</p>
			</aside>

			<p>
				Now split the fraction and note that <m>a+c</m> is just a number:
			</p>

			<me>
				Y(s) = \frac{s-a}{(s-a)^2 + b^2} + \frac{a + c}{(s-a)^2 + b^2}
			</me>.

			<p>
				The second term still needs a <m>b</m> in the numerator to match perfectly, so we multiply by <m>\sfrac{b}{b}</m>:
			</p>

			<me>
				Y(s) 
				= \ub{\frac{s-a}{(s-a)^2 + b^2}}_{\large\text{matches } <xref ref="lt-L8" text="custom">L_8</xref>} 
				+ \frac{a+c}{b}\ub{\frac{b}{(s-a)^2 + b^2}}_{\large\text{matches } <xref ref="lt-L7" text="custom">L_7</xref>}
			</me>.

			<p> 
				With this form, the inverse transform gives us
			</p>

			<me>
				y(t) = \cos(bt) + \frac{a+c}{b}\sin(bt)
			</me>.

	<subsection><title> Laplace Transform Method: A 3-Step Recap </title>
		<p><title> üëÄ <em>Reading Preparation</em> </title>
			<p>
				Needs to be completed.
			</p>
		</p>

		<p>
			Here is a quick slideshow of the full Laplace transform method. Each step corresponds to a move in the roadmap, and together they form a complete strategy for solving initial value problems.
		</p>

		<interactive xml:id="lt-slideshow" platform="javascript"
			dark-mode-enabled="yes"
			source="code/slideshow/slideshow.js"
			width="100%" aspect="1.52:1">
			<slate xml:id="lt-slideshow-canvas" surface="canvas" 
			width="100%" aspect="1.52:1" />
		</interactive>

		<p>
			In addition to the visual review, the complete strategy for this method is outlined as follows:
		</p>

		<exploration xml:id="lt-method"><title>Laplace Transform Method</title>

			<p>
				The particular solution to an initial-valued differential equation of the form
				<men xml:id="lt-method-de">
					y^{(n)} + a_{n-1}\, y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_1\, y' + a_0\, y = g(t)
				</men>
				with given initial conditions can be found through the three-step process:
			</p>

			<dl>
				<li xml:id="lt-step-1"><title>Step 1: Forward Transform</title>
					<p>
						Apply the Laplace transform, <m>\laplacesym</m>, to both sides of the differential equation. Use the <xref ref="lt-table-common-rules-merged" text="custom">transform table</xref> to rewrite each term as a function of <m>s</m>, inserting initial conditions as needed. You now have the Laplace equation.
					</p>
				</li>

				<li xml:id="lt-step-2"><title>Step 2: Solve for <m>Y</m></title>
					<p>
						Simplify the Laplace equation using algebra and isolate the Laplace solution, <m>Y(s)</m>.
					</p>
				</li>

				<li xml:id="lt-step-3"><title>Step 3: Prepare for the Inverse Transform</title>
					<p>
						Write <m>Y(s)</m> as a sum of terms that match entries in the <xref ref="lt-table-common-rules-merged" text="custom">transform table</xref>. This may involve partial fraction decomposition (PFD), or completing the square for irreducible denominators.
						<fn><title>Irreducible</title>
							<p>
								A quadratic denominator of the form
								<me>\frac{\text{numerator}}{as^2 + bs + c}</me>
								is irreducible if the discriminant, <m>\Delta = b^2 - 4ac</m>, is negative.
							</p>
						</fn>
					</p>

					
				</li>

				<li xml:id="lt-step-4"><title>Step 4: Inverse Transform</title>
					<p>
						Apply the inverse Laplace transform, <m>\laplacesym^{-1}</m>, to each term in <m>Y(s)</m> to recover the solution, <m>y(t)</m>.
					</p>
				</li>
			</dl>

		</exploration>

		<p>
			The three-step outline provides the general strategy. Let's now apply it to a variety of problems, ranging from basic to more involved. Each example will follow the same structure: transform the equation, solve algebraically in the Laplace domain, prepare the result, and recover the solution using the inverse transform.
		</p>
