<section><title> Transforming Equations Between Domains </title>

	<introduction>

		<p>
			Applying the Laplace transform method to a differential equation starts by transforming this equation into a new equation where the derivatives are replaced by algebraic expressions. To recover to the solution to the original equation the transformation must also be reversed. This process, which we will refer to as a <q>backwards transform</q>, corresponds to step 3 on the <xref ref="lt-roadmap" text="custom">Laplace transform roadmap</xref>.
		</p>

		<p>
			Just as the forward Laplace transform simplifies a differential equation by converting it into an algebraic one, the backward transform completes the cycle by converting the algebraic solution back into its original form. This chapter will focus on the techniques used to perform the inverse Laplace transform, guiding you through the steps needed to move from the transformed function, <m>F(s)</m>, back to the original function, <m>f(t)</m>.
		</p>

		<p>
			We will explore key methods, such as partial fraction decomposition and the use of Laplace transform tables, to help simplify complex transformed functions. Additionally, we will highlight common challenges and how to overcome them, ensuring a smooth transition back to the original domain.
		</p>

		<assemblage xml:id="ilt-linearity-property"><title> ✳️ Inverse Laplace Transform Property </title>
			<p>
				For any constants <m>a</m> and <m>b</m> and functions of <m>s</m> <m>F(s)</m> and <m>G(s)</m>,
				<dl width="narrow">
					<li xml:id="iP1">
						<m>\ds \ilap{ a f(t) \pm b g(t) } = a \ilap{f(t)} \pm b \ilap{g(t)}</m>
					</li>
				</dl>
			</p>
		</assemblage>
		
	</introduction>

	<subsection><title> Forward Transforming Equations </title>

		<p>
			In the previous sections, we've focused on applying Laplace transforms to individual functions. Now, we shift our focus to solving differential equations by transforming the entire equation, along with its initial conditions. This process brings us back to the first step of the Laplace Transform Method roadmap.
		</p>

		<p>
			<image source="figures/Laplace-Roadmap-Step-1.png" width="100%">
				<shortdescription>Step 1: The Forward Laplace Transform</shortdescription>
			</image>
		</p>

		<p>
			To solve a differential equation using Laplace transforms, we apply the Laplace transform to both sides of the equation, leveraging the common transforms and properties we've discussed so far. Applying the transform to a function can be thought of as multiplying the function by <m>e^{-st}</m> and integrating with respect to <m>t</m>. However, instead of rewriting this process for each term, we simply apply the Laplace operator, <m>\laplacesym</m>, to both sides of the equation. Let's illustrate this with an example.
		</p>

		<example>
			<p>
				Apply the Laplace Transform to the initial-value problem:
			</p>
			<me>
				y'' + 3y' + 2y = -40e^{3t}, \quad y(0) = 0, \quad y'(0) = 1.
			</me>

			<solution>
				<p>
					First, we apply <m>\laplacesym</m> to both sides:
					<me>
						\lap{y'' + 3y' + 2y} = \lap{-40e^{3t}}.
					</me>
					Using the linearity property <xref ref="P1" text="custom">P<sub>1</sub></xref>, we break this into four separate transforms:
					<me>
						\ub{\lap{y''}}_{(1)} + 3\ub{\lap{y'}}_{(2)} + 2\ub{\lap{y}}_{(3)} = -40\ub{\lap{e^{3t}}}_{(4)}.
					</me>
				</p>

				<p>
					Now, let's compute each transform:
					<md>
						<mrow>
							(1):\quad \amp\ \lap{y''} = s^2Y - s\os{\large 0}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y(0)}} - \os{\large 1}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y'(0)}} = s^2Y - 1 \quad <xref ref="P4" text="title"/>.
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							(2):\quad \amp\ \lap{y'} = sY - y(0) = sY \quad <xref ref="P3" text="title"/>.
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							(3):\quad \amp\ \lap{y} = Y.
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							(4):\quad \amp\ \lap{e^{3t}} = \frac{1}{s - 3} \quad <xref ref="L2" text="title"/>.
						</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>

				<p>
					Substituting these results back into the equation, we get:
					<md>
						<mrow>
							s^2Y - 1 + 3sY + 2Y \amp = \frac{-40}{s - 3}, \quad \text{or}
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							s^2Y + 3sY + 2Y - 1 \amp = \frac{-40}{s - 3}.
						</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>
			</solution>
		</example>

		<p>
			Let's look at one more example for additional clarity.
		</p>

		<example>
			<p>
				Apply the Laplace Transform to the initial-value problem:
			</p>
			<me>
				y'' - 4y' + 6y = t^2, \quad y(0) = 2, \quad y'(0) = 0.
			</me>

			<solution>
				<p>
					Applying the Laplace transform to both sides:
					<md>
						<mrow>
							\lap{y'' - 4y' + 6y} \amp = \lap{t^2}, \\
							\lap{y''} - 4\lap{y'} + 6\lap{y} \amp = \lap{t^2}, \\
							s^2Y - s\os{\large 2}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y(0)}} - \os{\large 0}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y'(0)}} - 4(sY - \os{\large 2}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y(0)}}) + 6Y \amp = \frac{2}{s^3}, \\
							s^2Y - 2s - 4sY + 8 + 6Y \amp = \frac{2}{s^3}, \\
							s^2Y - 4sY + 6Y - 2s + 8 \amp = \frac{2}{s^3}.
						</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>
			</solution>
		</example>

		<p>
			In both examples, the differential equations have been transformed into algebraic equations. Solving for <m>Y(s)</m> is the next step in the Laplace Transform Roadmap.
		</p>

		<xi:include href="./c7-exercises/c7-chkpts-1-4-1-forward-transforming-eqns.ptx" />
		
	</subsection>

	<subsection><title> Selecting Forms </title>

		<introduction>

			<p>
				Recall that the first step of the Laplace Transform Method involves a <xref ref="lt-roadmap-step1" text="custom">forward transformation</xref>, which converts the <m>y(t)</m>-equation into an equation containing <m>Y(S)</m> and <m>s</m>. Now, we skip ahead to the final step of the method, where we reverse this process to return to the <m>y(t)</m>-equation, revealing the solution to the original differential equation.
			</p>

			<figure xml:id="lt-roadmap-step3">
				<caption>
					The Backward Laplace Transform. Converting the <m>Y(s)</m>-algebra equation back into the <m>y(t)</m>-solution.
				</caption>
				<image source="figures/Laplace-Roadmap-Step-3.png" width="100%">
					<shortdescription>(for accessibility)</shortdescription>
				</image>
			</figure>
			
			<p>
				This section will guide you through Step 3 of the Laplace Transform Method: identifying common forms, matching them to <m>Y(s)</m>, and ultimately recovering <m>y(t)</m>. Mastering this step will enable you to complete the solution of differential equations using the Laplace Transform Method.
			</p>

		</introduction>

		<subsubsection><title> Matched Forms </title>

			<p>
				The Laplace Transform Method begins with the <xref ref="lt-roadmap-step1" text="custom">forward</xref> Laplace transform of a differential equation with an unknown function <m>y(t)</m> into an algebraic equation involving the transformed function <m>Y(s)</m>. Once we have solved for <m>Y(s)</m>, the final step is to recover the original function, <m>y(t)</m>, by applying the <xref ref="lt-roadmap-step3" text="custom">backward</xref> (inverse) Laplace transform, denoted as <m>\laplacesym^{-1}</m>.
			</p>

			<p>
				Much like how <m>\cos^{-1}</m> reverses the cosine function, applying <m>\laplacesym^{-1}</m> to both sides of a Laplace-transformed expression brings us back to the original function. For example, if
				<me>
					\lap{e^{-3t}} = \frac{1}{s+3},
				</me>
				then, applying the inverse Laplace transform gives:
				<me>
					\ilap{\frac{1}{s+3}} = e^{-3t}.
				</me>
				To identify the appropriate inverse Laplace transform, we rely on the common transforms in the table below, where we match the function of <m>s</m> on the right and the inverse is the function of <m>t</m> on the left.

		<subsubsection><title> Mismatched Forms </title>

			<p>
				When working with Laplace transforms, it's common to encounter algebraic expressions that don't perfectly match the standard forms we use for the inverse Laplace transform. In these cases, we need to manipulate the expression to make it fit one of the known forms.
			</p>

			<p>
				For example, consider the expression:
				<me> \frac{1}{s^2 + 25} \qquad \leftarrow \ \text{looks like} \quad \frac{b}{s^2 + b^2}. </me>
				While it's not an exact match, we can observe from the denominator that <m>b = 5.</m>
			</p>

			<p>
				However, the numerator doesn't match yet. To correct this, we need a 5 in the numerator. We can't just multiply the numerator by 5 without changing the value, so instead, we multiply the entire expression by <m>\ds\frac{5}{5}</m>, which is equivalent to multiplying by 1:
				<md>
					<mrow>\frac{1}{s^2 + 25} 	\amp = \frac{1}{s^2 + 5^2} </mrow>
					<mrow>						\amp = \frac{5}{5} \cdot \frac{1}{s^2 + 5^2} </mrow>
					<mrow>						\amp = \frac{1}{5} \cdot \frac{5}{s^2 + 5^2} \quad \us{\text{match}}{\os{\text{perfect}}{\leftarrow}}\ \frac{b}{s^2 + b^2}</mrow>
				</md>
				Multiplying by <m>\ds\frac{5}{5}</m> doesn't change the value of the expression, but it allows us to match the standard form.
			</p>

			<p>
				One of the most common adjustments is introducing a missing constant in the numerator, as we did earlier. By multiplying both the numerator and denominator by this constant, we maintain the expression's value while transforming it into a recognizable form. Let's look at some examples:
			</p>

			<example>
				<p>
					Compute the inverse Laplace transforms for each.
				</p>

				<tabular>
					<col width="20%" halign="center"/>
					<col width="75%"/>
					<row>
						<cell><m>\ds \frac{12}{s}</m></cell>
						<cell>
							<p>
								<m>\hspace{27em}</m>
								<solution>
									<p>
										The <m>s</m> in the denominator tells us that we need <m>L_1</m>. Before we do, let's factor out <m>12</m>:
										<me>\ds \ilap{\frac{12}{s}} = 12 \cdot \ilap{\frac{1}{s}} = 12 \cdot 1 = 12</me>.
									</p>
								</solution>
							</p>
						</cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell><m>\ds \frac{5}{s^2 + 4}</m></cell>
						<cell>
							<p>
								<m>\hspace{27em}</m>
								<solution>
									<p>
										Since the denominator has the form, <m>s^2 + \text{number}</m>, and there is no <m>s</m> in the numerator, we should apply <m>L_4</m>. As before, it is helpful to first factor out the constant <m>5</m>,
										<me>\ds \ilap{\frac{5}{s^2 + 4}} = 5\ \ilap{\frac{1}{s^2 + 4}} \quad \leftarrow <xref ref="L4" text="custom">L_4</xref> (b=2)</me>.
										According to <m>L_4</m>, we are missing <m>2</m> in the numerator. Let's put it there by multiplying by <m>2/2</m>, like so
										<md>
											<mrow> = 5\ \ilap{\frac{2}{2}\frac{1}{s^2 + 4}} \amp = 5\ \ilap{\frac{1}{2}\frac{2}{s^2 + 4}}</mrow>
											<mrow>										  \amp = \frac{5}{2} \ilap{\frac{2}{s^2 + 4}}</mrow>
											<mrow>										  \amp = \frac{5}{2} \sin(2t)</mrow>
										</md>
									</p>
								</solution>
							</p>
						</cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell><m>\ds \frac{17}{s^4}</m></cell>
						<cell>
							<p>
								<m>\hspace{27em}</m>
								<solution>
									<p>
										This denominator has the form <m>s^{\text{power}}</m>, which matches <m>L_3</m> with <m>n = 3</m>.
										<me>\ds \ilap{\frac{17}{s^4}} = 17\ \ilap{\frac{1}{s^4}} \quad \leftarrow <xref ref="L3" text="custom">L_3</xref> (n=3)</me>.
										In this case, the numerator is missing a <m>3!</m>. We can introduce it by multiplying by <m>3!/3!</m>, like so
										<md>
											<mrow> = 17\ \ilap{\frac{3!}{3!}\frac{1}{s^4}} = \frac{17}{3!} \ilap{\frac{3!}{s^4}} = \frac{17}{6} t^3</mrow>
										</md>
									</p>
								</solution>
							</p>
						</cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell><m>\ds \frac{7s}{s^2 + 25}</m></cell>
						<cell>
							<p>
								<m>\hspace{27em}</m>
								<solution>
									<p>
										<me><xref ref="L5" text="custom">L_5</xref>, b=5</me>
										<me> \ilap{\frac{7s}{s^2 + 25}} = 7\ \ilap{\frac{s}{s^2 + 25}} = 7\ \cos(5t)</me>
									</p>
								</solution>
							</p>
						</cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell><m>\ds \frac{10}{(s - 3)^2 + 11}</m></cell>
						<cell>
							<p>
								<m>\hspace{27em}</m>
								<solution>
									<p>
										The form of this denominator is <m>\ul{(s \pm \text{shift})^{2} + \text{number}}</m> and has no <m>s</m> in the numerator. Therefore,
										<md>
											<mrow> \ilap{\frac{10}{(s - 3)^2 + 11}} \amp\ \quad <xref ref="L7" text="custom"><m>L_7</m></xref> (a=3,\ b=\sqrt{11})</mrow>
											<mrow>	\amp = 10\ \ilap{\frac{\sqrt{11}}{\sqrt{11}}\frac{1}{(s - 3)^2 + 11}}</mrow>
											<mrow>	\amp = \frac{10}{\sqrt{11}} \ilap{\frac{\sqrt{11}}{(s - 3)^2 + 11}}</mrow>
											<mrow>	\amp = \frac{10}{\sqrt{11}} e^{3t} \sin(\sqrt{11}t)</mrow>
										</md>

									</p>
								</solution>
							</p>
						</cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell><m>\ds \frac{2}{(s+7)^5}</m></cell>

										<md>

											</mrow>

											</mrow>

	<subsection><title> Preparing the Backward Transform </title>

		<introduction>
			<p>
				So far, we have explored the forward (initial step) and backward (final step) Laplace transforms, as illustrated in the figures below.
				<image source="figures/Laplace-Roadmap-Step-1.png" width="100%">
					<shortdescription>(for accessibility)</shortdescription>
				</image>
				<image source="figures/Laplace-Roadmap-Step-3.png" width="100%">
					<shortdescription>(for accessibility)</shortdescription>
				</image>
				However, the transition between these two steps often requires some algebraic manipulation before the backward transform can be applied, as shown in the following figure.
				<image source="figures/Laplace-Roadmap-Step-2.png" width="45%">
					<shortdescription>(for accessibility)</shortdescription>
				</image>
			</p>
		
			<p>
				After solving for <m>Y</m> in Step 2a, the goal is to express <m>Y</m> as a sum of simpler components whose inverse Laplace transforms can be easily identified using the right-hand column of the <xref ref="lt-table-L1-12" text="custom">Laplace Transform Table</xref>. This may involve algebraic techniques such as splitting rational functions, completing the square, or partial fraction decomposition. These methods will be covered in this section.
			</p>
		</introduction>

		<subsubsection><title> Splitting Fractions </title>

			<p>
				One useful algebraic manipulation in preparing for the inverse Laplace transform is splitting a rational function that has a sum or difference in the numerator. This technique simplifies the expression into recognizable forms, making it easier to apply known Laplace transform pairs. Let's explore two examples where splitting fractions proves useful.
			</p>
			
			<example><title> <em>Find the Inverse Laplce Transform</em> </title>

				<solution><title> <m>\ds \ilap{\frac{2s - 3}{s^2 + 16}}</m> </title>
					<p>
						To do this, we split the fraction into two simpler terms:
						<me>\ds \frac{2s - 3}{s^2 + 16} = \frac{2s}{s^2 + 16} - \frac{3}{s^2 + 16}.</me>
						Now, we can apply the inverse Laplace transform to each term separately. The first term matches with <xref ref="L5" text="custom"><m>L_5</m></xref>, and the second term corresponds to <xref ref="L4" text="custom"><m>L_4</m></xref>:
						<md>
							<mrow>\amp\ \ilap{\frac{2s - 3}{s^2 + 16}} </mrow>
							<mrow>\amp\ = 2\ \ilap{\frac{s}{s^2 + 16}} - 3\ \ilap{\frac{1}{s^2 + 16}} </mrow>
							<mrow>\amp\ = 2 \cos(4t) - \frac{3}{4} \sin(4t). </mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
				</solution>
				<solution><title> <m>\ds \ilap{\frac{s^3 - 7}{s^7}}</m> </title>
					<p>
						We can split the fraction into the terms,
						<me>\ds \frac{s^3 - 7}{s^7} = \frac{s^3}{s^7} - \frac{7}{s^7} = \frac{1}{s^4} - \frac{7}{s^7}.</me>
						and apply the inverse Laplace transform, <xref ref="L3" text="custom"><m>L_3</m></xref>, to each
						<md>
							<mrow> \ilap{\frac{s^3 - 7}{s^7}}	\amp = \ilap{\frac{1}{s^4} - \frac{7}{s^7}}</mrow>
							<mrow>									\amp = \ilap{\frac{1}{s^4}} - 7\ilap{\frac{1}{s^7}}</mrow>
							<mrow>									\amp = \frac{1}{3!}\ilap{\frac{3!}{s^4}} - \frac{7}{6!}\ilap{\frac{6!}{s^7}}</mrow>
							<mrow>									\amp = \frac{1}{6}t^3 - \frac{7}{720}t^6</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>

			<p>
				This section demonstrates how relatively simple modifications, such as splitting fractions, can make it easier to find the inverse Laplace transform. However, some functions of <m>s</m> may require more advanced techniques. In the next section, we will explore how completing the square can help match the forms needed for a backward transform when the <m>s</m>-variable is shifted.
			</p>
		
			<xi:include href="./c7-exercises/c7-chkpts-2-1-3-splitting-fractions.ptx" />
		</subsubsection>

		<subsubsection><title> Completing the Square </title>
			<p>
				As you progress through the Laplace Transform Method, you will often encounter <xref ref="rational-function" text="custom">rational functions</xref> that do not immediately match any known form in the <xref ref="lt-table-L1-12" text="custom">table of Laplace transforms</xref>. For example, the function
				<me>\frac{s-1}{s^2 - 2s + 5}</me>
				does not align with standard forms. A common approach is to rewrite the denominator in the form <m>(s - a)^2 + b^2</m>, allowing it to match known transforms like <m>L_7</m> or <m>L_8</m>. This technique, called completing the square, is essential for converting quadratic expressions into forms that are easier to work with in inverse Laplace computations.
			</p>

			<p>
				Completing the square is particularly useful when the quadratic expression in the denominator has complex roots. As a quick check, recall from <xref ref="solving-the-quadratic-equation" text="custom">solving quadratic equations</xref> that if the discriminant
				<me>b^2 - 4ac</me>
				is negative, the quadratic function has complex roots. This indicates that completing the square is the correct approach, as the following examples will demonstrate.
			</p>
		
			<example><title> <em>Find the Inverse Laplce Transform</em> </title>

				<solution><title> <m>\ds H(s) = \frac{s-1}{s^2 - 2s + 5}</m>. Find <m>\ilap{H(s)}</m> </title>
					<p>
						The discriminant of the denominator <m>s^2 - 2s + 5</m> is
						<me>b^2 - 4ac = (-2)^2 - 4(1)(5) = 4 - 20 = -16</me>,
						which is negative. Therefore, completing the square is necessary:
						<md>
							<mrow> s^2 - 2s + 5 = (s^2 - 2s) + 5	\amp = (\ob{s^2 - 2s + 1}^{(s - 1)^2} - 1) + 5   </mrow>
							<mrow>								  \amp = (s - 1)^2 + 4							 </mrow>
						</md>.
						Rewriting <m>H(s)</m> gives:
						<me>\frac{s-1}{s^2 - 2s + 5} = \frac{s-1}{(s - 1)^2 + 4},</me>
						which matches <xref ref="L8" text="title"/> (<m>a=1, b=2</m>). Therefore,
						<me>h(t) = \ilap{H(s)} = e^{t}\cos(2t).</me>
					</p>
				</solution>
		
				<solution><title> <m>\ds K(s) = \frac{11}{s^2 - 6s + 14}</m>. Find <m>\ilap{K(s)}</m> </title>
					<p>
						The discriminant of the denominator <m>s^2 - 6s + 14</m> is:
						<me>b^2 - 4ac = (-6)^2 - 4(1)(14) = 36 - 56 = -20</me>,
						indicating that completing the square is necessary:
						<md>
							<mrow> s^2 - 6s + 14 = (s^2 - 6s) + 14  =\amp (\ob{s^2 - 6s + 9}^{(s - 3)^2} - 9) + 14  </mrow>
							<mrow>								  =\amp (s - 3)^2 + 5							 </mrow>
						</md>.
						Rewriting <m>K(s)</m> as:
						<me>
							\frac{11}{s^2 - 6s + 14}
								= \ub{\frac{11}{(s - 3)^2 + 5}}_{\large \text{Match with } L_7}
								= \frac{11}{\sqrt{5}}\ub{\frac{\sqrt{5}}{(s - 3)^2 + 5}}_{\large <xref ref="L7" text="custom"><m>L_7</m></xref> (a = 3, b = \sqrt{5})}
						</me>.
						Therefore,
						<md>
							<mrow>
								k(t) = \ilap{K(s)} \amp = \frac{11}{\sqrt{5}}e^{3t}\sin(\sqrt{5}t)
							</mrow>
						</md>.
					</p>
				</solution>
		
				<solution><title> <m>\ds P(s) = \frac{s+3}{s^2 + 2s + 10}</m>. Find <m>\ilap{P(s)}</m> </title>
					<p>
						Completing the square for the denominator of <m>P(s)</m> gives:
						<me>
							\frac{s+3}{s^2 + 2s + 10} = \frac{s+3}{(s + 1)^2 + 9}
						</me>.
						However, the numerator <m>s + 3</m> does not match <m>s + 1</m>. To resolve this, we rewrite <m>3</m> as <m>1 + 2</m> and group terms:
						<md>
							<mrow>
								\frac{s+3}{s^2 + 2s + 10}
									\amp = \frac{s + 1 + 2}{(s + 1)^2 + 9}
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									\amp = \frac{s+1}{(s + 1)^2 + 9} + \frac{2}{(s + 1)^2 + 9}
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									\amp = \ub{\frac{s+1}{(s + 1)^2 + 9}}_{\large <xref ref="L8" text="title"/>\ (a=-1, b=3)}
										+ \frac{2}{3}\ub{\frac{3}{(s + 1)^2 + 9}}_{\large <xref ref="L7" text="title"/>\ (a=-1, b=3)}
							</mrow>	
						</md>,
						Now, apply the inverse Laplace transform:
						<md>
							<mrow>
								p(t) \amp = \ilap{P(s)}
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									\amp = \ilap{ \frac{s+1}{(s + 1)^2 + 9} } + \frac{2}{3}\ilap{ \frac{3}{(s + 1)^2 + 9} }
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									\amp = e^{-t}\cos(3t) + \frac{2}{3}e^{-t}\sin(3t)
							</mrow>	
						</md>
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>

			<p>
				Completing the square is a crucial technique when working with quadratic expressions in the denominator of Laplace transforms. It allows for the backward transformations by rewriting the <m>s</m>-function in a form that aligns with known inverse Laplace transforms. In the next subsection, we will explore another technique: partial fraction decomposition, which helps break down more complex rational functions into simpler parts that can be easily matched to common inverse transforms.
			</p>

			<xi:include href="./c7-exercises/c7-chkpts-2-1-4-completing-the-sqr.ptx" />
		</subsubsection>
		
		<subsubsection><title> Partial Fraction Decomposition </title>

			<p>
				In the Laplace Transform Method, we often encounter rational functions with polynomial denominators that can be factored into simpler linear or quadratic terms. In such cases, partial fraction decomposition becomes a powerful technique. By breaking the original rational function into simpler fractions, each corresponding to a known form in the Laplace Transform Table, we can efficiently compute the inverse Laplace transform.
			</p>

			<p>
				The key idea is to express a complex rational function as a sum of simpler fractions, each with a simpler denominator. For example, if the denominator can be factored as <m>(s - a)(s - b)</m>, we can rewrite the function as:
				<me>\frac{f(s)}{(s - a)(s - b)} = \frac{A}{s - a} + \frac{B}{s - b}.</me>
				Once expressed in this way, we can directly apply the inverse Laplace transform to each term. Let's explore a series of examples to demonstrate this approach.
			</p>

			<example><title> <em>Find the Inverse Laplce Transform</em> </title>
				
				<solution><title> <m>\ds Y(s) = \frac{2s + 5}{(s + 1)(s + 4)}</m>. Find <m>\ilap{Y(s)}</m> </title>
					<p>
						First, decompose <m>Y(s)</m> into partial fractions:
						<me>\frac{2s + 5}{(s + 1)(s + 4)} = \frac{A}{s + 1} + \frac{B}{s + 4}.</me>
						Multiply both sides by <m>(s + 1)(s + 4)</m>,
						<me>2s + 5 = A(s + 4) + B(s + 1).</me>
						and solve for <m>A</m> and <m>B</m> by selecting convenient values for <m>s</m>:
						<sidebyside widths="8% 80%">
							<p>
								<md>
									<mrow>s = -1: \amp</mrow>
									<mrow></mrow>
									<mrow>s = -4: \amp</mrow>
									<mrow></mrow>
								</md>
							</p>
							<p>
								<md>
									<mrow>2(-1) + 5 	\amp = A(-1 + 4) + B(-1 + 1)</mrow>
									<mrow>3 	\amp = A(3) + B(0) \quad\implies\quad A=1</mrow>
									<mrow>2(-4) + 5 	\amp = A(-4 + 4) + B(-4 + 1)</mrow>
									<mrow>-3 	\amp = A(0) + B(-3) \quad\implies\quad B=1</mrow>
								</md>
							</p>>
						</sidebyside>
						Therefore, the partial fraction decomposition is:
						<me>\frac{2s + 5}{(s + 1)(s + 4)} = \frac{1}{s + 1} + \frac{1}{s + 4}.</me>
						Applying the inverse Laplace transform to each term:
						<me>\ilap{Y(s)} = \ilap{\frac{1}{s + 1}} + \ilap{\frac{1}{s + 4}} = e^{-t} + e^{-4t}.</me>
					</p>
				</solution>
		
				<solution><title> <m>\ds P(s) = \frac{s}{s^2 - s + 6}</m>. Find <m>\ilap{P(s)}</m> </title>
					<p>
						First, factor the quadratic denominator:
						<me>s^2 - s - 6 = (s + 2)(s - 3).</me>
						Now decompose <m>P(s)</m> into partial fractions:
						<me>\frac{s}{(s + 2)(s - 3)} = \frac{A}{s + 2} + \frac{B}{s - 3}.</me>
						Multiply both sides by <m>(s + 2)(s - 3)</m>,
						<me>s = A(s - 3) + B(s + 2).</me>
						and solve for <m>A</m> and <m>B</m> by selecting convenient values for <m>s</m>:
						<sidebyside widths="8% 80%">
							<p>
								<md>
									<mrow>s = -2: \amp</mrow>
									<mrow></mrow>
									<mrow>s = 3: \amp</mrow>
									<mrow></mrow>
								</md>
							</p>
							<p>
								<md>
									<mrow>-2 	\amp = A(-2 - 3) + B(-2 + 2)</mrow>
									<mrow>-2 	\amp = A(-5) + B(0) \quad\implies\quad A=2/5</mrow>
									<mrow>3 	\amp = A(3 - 3) + B(3 + 2)</mrow>
									<mrow>3 	\amp = A(0) + B(5) \quad\implies\quad B=3/5</mrow>
								</md>
							</p>
						</sidebyside>
						The partial fraction decomposition is:
						<me>\frac{s}{(s + 2)(s - 3)} = \frac{2/5}{s + 2} + \frac{3/5}{s - 3}.</me>
						Therefore:
						<me>\ilap{P(s)} = \frac25 e^{-2t} + \frac35 e^{3t}.</me>
					</p>
				</solution>
		
				<solution><title> <m>\ds Q(s) = \frac{3s + 4}{s^2 - 2s + 1}</m>. Find <m>\ilap{Q(s)}</m> </title>
					<p>
						Factor the quadratic denominator:
						<me>s^2 - 2s + 1 = (s - 1)^2.</me>
						Decompose <m>Q(s)</m> into partial fractions:
						<me>\frac{3s + 4}{(s - 1)^2} = \frac{A}{s - 1} + \frac{B}{(s - 1)^2}.</me>
						Multiply both sides by <m>(s - 1)^2</m>,
						<me>3s + 4 = A(s - 1) + B.</me>
						and solve for <m>A</m> and <m>B</m> by selecting convenient values for <m>s</m>:
						<sidebyside widths="8% 80%">
							<p>
								<md>
									<mrow>s = 1: \amp</mrow>
									<mrow></mrow>
									<mrow>s = 0: \amp</mrow>
									<mrow></mrow>
								</md>
							</p>
							<p>
								<md>
									<mrow>3(1) + 4 	\amp = A(1 - 1) + B</mrow>
									<mrow>7 	\amp = A(0) + B \quad\implies\quad B=7</mrow>
									<mrow>3(0) + 4 	\amp = A(0 - 1) + B</mrow>
									<mrow>4 	\amp = -A + 7 \quad\implies\quad A=3</mrow>
								</md>
							</p>
						</sidebyside>
						The partial fraction decomposition is:
						<me>\frac{3}{s - 1} + \frac{7}{(s - 1)^2}.</me>
						Therefore:
						<me>\ilap{Q(s)} = 3e^{t} + 7te^{t}.</me>
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>

			<p>
				Partial fraction decomposition also works for more complex rational functions with higher-degree polynomials in the denominator. The key is to factor the denominator as much as possible, then apply the decomposition to each term. Let's examine a more complex case involving cubic and quartic denominators.
			</p>

			<example><title> <em>Find <m>\ilap{R(s)}</m> and <m>\ilap{S(s)}</m></em> </title>

				<me>R(s) = \frac{2s^2 + 5s + 1}{s^3 - s}, \quad S(s) = \frac{4s^3 + 3s^2 - 2s + 1}{s^4 - 1}.</me>

				<solution><title> <m>\ilap{R(s)}</m> </title>
					<p>
						First, factor the cubic denominator:
						<me>s^3 - s = s(s - 1)(s + 1).</me>
						Now decompose <m>R(s)</m> into partial fractions:
						<me>R(s) = \frac{2s^2 + 5s + 1}{s(s - 1)(s + 1)} = \frac{A}{s} + \frac{B}{s - 1} + \frac{C}{s + 1}</me>
						and multiply both sides by <m>s(s - 1)(s + 1)</m> to get
						<me>2s^2 + 5s + 1 = A(s - 1)(s + 1) + B(s)(s + 1) + C(s)(s - 1).</me>
						and solve for <m>A, B,</m> and <m>C</m> by selecting convenient values for <m>s</m>:
						<sidebyside widths="10% 70% 18%">
							<p>
								<md>
									<mrow>s = 0: \amp</mrow>
									<mrow></mrow>
									<mrow>s = 1: \amp</mrow>
									<mrow></mrow>
									<mrow>s = -1: \amp</mrow>
									<mrow></mrow>
								</md>
							</p>
							<p>
								<md>
									<mrow>1 	\amp = A(-1)(1) + B(0)(1) + C(0)(-1)</mrow>
									<mrow>1 	\amp = -A</mrow>
									<mrow>8 	\amp = A(0)(2) + B(1)(2) + C(1)(0)</mrow>
									<mrow>8 	\amp = 2B</mrow>
									<mrow>-2 	\amp = A(1)(0) + B(-1)(0) + C(-1)(-2)</mrow>
									<mrow>-2 	\amp = 2C</mrow>
								</md>
							</p>
							<p>
								<md>
									<mrow></mrow>
									<mrow>A\amp = -1</mrow>
									<mrow></mrow>
									<mrow>B\amp = 4</mrow>
									<mrow></mrow>
									<mrow>C\amp = -1</mrow>
								</md>
							</p>
						</sidebyside>
						Therefore,
						<md>
							<mrow>\ilap{R(s)}	\amp = \ilap{\frac{-1}{s} + \frac{4}{s - 1} + \frac{-1}{s + 1}}.</mrow>
							<mrow>r(t)			\amp = -1 + 4e^{t} - e^{-t}.</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
				</solution>
		
				<solution><title> <m>\ilap{S(s)}</m> </title>
					<p>
						Factor the quartic denominator:
						<me>s^4 - 1 = (s^2 - 1)(s^2 + 1) = (s - 1)(s + 1)(s^2 + 1).</me>
						Decompose <m>S(s)</m> into partial fractions:
						<me>\frac{4s^3 + 3s^2 - 2s + 1}{(s - 1)(s + 1)(s^2 + 1)} = \frac{A}{s - 1} + \frac{B}{s + 1} + \frac{Cs + D}{s^2 + 1}.</me>
						and multiply both sides by <m>(s - 1)(s + 1)(s^2 + 1)</m> to get
						<md>
							<mrow>4s^3 + 3s^2 - 2s + 1 = A(s + 1)(s^2 +\amp\ 1)	+ B(s - 1)(s^2 + 1)</mrow>
							<mrow> 											+\amp\ (Cs+D)(s - 1)(s + 1).</mrow>
						</md>
						and solve for <m>A, B, C,</m> and <m>D</m> by selecting convenient values for <m>s</m>:
						<sidebyside widths="12% 80%">
							<p>
								<md>
									<mrow>s = 1: \amp</mrow>
									<mrow>\vphantom{\frac32}</mrow>
									<mrow>s = -1: \amp</mrow>
									<mrow>\vphantom{\frac32}</mrow>
									<mrow>s = 0: \amp</mrow>
									<mrow>\vphantom{\frac32}</mrow>
									<mrow>s = -2: \amp</mrow>
									<mrow>\vphantom{\frac32}</mrow>
								</md>
							</p>
							<p>
								<md>
									<mrow>6 	\amp = A(2)(2) + B(0)(2) + (C(1) + D)(0)(2)</mrow>
									<mrow>6 	\amp = 4A \qquad\implies\qquad A = \frac32</mrow>
									<mrow>2 	\amp = A(0)(2) + B(-2)(2) + (C(-1) + D)(-2)(0)</mrow>
									<mrow>2 	\amp = -4B \qquad\implies\qquad B = -\frac12</mrow>
									<mrow>1 	\amp = A(1)(1) + B(-1)(1) + (C(0) + D)(-1)(1)</mrow>
									<mrow>1 	\amp = \frac32 + \frac12 - D \qquad\implies\qquad D = 1</mrow>
									<mrow>-15 	\amp = A(-1)(3) + B(-3)(3) + (C(-2) + D)(-3)(-1)</mrow>
									<mrow>-15 	\amp = -3A - 9B + -6C + 3D</mrow>
									<mrow>-15 	\amp = -\frac92 + \frac92 + -6C + 3(1) \quad\implies\quad C = 3</mrow>
								</md>
							</p>
						</sidebyside>
						Therefore,
						<md>
							<mrow>\ilap{S(s)}	\amp = \ilap{\frac{\frac32}{s - 1} - \frac{\frac12}{s + 1} + \frac{3s + 1}{s^2 + 1}}.</mrow>
							<mrow>s(t)			\amp = \frac32 e^{t} - \frac12 e^{-t} + 3\cos(t) + \sin(t).</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>
