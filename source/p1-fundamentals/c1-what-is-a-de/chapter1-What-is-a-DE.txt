<chapter><title> What's a Differential Equation? </title>
	<introduction>
		<p>
			In this introductory section, we'll describe what a differential equation is from the perspective of algebra and calculus. Then, we'll delve into the concepts of independent and dependent variables, which play crucial roles in the structure and solutions of differential equations. By the end, you'll have a solid understanding of these fundamental ideas, setting the stage for learning the methods to find and analyze solutions of differential equations.
		</p>
	</introduction>
	
	<section xmlns:xi="http://www.w3.org/2001/XInclude"><title> An Analogy </title>
		<p>
			When you're learning something new, it's often helpful to connect it to concepts you already know. 
			To grasp what a differential equation is, let's first compare it to standard equations that might 
			feel more familiar. Consider the following three equations, where we aim to solve for <m>y</m>:
		</p>

		<sidebyside>
			<p><me>y + 3 = 11</me></p>
			<p><me>y + 3x = 11</me></p>
			<p><me>y^\prime + 3x = 11</me></p>
		</sidebyside>

		<p>
			All three are equations with the same goal—finding the unknown <m>y</m>. However, only the third equation 
			is a <em>differential equation</em> because it contains a derivative. 
		</p>

		<p>
			Now, let's try solving for <m>y</m> in each case.
		</p>

		<sidebyside>
			<p>
				<md>
					<mrow>y + 3 =\amp\ 11</mrow>
					<mrow>y =\amp\ 8</mrow>
				</md>
			</p>
			<p>
				<md>
					<mrow>y + 3x =\amp\ 11</mrow>
					<mrow>y =\amp\ 11 - 3x</mrow>
				</md>
			</p>
			<p>
				<md>
					<mrow>y^\prime + 3x =\amp\ 11</mrow>
					<mrow>y^\prime =\amp\ 11 - 3x</mrow>
					<mrow>y =\amp\ ?</mrow>
				</md>
			</p>
		</sidebyside>

		<p>
			In the first equation, we found that <m>y</m> is a number, and in the second, it's a function of <m>x</m>. 
			But in the third equation, how do we solve for <m>y</m> when there is a derivative attached to it? 
			This is exactly the kind of question that differential equations aim to answer. 
		</p>

		<p>
			We'll dive deeper into solving these types of equations soon. For now, there's still plenty more to learn 
			about the basics, so let's keep going!
		</p>

	<section xmlns:xi="http://www.w3.org/2001/XInclude"><title> Definition </title>
		<p>
			Here is the formal definition of a differential equation.
		</p>

		<definition xml:id="def-de">
			<title>Differential Equation</title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					A <term>differential equation (DE)</term> is an equation that involves one or more derivatives of an unknown function. If the function depends on a single variable, the equation is an <term>ordinary differential equation (ODE)</term>. 
					Otherwise, it is called a <term>partial differential equation (PDE)</term>.
				</p>
			</statement>
		</definition>

		<p>
			<note xml:id="de-convention"><title>Convention: DE means ODE</title>
				<p>
					Since this text focuses exclusively on ordinary differential equations, any use of <em>DE</em> will imply <em>ODE</em>.
				</p>
			</note>
			According to the definition, a differential equation must include at least one derivative (e.g., <m>f^\prime</m>, <m>\frac{dy}{dx}</m>) and an equality sign ("="). This distinction helps us identify the following expressions as differential equations:
			<me>
				\frac{dy}{dx} + 1 = y, \qquad f^{\prime\prime} + x^2 + 3x = 19, \qquad e^t = \tan(y^\prime)
			</me>
			In contrast, the following are not differential equations because they either lack a derivative or an equality sign:
			<me>
				\frac{d^2 y}{dx^2} + 2\frac{dy}{dx}, \qquad x^2 + 3x = 19, \qquad \sin y + e^x = 0
			</me>
		</p>

		<note xml:id="derivative-notation"><title>Derivative Notation</title>
			<p>
				We will use either prime notation or Leibniz notation to denote derivatives. For higher-order derivatives, the following conventions apply:
			</p>

			<tabular halign="center">
				<row>
					<cell />
					<cell colspan="6">Derivative Order</cell>
				</row>
				<row>
					<cell />
					<cell>1</cell>
					<cell>2</cell>
					<cell>3</cell>
					<cell>4</cell>
					<cell><m>...</m></cell>
					<cell>n</cell>
				</row>
				<row>
					<cell>Prime</cell>
					<cell><m>y^\prime</m></cell>
					<cell><m>y^{\prime\prime}</m></cell>
					<cell><m>y^{\prime\prime\prime}</m></cell>
					<cell><m>y^{(4)}</m></cell>
					<cell><m>...</m></cell>
					<cell><m>y^{(n)}</m></cell>
				</row>
				<row>
					<cell>Leibniz</cell>
					<cell><m>\ds\frac{dy}{dx}</m></cell>
					<cell><m>\ds\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}</m></cell>
					<cell><m>\ds\frac{d^3y}{dx^3}</m></cell>
					<cell><m>\ds\frac{d^4y}{dx^4}</m></cell>
					<cell><m>...</m></cell>
					<cell><m>\ds\frac{d^ny}{dx^n}</m></cell>
				</row>
			</tabular>

			<p>
				<em>Be careful not to confuse <m>y^{(7)}</m> with <m>y</m> raised to the power of 7!</em>
			</p>
		</note>

	<section xmlns:xi="http://www.w3.org/2001/XInclude"><title> Variables </title>
		<p>
			In this section, we'll explore the two types of variables found in differential equations: dependent and independent variables. Understanding the roles of these variables is crucial when solving and interpreting differential equations.
		</p>
		
		<p>
			Consider the differential equation:
			<men xml:id="variables-de-1">
				\frac{dy}{dx} + 2y = 3x^2.
			</men>
			This equation involves two variables: <m>x</m> and <m>y</m>. One of these variables is the unknown that we are trying to solve for, but which one?
		</p>

		<p>
			Typically, the variable you want to solve for is the one with a derivative applied to it. Here, the term <m>\frac{dy}{dx}</m> in <xref ref="variables-de-1" /> indicates:
			<ol>
				<li><p>The derivative is being taken with respect to <m>x</m>, and</p></li>
				<li><p><m>y</m> is a function of <m>x</m> (since it wouldn't make sense to take the derivative of <m>y</m> with respect to <m>x</m> unless <m>y</m> depends on <m>x</m>).</p></li>
			</ol>
		</p>

		<p>
			Therefore, <m>y</m> is called the <em>dependent variable</em> because it depends on <m>x</m>. On the other hand, <m>x</m> is the <em>independent variable</em>.
		</p>

		<definition xml:id="types-of-variables"><title> Dependent &amp; Independent Variables </title>
			<statement>
				<dl>
					<li xml:id="def-dependent-variable"><title>Dependent Variable:</title>
						<p>
							The variable representing the unknown function we are trying to find. It is always the variable with a derivative applied to it.
						</p>
					</li>
					<li xml:id="def-independent-variable"><title>Independent Variable:</title>
						<p>
							The variable that the dependent variable is a function of. It does not have a derivative applied to it.
						</p>
					</li>
				</dl>
			</statement>
		</definition>

		<example>

			<m>\ \ </m>Identify the dependent and independent variables in each equation and state which variable represents the unknown.

			<tabular>
				<col width="20%" halign="center"/>
				<col width="75%"/>
				<row>
					<cell><m>\ds \frac{dP}{ds} + \frac{P}{s^2} = 17s</m></cell>
					<cell>
						<p>
							<m>\hspace{27em}</m>
							<solution>
								Since the derivative <m>\ds\frac{dP}{ds}</m> appears, it implies that:
								<ul>
									<li><m>P</m> is a function of <m>s</m> and is the <em>dependent variable</em>.</li>
									<li><m>s</m> is the <em>independent variable</em>.</li>
									<li><m>P(s)</m> represents the <em>unknown</em> to this differential equation.</li>
								</ul>
							</solution>
						</p>
					</cell>
				</row>
				<row>
					<cell><m>\ds u'' + t^2 \ u = 0</m></cell>
					<cell>
						<p>
							<m>\hspace{27em}</m>
							<solution>
								<p>
									The second derivative <m>u''</m> suggests that:
									<ul>
										<li><m>u</m> is a function of <m>t</m> and is the <em>dependent variable</em>.</li>
										<li><m>t</m> is the <em>independent variable</em>.</li>
										<li><m>u(t)</m> represents the <em>unknown</em> to this differential equation.</li>
									</ul>
								</p>
							</solution>
						</p>
					</cell>
				</row>
				<row>
					<cell><m>\ds Q'' = 11Q</m></cell>
					<cell>
						<p>
							<m>\hspace{27em}</m>

	<section xmlns:xi="http://www.w3.org/2001/XInclude"><title> Terms &amp; Coefficients </title>
		<p>
			This text will frequently refer to <q>terms</q> and <q>coefficients</q>. Here is the definition.
		</p>

		<definition xml:id="def-terms-coefficients"><title> Terms &amp; Coefficients </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					In differential equations, terms and coefficients are defined as follows:
				</p>
				<dl>
					<li xml:id="def-terms"><title>Terms</title>
						<p>
							The expressions separated by <m>+</m>, <m>-</m>, or <m>=</m> signs.
						</p>
					</li>
					<li xml:id="def-coefficients"><title>Coefficients</title>
						<p>
							The objects multiplied by the dependent variable or one of its derivatives.
						</p>
					</li>
					<li xml:id="def-constant-term"><title>Constant Term</title>
						<p>
							A term without a dependent variable is called a <term>constant term</term> and is not a coefficient.
						</p>
					</li>
				</dl>
			</statement>
		</definition>

		<p>
			Consider the differential equation:
			<men xml:id="terms-and-coefficients-equation">
				\us{y^{(6)}	\text{ term}	}{\ub{\ \frac{3}{x}	{\color{blue}\ y^{(6)}	}	}	} +
				\us{y''		\text{ term}	}{\ub{\ 		5.3	{\color{blue}\ y''		} 	}	} +
				\us{y' 		\text{ term}	}{\ub{\ 		x^2	{\color{blue}\ y'		} 	}	} -
				\us{y 		\text{ term}	}{\us{\uparrow}{	{\color{blue}\ \ul{y}	} 	}	} =
				\us{\text{constant term}	}{\ub{\ \frac12\ln(x)\ 							}	}
			</men>.
			This equation has five terms and four coefficients: <m>\frac{3}{x}</m>, <m>5.3</m>, <m>x^2</m>, and <m>-1</m>. Notice that coefficients can be <em>functions of the independent variable</em> (like <m>\frac{3}{x}</m> and <m>x^2</m>) or <em>constants</em> (like <m>5.3</m> and <m>-1</m>). The distinction between constant and variable coefficients will become crucial when we study a group of differential equations known as <em>constant-coefficient equations</em>.
		</p>

		<example xml:id="terms-and-coefficients"><title> <em>Terms &amp; Coefficients in a Differential Equation</em> </title>
		
			<statement>
				<p>
					Identify the terms and coefficients of the differential equation
				</p>
				<me>
					3t^2\ y' - 4\cos t + \frac{y'y}{t} - 515 y = 0
				</me>
			</statement>

			<solution>
				<p>
					The equation can be broken down as follows:
					<me>
						\us{y' \text{ term}}{\ub{\ 3t^2{\color{blue} y'}\ }} -
						\us{\text{constant term}}{\ub{\ 4\cos t\ }} +
						\us{y'y \text{ term}}{\ub{\ \frac{1}{t}{\color{blue} y'y}\ }} - 
						\us{y \text{ term}}{\ub{\ 515{\color{blue} y}\ }} = 0
					</me>.
					The coefficients are <m>3t^2</m>, <m>\frac{1}{t}</m>, and <m>-515</m>. Notice that <m>3t^2</m> and <m>\frac{1}{t}</m> are functions of the independent variable <m>t</m>, whereas <m>-515</m> is a constant.
				</p>
			</solution>
			
		</example>

	<section xmlns:xi="http://www.w3.org/2001/XInclude"><title> Order </title>
		<p>
			In this section, we'll explore a key concept in differential equations known as <term>order</term>. Think of the order of a differential equation as the number of layers or steps you need to peel away to reveal the original function. The more layers there are, the higher the order, and usually, the more complex the equation.
		</p>
		
		<definition xml:id="def-order">
			<statement>
				<p>
					The <term>order</term> of a differential equation refers to the highest derivative that appears in the equation. For example, if the highest derivative is the first derivative, it's called a <em>first-order</em> differential equation. If the highest derivative is the second derivative, it's called a <em>second-order</em> differential equation, and so on.
				</p>
			</statement>
		</definition>
		
		<example>

			<m>\ \ </m>Give the order of the following differential equations:

			<tabular>
				<col width="30%" halign="center"/>
				<col width="65%"/>
				<row>
					<cell><m>\ds \frac{dy}{dx} + y = x</m></cell>
					<cell>
						<p>
							<m>\hspace{24em}</m>
							<solution>
								<p><term>Order = 1.</term> This is a <em>first-order</em> differential equation because the highest derivative is <m>\frac{dy}{dx}</m>.</p>
							</solution>
						</p>
					</cell>
				</row>
				<row>
					<cell><m>\ds x^2 y'' + y''' = \sin(x) + y^8</m></cell>
					<cell>
						<p>
							<m>\hspace{24em}</m>
							<solution>
								<p><term>Order = 3</term>. This is a <em>third-order</em> differential equation because the highest derivative is <m>y'''</m>.</p>
							</solution>
						</p>
					</cell>
				</row>
				<row>
					<cell><m>\ds \frac{d^2 A}{dt^2} + \frac{dA}{dt} + A = 17</m></cell>
					<cell>
						<p>
							<m>\hspace{24em}</m>
							<solution>
								<p><term>Order = 2</term> This is a <em>second-order</em> differential equation because the highest derivative is <m>\frac{d^2 A}{dt^2}</m>.</p>
							</solution>
						</p>
					</cell>
				</row>
			</tabular>
			
		</example>

				It's important to distinguish between exponents and derivatives. For example, in the second example, <m>y^8</m> indicates that the dependent variable is raised to the 8th power, but this is not related to the order of the differential equation.
			</p>
		</note>

	<section xmlns:xi="http://www.w3.org/2001/XInclude"><title> Linear Terms </title>
		<p>
			The most informative label you can place on a differential equation is whether it is linear or nonlinear. While linearity often refers to equations that graph as straight lines, the concept of linearity in differential equations is more nuanced. Understanding this distinction is crucial as it significantly influences how the equations are solved and interpreted.
		</p>

		<p>
			Before jumping in, it may be helpful to revisit meaning of <xref ref="def-dependent-variable" text="custom">dependent</xref> variables, <xref ref="def-terms" text="custom">terms</xref> and <xref ref="def-coefficients" text="custom">coefficients</xref> as we will be referencing them throughout this discussion.
		</p>

		<p>
			Now, let's define what it means for a term in a differential equation to be linear.
		</p>
		
		<definition><title> Linear Term </title>
			<statement>
				<dl width="normal">
					<li xml:id="def-linear-term"><title>Linear Term</title>
						<p>
							A term that has one of the forms
						</p>
						<p>
							<me>a(t)\ y,\ a(t)\ y',\ a(t)\ y'',\ a(t)\ y''',\ \ldots</me>
						</p>
						<p>
							where <m>y</m> is the dependent variable. The coefficient, <m>a(t)</m>, can be a function of the independent variable, <m>t</m>, but not the dependent variable.
						</p>
					</li>
				</dl>
			</statement>
		</definition>

		<note><title> The linearity of a term depends entirely on the dependent variable </title>
			<p>
				The linearity of a term is determined by the presence of a single <m>y</m> or derivative of <m>y</m> to the power of <m>1</m>. The coefficients, <m>a(t)</m> play no part in the linearity.
			</p>
		</note>

		<example>

			<m>\ \ </m>Given the differential equation,

			<solution>

Let's separate the different parts in the following table.
				</p>

				</p>
			</solution>

			To help identify nonlinear terms, here are some common characteristics:
		</p>

		<assemblage xml:id="identifying-nonlinear-terms"><title> Identifying Nonlinear Terms </title>
			<p>
				A term is nonlinear if it contains a dependent variable, <m>y</m> or <m>y^{(n)}</m>, that is
			</p>
			<p>
				<ul marker="square">
					<li>
						<p>
							Raised to a power other than 1, e.g., <m>y^2</m> or <m>(y')^{-4}</m>.
						</p>
					</li>
					<li>
						<p>
							Inside another function, e.g., <m>\ln(y)</m> or <m>\sin(y)</m>.
						</p>
					</li>
					<li>
						<p>
							Multiplied or divided by another <m>y</m> or <m>y^{(n)}</m>, e.g., <m>y y'''</m> or <m>\frac{y'}{y}</m>.
						</p>
					</li>
				</ul>
			</p>
		</assemblage>

	<section xmlns:xi="http://www.w3.org/2001/XInclude"><title> Linearity </title>
		<p>
			In the world of differential equations, distinguishing between linear and nonlinear equations is vital. This distinction will often guide you on how to approach solving the equation and what methods to use. The good news is, all the work we did in the previous section will make this task much easier.
		</p>

		<definition xml:id="def-linear">
			<statement>
				A differential equation is <term> linear </term> if it contains only linear terms. That is, it can be expressed as
				<men xml:id="linear-equation">
					\underset{\text{linear term}}{\underbrace{a_n(t)y^{(n)}}} + \cdots + 
					\underset{\text{linear term}}{\underbrace{a_2(t)y''}} + 
					\underset{\text{linear term}}{\underbrace{a_1(t)y'}} +
					\underset{\text{linear term}}{\underbrace{a_0(t)y}} = 
					\underset{\text{linear term}}{\underbrace{f(t).}}
				</men>
				If just one of the terms in the equation is nonlinear, then the entire differential equation is <term> nonlinear </term>.
			</statement>
		</definition>

		<p>
			This definition might seem abstract at first, but it encompasses all possible linear differential equations. In practice, most equations you'll encounter will involve only a few terms, and the challenge lies in identifying whether any of those terms break the rule of linearity. If they do, the equation is nonlinear.
		</p>

		<example>
			<m>\ \ </m>Let's determine whether the following differential equation is linear:
			
			<me>y'' + \frac{y'}{t^2} + y = 17t</me>
		
			<solution>
				<p>
					To classify this equation, we need to verify if every term involving <m>y</m> and its derivatives is linear. Let's break it down term by term:
				</p>
				<md>
					<mrow> y'' + \frac{y'}{t^2} + y =\ 17t </mrow>
					<mrow> 
						\underset{\text{linear}}{\underline{\color{blue}{y''}}} + 
						\underset{\text{linear}}{\underline{\color{blue}{\frac{1}{t^2} y'}}} + 
						\underset{\text{linear}}{\underline{\color{blue}{y}}} = 
						\underset{\text{constant term}}{\underline{\color{blue}{17t}}}
					</mrow>
				</md>
				<p>
					Since each term involving <m>y</m> or its derivatives is linear, this differential equation is indeed linear.
				</p>
			</solution>
		</example>
		
		<p>
			In summary, recognizing whether a differential equation is linear or nonlinear will help you determine the appropriate methods for solving it. Linear equations allow for a more straightforward approach, while nonlinear equations often require specialized techniques.
		</p>

		<introduction>
			<p>
				<assemblage color="green"><title>Summary of the Key Ideas</title>
					<p>
						<ul marker="">
							<li xml:id="summary-de">
								<term><em>Differential Equation</em></term>
								<ul marker="square">
									<li><xref ref="def-de" text="custom">Differential equations </xref> are equations that contain derivatives, where the unknowns are functions.</li>
									<li>They describe how quantities change over time or space.</li>
								</ul>
							</li>
							<li xml:id="summary-analogy"> 
								<term><em>An Analogy</em></term>
								<ul marker="square">
									<li>Like standard equations, differential equations aim to solve for an unknown, but this unknown is a function rather than a number.</li>
								</ul>
							</li>
							<li xml:id="summary-variables"> 
								<term><em>Dependent &amp; Independent Variables</em></term>
								<ul marker="square">
									<li>Differential equations involve both <xref ref="def-dependent-variable" text="custom">dependent</xref> (unknown function) and <xref ref="def-independent-variable" text="custom">independent</xref> variables.</li>
									<li>The derivatives in the equation apply to the dependent variable.</li>
								</ul>
							</li>
							<li xml:id="summary-terms-and-coefficients"> 
								<term><em>Terms &amp; Coefficients</em></term>
								<ul marker="square">
									<li>Terms in a differential equation are separated by <m>+</m>, <m>-</m>, or <m>=</m> signs.</li>
									<li>Coefficients are constants or functions that multiply the dependent variable or its derivatives.</li>
								</ul>
							</li>
							<li xml:id="summary-order"> 
								<term><em>Order of a Differential Equation</em></term>
								<ul marker="square">
									<li>The order of a differential equation is determined by the highest derivative present.</li>
								</ul>
							</li>
							<li xml:id="summary-linearity"> 
								<term><em>Linearity of a Differential Equation</em></term>
								<ul marker="square">
									<li>A differential equation is <xref ref="def-linear" text="custom">linear</xref> if it contains only linear combinations of the dependent variable and its derivatives.</li>
									<li>Otherwise, it is nonlinear.</li>
								</ul>
							</li>
						</ul>
					</p>
				</assemblage>
			</p>
		</introduction>

					<statement>
						<ol>
							<li>
								A differential equation is an equation that involves one or more integrals of an unknown function.
								<answer> 
									<term>False</term>. A differential equation is an equation that involves one or more <em>derivatives</em> of an unknown function, not integrals. 
								</answer>
							</li>
							<li>
								The dependent variable is a function of the independent varaible.
								<answer> 
									<term>True</term>. 
								</answer>
							</li>
							<li>
								The independent variable is a function of the dependent varaible.
								<answer> 
									<term>False</term>. The dependent variable is the function, which depends on the independent variable.
								</answer>
							</li>
							<li>
								An Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) contains more than one independent variable.
								<answer> 
									<term>False</term>. An Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) contains exactly one independent variable. If it contained more than one, it would be a Partial Differential Equation (PDE).
								</answer>
							</li>
						</ol>
					</statement>
				</exercise>
