<section><title> Homogeneous (LHCC) Equations </title>

	<introduction>
		<p>
			Before learning how to solve Linear Homogeneous Constant Coefficient (LHCC) differential equations, it's important to identify these equations and understand the nature of their solutions.
		</p>

		<p>
			In this section, we will cover the fundamentals of LHCC equations by breaking down their key structural components: <em>linearity</em>, <em>homogeneity</em>, and <em>constant coefficients</em>. We will then explore why <em>exponential functions</em> naturally emerge as solutions and why other types of functions do not fit. Finally, we will introduce the <em>characteristic equation</em>, a powerful algebraic tool that helps us determine the specific exponential solutions that satisfy these equations.
		</p>
	</introduction>

	<paragraphs xml:id="homogeneous">
		<title> Homogeneous </title>

		<p>
			A linear differential equation is <term>homogeneous</term> if the right-hand side is zero—that is, there is no forcing term. Otherwise, it is <term>nonhomogeneous</term>.
		</p>

		<p>
			For example, the following equations are homogeneous:
		</p>

		<me>
			x y'' - \cos x y' + 6y = 0, \qquad y''' + 2y'' = y'
		</me>

		<p>
			These are nonhomogeneous because of the (boxed) right-hand side:
		</p>

		<me>
			y'' + 4xy = \boxed{x}, \qquad y' + \boxed{e^x} = 0
		</me>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs xml:id="definition-of-lhcc">
		<title> The LHCC Equation </title>

		<p>
		Putting it all together, we arrive at the definition of an LHCC equation.
		</p>

		<definition xml:id="def-lhcc-eqn">
		<statement>
			<p>
			A linear homogeneous differential equation with constant coefficients (LHCC) has the form:
			</p>
			<men xml:id="lhcc-equation">
			a_n\ y^{(n)} + \cdots + a_2\ y'' + a_1\ y' + a_0\ y = 0
			</men>
			<p>
			where all coefficients <m>a_n, \dots, a_0</m> are constants.
			</p>
		</statement>
		</definition>

		<p>
			The following equations are examples of LHCC equations:
		</p>

		<me>
			2y'' - 3y' + 5y = 0, \qquad \frac{d^2s}{dt^2} + \frac{ds}{dt} - 4s = 0 \qquad u'' = u
		</me>.
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs><title> What Kind of Functions Satisfy LHCC Equations? </title>

		<introduction>
			<p>
				Now that we can recognize an LHCC equation, we want to use this information to gain insight into their solutions. In this section, you will see why the properties of exponential functions make them a <q>perfect fit</q> for these equations.
			</p>

			<note><title> In this discussion, homogenous equations will have a <m>y</m> term </title>
				<statement>
					<p>
						For this discussion, we will assume that homogeneous equations contain a <m>y</m> term. For if they didn't, then you could integrate both sides leaving you with a nonhomogeneous equation.
					</p>
				</statement>
			</note>
		</introduction>

		<p>
			Simply speaking, an <xref ref="lhcc-equation" text="custom">LHCC equation</xref> is just the sum or difference of functions that equal zero. For example, here is a first-order LHCC equation
		</p>

		<me>
			y' - 2y = 0.
		</me>

		<p>
			Whatever the <q>solution</q> is, we must get <m>0 = 0</m> when we plug it in. That is,
		</p>

		<md>
			<mrow>	(\text{solution})' - 2(\text{solution}) \amp = 0	</mrow>
			<mrow>	\text{derivative terms} - 2(\text{solution}) \amp = 0	</mrow>
		</md>
		
		<p>
			In order to get <m>0 = 0</m> from this last equation, all the terms on the left must cancel out. However, the only terms that cancel are <xref ref="review-like-terms" text="custom"><term>like-terms</term></xref>. Meaning, the solution and the derivative terms must all be like-terms! This leads to the key question:
		</p>

		<blockquote>
			<p>
				<q>Which types of functions are like-terms with their own derivatives?</q>
			</p>
		</blockquote>

		<p>
			You may recall from calculus that the derivative of an exponential function is <em>another exponential function</em>. For example, the derivative of <m>e^{7x}</m> is <m>7e^{7x}</m>, which are like-terms. This property is unique to exponentials and is exactly what makes them <q>perfect</q> solutions for LHCC equations. To see this, let's substitute different functions into this equation to see what kind of terms they produce.
		</p>

		<sidebyside widths="28% 5% 32% 5% 30%">
			<p>
				<em>Case 1:</em> <m>\quad y = x^2</m>
				<md>
					<mrow> \left[x^2\right]' - 2 \left[x^2\right] = 0\amp </mrow>
					<mrow> \us{\large\text{no cancellation}}{\ub{2x - 2x^2}_{\large\text{non like-terms,}}} = 0\amp </mrow>
				</md>
			</p>
			<p/>
			<p>
				<em>Case 2:</em> <m>\quad y = \ln x</m>
				<md>
					<mrow> \left[2\ln x\right]' - 2\left[\ln x\right] = 0\amp </mrow>
					<mrow> \us{\large\text{no cancellation}}{\ub{\frac{2}{x} - 2\ln x}_{\large\text{non like-terms,}}} = 0\amp </mrow>
				</md>
			</p>
			<p/>
			<p>
				<em>Case 3:</em> <m>\quad y = e^{2x}</m>
				<md>
					<mrow> \left[e^{2x}\right]' - 2 \left[e^{2x}\right] = 0\amp </mrow>
					<mrow> \us{\large\text{that cancel}}{\ub{2e^{2x} - 2e^{2x}}_{\large\text{like-terms}}} = 0\amp </mrow>
					<mrow> 0 = 0\amp </mrow>
				</md>
			</p>
		</sidebyside>

		<p>
			As you can see, only the exponential function is a solution since it produced like-terms that simplify to <m>0</m>. The next example shows that a similar result holds for a second-order equation.
		</p>

		<example xml:id="lhcc-soln-example-1"><title> Multiple Exponential Solutions </title>
			<p>
				<statement>
					<p>
						Verify that <m>y = e^{4x}</m> and <m>y = e^{-x}</m> are solutions to the differential equation
					</p>
					<me>
						y'' - 3y' - 4y = 0
					</me>,
					<p>
						but <m>y = x^4</m>, <m>y=\ln x</m> and <m>y=e^{3x}</m> are not.
					</p>
				</statement>
			</p>

			<solution>
				<p>
					First, let's check the first two exponential functions:
				</p>

				<sidebyside widths="20% 80%">
					<p>
						<me>
							y=e^{4x}:
						</me>
					</p>
					<p>
						<md>
							<mrow> 	[e^{4x}]'' - 3\left[e^{4x}\right]' - 4\left[e^{4x}\right]	\amp = 0	</mrow>
							<mrow> 	
								\us{\large\text{like-terms simplify to }0}{\ub{
									16e^{4x} - 12e^{4x} - 4e^{4x}	
								}}
								\amp = 0	
							</mrow>
							<mrow> 	0	\amp = 0	\ \ ✅ </mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<sidebyside widths="20% 80%">
					<p>
						<me>
							y=e^{-x}:
						</me>
					</p>
					<p>
						<md>
							<mrow> 	[e^{-x}]'' - 3\left[e^{-x}\right]' - 4\left[e^{-x}\right]	\amp = 0	</mrow>
							<mrow> 	
								\us{\large\text{like-terms simplify to }0}{\ub{
									e^{-x} + 3e^{-x} - 4e^{-x}	
								}}
								\amp = 0	
							</mrow>
							<mrow> 	0	\amp = 0	\ \ ✅ </mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<p>
					As you can see, the exponential functions produce like-terms that cancel out and simplify to zero.
				</p>

				<p>
					Let's see what happens when we plug in non-exponential functions:
				</p>

				<sidebyside widths="20% 80%">
					<p>
						<me>
							y=x^4:
						</me>
					</p>
					<p>
						<md>
							<mrow> 	[x^4]'' - 3\left[x^4\right]' - 4\left[x^4\right]	\amp = 0	</mrow>
							<mrow> 	
								\us{\large\text{not like-terms, do not combine}}{\ub{
									12x^2 - 12x^3 - 4x^4	
								}}
								\amp = 0	\ \ ❌
							</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<sidebyside widths="20% 80%">
					<p>
						<me>
							y=\ln x:
						</me>
					</p>
					<p>
						<md>
							<mrow> 	[\ln x]'' - 3\left[\ln x\right]' - 4\left[\ln x\right]	\amp = 0	</mrow>
							<mrow> 	
								\us{\large\text{not like-terms, do not combine}}{\ub{
									-x^{-2} - 3x^{-1} - 4\ln x	
								}}
								\amp = 0	\ \ ❌
							</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<p>
					In this case, the non-exponential functions produce non-like-terms that cannot be simplified and are not solutions.
				</p>

				<p>
					Finally, let's check the exponential function, <m>y = e^{3x}</m>:
				</p>

				<sidebyside widths="20% 80%">
					<p>
						<me>
							y=e^{3x}:
						</me>
					</p>
					<p>
						<md>
							<mrow> 	[e^{3x}]'' - 3\left[e^{3x}\right]' - 4\left[e^{3x}\right]	\amp = 0	</mrow>
							<mrow> 	
								\us{\large\text{like-terms don't simplify to }0}{\ub{
									9e^{3x} - 9e^{3x} - 4e^{3x}	
								}}
								\amp = 0	
							</mrow>
							<mrow> 	- 4e^{3x}	\amp = 0	\ \ ❌ </mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<p>
					In this case, we get like-terms, but the coefficients do not sum to zero and the terms do not cancel. Therefore, <m>y=e^{3x}</m> is not a solution.
				</p>
			</solution>
		</example>

		<conclusion>
			<p>
				These examples provide strong evidence that exponentials are a natural choice for solutions to LHCC equations. Moreover, the last example shows that not all exponential functions work, only those where the coefficients sum to zero. In the next section, we will introduce the <em>characteristic equation</em>, a tool that tells us exactly which of the exponential functions work for a given equation.
			</p>
			
			<theorem><title> What about other functions, like sine and cosine? </title>
				<statement>
					<p>
						Interestingly, sine and cosine also satisfy certain LHCC equations, but they are actually a <em>special case</em> of exponentials when complex numbers are involved. This will become clearer when we explore the <em>characteristic equation</em> in a later section.
					</p>
				</statement>
			</theorem>
		</conclusion>

	<paragraphs><title> The Characteristic Equation </title>

		<introduction>
			<p>
				So far, we have established that the best candidates for the solutions of <em>Linear Homogeneous Constant Coefficient (LHCC) equations</em> are <em>exponential functions</em>, but we don't know which ones work and how many there are. To determine this, we need the <em>characteristic equation</em>, which we will cover in this subsection.
			</p>
		</introduction>

		<p>
			To motivate the characteristic equation, let's start with the simple first-order LHCC equation:
		</p>

		<me>
			y' - 5y = 0.
		</me>

		<p>
			To get the correct exponential function, we start by substituting the generic exponential, <m>y = e^{rx}</m>, into the equation, giving us
		</p>

		<md>
			<mrow>	\left[e^{rx}\right]' - 5 e^{rx}	\amp = 0	</mrow>
		</md>.

		<p>
			Taking the derivative and factoring out the exponential we are left with
		</p>

		<md>
			<mrow>	r e^{rx} - 5 e^{rx}				\amp = 0	</mrow>
			<mrow>	(r - 5) e^{rx} 					\amp = 0	</mrow>
		</md>.

		<p>
			Since <m>e^{rx}</m> is never zero, we have
		</p>

		<me>
			r - 5 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad r = 5.
		</me>

		<p>
			Thus, <m>y = e^{5x}</m> is a solution and the equation where it came from, <m>r - 5 = 0</m>, is known as the <em>characteristic equation</em> for this LHCC equation.
		</p>

		<p>
			Now, let's try the sam approach with the second-order equation
		</p>

		<me>
			y'' - 3y' - 4y = 0.
		</me>

		<p>
			Again, substitute <m>y = e^{rx}</m> into <m>y</m>, <m>y'</m>, and <m>y''</m> to obtain
		</p>

		<md>
			<mrow>	(r^2 e^{rx}) - 3 (r e^{rx}) - 4 (e^{rx})	\amp = 0	</mrow>
			<mrow>	(r^2 - 3r - 4) e^{rx}						\amp = 0	</mrow>
		</md>

		<p>
			Since <m>e^{rx}</m> is never zero, this leads to the <em>characteristic equation</em>,
		</p>

		<me>
			r^2 - 3r - 4 = 0.
		</me>

		<p>
			that we solve for <m>r</m> like so
		</p>

		<md>
			<mrow> (r - 4)(r + 1) = 0 \amp </mrow>
			<mrow> r = 4, \quad r = -1. \amp </mrow>
		</md>

		<p>
			and finally, we get the solutions:
		</p>

		<me>
			y_1 = e^{4x}, \quad y_2 = e^{-x}.
		</me>

		<p>
			In general, when you plug <m>y = e^{rx}</m>, you will always end up with a polynomial equation that you can use to find all the <m>r</m>-values that make <m>e^{rx}</m> a solution to the differential equation. This polynomial equation is called the <em>characteristic equation</em>, which we formally defined as follows.
		</p>

		<definition xml:id="def-characteristic-equation"><title> The Characteristic Equation </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					The <term>characteristic equation</term> of an <m>n</m>-th order LHCC equation
				</p>

				<me>
					a_n\ y^{(n)} + a_{n-1}\ y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_1\ y' + a_0\ y = 0
				</me>

				<p>
					is the <m>n</m>-th degree polynomial
				</p>

				<me>
					a_n\ r^n + a_{n-1}\ r^{n-1} + \cdots + a_1 r + a_0 = 0
				</me>.

				<p>
					Then the <term>fundamental solutions</term> are the exponential functions
				</p>

				<me>
					y_1=e^{r_1 x},\quad y_2=e^{r_2 x},\quad \dots,\quad y_n=e^{r_n x}
				</me>

				<p>
					where <m>r_1, r_2, \dots, r_n</m> are the <m>n</m> solutions to the characteristic equation.
				</p>

				<theorem><title> Repeated <m>r</m>-values modify the Fundamental Solution </title>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Later, we will see that if <m>r</m>-values repeat in the characteristic equation, the fundamental solutions corresponding to these <m>r</m>-values must be modified by a factor of <m>x</m> for each repeat. For example, if <m>r = 5</m> appears three times as a characteristic equation solution, the fundamental solutions corresponding to these three <m>r</m>-values,
							<me>
								y_1 = e^{5x}, \quad y_2 = e^{5x}, \quad y_3 = e^{5x}
							</me>,
							are replaced with
							<me>
								y_1 = e^{5x}, \quad y_2 = x^1 e^{5x}, \quad y_3 = x^2 e^{5x}.
							</me>
						</p>
					</statement>
				</theorem>
				
			</statement>
		</definition>

		<theorem xml:id="characteristic-eqn-details"><title> Derivation of the General Characteristic Equation </title>
			<p>
				To find the characteristic equation for an <xref ref="lhcc-equation" text="custom"><m>n</m>-th order LHCC equation</xref>, we substitute <m>y = e^{rx}</m> into the equation and solve for <m>r</m>. Doing this gives us
				<md>
					<mrow>	
						a_n\ \left[e^{rx}\right]^{(n)} + \cdots + a_2\ \left[e^{rx}\right]'' + a_1\ \left[e^{rx}\right]' + a_0\ e^{rx} \amp = 0
					</mrow>
					<mrow>	a_n\ r^n e^{rx} + \cdots + a_2\ r^2 e^{rx} + a_1\ r e^{rx} + a_0\ e^{rx} \amp = 0 </mrow>
					<mrow>	\left(a_n\ r^n + \cdots + a_2\ r^2 + a_1\ r + a_0\right)e^{rx}  \amp = 0 </mrow>
				</md>.
				Since <m>e^{rx}</m> is never zero, we must have
				<me>
					a_n\ r^n + \cdots + a_2\ r^2 + a_1\ r + a_0 = 0
				</me>
				which is an <m>n</m>-th order polynomial in <m>r</m>.
			</p>
		</theorem>

		<example><title> Characteristic Equation &amp; Exponential Solutions </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					For each of the following equations, determine the characteristic equation and the fundamental exponential solutions.
				</p>
				<p>
					<sidebyside widths="40% 60%">
						<p>
							<line>	1.	<m>\ds\ 5y' + 6y = 0</m>	</line>
							<line>		<m></m>						</line>
						</p>
						<p>
							<solution>
								<p>
									Assuming <m>y = e^{rx}</m>, we have
								</p>
								<md>
									<mrow>	5\left[e^{rx}\right]' + 6\left[e^{rx}\right] = 0	\amp </mrow>
									<mrow>	5r e^{rx} + 6 e^{rx} = 0							\amp </mrow>
									<mrow>	(5r + 6) e^{rx} = 0									\amp </mrow>
								</md>.
								<p>
									Thus, the characteristic equation is
								</p>
								<me>
									5r + 6 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad r = -\frac{6}{5}
								</me>,
								<p>
									and the fundamental solution is
								</p>
								<me>
									y = e^{-\frac{6}{5}x}.
								</me>
							</solution>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
					<sidebyside widths="40% 60%">
						<p>
							<line>	2.	<m>\ds\ y'' - 4y = 0</m>	</line>
							<line>		<m></m>						</line>
						</p>
						<p>
							<solution>
								<p>
									Assuming <m>y = e^{rx}</m>, we have
								</p>
								<md>
									<mrow>	r^2 e^{rx} - 4 e^{rx} = 0	\amp </mrow>
									<mrow>	(r^2 - 4) e^{rx} = 0		\amp </mrow>
								</md>
								<p>
									Thus, the characteristic equation is
								</p>
								<md>
									<mrow>	r^2 -	\amp 4 = 0								</mrow>
									<mrow>			\amp \Rightarrow\ (r - 2)(r + 2) = 0	</mrow>
								</md>
								<p>
									and the fundamental solutions are
								</p>
								<me>
									y_1 = e^{2x}, \quad y_2 = e^{-2x}.
								</me>
							</solution>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
					<sidebyside widths="40% 60%">
						<p>
							<line>	3.	<m>\ds\ y''' - 6y'' + y' - 6y = 0</m>	</line>
							<line>		<m></m>									</line>
						</p>
						<p>
							<solution>
								<p>
									Assuming <m>y = e^{rx}</m>, we have
								</p>
								<md>
									<mrow>	r^3 e^{rx} - 6r^2 e^{rx} + r e^{rx} - 6 e^{rx} = 0	\amp </mrow>
									<mrow>	(r^3 - 6r^2 + r - 6) e^{rx} = 0						\amp </mrow>
								</md>
								<p>
									Thus, the characteristic equation is
								</p>
								<me>
									r^3 - 6r^2 + r - 6 = 0
								</me>.
								<p>
									We can factor this as
								</p>
								<md>
									<mrow>	r^2(r - 6) + (r - 6) = 0	\amp </mrow>
									<mrow>	(r^2 + 1)(r - 6) = 0		\amp </mrow>
									<mrow>	r = -i,\ r = i,\ r = 6		\amp </mrow>
								</md>.
								<p>
									Therefore, the fundamental solutions are
								</p>
								<me>
									y_1 = e^{-ix}, \quad y_2 = e^{ix}, \quad y_3 = e^{6x}.
								</me>
							</solution>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
				</p>
			</statement>
		</example>
		
		<p>

			This last example demostrates that, in general, an <m>n</m>-th order LHCC equation has <m>n</m> exponential solutions of the form <m>e^{rx}</m>, where <m>r</m> is a solution (real or complex) to the <m>n</m>-th degree characteristic equation.
		</p>

		<conclusion>
			<p>
				Now that we understand how to systematically find the fundamental exponential solutions, the last and easiest step is to construct the <em>general solution</em> by combining these solutions appropriately.
			</p>
		</conclusion>
