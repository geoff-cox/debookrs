<section><title> Second-Order LHCC Equations </title>

	<introduction>
		<p>
			Any <em>second-order linear homogeneous constant coefficient (LHCC) equations</em> can be written in the form
			<men xml:id="second-order-lhcc-de">
				a y'' + b y' + c y = 0
			</men>
			where <m>a, b, c</m> are constants.
		</p>

		<p>
			These equations are not only incredibly common in applications, but they provide the fundation for constructing the general solutions to all LHCC equations with a higher-order. For this discussion, we will explore the general solution to second-order LHCC equations and the different cases that arise based on the nature of the characteristic solutions. 
		</p>
	</introduction>

<paragraphs><title> Fundamental Solutions </title>

		<p>
			The <em>characteristic equation</em> of equation <xref ref="second-order-lhcc-de"/> is given by the <xref ref="solving-the-quadratic-equation" text="custom">quadratic equation</xref>: 
			<men xml:id="second-order-lhcc-characteristic-equation">
				a r^2 + b r + c = 0
			</men>
			and we can solve it using the <xref ref="quadratic-formula" text="custom">quadratic formula</xref> to get the two solutions:
		</p>

		<men xml:id="second-order-lhcc-char-eqn-solns">
			r_1 = \frac{-b - \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}, \quad r_2 = \frac{-b + \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}
		</men>
		
		<p>
			The <em>fundamental solutions</em> to the LHCC equation are given by
			<me>
				e^{r_1 x},\quad e^{r_2 x}
			</me>.
		</p>
	</paragraphs>

<paragraphs><title> The General Solution </title>

		<p>
			With the fundamental solutions, <m>e^{r_1 x}</m> and <m>e^{r_2 x}</m>, in hand, a result known as the <em>superposition principle</em> tells us that both <m>c_1 e^{r_1 x}</m> and <m>c_2 e^{r_2 x}</m> are also solutions for any constants <m>c_1</m> and <m>c_2</m>.
		</p>

		<p>
			More importantly, it also leads to two possible cases for the general solution: 
			<dl width="wide">
				<li><title> Case 1 </title>
					<p>
						If <m>e^{r_1 x}</m> and <m>e^{r_2 x}</m> are not like-terms,<fn>This property is actually known as linear independence and also takes the domain (interval) of <m>y_1</m> and <m>y_2</m> into consideration.</fn> then the <em>general solution</em> is
						<me>c_1 e^{r_1 x} + c_2 e^{r_2 x}</me>.
					</p>
				</li>
				<li><title> Case 2 </title>
					<p>
						If <m>e^{r_1 x}</m> and <m>e^{r_2 x}</m> are like-terms, then the general solution is
						<me>c_1 e^{r_1 x} + c_2 x e^{r_2 x}</me>.
					</p>
					<p>
						Note that <m>e^{r_1 x}</m> and <m>e^{r_2 x}</m> are like-terms only if <m>r_1 = r_2</m>.
					</p>
				</li>
			</dl>
		</p>

		<p>
			In practice, it is standard to split Case 1 into two cases based on whether <m>r_1</m> and <m>r_2</m> are real or complex numbers. This allows us to define each case based on the discriminant <m>(\Delta = b^2 - 4ac)</m> of the characteristic equation as follows: 
			<dl width="wide">
				<li><title> Case 1 <m>(\Delta &gt; 0)</m> </title>
					<p>
						<m>r_1</m> and <m>r_2</m> are real &amp; non-repeating.
					</p>
				</li>
				<li><title> Case 2 <m>(\Delta &lt; 0)</m> </title>
					<p>
						<m>r_1</m> and <m>r_2</m> are complex conjugates.
					</p>
				</li>
				<li><title> Case 3 <m>(\Delta = 0)</m> </title>
					<p>
						<m>r_1</m> and <m>r_2</m> are real &amp; repeating.
					</p>
				</li>
			</dl>
		</p>

		<p>
			Now, let's explore each of these cases in detail.
		</p>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs><title> Case 1 <m>(\Delta &gt; 0)</m>: Real, Non-Repeating Solutions </title>

		<p>
			If <m>\Delta</m> is positive, then characteristic equation has the two non-equal real solutions:
		</p>

		<me>
			r_1 = \frac{-b + \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \quad \text{and} \quad
			r_2 = \frac{-b - \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}
		</me>,

		<p>
			so the general solution is
		</p>

		<me>
			y = c_1 e^{r_1 x} + c_2 e^{r_2 x}
		</me>.
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs><title> Case 2 <m>(\Delta &lt; 0)</m>: Two Complex Solutions </title>
		<p>
			If <m>\Delta</m> is negative, the characteristic equation has two <em>complex conjugate solutions</em>:
		</p>

		<me>
			r_1 = \alpha + i \beta, \quad r_2 = \alpha - i \beta
		</me>

		<p>
			and the general solution is
		</p>

		<me>
			y = c_1 e^{(\alpha + i \beta)x} + c_2 e^{(\alpha - i \beta)x}
		</me>.

		<p>
			However, it is common to express this in terms of <em>sine</em> and <em>cosine</em> (via <xref ref="eulers-formula" text="title"/>):
		</p>

		<me>
			y = e^{\alpha x} (c_1 \cos(\beta x) + c_2 \sin(\beta x))
		</me>.
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs><title> Case 3 <m>(\Delta = 0)</m>: Real, Repeating Solutions </title>
		<p>
			If <m>\Delta</m> is zero, then the characteristic equation has the <em>repeated solutions</em>
		</p>

		<me>
			r_1 = \frac{-b + 0}{2a} = \frac{-b}{2a}, \quad r_2 = \frac{-b - 0}{2a} = \frac{-b}{2a}
		</me>.

		<p>
			and the general solution, in this case, is
		</p>

		<me>
			y = c_1 e^{r_1 x} + c_2 x e^{r_1 x}
		</me>

		<p>
			This modification ensures that our solution has two independent terms and preserves the desired properties of exponential functions that make them compatible solutions.
		</p>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs>
		<title> Summary &amp; Examples </title>

		<p>
			The following summarizes the three different forms of the general solution to a second-order LHCC differential equation.
		</p>

		<assemblage><title> ✳️ 2nd-Order LHCC General Solution Cases </title>
			<p>
				Let <m>r_1</m>, <m>r_2</m> be the characteristic solutions of a second-order LHCC equation.
				<dl width="narrow">
					<li xml:id="lhcc-case-1">
						<title> Case 1 <m>(\Delta &gt; 0)</m> <m>\small \text{real &amp;}\ r_1 \ne r_2</m> </title>
						<p><me>y = c_1 e^{r_1 x} + c_2 e^{r_2 x}</me></p>
					</li>
					<li xml:id="lhcc-case-2">
						<title> Case 2 <m>(\Delta &lt; 0)</m> <m>\small \text{complex &amp;}\ r=\alpha \pm i\beta</m> </title>
						<p><me>y = e^{\alpha x} (c_1 \cos(\beta x) + c_2 \sin(\beta x))</me></p>
					</li>
					<li xml:id="lhcc-case-3">
						<title> Case 3 <m>(\Delta = 0)</m> <m>\small \text{real &amp;}\ r_1 = r_2</m> </title>
						<p><me>y = c_1 e^{r_1 x} + c_2 x e^{r_1 x}</me></p>
					</li>
				</dl>
			</p>
		</assemblage>

		<example><title> Case 1 Examples </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Find the general solutions to each 2nd-order LHCC equation.
				</p>

				<sidebyside widths="45% 55%">
					<p>
						<p>
							<m>\ds y'' - 5y' + 6y = 0</m>
						</p>
						<p>
							<solution>
								<p>
									The characteristic equation
								</p>

								<me>
									r^2 - 5r + 6 = 0
								</me>

								<p>
									can be factored as
								</p>

								<me>
									(r - 2)(r - 3) = 0
								</me>,

								<p>
									which gives the non-repeating real solutions
								</p>

								<me>
									r_1 = 2, \qquad r_2 = 3
								</me>
								
								<p>
									and the general solution
								</p>

								<me>
									y = c_1 e^{2x} + c_2 e^{3x}
								</me>
							</solution>
						</p>
					</p>
					<p>
						<p>
							<m>\ds 5y'' + 13y' - 2y = 0</m>
						</p>
						<p>
							<solution>
								<p>
									The characteristic equation is 
								</p>

								<me>
									5r^2 + 9r - 2 = 0
								</me>.

								<p>
									Using the quadratic formula,
								</p>

								<md>
									<mrow> r	\amp = \frac{-9 \pm \sqrt{9^2 - 4 (5)(-2)}}{2 \cdot 5}	</mrow>
									<mrow>		\amp = \frac{-9 \pm \sqrt{121}}{10}						</mrow>
									<mrow>		\amp = \frac{-9 \pm 11}{10}								</mrow>
								</md>,

								<p>
									we get the real and non-repeating solutions
								</p>

								<me>
									r_1 = -2, \quad r_2 = \frac{1}{5}
								</me>,

								<p>
									which lead to the general solution
								</p>

								<me>
									y = c_1 e^{-2x} + c_2 e^{\frac{1}{5}x}
								</me>.
							</solution>
						</p>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>
			</statement>
		</example>

		<example><title> Case 2 Examples </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Find the general solutions to each 2nd-order LHCC equation.
				</p>

				<sidebyside widths="50% 50%">
					<p>
						<p>
							<m>\ds \omega'' + 4\omega' + 5\omega = 0</m>
						</p>
						<p>
							<solution>
								<p>
									The characteristic equation
								</p>

								<me>
									r^2 + 4r + 5 = 0
								</me>.

								<p>
									is solved with the quadratic formula:
								</p>

								<md>
									<mrow> r	\amp = \frac{-4 \pm \sqrt{4^2 - 4 (1)(5)}}{2 \cdot 1}	</mrow>
									<mrow> 		\amp = \frac{-4 \pm 2i}{2} = -2 \pm i					</mrow>
								</md>.

								<p>
									Using <m>\alpha = -2</m> and <m>\beta = 1</m> in the complex formula, we get the general solution
								</p>

								<me>
									\omega(t) = c_1 e^{-2t}\left(c_1\cos t + c_2\sin t\right)
								</me>
							</solution>
						</p>
					</p>
					<p>
						<p>
							<m>\ds y'' + 25y = 0</m>
						</p>
						<p>
							<solution>
								<p>
									The characteristic equation
								</p>

								<me>
									r^2 + 25 = 0
								</me>

								<p>
									has the complex solutions
								</p>

								<me>
									r = \pm 5i
								</me>.

								<p>
									Using <m>\alpha = 0</m> and <m>\beta = 5</m> in the complex case formula, we get the general solution
								</p>

								<me>
									y = e^{0x}\left(c_1\cos(5x) + c_2\sin(5x)\right)
								</me>

								<p>
									or simply
								</p>

								<me>
									y = c_1\cos(5x) + c_2\sin(5x)
								</me>
							</solution>
						</p>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>
			</statement>
		</example>

		<example><title> Case 3 Examples </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Find the general solutions to each 2nd-order LHCC equation.
				</p>

				<sidebyside widths="45% 55%">
					<p>
						<p>
							<m>\ds y'' + 4y' + 4y = 0</m>
						</p>
						<p>
							<solution>
								<p>
									The characteristic equation
								</p>

								<me>
									r^2 + 4r + 4 = 0
								</me>

								<p>
									can be factored as
								</p>

								<me>
									(r + 2)(r + 2) = 0
								</me>

								<p>
									which gives the repeating real solutions
								</p>

								<me>
									r_1 = -2, \qquad  r_2 = -2
								</me>

								<p>
									and modified general solution
								</p>

								<me>
									y = c_1 e^{-2x} + c_2 x e^{-2x}
								</me>.
							</solution>
						</p>
					</p>
					<p>
						<p>
							<m>\ds 16\frac{d^2q}{dr^2} - 8\frac{dq}{dr} + q = 0</m>
						</p>
						<p>
							<solution>
								<p>
									The characteristic equation is:
								</p>

								<me>
									16r^2 - 8r + 1 = 0
								</me>

								<p>
									Using the quadratic formula,
								</p>

								<md>
									<mrow> r	\amp = \frac{8 \pm \sqrt{8^2 - 4 (16)(1)}}{2 \cdot 16}	</mrow>
									<mrow>		\amp = \frac{8 \pm \sqrt{0}}{32}					</mrow>
									<mrow>		\amp = \frac{8}{32}	= \frac{1}{4}						</mrow>
								</md>,

								<p>
									This shows that <m> r = \frac{1}{4} </m> is a repeated root and the general solution is
								</p>

								<me>
									q(r) = c_1 e^{\frac{1}{4}r} + c_2 r e^{\frac{1}{4}r}
								</me>
								
							</solution>
						</p>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>
			</statement>
		</example>

		<example><title> LHCC Equation General Solution </title>

			<p>
				<statement>
					<p>
						Find the general solution to each of the equation below.
					</p>
					<p>
						<sidebyside widths="40% 60%">
							<p>
								<line>	1.	<m>\ds\ y'+\pi\ y = 0</m>	</line>
								<line>		<m></m>						</line>
							</p>
							<p>
								<solution>
									<p>
										The characteristic equation
									</p>

									<me>
										r + \pi = 0
									</me>,

									<p>
										has the solution <m>r = -\pi</m>. So, the equation has a fundamental solution of the form
									</p>

									<me>
										y_1 = e^{-\pi x}
									</me>.

									<p>
										Since the equation is first-order, this is the only fundamental solution.
									</p>

									<p>
										Therefore, the general solution is
									</p>

									<me>
										y = c_1 e^{-\pi x}
									</me>.
								</solution>
							</p>
						</sidebyside>

						<sidebyside widths="40% 60%">
							<p>
								<line>	2.	<m>\ds\ \omega'' - 7\omega' = 0</m>	</line>
								<line>		<m></m>						</line>
							</p>
							<p>
								<solution>
									<p>
										The characteristic equation
									</p>

									<me>
										r^2 - 7r = 0
									</me>,

									<p>
										has the solutions <m>r = 0,\ 7</m>. So, the fundamental solutions are
									</p>

									<me>
										\omega_1 = e^{0x} = 1, \quad \omega_2 = e^{7x}
									</me>.

									<p>
										Since these are not like-terms, they are independent and by the superposition principle, the general solution is
									</p>

									<me>
										\omega = c_1 + c_2\ e^{7x}
									</me>.
								</solution>
							</p>
						</sidebyside>
						<sidebyside widths="40% 60%">
							<p>
								<line>	3.	<m>\ds\ 2\frac{d^{3}M}{dt^{3}} - 6\frac{dM}{dt} = 0</m>	</line>
								<line>		<m></m>									</line>
							</p>
							<p>
								<solution>
									<p>
										The characteristic equation
									</p>

									<me>
										2r^3 - 6r = 0
									</me>,

									<p>
										can be factored as
									</p>

									<md>
										<mrow>	2r(r^2 - 3) = 0	\amp </mrow>
									</md>.

									<p>
										So, <m>r = 0,\ \sqrt{3},\ -\sqrt{3}\ </m>, giving the 3 independent fundamental solutions
									</p>

									<me>
										e^{0t}, \quad e^{\sqrt{3}t}, \quad e^{-\sqrt{3}t}
									</me>.

									<p>
										By the superposition principle, the general solution is
									</p>

									<me>
										M(t) = c_1 + c_2 e^{\sqrt{3}t} + c_3 e^{-\sqrt{3}t}
									</me>.
								</solution>
							</p>
						</sidebyside>
					</p>
				</statement>
			</p>
		</example>

		<p>
			These examples illustrate how second-order LHCC equations are solved using the <em>standard</em>, <em>repeating</em>, and <em>complex</em> cases discussed above. In the next section, we will see that a similar process can be generalized to higher-order LHCC equations.
		</p>
	</paragraphs>
