<chapter><title> Laplace Transform Method </title>

	<introduction>

		<p>
			The Laplace transform method is a powerful technique that solves a differential equation by temporarily injecting it into a new setting called the <term>Laplace domain</term>. In this domain, our original differential equation with unknown, <m>y</m> is replaced with an algebraic <term>Laplace equation</term> with unknown, <m>Y</m>. The beauty of this approach lies in the simple fact that as we isolate <m>Y</m> with algebra, we are simultaneously isolating <m>y</m>. Amazingly, once you have solved for <m>Y</m>, the process can be reversed to recover the solution, <m>y</m>, to the original equation. 
		</p>

		<p>
			A big picture sample of this process can be visualized in the <xref ref="lt-roadmap" text="custom">Laplace transform roadmap</xref>. As you can see, this four-step process begins with a forward transformation of the original equation into a Laplace domain (step 1). The new Laplace equation is solved (step 2) and the Laplace solution is prepared for its trip out of the Laplace domain (step 3). The process ends with a backward transformation which leads to the solution of the original equation (step 4). 
		</p>

		<figure xml:id="lt-roadmap"><title> ✳️ Laplace Transform Roadmap </title>
			<caption>
				A roadmap for solving an initial-valued problem using the Laplace Transform method.
				A visual guide outlining the steps of the Laplace Transform method used to solve an initial-value problem.
			</caption>
			<image source="figures/LT-Roadmap-steps-1-4.png" width="100%"/>
		</figure>

		<p>
			This chapter breaks down the Laplace transform method into three sections. The first section aims to define, motivate, and build a solid collection of Laplace transform properties and rules. Then we explore details invloved in moving through each of the steps in the <xref ref="lt-roadmap" text="custom">roadmap</xref>. We will finish the chapter with a variety of examples that demonstrate, step-by-step, how this method is used to solve initial value problems.
		</p>

	</introduction>

	<section><title> The Laplace Transform </title>
		
		<introduction>

			<p>
				Before we can apply the Laplace transform to solve differential equations, we need to understand the fundamental concept that makes the method work: transferring derivatives inside an integral. This section begins with a review of integration by parts, illustrating how derivatives applied to one function can be shifted onto another. 
			</p>

			<p>
				Building on this idea, we define the Laplace transform as an integral that takes a function of <m>t</m> and produces a new function of <m>s</m>. Through detailed examples, we compute transforms of simple functions and highlight when these transforms exist. By the end of this section, you'll see how Laplace transforms simplify calculus problems into algebraic ones—and how this shift makes solving differential equations far more manageable.
			</p>

		</introduction>

		<subsection><title> Transferring Derivatives Inside an Integral </title>

			<introduction>

				<p>
					To understand how the Laplace transform method simplifies differential equations into algebraic equations, we must revisit integration by parts from calculus. The minimalistic version of integration by parts formula is given by
					<me>
						\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du
					</me>.
				</p>

				<p>
					Assuming <m>u</m> and <m>v</m> are functions of <m>t</m> on <m>[a,b]</m>, another version is given by
					<me>
						\int_{a}^{b} \os{{\color{RoyalBlue}\large\text{derivative on }v}}{
							\us{u}{ \us{\uparrow}{\ul{ 
								u(t)
							}}} \ 
							\us{dv}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{
								{\color{RoyalBlue}\os{\downarrow}{v'\vphantom{\Large\Gamma}}(t)} dt
							}}}
						}
						= \us{u}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{ u(t) }}}\ \us{v}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{ v(t) }}} \Big|_{a}^{b}
							- \int_{a}^{b} \os{{\color{RoyalBlue}\large\text{derivative on }u}}{
								\us{v}{ \us{\uparrow}{\ul{ v(t) }}} \ 
								\us{du}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{ {\color{RoyalBlue}\os{\downarrow}{u'\vphantom{\Large\Gamma}}(t)} dt }}}
							}
					</me>.
				</p>

				<p>
					This version of the formula clearly shows how the derivative on <m>v</m> (left integral) is transferred to <m>u</m> (right integral). This concept is fundamental to the Laplace transform method, especially when <m>u = e^{-st}</m>, where <m>s</m> is a positive constant.
				</p>

			</introduction>

			<p>
				To see why this may be useful, let <m>u(t) = e^{-st}</m> and <m>v(t) = y(t)</m>. Then, <m>u'(t) = -se^{-st}</m> and <m>v'(t) = y'(t)</m> and the formula becomes
				<me>
					\int_{a}^{b} \os{{\color{RoyalBlue}\large\text{derivative on }y}}{ 
						e^{-st} \ {\color{RoyalBlue}\os{\downarrow}{y'\vphantom{\Large\Gamma}}(t)}\ dt
					} = e^{-st} \ y(t) \Big|_{a}^{b}
					- \int_{a}^{b} \os{{\color{RoyalBlue}\large\text{derivative on }e^{-st}}}{ 
						y(t) \ {\color{RoyalBlue}\os{\downarrow}{\left(-s e^{-st}\right)\vphantom{\Large\Gamma}}}\ dt
					}
				</me>.
			</p>

			<aside><title> Clarification </title>
				<p>
					Since the integral is in terms of <m>t</m>, <m>s</m> is constant and can be pulled out of the integral.
				</p>
			</aside>
			
			<p>
				Here, the derivative on <m>y'</m> moved onto the exponential function <m>e^{-st}</m>. Simplifying the integral on the right yields
				<me>
					\int_{a}^{b} e^{-st}\vphantom{y'} \ y'(t) dt = \ub{e^{-st} \ y(t) \Big|_{a}^{b}  + s \int_{a}^{b} e^{-st} \, y(t) \, dt}_{\large\text{no derivatives of }y}
				</me>.
			</p>

			<p>
				We can now make two important observations. First, the integral on the left, containing <m>y'</m>, can be rewritten in terms of an expression containing no <m>y</m> derivatives. Second, the only difference between the two integrals is an extra factor of <m>s</m>. Thus, we can think of the derivative as being encoded in <m>s</m>. This viewpoint is the essence behind the Laplace transform method and, in the next section, we will have a more clear view of differential equation's in this context.
			</p>

		</subsection>

		<subsection><title> Laplace Transform Definition </title>

			<introduction>

				<p>
					In the previous section, we saw that multiplying a differential equation by <m>e^{-st}</m> and integrating allows us to shift derivatives away from the unknown function and onto the exponential. But most differential equations don't come with an <m>e^{-st}</m> built in—so we must introduce it ourselves.
				</p>

			</introduction>

			<p>
				Let's try this with a specific example:
			</p>

			<p>
				Suppose we are given the equation
				<men xml:id="lt-model-eqn">
					y'' - 9y = 10e^{2t}
				</men>.
			</p>

			<p>
				Multiplying both sides by <m>e^{-st}</m> and integrating with respect to <m>t</m> gives
				<me>
					\int \left(y'' - 9y\right) e^{-st} \, dt = \int 10e^{2t} e^{-st} \, dt.
				</me>
				Using the linearity of the integral, this expands to
				<me>
					\ub{\int e^{-st} y''\ dt}_{\text{transfer 2 derivatives}}
					- 9\ub{\int e^{-st} y\ dt}_{\text{leave as-is}}
					= \ub{\int e^{-st} \left( 10e^{2t} \right)\ dt}_{\text{integrate directly}}.
				</me>
			</p>

			<p>
				This transformation rewrites the differential equation in a form where each term is ready for conversion—either by transferring derivatives or by direct integration. This leads us to the key idea behind the Laplace transform.
			</p>

			<definition xml:id="laplace-transform-definition"><title>Laplace Transform</title>
				<statement>
					<p>
						Given a function <m>f(t)</m> defined for <m>t \geq 0</m>, its Laplace transform, denoted <m>\lap{f(t)}</m>, is defined by
						<me>
							\lap{f(t)} = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} f(t) \, dt,
						</me>
						provided the integral converges. The result is a new function, typically written as <m>F(s)</m>:
						<me>
							F(s) = \lap{f(t)}.
						</me>
					</p>
				</statement>
			</definition>

			<p>
				Since the upper limit is <m>\infty</m>, this is an improper integral. From calculus, you may remember that we evaluate such integrals by taking a limit:
				<me>
					\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} f(t)\, dt = \ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} \ob{\int_0^b e^{-st} f(t)\, dt}^{\large\text{(step 1)}}}_{\large\text{(step 2)}}
				</me>.
			</p>

			<p>
				During the integration step, treat <m>s</m> and <m>b</m> as constants and compute the integral with respect to <m>t</m>. Then, in the limit step, let <m>b \to \infty</m> and check whether the result converges. If it does, the Laplace transform exists.
			</p>

			<p>
				Let's walk through a simple example to see this in action.
			</p>

			<example xml:id="laplace-example-constant"><title> Laplace Transform of a Constant Function </title>
				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Find the Laplace transform of the constant function <m>f(t) = 5</m>.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>

				<solution>
					<p>
						By the definition above, we have the improper integral
						<men xml:id="lt-def-example-eqn-1">
							\lap{5} = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \cdot 5\ dt = \lim_{b \to \infty} \ub{\int_0^b 5e^{-st}dt}_{I}
						</men>.
					</p>

					<p>
						Assuming <m>s</m> and <m>b</m> are constant, we integrate <m>I</m> with respect to <m>t</m>:
						<me>
							I = \int_0^b 5e^{-st}dt = -\frac{5}{s}e^{-st}\Big|_{t=0}^{t=b} = -\frac{5}{s}\left[ e^{-sb} - 1 \right]
						</me>.
					</p>

					<p>
						Next, we compute the limit of <m>I</m> as <m>b \to \infty</m> (with <m>s</m> constant):
						<md>
							<mrow>
								\lim_{b \to \infty} I = \lim_{b \to \infty} -\frac{5}{s}\left[ e^{-sb} - 1 \right] 
									\amp <xref text="custom" ref="lt-example-1-details"><m>\os{\large ❔}{=}</m></xref> -\frac{5}{s} \lim_{b \to \infty} \Big[ e^{-sb} - 1 \Big]
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									\amp = -\frac{5}{s} \Big[ \ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{-sb}}_{L} - 1 \Big]
							</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
			
					<p>
						Since the limit, <m>L</m> as <m>b \to \infty</m>, depends on the <term><xref ref="graph-of-e-to-x" text="custom">power of the exponential function</xref></term>, there are two cases based on the sign of <m>s</m>:
						<aside>
							<p>
								Note, <m>s = 0</m> is not possible since <m>I</m> contains <m>\sfrac{5}{s}</m> and would be undefined.
							</p>
						</aside>
						<ol>
							<li> <m>s &gt; 0</m>: <m>\ -sb \to -\infty\ </m>, so <m>\ e^{-sb} \to 0\ </m> &amp; <m>L=0\ </m> (converges) </li>
							<li> <m>s &lt; 0</m>: <m>\ -sb \to +\infty\ </m>, so <m>\ e^{-sb} \to \infty\ </m> &amp; <m>L=\infty\ </m> (diverges) </li>
						</ol>
						By definition, the integral in a Laplace transform must converge, meaning <m>L</m> must converge. So, <m>L=0</m> and the Laplace transform is
						<me>
							\lap{5} = -\frac{1}{s} [0 - 1] = \frac{1}{s} \quad \text{for } s \gt 0
						</me>.
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>

			<example><title> Laplace Transform of <m>t</m> </title>
				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Find the Laplace transform of the function <m>f(t)=t</m>.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>
				<solution xml:id="laplace-transform-of-t-power-1"><title> <m>\rightarrow \lap{t}</m> </title>

					<p>
						Applying the definition and writing the integral as a limit, we have
						<me>
							\lap{ t }	= \lim_{b \to \infty} {\color{BurntOrange}\ub{\int_0^b e^{-st} \cdot t\ dt}_{I}}
						</me>.
					</p>

					<p>
						Computing <xref text="custom" ref="lt-example-t-IVP-details"><m>I</m> with integration by parts</xref>, gives us
						<md>
							<mrow>
								{\color{BurntOrange}I}	\amp\ {\color{BurntOrange} = -\frac{b}{s}e^{-sb} - \frac{1}{s^2}e^{-sb} + \frac{1}{s^2}}
							</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>

					<p>
						Next, we compute the limit of <m>I</m> as <m>b \to \infty</m>:
						<md>
							<mrow>
								\lim_{b \to \infty} {\color{BurntOrange}I} \amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left({\color{BurntOrange} -\frac{b}{s}e^{-sb} - \frac{1}{s^2}e^{-sb} + \frac{1}{s^2}}\right)
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\amp = -\frac{1}{s}\lim_{b \to \infty} be^{-sb} - \frac{1}{s^2}\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{-sb} + \lim_{b \to \infty} \frac{1}{s^2}
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\amp = -\frac{1}{s}\ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} \frac{b}{e^{sb}}}_{L_1} - \frac{1}{s^2}\ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{-sb}}_{L_2} + \frac{1}{s^2}
							</mrow>
						</md> 
					</p>

					<p>
						As long as <m>s \gt 0</m>, both <term><xref ref="lt-example-t-LH-details" text="custom"><m>L_1\to 0</m> &amp; <m>L_2\to 0</m></xref></term>. Therefore, the Laplace transform of <m>t</m> is
						<me>
							\lap{ t } = \frac{1}{s^2}, \quad s &gt; 0
						</me>.
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>

			<p>
				These examples demonstrate the typical process involved in applying the Laplace transform to a function. As we move forward, we'll use this definition to build a library of Laplace transforms, which we will frequently reference when we begin solving equations using this new method.
			</p>

		</subsection>

		<subsection><title> Common Transforms </title>

			<introduction>

				<p>
					Now that we've defined the Laplace transform, it's time to build our toolbox. In this section, we'll compute the Laplace transforms of several commonly used functions:
					<me>
						1, \quad e^{at}, \quad t^n, \quad \sin(bt), \quad \text{and} \quad \cos(bt)
					</me>.
					Since these functions appear so frequently in differential equations, having a general formula for their transforms will be vital in the solution process.
				</p>

			</introduction>

			<p>
				Since the process for computing the Laplace transform of the constant function <m>f(t) = 1</m> mirrors what we did for <m>f(t) = 5</m> in the previous section, we'll simply state the result here:
				<me>
					\lap{1} = \frac{1}{s}, \quad s \gt 0.
				</me>
			</p>

			<p>
				The transforms of other basic functions are computed in the examples that follow, beginning with exponential functions.
			</p>

			<example><title> Laplace Transform of an Exponential Function </title>
				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Compute <m>\lap{e^{7t}}</m>, then use it as a template to determine the formula
							<me>
								\lap{e^{at}} = \frac{1}{s - a}, \quad s \gt a
							</me>.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>
		
				<p>
					<solution><title> <m>\rightarrow \lap{e^{7t}}</m> </title>
						<p>
							Applying the definition of the Laplace transform and writing the integral as a limit, we have
							<me>
								\lap{ e^{7t} }
								= \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \cdot e^{7t}\ dt
								= \lim_{b \to \infty}\int_0^b e^{(7-s)t}\ dt
							</me>.
						</p>

						<aside>
							<p>
								For any constant <m>K</m>, 
								<me>
									\int e^{K x} dx = \frac{1}{K} e^{K x} + C
								</me>
								by u-substitution.
							</p>
						</aside>

						<p>
							Focusing on the integral first and treating <m>7-s</m> as constant, we have
							<md>
								<mrow>
									\int_0^b e^{(7-s)t}\, dt
									<xref text="custom" ref="lt-example-2-details"><m>\os{\large ❔}{=}</m></xref> 
									\frac{1}{7-s} e^{(7-s)t} \Bigg|_0^b
									\amp = \frac{1}{7-s} \left( e^{(7-s)b} - e^{0} \right)
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = \frac{1}{7-s} \left( e^{(7-s)b} - 1 \right)
								</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>

						<p>
							Now, we compute the limit of this expression as <m>b \to \infty</m>:
							<md>
								<mrow>
									\lim_{b \to \infty} \frac{1}{7-s} \left( e^{(7-s)b} - 1 \right)
									\amp = \frac{1}{7-s} \Bigg[ \ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{(7-s)b}}_{L} - \lim_{b \to \infty} 1 \Bigg]
								</mrow>
							</md>.
						</p>

						<p>
							As before, the <term><xref ref="graph-of-e-to-x" text="custom">limit of the exponential function</xref></term> converges if its power, <m>(7-s)b</m>, goes to <m>-\infty</m>. This happens if <m>7-s</m> is negative (i.e., <m>s \gt 7</m>). 
						</p>

						<p>
							So, as long as <m>s \gt 7</m>, then <m>L \to 0</m> and the transform becomes 
							<me>
								\lap{e^{7t}} = \frac{1}{s - 7}, \quad s \gt 7
							</me>.
						</p>
					</solution>

					<solution><title> <m>\rightarrow \lap{e^{at}}</m> </title>
						<p>
							By swapping every <m>7</m> with an <m>a</m> in the previous solution, you will arrive at the following formula for the common Laplace transform:
							<me>
								\lap{e^{at}} = \frac{1}{s - a}, \quad s \gt a
							</me>,
							where <m>a</m> is a constant.
						</p>
					</solution>
				</p>
			</example>

			<p>
				Next, we compute the transforms of the polynomial terms, <m>t</m> and <m>t^2</m>, and use the result to uncover a recursive structure that helps generalize to <m>t^n</m>.
			</p>

			<example><title> Laplace Transform of the Power Function </title>
				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Show that you can relate the Laplace transforms of <m>t</m> and <m>t^2</m> by
							<me>
								\lap{t^2} = \frac{2}{s}\cdot \lap{t}, \quad s \gt 0
							</me>.
							Using this as a template, derive the general recursive relationship
							<me>
								\lap{t^n} = \frac{n}{s}\cdot \lap{t^{n-1}}, \quad s \gt 0
							</me>.
							Finally, hypothesize the general formula for the Laplace transform of <m>t^n</m>,
							<me>
								\lap{t^n} = \frac{n!}{s^{n+1}}, \quad s \gt 0
							</me>.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>
				<p>
					<solution><title> <m>\rightarrow \lap{t^2}</m> </title>
						<p>
							Applying the definition and writing the integral as a limit, we have
							<me>
								\lap{ t^2 } = \lim_{b \to \infty} \ub{\int_0^b e^{-st} \cdot t^2\ dt}_{I}
							</me>.
						</p>

						<p>
							Applying integration by parts to <m>I</m> once in <m>\lap{ t^2 }</m> shows
							<md>
								<mrow>
									\lap{ t^2 }
									\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{b^2}{s}e^{-sb} + \frac{2}{s}\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot t\ dt\right]
									<xref ref="lt-example-tsqrd-IVP-details" text="custom">🔍How?</xref>
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = -\frac{1}{s} \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{b^2}{e^{sb}}\right] + \frac{2}{s}\lim_{b \to \infty}\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot t\ dt
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = -\frac{1}{s}\ub{\lim_{b \to \infty}\frac{b^2}{e^{sb}}}_{L} + \frac{2}{s}\ub{\int_0^\infty e^{-st}\cdot t\ dt}_{\large\lap{t}}
								</mrow>.
							</md>
						</p>

						<p>
							Noticing that the improper integral is just <m>\lap{t}</m>, we have
							<me>
								\lap{ t^2 } = -\frac{1}{s}\cdot L + \frac{2}{s}\cdot \lap{t}
							</me>,
							but <m>L=0</m> if <m>s \gt 0</m> <xref ref="lt-example-tsqrd-LH-details" text="custom">(🔍How?)</xref>, so we have the relationship
						</p>

						<me>
							\lap{t^2} = \frac{2}{s}\cdot \lap{t}, \quad s \gt 0
						</me>.
					</solution>

					<solution><title> <m>\rightarrow \lap{t^n}</m> (recursive) </title>
						<p>
							Replacing <m>2</m> with <m>n</m> in the previous solution, we have
							<me>
								\lap{ t^n } = \lim_{b \to \infty} \ub{\int_0^b e^{-st} \cdot t^n\ dt}_{I}
							</me>.
						</p>

						<p>
							Applying integration by parts to <m>I</m> once in <m>\lap{ t^{n} }</m> shows
							<md>
								<mrow>
									\lap{ t^{n} }
									\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{b^n}{e^{sb}} + \frac{n}{s}\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot t^{n-1}\ dt\right]
									<xref ref="lt-example-tnth-IVP-details" text="custom">🔍How?</xref>
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = -\frac{1}{s} \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{b^n}{e^{sb}}\right] + \frac{n}{s}\lim_{b \to \infty}\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot t^{n-1}\ dt
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = -\frac{1}{s}\ub{\lim_{b \to \infty}\frac{b^n}{e^{sb}}}_{L} + \frac{n}{s}\ub{\int_0^\infty e^{-st}\cdot t^{n-1}\ dt}_{\large\lap{t^{n-1}}}
								</mrow>.
							</md>
						</p>

						<p>
							Noticing that the improper integral is just <m>\lap{t^{n-1}}</m>, we have
							<me>
								\lap{ t^{n} } = -\frac{1}{s}\cdot L + \frac{n}{s}\cdot \lap{t^{n-1}}
							</me>,
							but <m>L=0</m> if <m>s \gt 0</m> <xref ref="lt-example-tnth-LH-details" text="custom">(🔍How?)</xref>, so we have the relationship
						</p>

						<me>
							\lap{t^{n}} = \frac{n}{s}\cdot \lap{t^{n-1}}, \quad s \gt 0
						</me>.
					</solution>

					<solution><title> <m>\rightarrow \lap{t^n}</m> (formula) </title>
						<p>
							Starting with the recursive relationship for <m>t^n</m>, we can apply it repeatedly to find the Laplace transform of <m>t^n</m> in terms of <m>t^0 = 1</m>. This gives us
							<md>
								<mrow>
									\lap{t^n} 
									\amp = \frac{n}{s}\cdot \ub{\lap{t^{n-1}}}_{\downarrow}
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = \frac{n}{s}\cdot \Big(\frac{n-1}{s}\cdot \ub{\lap{t^{n-2}}}_{\downarrow}\Big)
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = \frac{n(n-1)}{s^2}\cdot \Big(\frac{n-2}{s}\cdot \ub{\lap{t^{n-3}}}_{\downarrow}\Big)
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp \qquad\qquad\vdots
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = \frac{n(n-1)\ldots 4}{s^{n-3}}\cdot \Big(\frac{3}{s}\cdot \lap{t^{2}}\Big)
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = \frac{n(n-1)\ldots 4\cdot 3}{s^{n-2}}\cdot \Big(\frac{2}{s^3}\Big)
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = \frac{n!}{s^{n+1}}
								</mrow>
							</md>.
						</p>
						<p>
							Thus, the Laplace transform of <m>t^n</m> is 
							<me>
								\lap{t^n} = \frac{n!}{s^{n+1}}, \quad s \gt 0, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots
							</me>.
						</p>

					</solution>
				</p>
			</example>

			<p>
				Finally, we move on to trigonometric functions, which show up in systems with oscillatory behavior. The transform of <m>\cos(bt)</m> is shown here, which provides a model for deriving the corresponding sine transform.
			</p>

			<example><title> Laplace Transform of Cosine </title>
				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Compute <m>\lap{\cos(3t)}</m>, then use it as a template to determine the formula
							<me>
								\lap{\cos(bt)} = \frac{s}{s^2 + b^2}, \quad s \gt 0
							</me>.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>
				
				<p>
					<solution><title> <m>\rightarrow \lap{\cos(3t)}</m> </title>
						<p>
							Applying the definition and writing the integral as a limit, we have
							<me>
								\lap{ \cos(3t)} = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \cdot \cos(3t)\ dt = \lim_{b \to \infty} \ub{\int_0^{b} e^{-st} \cos(3t)\ dt}_{I}
							</me>.
						</p>

						<p>
							Treating <m>s</m> constant, we apply integration by parts to <m>I</m> as follows:
						</p>

						<me>
							I = -\frac{e^{-st}}{s} \cos(3t) \Bigg|_0^b - \frac{3}{s}\int_0^{b} e^{-st} \sin(3t)\ dt
						</me>,

						<aside><title> Integration by Parts </title>
							<p>
								1st Integration by Parts
								<md>
									<mrow>	u	\amp = \cos(3t)				</mrow>
									<mrow>	du	\amp = -3\sin(3t)\ dt		</mrow>
									<mrow>	v	\amp = -\sfrac{e^{-st}}{s}	</mrow>
									<mrow>	dv	\amp = e^{-st}\ dt			</mrow>
								</md>
								2nd Integration by Parts
								<md>
									<mrow>	u	\amp = \sin(3t)				</mrow>
									<mrow>	du	\amp = 3\cos(3t)\ dt		</mrow>
									<mrow>	v	\amp = -\sfrac{e^{-st}}{s}	</mrow>
									<mrow>	dv	\amp = e^{-st}\ dt			</mrow>
								</md>
							</p>
						</aside>

						<p>
							but this requires a second integration by parts:
							<md>
								<mrow>
									I
									\amp = -\frac{e^{-st}}{s} \cos(3t) \Bigg|_0^b - \frac{3}{s} \left[ -\frac{e^{-st}}{s} \sin(3t)\Bigg|_0^b + \frac{3}{s} \int_0^{b} e^{-st} \cos(3t)\ dt \right]
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = \left(-\frac{e^{-st}}{s} \cos(3t) + \frac{3e^{-st}}{s^2} \sin(3t)\right)\Bigg|_0^b - \frac{9}{s^2} \ub{\int_0^{b} e^{-st} \cos(3t)\ dt}_{I}
								</mrow>
							</md>.
						</p>

						<p>
							Since the integral <m>I</m> reappeared, we actually have
							<me>
								I = \left(-\frac{e^{-st}}{s} \cos(3t) + \frac{3e^{-st}}{s^2} \sin(3t)\right)\Bigg|_0^b - \frac{9}{s^2} I
							</me>.
							Evaluating the limits of integration and solving for <m>I</m>, we find
							<md>
								<mrow>
									I + \frac{9}{s^2} I  \amp = \left(-\frac{e^{-st}}{s} \cos(3t) + \frac{3e^{-st}}{s^2} \sin(3t)\right)\Bigg|_0^b
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									I\left(\frac{s^2+9}{s^2}\right) \amp = \left(-\frac{e^{-sb}}{s} \cos(3b) + \frac{3e^{-sb}}{s^2} \sin(3b) + \frac{1}{s} \right)
								</mrow>
								<mrow> 
									I	\amp = \frac{s^2}{s^2+9}\left(-\frac{e^{-sb}}{s} \cos(3b) + \frac{3e^{-sb}}{s^2} \sin(3b) + \frac{1}{s} \right)
								</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>

						<p>
							Finally, the limit of <m>I</m> as <m>b \to \infty</m> is the sum of the limits
							<md>
								<mrow>
									\lim_{b \to \infty} I = \amp
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\frac{s^2}{s^2+9} \amp \Bigg(
										- \frac{1}{s}\ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{-sb} \cos(3b)}_{L_1}
										+ \frac{3}{s^2}\ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{-sb} \sin(3b)}_{L_2} 
										+ \frac{1}{s}
									\Bigg)
								</mrow>
							</md>
							Since <xref ref="lt-example-4-details" text="custom"><m>L_1\to0</m> &amp; <m>L_2\to0</m> if <m>s \gt 0</m></xref>, we can conclude that
							<me>
								\lap{\cos(3t)} = \frac{s}{s^2 + 9}, \quad s \gt 0
							</me>.
						</p>
					</solution>

					<solution><title> <m>\rightarrow \lap{\cos(bt)}</m> </title>
						<p>
							Replacing the constants <m>3</m> and <m>9</m> with <m>b</m> and <m>b^2</m>, respectively, shows that
							<me>
								\lap{\cos(bt)} = \frac{s}{s^2 + b^2}, \quad s \gt 0
							</me>.
						</p>
					</solution>
				</p>
			</example>

			<p>
				The derivation of the formula for the Laplace transform of sine, given by
				<me>
					\lap{\sin(bt)} = \frac{b}{s^2 + b^2}, \quad s \gt 0
				</me>,
				follows a similar approach and is left as exercise for you to try on your own.
			</p>

			<p>
				The transforms derived in this section will serve as the foundational building blocks in the Laplace method. In the next section, we'll expand this toolbox further by exploring the rules and properties of the Laplace transform that allow us to combine and manipulate transforms more flexibly.
			</p>

		</subsection>

		<subsection><title> Properties and Rules </title>

			<introduction>

				<p>
					Now that we've built a foundation of basic Laplace transforms, it's time to expand our toolbox by exploring the key properties that make the Laplace transform such a powerful problem-solving technique. These properties allow us to transform combinations of functions—like sums, products, and derivatives—more easily and systematically.
				</p>
				
				<p>
					In this section, you'll learn how the Laplace transform behaves under linear operations, how exponential functions shift the resulting transform, and how to efficiently handle derivatives and polynomial factors. Each property is introduced with a formal statement, illustrated through worked examples, and connected to its role in solving differential equations. Mastery of these rules is essential for applying the Laplace method effectively.
				</p>

			</introduction>

			<subsubsection><title> Sums and Constant Multiples (Linearity) </title>

				<p>
					As with integrals, we can pull out constants from Laplace transforms and apply them, term-by-term, to sums and differences of functions. This linear property streamlines the computation of Laplace transforms when working with expressions that combine simpler functions.
				</p>

				<assemblage xml:id="lt-linearity-property"><title> ✳️ Laplace Transform Property: Linearity </title>
					<p>
						If <m>a</m> and <m>b</m> are constants, and <m>f(t)</m> and <m>g(t)</m> have Laplace transforms, then
						<me>
							\lap{ a f(t) \pm b g(t) } = a \lap{f(t)} \pm b \lap{g(t)}.
						</me>
					</p>
				</assemblage>
				
				<p>
					Let's look at an example to see this in action.
				</p>

				<example><title> Transforming a Sum of Functions </title>
					<p>
						<statement>
							<p>
								Compute the Laplace transform
								<me> 
									\lap{15 + 6e^{7t} - 11t}
								</me>.
							</p>
						</statement>
					</p>
					
					<solution>
						<p>
							We apply the linearity property to break the transform down as follows:
							<md>
								<mrow>
									\lap{15 + 6e^{7t} - 11t}
									\amp = \lap{15} + \lap{6e^{7t}} - \lap{11t}
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = 15\lap{1} + 6\lap{e^{7t}} - 11\lap{t}
								</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>

						<p>
							Then, using the common transforms, <xref ref="L1" text="title"/>, <xref ref="L2" text="title"/> (<m>a=7</m>), and <xref ref="L3" text="title"/> (<m>n=1</m>), we get 
						</p>

						<md>
							<mrow>
								\lap{15 + 6e^{7t} - 11t}
								\amp = 15\cdot \os{\large s\gt0}{\vphantom{\int^{|}}\frac{1}{s}} + 6\cdot \os{\large s\gt7}{\vphantom{\int^{|}}\frac{1}{s-7}} - 11\cdot \os{\large s\gt0}{\vphantom{\int^{|}}\frac{1}{s^2}}
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\amp = \frac{15}{s} + \frac{6}{s-7} - \frac{11}{s^2}, \quad s \gt 7
							</mrow>.
						</md>
						<p>
							We use <m>s \gt 7</m>, because it guarantees that <m>s \gt 0</m> is also true.
						</p>
					</solution>
				</example>
				
			</subsubsection>

			<subsubsection><title> Multiplication by <m>e^{at}</m> (Exponential Shifting) </title>

				<p>
					The translation property, also known as the first shifting theorem, allows us to handle functions multiplied by an exponential term, <m>e^{at}</m>. This property is particularly useful for simplifying the Laplace transforms of products of exponential functions and other functions, such as sine, cosine, or polynomials.
				</p>

				<assemblage xml:id="lt-exp-shifting-property"><title> ✳️ Laplace Transform Property: Exponential Shifting </title>
					<p>
						Let <m>F(s) = \lap{f(t)}</m>. Then the Laplace transform of <m>e^{at} f(t)</m> is given by
						<me>
							\lap{ e^{at} f(t) } = F(s-a), \quad a \text{ is a constant}.
						</me>
					</p>
				</assemblage>

				<example><title> Laplace Transform of an Exponential Function </title>
					<p>
						<statement>
							<p>
								Use the exponential shifting property to compute the following
								<ol>
									<li>
										<p> 
											<m>\ds\lap{e^{7t}\cos(3t)}</m>
										</p>
									</li>
									<li>
										<p> 
											<m>\ds\lap{e^{-1.3t}\sin(\pi t)}</m>
										</p>
									</li>
									<li>
										<p> 
											<m>\ds\lap{e^{2t}t^3}</m>
										</p>
									</li>
								</ol>
							</p>
						</statement>
					</p>
					
					<p>				
						<solution>
							<p>
								To apply the <xref ref="lt-exp-shifting-property" text="custom">shifting property</xref>, we identify <m>a=7</m> and <m>f(t)</m> as follows:
								<me>
									\laplacesym \Big\{ \us{\large e^{at}}{\ul{e^{7t}\vphantom{()}}}\ \us{\large f(t)}{\ul{\cos(3t)}} \Big\} = F(s-7), \quad s \gt 7
								</me>
							</p>

							<p>
								By definition, <m>F(s) = \lap{ f(t) }</m>, so 
								<me>
									F(s) = \lap{ \cos(3t) } = \frac{s}{s^2 + 9}
								</me>.
							</p>
							<p>
								Finally, substituting <m>s-7</m> for <m>s</m> in the formula for <m>F(s)</m>, we have
								<me>
									\lap{ e^{7t} \cos(3t) } = F(s-7) = \frac{s-7}{(s-7)^2 + 9}, \quad s \gt 7
								</me>.
							</p>
						</solution>

						<solution>
							<p>
								In this case, <m>a=-1.3</m> and <m>f(t)=\sin(\pi t)</m> since
								<me>
									\laplacesym \Big\{ \us{\large e^{at}}{\ul{e^{-1.3t}\vphantom{()}}}\ \us{\large f(t)}{\ul{\sin(\pi t)}} \Big\} = F(s-(-1.3)) = F(s+1.3), \quad s \gt -1.3
								</me>
							</p>

							<p>
								By definition, <m>F(s) = \lap{ f(t) }</m>, so 
								<me>
									F(s) = \lap{ \sin(\pi t) } = \frac{\pi}{s^2 + \pi^2}
								</me>.
							</p>

							<p>
								Finally, substituting <m>s+1.3</m> for <m>s</m> in the formula for <m>F(s)</m>, we have
								<me>
									\lap{ e^{-1.3t} \sin(\pi t) } = F(s+1.3) = \frac{\pi}{(s+1.3)^2 + \pi^2}, \quad s \gt -1.3
								</me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
						
						<solution>
							<p>
								In this case, <m>a=2</m> and <m>f(t)=t^3</m> since
								<me>
									\laplacesym \Big\{ \us{\large e^{at}}{\ul{e^{2t}\vphantom{()}}}\ \us{\large f(t)}{\ul{t^3\vphantom{()}}} \Big\} = F(s-2), \quad s \gt 2
								</me>
							</p>

							<p>
								By definition, <m>F(s) = \lap{ f(t) }</m>, so 
								<me>
									F(s) = \lap{ t^3 } = \frac{3!}{s^4} = \frac{6}{s^4}
								</me>.
							</p>
							
							<p>
								Finally, substituting <m>s-2</m> for <m>s</m> in the formula for <m>F(s)</m>, we have
								<me>
									\lap{ e^{2t} \sin(\pi t) } = F(s-2) = \frac{6}{(s-2)^4}, \quad s \gt 2
								</me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</example>

				<p>
					By applying this property to the functions <m>t^n</m>, <m>\cos(bt)</m>, and <m>\sin(bt)</m>, we can derive additional common Laplace transforms:
				</p>

				<example><title> New Formulas from Exponential Shifting </title>
					<p>
						<statement>
							<p>
								Use the exponential shifting property to derive general formulas for
								<ol>
									<li>
										<p>
											<m>\ds \lap{e^{at}\cos(bt)}</m>
										</p>
									</li>
									<li>
										<p>
											<m>\ds \lap{e^{at}\sin(bt)}</m>
										</p>
									</li>
									<li>
										<p>
											<m>\ds \lap{e^{at}t^n}</m>
										</p>
									</li>
								</ol>
							</p>
						</statement>
					</p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								There is not much difference between these and the previous example. 
								<ol>
									<li>
										<p>
											For <m>\lap{e^{at}\cos(bt)}</m>, <m>f(t)=\cos(bt)</m> and
											<md>
												<mrow>					\amp F(s) = \lap{ \cos(bt) } = \frac{s}{s^2 + b^2}			</mrow>
												<mrow>	\implies \quad	\amp F(s-a) = \frac{s-a}{(s-a)^2 + b^2}, \qquad	\text{so,}	</mrow>
											</md>
											<me>
												\boxed{\lap{ e^{at}\cos(bt) } = \frac{s-a}{(s-a)^2 + b^2}, \quad s \gt a}
											</me>
										</p>
									</li>
									<li>
										<p>
											For <m>\lap{e^{at}\sin(bt)}</m>, <m>f(t)=\sin(bt)</m> and
											<md>
												<mrow>					\amp F(s) = \lap{ \sin(bt) } = \frac{b}{s^2 + b^2}			</mrow>
												<mrow>	\implies \quad	\amp F(s-a) = \frac{b}{(s-a)^2 + b^2}, \qquad \text{so,}	</mrow>
											</md>
											<me>
												\boxed{\lap{ e^{at}\sin(bt) } = \frac{b}{(s-a)^2 + b^2}, \quad s \gt a}
											</me>
										</p>
									</li>
									<li>
										<p>
											For <m>\lap{e^{at}t^n}</m>, <m>f(t)=t^n</m> and
											<md>
												<mrow>					\amp F(s) = \lap{ t^n } = \frac{n!}{s^{n+1}}			</mrow>
												<mrow>	\implies \quad	\amp F(s-a) = \frac{n!}{(s-a)^{n+1}}, \qquad \text{so,}	</mrow>
											</md>
											<me>
												\boxed{\lap{ e^{at}t^n } = \frac{n!}{(s-a)^{n+1}}, \quad s \gt a}
											</me>
										</p>
									</li>
								</ol>
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</example>

			</subsubsection>

			<subsubsection><title> Transforms of Derivatives (Derivative Transfer) </title>

				<p>
					The derivative property of the Laplace transform is one of its most powerful aspects, especially when solving differential equations. This property allows us to transform differential equations into algebraic equations, making them easier to solve.
				</p>

				<assemblage xml:id="lt-derivative-transfer-property"><title> ✳️ Laplace Transform Property: Derivative Transfer </title>
					<p>
						Let <m>F(s) = \lap{ f(t) }</m>. If the Laplace transforms of <m>f'(t)</m> exists, then
						<md>
							<mrow>	\lap{ f'(t) }	\amp = s\lap{ f(t) } - f(0)			</mrow>
							<mrow>					\amp \quad\text{ or }				</mrow>
							<mrow>	\lap{ f'(t) }	\amp = sF(s) - f(0), \qquad s \gt 0	</mrow>
						</md>.
					</p>
				</assemblage>

				<theorem xml:id="lt-derivative-transfer-details"><title> Derivative Transfer Property Derivation </title>
					<statement>
						<p>
							To show that
							<me>
								\lap{f'(t)} = s\lap{f(t)} - f(0), \quad s \gt 0
							</me>,
							we start with the definition of the Laplace transform:
						</p>

						<p>
							<me>
								\lap{ f'(t) } = \lim_{b \to \infty} \ub{\int_0^b e^{-st} \cdot f'(t)\ dt}_{I}
							</me>.
						</p>

						<p>
							Applying integration by parts to <m>I</m> once in <m>\lap{ f'(t) }</m> with
							<md>
								<mrow> u = e^{-st},			\quad\amp	dv = f'(t)\ dt,	</mrow>
								<mrow> du = -se^{-st}\ dt,	\quad\amp	v = f(t)		</mrow>
							</md>
							integration by parts gives
							<md>
								<mrow>
									\lap{ t^{n} }
									\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[f(t)e^{-sb}\Bigg|_0^b - \int_0^b f(t)\cdot\left(-se^{-st}\right)\ dt\right]
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\left(f(b)e^{-sb} - f(0)e^{0}\right) + s\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot f(t)\ dt\right]
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[f(b)e^{-sb} - f(0) + s\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot f(t)\ dt\right]
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[f(b)e^{-sb}\right] - f(0) +  s\lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot f(t)\ dt\right]
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[f(b)e^{-sb}\right] - f(0) +  s\ub{\int_\infty^b e^{-st}\cdot f(t)\ dt}_{\large\lap{f(t)}}
								</mrow>.
							</md>
						</p>

						<p>
							Recall that a requirement for the Laplace transform of <m>f(t)</m> to exist is that
							<me>
								\lim_{b \to \infty} \left[f(b)e^{-sb}\right] = 0, \quad s \gt 0
							</me>.
						</p>

						<p>
							Thus, setting the limit to zero gives us the desired result
							<me>
								\lap{ f'(t) } = s\lap{f(t)} - f(0)
							</me>.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</theorem>

				<p>
					This result is crucial for solving differential equations. Essentially, it allows us to eliminate derivatives from an equation, transforming the problem into an algebraic form that's easier to solve. But there's more—this property also works recursively, allowing us to handle higher-order derivatives as well.
				</p>

				<p>
					For example, you can see how this property extends to higher derivatives:
					<md>
						<mrow>
							\lap{ f'(t) }	
							\amp = \us{\large\downarrow}{\ul{\color{green} sF(s) - f(0)}}
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\lap{ f''(t) }
							\amp = s {\color{green}\lap{f'(t)}} - f'(0) = s \Big({\color{green}sF(s) - f(0)}\Big) - f'(0)
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = \us{\large\downarrow \hspace{5em}}{\ul{\color{blue}s^2F(s) - sf(0) - f'(0)}}
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\lap{ f'''(t) }	
							\amp = s {\color{blue}\lap{f''(t)}} - f''(0) = s \Big({\color{blue}s^2F(s) - sf(0) - f'(0)}\Big) - f''(0)
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = s^3F(s) - s^2f(0) - sf'(0) - f''(0)
						</mrow>
						<mrow> \vdots </mrow>
						<mrow> 
							\text{and the }
							\amp \text{pattern continues.}
						</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>

			</subsubsection>

			<subsubsection><title> Multiplication by <m>t^n</m> (Laplace Derivatives) </title>
				
				<p>
					The final property we'll explore is how the Laplace transform handles functions multiplied by a power of <m>t</m>. This property is especially useful when dealing with polynomial terms in differential equations.
				</p>

				<assemblage xml:id="lt-laplace-derivative-property"><title> ✳️ Laplace Transform Property: Laplace Derivatives </title>
					<p>
						Let <m>F(s) = \lap{ f(t) }</m>. Then we have the following property:
						<md>
							<mrow>
								\lap{t^n f(t)}
								\amp = (-1)^n \frac{d^n}{ds^n}\Big[\lap{f(t)}\Big]
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\amp \quad\text{ or }
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\lap{t^n f(t)}	
								\amp = (-1)^n \frac{d^n}{ds^n}\Big[F(s)\Big],\quad s &gt; 0,\ n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots
							</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
				</assemblage>

				<theorem xml:id="lt-laplace-derivatives-details"><title> Derivative Transfer Property Derivation </title>
					<statement>
						<p>
							To show that
							<me>
								\lap{t^n f(t)} = (-1)^n \frac{d^n}{ds^n}\left[\lap{f(t)}\right]
							</me>,
							we start on the right side and show that it leads to the left side.
						</p>

						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow>
									(-1)^n \frac{d^n}{ds^n}\left[\lap{f(t)}\right]
									\amp = (-1)^n \frac{d^n}{ds^n}\left[\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} f(t)\ dt\right]
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = (-1)^n \int_0^{\infty} \frac{\partial^n}{\partial s^n}\left[e^{-st} f(t)\right]\ dt
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = (-1)^n \int_0^{\infty} f(t)\frac{\partial^n}{\partial s^n}\left[e^{-st}\right]\ dt
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = (-1)^n \int_0^{\infty} f(t) \left((-t)^n e^{-st}\right)\ dt
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = (-1)^n \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} t^n f(t)\ dt
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} t^n f(t)\ dt
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = \lap{t^n f(t)}
								</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
					</statement>
				</theorem>

				<example>
					<p>
						<statement>
							<p>
								Use the <xref ref="lt-laplace-derivative-property" text="custom">Laplace derivative property</xref> to compute
								<ol>
									<li>
										<p>
											<m>\ds\lap{t \cos(t)}</m>
										</p>
									</li>
									<li>
										<p>
											<m>\ds\lap{t^2 \sin(-5t)}</m>
										</p>
									</li>
								</ol>
							</p>
						</statement>
					</p>
					
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								To apply the <xref ref="lt-laplace-derivative-property" text="custom">Laplace derivative property</xref>, we identify <m>n=1</m> and <m>f(t)</m>:
								<me>
									\laplacesym \Big\{ t^1 \ \us{\large f(t)}{\ul{\cos(t)}} \Big\} = (-1)^1 F'(s) = -F'(s), \quad s \gt 0
								</me>
							</p>

							<p>
								By definition, <m>F(s) = \lap{ f(t) }</m>, so 
								<me>
									F(s) = \lap{ \cos(t) } = \frac{s}{s^2 + 1}
								</me>
								and the quotient rule gives us
								<me>
									F'(s) = \frac{(s^2 + 1)(1) - s(2s)}{(s^2 + 1)^2} = \frac{1 - s^2}{(s^2 + 1)^2}
								</me>
							</p>
							<p>
								Therefore,
								<me>
									\lap{t \cos(t)} = -\frac{1 - s^2}{(s^2 + 1)^2} = \frac{s^2 - 1}{(s^2 + 1)^2}, \quad s \gt 0
								</me>.
							</p>
						</solution>

						<solution>
							<p>
								In this case, <m>n=2</m> and <m>f(t) = \sin(-5t)</m>, so 
								<me>
									\laplacesym \Big\{ t^2 \ \us{\large f(t)}{\ul{\sin(-5t)}} \Big\} = (-1)^2 F''(s) = F''(s), \quad s \gt 0
								</me>
							</p>

							<p>
								By definition, <m>F(s) = \lap{ f(t) }</m>, so 
								<me>
									F(s) = \lap{ \sin(-5t) } = \frac{-5}{s^2 + 25} = -5(s^2 + 25)^{-1}
								</me>
								and two derivatives of this function gives us
								<md>
									<mrow>	F'(s)	\amp = \frac{d}{ds}\left[-5(s^2 + 25)^{-1}\right]				</mrow>
									<mrow>			\amp = -5\left(-1\right)(s^2 + 25)^{-2}(2s)						</mrow>
									<mrow>			\amp = 10s(s^2 + 25)^{-2}										</mrow>
									<mrow>	F''(s)	\amp = \frac{d}{ds}\left[10s(s^2 + 25)^{-2}\right]				</mrow>
									<mrow>			\amp = 10(s^2 + 25)^{-2} + 10s(-2)(s^2 + 25)^{-3}(2s)			</mrow>
									<mrow>			\amp = \frac{10}{(s^2 + 25)^{2}} - \frac{40s^2}{(s^2 + 25)^{3}}	</mrow>
								</md>
							</p>
							<p>
								Therefore,
								<me>
									\lap{t^2 \sin(-5t)} = \frac{10}{(s^2 + 25)^{2}} - \frac{40s^2}{(s^2 + 25)^{3}}, \quad s \gt 0
								</me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</example>
			
				<p>
					This shows that multiplying a function by <m>t</m> inside a Laplace transform is equivalent to taking the derivative of the Laplace transform of the same function, multiplied by <m>-1</m>. It turns out that each additional power of <m>t</m> adds another negative sign and derivative.
				</p>

			</subsubsection>

		</subsection>

	<section><title> The Laplace Domain </title>

		<introduction>

			<p>
				The heart of the Laplace transform method lies in its ability to convert a differential equation into an algebraic equation. This transformation takes place in what we call the <em>Laplace domain</em>, a mathematical space where derivatives become algebraic expressions and initial conditions are naturally incorporated into the equation.
			</p>

			<p>
				In this section, we will break down how to solve the model equation
				<men xml:id="lt-model-eqn-w-ics"> 
					y'' - 9y = 10e^{2t}, \quad y(0) = 1, \quad y'(0) = -7
				</men>
				using the steps outlined in the <xref ref="lt-roadmap" text="custom">Laplace Transform Roadmap</xref>:
			</p>
				
			<ol marker="(i)">
				<li>
					<p>
						We begin with <xref ref="lt-roadmap-step-1" text="custom">step 1</xref>, where the forward transform moves the differential equation into the Laplace domain.
					</p>
				</li>
				<li>
					<p>
						Then, we jump to <xref ref="lt-roadmap-step-4" text="custom">step 4</xref> to explore how the transformed equation is converted back into the original domain using a backward transform.
					</p>
				</li>
				<li>
					<p>
						Finally, we examine <xref ref="lt-roadmap-step-2-3" text="custom">steps 2 and 3</xref>, which take place entirely within the Laplace domain and lead to the solution of the original equation.
					</p>
				</li>
			</ol>
			
		</introduction>

		<subsection><title> Into the Laplace Domain (Forward Transform) </title>

			<introduction>
				<p>
					According to <xref ref="lt-roadmap" text="custom">step 1 of the roadmap</xref>, moving into the Laplace domain means applying the Laplace transform to a differential equation to convert it into an algebraic equation we will refer to as the <q>Laplace equation</q>. This transformation is a key step in the Laplace transform method, as it allows us to work with algebraic equations instead of differential equations. In particular,
				</p>

				<assemblage xml:id="lt-roadmap-step-1"><title> Step 1 - Into the Laplace Domain (Forward Transform) </title>
					<p>
						<sidebyside widths="39% 14% 47%" valign="top">
							<p>
								<md>
									<mrow>	\vphantom{m}													</mrow>
									<mrow>	y'' - 9y = 10e^{2t}				</mrow>
									<mrow>	\ub{y(0) = 1, \quad y(0) = -7}	</mrow>
								</md>
							</p>
							<p>
								<me>
									\os{\Large\text{1️⃣}}{\us{\large\text{forward}}{\os{\large\laplacesym}{\longrightarrow}}}
								</me>
							</p>
							<p>
								<md>
									<mrow>	\vphantom{M}													</mrow>
									<mrow>	\ub{s^2Y - 9Y - s + 7 = \frac{10}{s - 2}}	</mrow>
								</md>
								<me>
									
								</me>
							</p>
						</sidebyside>
						<sidebyside widths="39% 14% 47%" valign="middle">
							<p>
								<me>
									\text{Differential Equation}
								</me>
							</p>
							<p/>
							<p>
								<me>
									\text{Laplace Equation}
								</me>
							</p>
						</sidebyside>
					</p>
				</assemblage>

				<p>
					Although using the table of Laplace transforms makes this step straightforward, understanding what's happening behind the scenes is crucial.
				</p>
			</introduction>

			<p>
				Recall that applying the Laplace transform to an equation is equivalent to multiplying the equation by <m>e^{-st}</m> and integrating with respect to <m>t</m>.
				<me>
					\ub{\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} (y'') dt}_{\large\lap{y''}} 
					- \ub{\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} (9y) dt}_{\large\lap{9y}}
					= \ub{\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \left( 10e^{2t} \right) dt}_{\large\lap{10e^{2t}}}
				</me>
			</p>

			<p>
				Applying integration by parts twice to the first integral, and evaluating the improper integral on the right-hand side, gives us
			</p>

			<me>
				-sy(0) - y'(0) + s^2\ub{\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} (y) dt}_{\large\lap{y} = Y} 
				- 9\ub{\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} (y) dt}_{\large\lap{y} = Y}
				= \frac{10}{s - 2}
			</me>

			<p>
				To simplify further, we set <m>\lap{y} = Y</m>. We can also plug in the initial conditions <m>y(0) = 1</m> and <m>y'(0) = -7</m>, giving us
			</p>
			
			<me>
				s^2Y - 9Y - s + 7 = \frac{10}{s - 2}
			</me>.

			<p>
				Notice how we've turned the problem into an algebraic equation involving the variable <m>Y</m>. Solving for <m>Y</m> actually means solving for the original unknown <m>y</m>, which is neatly encapsulated inside <m>Y</m>!
			</p>

			<example><title> Forward Transforming Equations </title>
			
			
				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Transform the initial-value problems into the Laplace domain:
							<ol>
								<li>
									<p>
										<m>\ds y'' - 9y = 10e^{2t}, \quad y(0) = 1, \quad y(0) = -7.</m>
									</p>
								</li>
								<li>
									<p>
										<m>\ds y'' - 4y' + 6y = t^2, \quad y(0) = 2, \quad y'(0) = 0.</m>
									</p>
								</li>
							</ol>
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>

				<p>
					<solution>
						<p>
							First, we apply <m>\laplacesym</m> to both sides:
							<me>
								\lap{y'' - 9y} = \lap{10e^{2t}}
							</me>.
						</p>

						<p>
							Using the <xref ref="lt-linearity-property" text="custom">linearity property</xref>, we break this into four transforms
							<me>
								\ub{\lap{y''}}_{\large L_{10}} 
								+ 3\ub{\lap{y'}}_{\large L_9} 
								+ 2\ub{\lap{y}}_{\large Y} = -40\ub{\lap{e^{3t}}}_{\large L_2}
							</me>,
							and using <xref ref="L10" text="title"/>, <xref ref="L9" text="title"/>, and <xref ref="L2" text="title"/> from the <xref ref="lt-table-L1-12" text="custom">Laplace transform table</xref>, we transform the equation as follows:
						</p>

						<p>
							<me>
								s^2Y - s\os{\large 0}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y(0)}} - \os{\large 1}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y'(0)}} 
								+ 3s\left(sY - y(0)\right) + 2Y = -40\frac{1}{s - 3}
							</me>,
							or
							<me>
								s^2Y - 9Y - s + 7 = \frac{10}{s - 2}.
							</me>
						</p>
					</solution>

					<solution>
						<p>
							Applying the Laplace transform to both sides and using the Laplace transform table, we have:
							<md>
								<mrow>
									\lap{y'' - 4y' + 6y} 
									\amp = \lap{t^2}
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\lap{y''} - 4\lap{y'} + 6\lap{y} 
									\amp = \lap{t^2}
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									s^2Y - s\os{\large 2}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y(0)}} 
									- \os{\large 0}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y'(0)}} 
									- 4(sY - \os{\large 2}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y(0)}}) + 6Y 
									\amp = \frac{2}{s^3}
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									s^2Y - 2s - 4sY + 8 + 6Y 
									\amp = \frac{2}{s^3}
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									s^2Y - 4sY + 6Y - 2s + 8 
									\amp = \frac{2}{s^3}
								</mrow>.
							</md>
						</p>
					</solution>
				</p>
				
			</example>
			
		</subsection>

		<subsection><title> Out of the Laplace Domain (Backward Transform) </title>

			<introduction>

				<p>
					Just as the forward Laplace transform converts a differential equation into an algebraic one, the backward transform completes the cycle by converting the algebraic solution back into its original form. Its job is to recover the solution to our original differential equation from the solution we found in the Laplace domain. This is the <xref ref="lt-roadmap" text="custom">final step of the roadmap</xref> and it involves applying the <q>inverse Laplace transform</q>, <m>\laplacesym^{-1}</m>, to the Laplace solution.
				</p>

			</introduction>

			<subsubsection><title> Using the Table Laplace Transforms for Inverses </title>

				<p>
					Not only does the table of transforms quickly find forward transformations, but it conveniently provides the corresponding backward transforms as well. The difference is that you must match the <m>F(s)</m>-function, on the right, to get the inverse <m>f(t)</m>-function, on the left. For example, <xref ref="lt-table-row-L2" text="title"/> gives us both the forward and backward transforms:
				</p>

				<p>
					<sidebyside widths="10% 40% 10% 40%" valign="top">
						<p>
							Foward transform:
						</p>
						<p>
							<me>
								\lap{ e^{at} } = \frac{1}{s - a}
							</me>
						</p>
						<p>
							Backward transform:
						</p>
						<p>
							<me>
								\ilap{ \frac{1}{s - a} } = e^{at}
							</me>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
				</p>

				<p>
					Using the table in this way is precisely how you will leave the Laplace domain, completing step 4. Once you've prepared the Laplace solution, <m>Y(s)</m> (step 3), finding its inverse becomes a matter of just looking it up in the table. Let's see a few examples to get some practice with this. 
				</p>

				<example><title> Inverses using the Laplace Transform Table </title>
					<p>
						<statement>
							<p>
								Apply a backward transform (step 4) to revert each Laplace solution back into a function of <m>t</m>.
							</p>
							<p>
							<sidebyside widths="35% 65%">
								<p>
									<me>
										Y(s) = \frac{3}{s^2 + 9}
									</me>
								</p>
								<p>
									<solution>
										<p>
											This matches <xref ref="lt-table-row-L4" text="title"/> with <m>b=3</m>. So,
											<md>
												<mrow>	\ilap{Y(s)}	\amp = \ilap{\frac{3}{s^2 + 9}}	</mrow>
												<mrow>	y(t)		\amp = \sin(3t)					</mrow>
											</md>.
										</p>
									</solution>
								</p>
							</sidebyside>
							</p>
							<p>
							<sidebyside widths="35% 65%">
								<p>
									<me>
										Q(s) = \frac{s - 2}{(s - 2)^2 + 16}
									</me>
								</p>
								<p>
									<solution>
										<p>
											This matches <xref ref="lt-table-row-L7" text="title"/> with <m>a=2</m> and <m>b=4</m>. So,
											<md>
												<mrow>	\ilap{Q(s)}	\amp = \ilap{\frac{s - 2}{(s - 2)^2 + 16}}	</mrow>
												<mrow>	q(t)		\amp = e^{2t}\cos(4t)						</mrow>
											</md>.
										</p>
									</solution>
								</p>
							</sidebyside>
							</p>
							<p>
							<sidebyside widths="35% 65%">
								<p>
									<me>
										D(s) = \frac{24}{s^5}
									</me>
								</p>
								<p>
									<solution>
										<p>
											Noting that <m>24=4!</m> and using <xref ref="lt-table-row-L3" text="title"/> with <m>n=4</m>, we get
											<md>
												<mrow>	\ilap{D(s)}	\amp = \ilap{\frac{4!}{s^5}}	</mrow>
												<mrow>	d(t)		\amp = t^4						</mrow>.
											</md>.
										</p>
									</solution>
								</p>
							</sidebyside>
							</p>
						</statement>
					</p>
				</example>

				<p>
					These examples demonstrate how to find the inverse Laplace transform when the Laplace solution perfectly matches one of the forms in the transforms table. In practice, however, additional adjustments are needed to match the forms.
				</p>

				

			</subsubsection>

			<subsubsection><title> Linearity of <m>\laplacesym^{-1}</m> </title>
				
				<p>
					In general, the Laplace solution that you need to invert will be composed of multiple terms. Fortunately, the inverse Laplace transform is linear, which means we have the following property:
				</p>

				<assemblage xml:id="lt-inverse-linearity"><title> Linearity of the Inverse Laplace Transform </title>
					<p>
						Suppose <m>f(s)</m> and <m>g(s)</m> are functions in the Laplace domain, and <m>a</m> and <m>b</m> are constants. Then
						<me>
							\ilap{ af(s) \pm bg(s) } = a\ilap{ f(s) } + b\ilap{ g(s) }
						</me>
					</p>
				</assemblage>

				<p>
					Let's walk through an example to see how linearity helps with backward transformations.
				</p>

				<example><title> Inverse Transforms using Linearity </title>
					<p>
						<statement>
							<p>
								Apply a backward transform (step 4) to revert each Laplace solution back into a function of <m>t</m>.
								<ol>
									<li>
										<p>
											<m>\ds Y(s) = \frac{12}{s} + \frac{7s}{s^2 + 25} </m>
										</p>
									</li>
									<li>
										<p>
											<m>\ds Y(s) = \frac{4}{(s - 2)^2 + 16} + \frac{2}{(s+7)^5}</m>
										</p>
									</li>
								</ol>
							</p>
						</statement>
					</p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								The <m>s</m> in the denominator tells us that we need <m>L_1</m>. Before we do, let's factor out <m>12</m>:
								<me>\ds \ilap{\frac{12}{s}} = 12 \cdot \ilap{\frac{1}{s}} = 12 \cdot 1 = 12</me>.
							</p>
							<p>
								<me><xref ref="L5" text="custom">L_5</xref>, b=5</me>
								<me> \ilap{\frac{7s}{s^2 + 25}} = 7\ \ilap{\frac{s}{s^2 + 25}} = 7\ \cos(5t)</me>
							</p>
						</solution>

						<solution>
							<p>
								The form of this denominator is <m>\ul{(s \pm \text{shift})^{2} + \text{number}}</m> and has no <m>s</m> in the numerator. Therefore,
								<md>
									<mrow> \ilap{\frac{10}{(s - 3)^2 + 11}} \amp\ \quad <xref ref="L7" text="custom"><m>L_7</m></xref> (a=3,\ b=\sqrt{11})</mrow>
									<mrow>	\amp = 10\ \ilap{\frac{\sqrt{11}}{\sqrt{11}}\frac{1}{(s - 3)^2 + 11}}</mrow>
									<mrow>	\amp = \frac{10}{\sqrt{11}} \ilap{\frac{\sqrt{11}}{(s - 3)^2 + 11}}</mrow>
									<mrow>	\amp = \frac{10}{\sqrt{11}} e^{3t} \sin(\sqrt{11}t)</mrow>
								</md>

							</p>
							<p>
								The denominator, <m>\ul{(s \pm \text{shift})^{\text{power}}}</m>, indicates an <m>e^{at}</m> and a <m>t^n</m> term. Therefore,
								<md>
									<mrow> \ilap{\frac{2}{(s+7)^5}} \amp\ \quad <xref ref="L6" text="custom"><m>L_6</m></xref> (a=-7, n=4)</mrow>
									<mrow>
											\amp = 2\ \ilap{\frac{4!}{4!}\frac{1}{(s+7)^5}}
									</mrow>
									<mrow>
											\amp = \frac{2}{4!} \ilap{\frac{4!}{(s+7)^5}}
									</mrow>
									<mrow>
										\amp = \frac{1}{12} t^4 e^{-7t}
								</mrow>
								</md>
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</example>
			
			</subsubsection>

			<subsubsection><title> Minor Adjustments for a Perfect Match </title>
				
				<p>
					A key indicator of how close a Laplace solution matches with a form in the tranform table, is its denominator. If the denominators make a perfect match, then typically a only a small adjustment is required to get a full match. In these cases, a perfect match can be attained by either providing a missing constant or <q>splitting the fraction</q>. 
				</p>

				<p>
					For example, suppose we have the Laplace solution
				</p>

				<me>
					Y(s) = \frac{2s + 3}{s^2 + 4}
				</me>.
				
				<p>
					Notice that the denominator, <m>s^2 + 4</m>, matches the form in either <xref ref="lt-table-row-L4" text="title"/> or <xref ref="lt-table-row-L5" text="title"/>. However, the numerator is a sum of two terms. To match the form in the table, we need to split the fraction into two parts, like so
				</p>

				<me>
					Y(s) = \frac{2s}{s^2 + 4} + \frac{3}{s^2 + 4}
				</me>.

				<p>
					Separating the constants in the numerators, we have
				</p>

				<me>
					Y(s) 
					= 2\ub{\frac{s}{s^2 + 4}}_{\large<xref ref="lt-table-row-L5" text="custom">L_5</xref>\text{ perfect}} 
					+ 3\ub{\frac{1}{s^2 + 4}}_{\large<xref ref="lt-table-row-L4" text="custom">L_4</xref>\text{ close}}
				</me>,

				<p>
					where the first term matches with <xref ref="lt-table-row-L5" text="title"/> (<m>b=2</m>), but the second term needs a <m>2</m> in the numerator to match with <xref ref="lt-table-row-L4" text="title"/>. To resolve this, we multiply the second term by <m>\sfrac{2}{2}</m>, giving us
				</p>

				<aside><title> Multiplying by <m>\sfrac{2}{2}</m> </title>
					<p>
						Multiplying by <m>\sfrac{2}{2}</m> is equivalent to multiplying by <m>1</m>, which doesn't change the value of the term. This is a common technique used to adjust fractions without altering their overall value.
					</p>
				</aside>

				<md>
					<mrow>	
						Y(s) \amp = 2\frac{s}{s^2 + 4} + 3\frac{1}{s^2 + 4} \cdot \frac{2}{2}
					</mrow>
					<mrow>	
						Y(s)	
						\amp = 2\ub{\frac{s}{s^2 + 4}}_{\large<xref ref="lt-table-row-L5" text="title"/>\text{ perfect}} 
						+ \frac{3}{2}\ub{\frac{2}{s^2 + 4}}_{\large<xref ref="lt-table-row-L4" text="title"/>\text{ perfect}}
					</mrow>
				</md>.

				<p>
					Now that we have perfect matches, the backward transform is straightforward:
					<md>
						<mrow>	
							\ilap{Y(s)} \amp = 2\ilap{\frac{s}{s^2 + 4}} + \frac{3}{2}\ilap{\frac{2}{s^2 + 4}}
						</mrow>
						<mrow>	
							y(t) \amp = 2\cos(2t) + \frac{3}{2}\sin(2t)
						</mrow>
					</md>.
				</p>

				<p>
					These adjustments are critical steps for matching Laplace solutions to the transform table and will be used frequently in the examples that follow. The key is to recognize the forms in the denominator and make the necessary adjustments to the numerator. Here are additional examples to illustrate this process.
				</p>
				
				<example><title> Minor Adjustments of Laplace Solutions </title>
					<p>
						<statement>
							<p>
								Apply a backward transform (step 4) to revert the Laplace solution back into a function of <m>t</m>.
							</p>
							<p>
								<me>
									Y(s) = \frac{s^6 - 7s^3}{s^7}
								</me>
							</p>
						</statement>
					</p>

					<solution>
						<p>
							Since the denominator of <m>Y</m> already matches the form of the denominator in <xref ref="lt-table-row-L3" text="title"/>, we split the fraction and simplify:
							
							<me>
								Y(s) = \frac{s^6}{s^7} - \frac{7s^3}{s^7} = \frac{1}{s} - 7\frac{1}{s^4}
							</me>.
						</p>

						<p>
							The first term matches <xref ref="lt-table-row-L1" text="title"/> and the second term needs <m>3!</m> in the numerator to match <xref ref="lt-table-row-L3" text="title"/>. So, updating the second term gives us
							<me>
								Y(s) = \frac{1}{s} - \frac{7}{3!}\frac{3!}{s^4}
							</me>.
						</p>
						<p>
							Now it is ready for the backward transform:
							<md>
								<mrow>	\ilap{Y(s)}	\amp = \ilap{\frac{1}{s}} - \frac{7}{3!}\ilap{\frac{3!}{s^4}}	</mrow>
								<mrow>	y(t)		\amp = 1 - \frac{7}{3!}t^3										</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
					</solution>
				</example>

				<p>
					There is one other adjustment that is required when matching a Laplace solution that has a denominator like <xref ref="lt-table-row-L8" text="title"/>. Specifically, the form
				</p>

				<me>
					Y(s) = \frac{s + c}{(s-a)^2 + b^2}
				</me>

				<p>
					requires an <m>s-a</m> in the numerator to match with <xref ref="lt-table-row-L8" text="title"/>. In this situation, we move <m>c</m> over to make room for <m>-a + a</m> in the numerator, giving us
				</p>

				<me>
					Y(s) = \frac{s - a + a + c}{(s-a)^2 + b^2}
				</me>.

				<aside><title> Adding and Subtracting <m>a</m> </title>
					<p>
						Adding and subtracting the numerator by the same number doesn't change its value, but it is useful for introducing <m>s-a</m> into it.
					</p>
					<p/>
				</aside>

				<p>
					Once in this form we splitting the fraction,
				</p>

				<me>
					Y(s) = \frac{s-a}{(s-a)^2 + b^2} + \frac{a + c}{(s-a)^2 + b^2}
				</me>

				<p>
					and multiply the second term by <m>\sfrac{b}{b}</m> to get
				</p>

				<me>
					Y(s) 
					= \ub{\frac{s-a}{(s-a)^2 + b^2}}_{\large\text{matches } <xref ref="lt-table-row-L8" text="custom">L_8</xref>} 
					+ \frac{a+c}{b}\ub{\frac{b}{(s-a)^2 + b^2}}_{\large\text{matches } <xref ref="lt-table-row-L7" text="custom">L_7</xref>}
				</me>.

				<p>
					Now we have a form ready for the inverse Laplace transform:
				</p>

				<me>
					y(t) = \cos(bt) + \frac{a+c}{b}\sin(bt)
				</me>.

				<example><title> Minor Adjustments of Laplace Solutions </title>
					<p>
						<statement>
							<p>
								Apply a backward transform (step 4) to revert each Laplace solution back into a function of <m>t</m>.
								<ol>
									<li>
										<p>
											<m>\ds Y(s) = \frac{s - 5}{(s+4)^2 + 6}</m>
										</p>
									</li>
									<li>
										<p>
											<m>\ds Y(s) = \frac{3s + 9}{(s-2)^2 + 16}</m>
										</p>
									</li>
								</ol>
							</p>
						</statement>
					</p>
					
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								Noticing that the first term needs <m>s+4</m> in the numerator, we add and subtract <m>4</m> to the numerator, then split the fraction:
							</p>
							<md>
								<mrow>	Y(s)	\amp = \frac{s + 4 - 4 - 5}{(s+4)^2 + 6}	</mrow>
								<mrow>	\amp = \frac{s + 4}{(s+4)^2 + 6} - 9\frac{1}{(s+4)^2 + 6}	</mrow>
							</md>
							<p>
								The first term matches with <xref ref="lt-table-row-L8" text="title"/>, but the second term needs a <m>\sqrt{6}</m> in the numerator:
							</p>

							<me>
								Y(s)	
								= \ub{\frac{s + 4}{(s+4)^2 + 6}}_{\large\text{matches } <xref ref="lt-table-row-L8" text="custom">L_8</xref>} 
								- \ub{\frac{9}{\sqrt{6}}\frac{\sqrt{6}}{(s+4)^2 + 6}}_{\large\text{matches } <xref ref="lt-table-row-L7" text="custom">L_7</xref>}
							</me>
							<p>
								Now we have a form ready for the inverse Laplace transform:
							</p>
							<md>
								<mrow>	y(t)	\amp = e^{-4t}\cos(\sqrt{6}t) - \frac{9}{\sqrt{6}}e^{-4t}\sin(\sqrt{6}t)	</mrow>
							</md>.
						</solution>

						<solution>
							<p>
								The first term needs <m>s-2</m> in the numerator, but before adding and subtracting <m>2</m>, lets get <m>3</m> out of the way by factoring it out: 
							</p>

							<md>
								<mrow>	Y(s) = 3\frac{s + 3}{(s-2)^2 + 16}	\amp = 3\left(\frac{s - 2 + 2 + 3}{(s-2)^2 + 16}\right)	</mrow>
							</md>.

							<p>
								Now, splitting the fraction inside the parentheses and distributing the <m>3</m> gives us
							</p>

							<md>
								<mrow>	
									Y(s)
									\amp = 3\left(\frac{s - 2}{(s-2)^2 + 16} + \frac{5}{(s-2)^2 + 16}\right)
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = 3\frac{s - 2}{(s-2)^2 + 16} + 15\frac{1}{(s-2)^2 + 16}
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = 3\ub{\frac{s - 2}{(s-2)^2 + 16}}_{\large\text{matches } <xref ref="lt-table-row-L8" text="title"/>}
									+ \frac{15}{4}\ub{\frac{4}{(s-2)^2 + 16}}_{\large\text{matches } <xref ref="lt-table-row-L7" text="title"/>}
								</mrow>
							</md>.

							<p>
								Finally, the backward transform is given by
							</p>

							<md>
								<mrow>	y(t)	\amp = 3e^{2t}\cos(4t) + \frac{15}{4}e^{2t}\sin(4t)	</mrow>
							</md>.
						</solution>
					</p>
						
				</example>
			
			</subsubsection>

		</subsection>

		<subsection><title> Within the Laplace Domain </title>

			<introduction>
				<p>
					Once we've transformed our equation into the Laplace domain, we've effectively converted a differential equation problem into an algebra problem. Our original differential equation in <m>t</m> has become a "Laplace Equation" in <m>s</m>, and our unknown function <m>y(t)</m> has become <m>Y(s)</m>. Now comes the next challenge: solving this new equation and preparing it for the trip back out of the Laplace domain.
				</p>

				<figure xml:id="lt-roadmap-steps-2-3">
					<caption>
						Once inside the Laplace domain, solve the Laplace equation for <m>Y</m> (step 2), then prepare the Laplace solution for a backward transform (step 3).
					</caption>
					<image source="figures/LT-Roadmap-steps-2-3.png" width="100%"/>
				</figure>

				<p>
					This subsection focuses on two closely connected steps in the Laplace Transform Method:
				</p>

				<ol>
					<li>
						<term>Step 2: Solving the Laplace Equation.</term> Here, we isolate <m>Y</m> by treating the transformed equation like any other algebraic expression. Since derivatives have already been transformed, we're simply solving for the Laplace solution (a function of <m>s</m>).
					</li>
					<li>
						<term>Step 3: Preparing the Laplace Solution.</term> This is where the heart of the Laplace method lies. Even after solving for <m>Y</m>, we're not done. To apply the backward Laplace transform (Step 4), we must rewrite <m>Y</m> into a form that matches entries in the Laplace table. This step is often the most challenging part of the entire process.
					</li>
				</ol>

				<p>
					You can think of Step 2 as getting an answer, and Step 3 as breaking the answer down into simpler pieces—ones that we can recognize in the table. That's where techniques like partial fraction decomposition, completing the square, and clever algebraic manipulation come into play.
				</p>

			</introduction>

			<subsubsection><title> Laplace Domain Guiding Principle </title>

				<p>
					After step 2, the Laplace solution will be a rational function of the form
					<me>
						Y(s) = \frac{P(s)}{Q(s)}
					</me>
					where <m>P(s)</m> and <m>Q(s)</m> are polynomials. The job of step 3 is to prepare <m>Y(s)</m> for a backward transform. This means rewriting <m>Y(s)</m> into a sum of simpler functions resembling the forms in the right column of the Laplace transform table. 
				</p>
					
				<p>
					Fortunately, rational functions have a property that ensures they can be rewritten as the sum of the following types of terms 
					<men xml:id="lt-pfd-forms">
						\frac{k}{s-a},\quad\frac{k}{(s-a)^n},\quad\frac{k}{as^2 + bs + c},\quad\frac{ks}{as^2 + bs + c}
					</men>
					where <m>a, b, c,</m> &amp; <m>k</m> are constants.	The process by which this is done is called <term>partial fraction decomposition (PFD)</term>. Therefore, following any partial fraction decomposition, we only need to match these types of terms in the transform table. Since the first two forms are already in the table, all possible cases will be covered if know how to handle the last two forms with quadratic denominators.
				</p>

				<p>
					So, if the denominator of <m>Y(s)</m> is a polynomial of degree 3 or higher, use partial fraction decomposition to rewrite it as a sum of simpler terms like <xref ref="lt-pfd-forms"/>. If the denominator is a polynomial of degree 2, then there are a few cases to consider and is the topic for the next discussion.
				</p>

			</subsubsection>

			<subsubsection><title> Preparing Terms with Quadratic Denominators </title>

				<p>
					Suppose you head into step 3 with a Laplace solution containing a term like
					<me>
						\frac{*}{as^2 + bs + c}
					</me>
					where <m>a, b, c</m> are constants and the numerator, <m>*</m>, is irrelevant. We also assume that the denominator has a <m>bs</m> term. Since if it didn't, we could proceed as we did in the previous section. The way we prepare this term for the backward transform is based on the discriminant of the denominator, as follows:
					<ol>
						<li>
							<p>
								If <m>\Delta = b^2 - 4ac \ge 0</m>, then we factor the denominator and use partial fraction decomposition to write the term in the form
								<me>
									\frac{A}{s-r_1} + \frac{B}{s-r_2}
								</me>
							</p>
						</li>
						<li>
							<p>
								If <m>\Delta = b^2 - 4ac \lt 0</m>, then we complete the square of the denominator to write the term in the form
								<me>
									\frac{*}{(s-A)^2 + B^2}
								</me>
								where <m>A</m> and <m>B</m> are constants from the process of completing the square.
							</p>
						</li>
					</ol>
				</p>

				<p>
					In either case, the term is in a form that can be inverted using the process discussed in the previous section and we now have all the tools we need to prepare the Laplace solution for a backward transform. Now we need to put our strategies into practice by considering a number of examples.
				</p>

				<example><title> Preparing the Laplace Solution </title>

					<p>
						<statement>
							<p>
								Prepare the Laplace solution for a backward transform (step 3), then apply the inverse transform to convert it back into a function of <m>t</m> (step 4).
							</p>
							<ol>
								<li>
									<p>
										<m>\ds Y(s) = \frac{2s + 5}{s^2 + 5s + 4}</m>
									</p>
									<solution>
										<p>
											Since the denominator is quadratic and 
											<me>
												\Delta = b^2 - 4ac = 5^2-4(1)(4) = 9 \gt 0
											</me>,
											then we proceed with partial fraction decomposition. Since the denominator can be factored as <m>(s+1)(s+4)</m>,  we know this rational function can be written as
											<me>
												\frac{2s+5}{(s+1)(s+4)} = \frac{A}{s+1} + \frac{B}{s+4}
											</me>
										</p>

										<p>
											To find <m>A</m> and <m>B</m>, we multiply both sides by the common denominator, <m>(s+1)(s+4)</m>, eliminating all the fractions and leaving us with
											<me>
												2s + 5 = A(s + 4) + B(s + 1)
											</me>.
										</p>

										<p>
											Strategically selecting <m>s</m> as <m>-1</m> and <m>-4</m> leads directly to <m>A</m> and <m>B</m>:
										</p>

										<sidebyside widths="16% 70% 14%">
											<p>

											</p>
											<p>

											</p>
											<p>

										</sidebyside>

											This completes step 3 since the Laplace solution can now be written as
											<me>
												Y(s) = \frac{1}{s + 1} + \frac{1}{s + 4}
											</me>
											and is prepared for the backward transform (step 4):
											<md>
												<mrow>	\ilap{Y(s)}	\amp = \ilap{\frac{1}{s + 1}} + \ilap{\frac{1}{s + 4}}	</mrow>
											</md>,
											resulting in the solution <m>y(t) = e^{-t} + e^{-4t}</m>.
										</p>
									</solution>
								</li>

								<li>
									<p>
										<m>\ds H(s) = \frac{s-1}{s^2 - 2s + 5}</m>
									</p>
									<solution>

										<p>

											Since the denominator is quadratic and 
											<me>

											</me>,
											which is a perfect match with <xref ref="L8" text="title"/> and we can move on to step 4.
										</p>

										<p>
											Applying the inverse Laplace transform to <m>H(s)</m> gives us the solution
											<me>
												h(t) = e^{t}\cos(2t)
											</me>.
										</p>
									</solution>
								</li>

								<li>
									<p>

									</p>
									<solution>
										<p>
											Completing the square for the denominator of <m>P(s)</m> gives:

											<md>
												<mrow>

												<mrow>

											<md>
												<mrow>

											</md>
										</p>
									</solution>
								</li>

								<li>

											<me>\frac{3s + 4}{(s - 1)^2} = \frac{A}{s - 1} + \frac{B}{(s - 1)^2}.</me>
											Multiply both sides by <m>(s - 1)^2</m>,
											<me>3s + 4 = A(s - 1) + B.</me>
											and solve for <m>A</m> and <m>B</m> by selecting convenient values for <m>s</m>:
											<sidebyside widths="8% 80%">

														<mrow>s = 1: \amp</mrow>
														<mrow></mrow>

														<mrow></mrow>
													</md>
												</p>
												<p>

											<me>\frac{3}{s - 1} + \frac{7}{(s - 1)^2}.</me>

										</p>
									</solution>
								</li>

									<p>
										<m>\ds U(s) = \frac{2s^2 + 5s + 1}{s^3 - s}</m>
									</p>
									<solution>
										<p>
											Since the degree of the denominator is greater than <m>2</m>, we use PFD and we factor the denominator as
											<me>
												s^3 - s = s(s - 1)(s + 1)
											</me>
											so that <m>U(s)</m> has the form
											<me>
												U(s) = \frac{2s^2 + 5s + 1}{s(s - 1)(s + 1)} = \frac{A}{s} + \frac{B}{s - 1} + \frac{C}{s + 1}
											</me>.
										</p>

										<p>
											Multiply both sides by <m>s(s - 1)(s + 1)</m> to get
											<me>
												2s^2 + 5s + 1 = A(s - 1)(s + 1) + B(s)(s + 1) + C(s)(s - 1)

											and choose convenient values for <m>s</m> so that
										</p>

													<mrow>					</mrow>

													<mrow>					</mrow>

										</p>

	<section><title> Summary &amp; Examples </title>

		<introduction>

			<p>
				The Laplace Transform Method provides a powerful toolset for solving initial-value differential equations, transforming complex calculus problems into simpler algebraic ones. In the previous sections, we explored how to compute the forward Laplace transform of a function and how to apply the inverse Laplace transform to return to the time domain. These two operations are the fundamental components of the Laplace Transform Method.
			</p>

			<p>
				This section brings together the forward and backward transformations to solve entire initial-value differential equations. By applying the forward Laplace transform to both sides of a differential equation, we convert the problem into an algebraic equation in the <m>s</m>-domain. The algebraic equation is typically easier to solve, and once we find the solution in the <m>s</m>-domain, we apply the inverse Laplace transform to recover the solution in the time domain.
			</p>

			<p>
				We will begin with simple examples and progressively tackle more complex problems, incorporating the initial conditions of the differential equations directly into the transformed equations. By mastering these techniques, you will be able to solve a wide range of differential equations using the Laplace Transform Method.
			</p>
			
		</introduction>

		<p>
			At this point, the <xref ref="lt-roadmap" text="custom">roadmap</xref> introduced at the beginning of this chapter should start to come together. The steps have been explored in previous sections, and now it is time to apply them in sequence to solve a complete initial-value problem. We start by applying the forward Laplace transform to the entire differential equation (Step 1), which temporarily encases the unknown inside <m>Y(s)</m>. Next, you use algebra to isolate <m>Y(s)</m> (Step 2a). After that, you prepare for the backward transform by breaking <m>Y(s)</m> into a sum of functions that correspond to known Laplace transforms (Step 2b). Finally, you apply the backward transform to <m>Y(s)</m> to recover the solution, <m>y(t)</m> (Step 3).
		</p>
		
		<p>
			The complete strategy for this method is outlined as follows:
		</p>

		<exploration xml:id="lt-method"><title> Laplace Transform Method </title>
			<p>
				The particular solution to the initial-valued differential equation
				<men xml:id="lt-method-de">
					y^{(n)} + a_{n-1}\, y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_1\, y' + a_0\, y = g(t)
				</men>
				and given initial conditions, follows from these steps:
			
				<dl>
					<li xml:id="lt-step-1"><title> Step 1: Into the Laplace Domain (Forward) </title>
						<p>
							Apply the Forward Laplace transform, <m>\laplacesym</m>, to both sides of <xref ref="lt-method-de" />. Use the <xref ref="lt-table-L1-12" text="custom">transform table</xref> to convert each term into a function of <m>s</m>.
						</p>
					</li>
					<li xml:id="lt-step-2"><title> Step 2: Find the Laplace Solution </title>
						<p>
							Use algebra to isolate the Laplace solution, <m>Y(s)</m>.
						</p>
					</li>
					<li xml:id="lt-step-3"><title> Step 3: Prepare the Laplace Solution for a Backward Transform </title>
						<p>
							Using PFD, rewrite the Laplace solution, <m>Y(s)</m>, as the sum of terms resembling the <m>s</m>-functions in the <xref ref="lt-table-L1-12" text="custom">transforms table</xref>. Terms with irreducible denominators require completing the square.
							<fn><title>Irreducible</title>
								<p>
									The quadratic denominator of the term
									<me>
										\frac{*}{as^2 + bs + c}
									</me>
									is irreducible if the discriminant, <m> \Delta = b^2 - 4ac </m>, is negative.
								</p>
							</fn>
								
						</p>
					</li>
					<li xml:id="lt-step-4"><title> Step 4: Out of the Laplace Domain (Backward) </title>
						<p>
							Recover the solution, <m>y(t)</m>, by applying a backward (inverse) Laplace transform, <m>\laplacesym^{-1}</m>, to the prepared Laplace solution from step 3.
						</p>
					</li>
				</dl>
			</p>
		</exploration>

		<p>
			By following these steps, the Laplace Transform Method provides a clear path from the original differential equation to its solution, leveraging the power of both forward and backward transforms.
		</p>

		<p>
			Now that we've explored theory behind the Laplace Transform Method, it's time to put it into action by solving some initial-value problems. We'll walk through two examples that will illustrate each of the four key steps in the method. Along the way, we'll highlight how the forward and backward Laplace transforms work together to convert a differential equation into an algebraic problem and then back to a solution. Let's get started!
		</p>
		
		<example><title> Solve the initial-value problem </title>
			<p>
				<men xml:id="lt-example-2-eqn">
					y' + y = 4, \quad y(0) = 2
				</men>.
			</p>
			
			<solution><title> Step 1: Apply the Forward Transform </title>
				<p>
					<xref ref="lt-step-1" text="custom">Step 1</xref>. Applying <m>\laplacesym</m> to both sides of <xref ref="lt-example-2-eqn"/>, we have
					<md>
						<mrow>\lap{y' + y} = \lap{4}</mrow>
						<mrow>\lap{y'} + \lap{y} = \lap{4}</mrow>
						<mrow>sY(s) - 2 + Y(s) = \frac{4}{s}</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>
			</solution>
			
			<solution><title> Step 2a: Solve for <m>Y(s)</m> </title>
				<p>
					<xref ref="lt-step-2" text="custom">Step 2a</xref>. Next, we isolate <m>Y(s)</m> on one side of the equation:
					<md>
						<mrow> sY(s) - 2 + Y(s)	\amp = \frac{4}{s}													</mrow>
						<mrow>		(s + 1)Y(s)	\amp = \frac{4}{s} + 2												</mrow>
						<mrow> 			Y(s)	\amp = \frac{1}{s + 1}\left(\frac{4}{s} + 2\right)					</mrow>
						<mrow> 			Y(s)	\amp = \ub{\frac{4}{s(s + 1)}}_{(*)} + \ub{\frac{2}{s + 1}}_{(**)}	</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>
			</solution>

			<solution><title> Step 2b: Prepare for the Backward Transform </title>
				<p>
					<xref ref="lt-step-3" text="custom">Step 2b</xref>. We need to express <m>Y(s)</m> as a sum of functions that match known forms in the Laplace transform table. We see from the last equation, <m>(**)</m> is ready to go, but <m>(*)</m> requires partial fraction decomposition. We start by writing down the form of the decomposition,
					<me>
						(*) = \frac{4}{s(s + 1)} = \frac{A}{s} + \frac{B}{s + 1} \quad \text{or}
					</me>
					<men xml:id="lt-example-2-eqn-2">
						4 = A(s + 1) + Bs
					</men>
					Now, we find <m>A</m> and <m>B</m> by plugging in values of <m>s</m> into <xref ref="lt-example-2-eqn-2"/>,
				</p>
				<tabular>
					<col halign="right"/>
					<col halign="right"/>
					<col halign="center"/>
					<col halign="left"/>
					<row>
						<cell><m>s=0</m> :</cell>
						<cell><m>4</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>A(0 + 1) + B(0)</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell></cell>
						<cell><m>4</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>A</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell><m>s=-1</m> :</cell>
						<cell><m>4</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>A(-1 + 1) + B(-1)</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell></cell>
						<cell><m>-4</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>B</m></cell>
					</row>
				</tabular>
				<p>
					Thus, the prepared <m>Y(s)</m> is
					<me>Y(s) = \ub{\frac{4}{s} + \frac{-4}{s + 1}}_{(*)} + \ub{\frac{2}{s + 1}}_{(**)}</me>
				</p>
			</solution>
		
			<solution><title> Step 3: Apply the Backward Transform </title>
				<p>
					<xref ref="lt-step-4" text="custom">Step 2b</xref>. Finally, we apply the backward step to get the solution:
					<md>
						<mrow>\ilap{Y(s)}	\amp = \ilap{\frac{4}{s} + \frac{-4}{s + 1}}</mrow>
						<mrow>		y(t)	\amp = 4 - 4e^{-t}</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>
			</solution>
		
		</example>

		<example><title> Solve the initial-value problem </title>
			<p>
				<men xml:id="lt-example-1-eqn">
					y'' - 3y' + 2y = e^{2t}, \quad y(0) = 1, \quad y'(0) = 0
				</men>
			</p>
			
			<solution><title> Step 1: Apply the Forward Transform </title>
				<p>
					<xref ref="lt-step-1" text="custom">Step 1</xref>. Applying <m>\laplacesym</m> to both sides of <xref ref="lt-example-1-eqn"/>, we have
					<md>
						<mrow>\lap{y'' - 3y' + 2y} \amp = \lap{e^{2t}}</mrow>
						<mrow>\lap{y''} - 3\lap{y'} + 2\lap{y} \amp = \frac{1}{s - 2}</mrow>
					</md>
					where <m>\lap{y(t)} = Y(s)</m> and
					<ul>
						<li><m>\lap{y'} = sY(s) - y(0) = sY(s) - 1</m>,</li>
						<li><m>\ds \lap{y''} = s^2Y(s) - sy(0) - y'(0) = s^2Y(s) - s</m>.</li>
					</ul>
					So the complete forward transform of <xref ref="lt-example-1-eqn"/> is
					<me>s^2Y(s) - s - 3[sY(s) - 1] + 2Y(s) = \frac{1}{s - 2}</me>
				</p>
			</solution>

			<solution><title> Step 2a: Solve for <m>Y(s)</m> </title>
				<p>
					<xref ref="lt-step-2" text="custom">Step 2a</xref>. Next, we isolate <m>Y(s)</m> on one side of the equation:
				<md>
					<mrow>s^2Y(s) - s - 3sY(s) + 3 + 2Y(s) = \frac{1}{s - 2}</mrow>
					<mrow>[s^2 - 3s + 2]Y(s) - s + 3 = \frac{1}{s - 2}</mrow>
					<mrow>Y(s)[(s - 1)(s - 2)] = \frac{1}{s - 2} + s - 3</mrow>
					<mrow>Y(s) = \frac{1}{(s - 1)(s - 2)}\ub{\left(\frac{1}{s - 2} + s - 3\right)}_{(*)}</mrow>
				</md>
				</p>
			</solution>

			<solution><title> Step 2b: Prepare for the Backward Transform </title>
				<p>
					<xref ref="lt-step-3" text="custom">Step 2b</xref>. Now, we need to express <m>Y(s)</m> as a sum of functions that match known forms in the Laplace transform table. This can be simplified slightly by first combining the terms in <m>(*)</m> as a single fraction, like so
					<me>
						(*)	= \frac{1}{s - 2} + \frac{(s - 3)(s - 2)}{s - 2}
							= \frac{1 + s^2 - 5s + 6}{s - 2} = \frac{s^2 - 5s + 7}{s - 2}
					</me>.
					Plugging this back into the equation for <m>(*)</m> gives the new <m>Y(s)</m>,
					<me>Y(s) = \frac{1}{(s - 1)(s - 2)}\cdot \frac{s^2 - 5s + 7}{s - 2} = \frac{s^2 - 5s + 7}{(s - 1)(s - 2)^2}</me>
					and we are ready to apply partial fraction decomposition. The form of the decomposition is
					<md>
						<mrow>
							\frac{s^2 - 5s + 7}{(s - 1)(s - 2)^2}	
								\amp = \frac{A}{s - 1} + \frac{B}{s - 2} + \frac{C}{(s - 2)^2} \quad \text{or}
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							s^2 - 5s + 7
								\amp = A(s - 2)^2 + B(s - 1)(s - 2) + C(s - 1).
						</mrow>
					</md>
					Now, we find <m>A, B, </m> and <m>C</m> by plugging in values of <m>s</m>,
				</p>
				<tabular>
					<col halign="right"/>
					<col halign="right"/>
					<col halign="center"/>
					<col halign="left"/>
					<row>
						<cell><m>s=1</m> :</cell>
						<cell><m>1 - 5 + 7</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>A(1 - 2)^2 + B(1 - 1)(1 - 2) + C(1 - 1)</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell></cell>
						<cell><m>3</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>A</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell><m>s=2</m> :</cell>
						<cell><m>4 - 10 + 7</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>3(2 - 2)^2 + B(2 - 1)(2 - 2) + C(2 - 1)</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell></cell>
						<cell><m>1</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>C</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell><m>s=0</m> :</cell>
						<cell><m>7</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>3(0 - 2)^2 + B(0 - 1)(0 - 2) + 1(0 - 1)</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell></cell>
						<cell><m>7</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>12 + 2B - 1</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell></cell>
						<cell><m>-2</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>B</m></cell>
					</row>
				</tabular>
				<p>
					Thus, the prepared <m>Y(s)</m> is
					<me>Y(s) = \frac{3}{s - 1} + \frac{-2}{s - 2} + \frac{1}{(s - 2)^2}</me>
				</p>
			</solution>

			<solution><title> Step 3: Apply the Backward Transform </title>
				<p>
					<xref ref="lt-step-4" text="custom">Step 3</xref>. Finally, we perform the backward step to get the solution:
					<md>
						<mrow>
							\ilap{Y(s)}
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = \ilap{\frac{3}{s - 1}} + \ilap{\frac{-2}{s - 2}} + \ilap{\frac{1}{(s - 2)^2}}
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							y(t) \amp = 3\, e^{t} - 2\, e^{2t} + t\, e^{2t}
						</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>
			</solution>

		</example>

		<example><title> Solve the initial-value problem </title>
			<p>
				<men xml:id="lt-example-3-eqn">
					x'' - 4x' + 13x = 54e^{-t}, \quad x(0) = 0, \quad x'(0) = 0
				</men>
			</p>

			<solution><title> Step 1: Apply the Forward Transform </title>
				<p>
					<xref ref="lt-step-1" text="custom">Step 1</xref>. Applying <m>\laplacesym</m> to both sides of <xref ref="lt-example-3-eqn"/>, we have
					<md>
						<mrow>\lap{x'' - 4x' + 13x} = \lap{54e^{-t}}</mrow>
						<mrow>\lap{x''} - 4\lap{x'} + 13\lap{x} = \frac{54}{s + 1}</mrow>
					</md>
					where <m>\lap{x(t)} = X(s)</m> and
					<ul>
						<li><m>\lap{x'} = sX(s) - x(0) = sX(s)</m>,</li>
						<li><m>\ds \lap{x''} = s^2X(s) - sx(0) - x'(0) = s^2X(s)</m>.</li>
					</ul>
					So the complete forward transform of <xref ref="lt-example-3-eqn"/> is
					<me>s^2X(s) - 4sX(s) + 13X(s) = \frac{54}{s + 1}</me>
				</p>
			</solution>

			<solution><title> Step 2a: Solve for <m>X(s)</m> </title>
				<p>
					<xref ref="lt-step-2" text="custom">Step 2a</xref>. Next, we isolate <m>X(s)</m> on one side of the equation:
				<md>
					<mrow>s^2X(s) - 4sX(s) + 13X(s) = \frac{54}{s + 1}</mrow>
					<mrow>[s^2 - 4s + 13]X(s) = \frac{54}{s + 1}</mrow>
					<mrow>X(s) = \frac{54}{(s + 1)(s^2 - 4s + 13)}</mrow>
				</md>
				</p>
			</solution>

			<solution><title> Step 2b: Prepare for the Backward Transform </title>
				<p>
					<xref ref="lt-step-3" text="custom">Step 2b</xref>. Now, we need to express <m>X(s)</m> as a sum of functions that match known forms in the Laplace transform table, which requires partial fraction decomposition. We start by writing down the form of the decomposition,
					<md>
						<mrow>
							\frac{54}{(s + 1)(s^2 - 4s + 13)} = \frac{A}{s + 1} + \frac{Bs + C}{s^2 - 4s + 13} \quad \text{or}
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							54 = A(s^2 - 4s + 13) + (Bs + C)(s + 1)
						</mrow>
					</md>
					Now, we find <m>A, B, </m> and <m>C</m> by plugging in values of <m>s</m>,
				</p>
				<tabular>
					<col halign="right"/>
					<col halign="right"/>
					<col halign="center"/>
					<col halign="left"/>
					<row>
						<cell><m>s=-1</m> :</cell>
						<cell><m>54</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>A(-1)^2 - 4(-1) + 13</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell></cell>
						<cell><m>54</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>A + 4 + 13</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell></cell>
						<cell><m>37</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>A</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell><m>s=0</m> :</cell>
						<cell><m>54</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>37(13) + (B(0) + C)(0 + 1)</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell></cell>
						<cell><m>54</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>481 + C</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell></cell>
						<cell><m>-427</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>C</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell><m>s=1</m> :</cell>
						<cell><m>54</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>37(-10) + (B(1) - 427)(1 + 1)</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell></cell>
						<cell><m>54</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>-370 + 2B - 854</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell></cell>
						<cell><m>1170</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>2B</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell></cell>
						<cell><m>585</m></cell>
						<cell><m>=</m></cell>
						<cell><m>B</m></cell>
					</row>
				</tabular>
				<p>
					So the updated <m>X(s)</m> is
					<me>X(s) = \frac{37}{s + 1} + \frac{585s - 427}{s^2 - 4s + 13}</me>.
					Note, the second term is not yet ready and we need to complete the square of its denominator before we can do the backward step.
					<md>
						<mrow> X(s)	\amp = \frac{37}{s + 1} + \frac{585s - 427}{(s - 2)^2 + 9}</mrow>
						<mrow> 		\amp = \frac{37}{s + 1} + \frac{585s - 2(585) + 2(585)- 427}{(s - 2)^2 + 9}</mrow>
						<mrow> 		\amp = \frac{37}{s + 1} + \frac{585s - 2(585)}{(s - 2)^2 + 9} + \frac{2(585)- 427}{(s - 2)^2 + 9}</mrow>
						<mrow> 		\amp = \frac{37}{s + 1} + 585\frac{s - 2}{(s - 2)^2 + 9} + 743\frac{1}{(s - 2)^2 + 9}</mrow>
						<mrow> 		\amp = \frac{37}{s + 1} + 585\frac{s - 2}{(s - 2)^2 + 9} + \frac{743}{3}\frac{3}{(s - 2)^2 + 9}</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>
			</solution>

			<solution><title> Step 3: Apply the Backward Transform </title>
				<p>
					<xref ref="lt-step-4" text="custom">Step 3</xref>. Now for the backward step to get the solution:
					<md>
						<mrow>
							x(t) \amp = 37e^{-t} + 585\cos(3t) + \frac{743}{3}\sin(3t)
						</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>
			</solution>
			
		</example>
		
		<p>
			These examples highlight how the Laplace Transform Method simplifies solving differential equations by converting them into algebraic equations and then back into the time domain. By mastering these steps, you can tackle a wide range of initial-value problems with ease and precision.
		</p>

		<example>
			<p>
			Solve
			<me> y'' + 3y' + 2y = 4t,  y(0) = 1,  y'(0) = 0 </me>
			</p>
			<solution>
				<p>
					We intend to use Laplace transforms to solve this IVP, but we need to verify that  this is an appropriate technique. We can verify that this is
					<ul>
						<li>
							the DE is linear,
						</li>
						<li>
							the DE has constant coefficients, and
						</li>
						<li>
							initial conditions are provided.
						</li>
					</ul>
				</p>

				<p>
					Hence, it is appropriate to proceed with the Laplace transform solution technique. We also note that <m>y</m> is the dependent variable and <m>t</m> is the independent variable, so our goal is to find a function <m>y(t)</m> that satisfies the DE and the initial conditions.
				</p>

				<aside>
					<p>
						Margin note:  We could actually use the method of undetermined coefficients. You are encouraged to do this and verify that the solution is the same.
					</p>
				</aside>

				<p>
					We begin by taking the Laplace transform of both sides of the DE, using linearity as needed.
					<md>
						<mrow>\lap{y'' + 3y' + 2y}	\amp = \lap{ 4t }</mrow>
						<mrow>\lap{y''} + 3\lap{y'} + 2\lap{y}	\amp = 4\cdot\lap{ t }</mrow>
						<mrow>\Big[s^2Y(s) - sy(0) - y'(0) \Big] + 3\Big[ sY(s) - y(0) \Big] + 2Y(s)	\amp = \frac{4}{s^2}</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>

				<p>
					Now we use the provided initial conditions.
					<md>
						<mrow>\Big[s^2Y(s) - s\cdot 1 - 0 \Big] + 3\Big[ sY(s) - 1 \Big] + 2Y(s)	\amp = \frac{4}{s^2}</mrow>
						<mrow>s^2Y(s) - s  + 3sY(s) - 3 + 2Y(s)	\amp = \frac{4}{s^2}</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>

				<p>
					Next we will use algebra to solve for <m>Y(s).</m>
					<md>
						<mrow>s^2Y(s)+ 3sY(s) + 2Y(s)	\amp = \frac{4}{s^2} + s + 3</mrow>
						<mrow>Y(s)\Big[ s^2 + 3s + 2 \Big]	\amp = \frac{4}{s^2} + \frac{s^3}{s^2} + \frac{3s^2}{s^2}</mrow>
						<mrow>\amp = \frac{4 + s^3 + 3s^2}{s^2}</mrow>
						<mrow>Y(s)	\amp = \frac{s^3 + 3s^2 + 4}{s^2(s^2 + 3s + 2)}</mrow>
						<mrow>\amp = \frac{s^3 + 3s^2 + 4}{s^2(s + 2)(s + 1)}</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>

				<p>
					We need to find the inverse Laplace transform of both sides of the equation. In order to do that, we apply partial fraction decomposition to the rational function on the right hand side, giving
					<me>\frac{s^3 + 3s^2 + 4}{s^2(s + 2)(s + 1)} = \frac{-3}{s} + \frac{2}{s^2} + \frac{-2}{s+2} + \frac{6}{s+1}.</me>
				</p>

				<p>
					Now we are prepared to take the inverse Laplace transform.
					<md>
						<mrow>Y(s)	\amp = \frac{-3}{s} + \frac{2}{s^2} + \frac{-2}{s+2} + \frac{6}{s+1}</mrow>
					\lap^{-1}\left\{ Y(s) \right\}	
						<mrow>\amp = \lap^{-1}\left\{ \frac{-3}{s} + \frac{2}{s^2} + \frac{-2}{s+2} + \frac{6}{s+1} \right\}</mrow>
						<mrow>y(t)	\amp = -3\cdot\lap^{-1}\left\{ \frac{1}{s} \right\}</mrow>
							+ 2\lap^{-1}\left\{ \frac{1}{s^2} \right\}
