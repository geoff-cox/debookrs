	<section><title> Completing the Derivative </title>	
		<introduction>
			<p>
				The integrating factor method is a systematic process that converts a first-order linear differential equation into a direct integration problem. In terms of equation, it looks like
				<men xml:id="if-completed-derivative-eqn1">
					\ub{\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x) y = Q(x)}_{\text{Given Equation}} \quad 
					\os{\text{Integrating}}{\us{\text{Factor Method}}{\longrightarrow}} \quad 
					\ub{\frac{d}{dx}[ \mu(x) y ] = \mu(x) Q(x)}_{\text{Completed Derivative Equation}}
				</men>.
			</p>

			<p>
				The method gets its name from the function <m>\mu(x)</m> that must be included to get the <q>completed derivative equation</q> in <xref ref="if-completed-derivative-eqn1"/>.
			</p>

			<p>
				The goal of this section is to develop an intuition for the integrating factor method by first exploring the idea of <q>completing the derivative</q>. We will start by drawing a parallel to completing the square, then move on to reversing the product rule, and finally examine how to introduce the missing component‚Äî<em>the integrating factor</em>‚Äîthat makes the method work.
			</p>

			<outcomes>
				<ul>
					<li>
						<p>
							Explain the analogy between solving a quadratic equation by completing the square and solving a differential equation using the integrating factor method.
						</p>
					</li>
					<li>
						<p>
							Recognize how the product rule from calculus plays a central role in <q>completing the derivative</q> of a first-order linear differential equation.
						</p>
					</li>
					<li>
						<p>
							Practice reversing the product rule to rewrite expressions as a single derivative.
						</p>
					</li>
					<li>
						<p>
							Apply the concept of <q>completing the derivative</q> to transform a first-order linear differential equation into a form that can be solved by direct integration.
						</p>
					</li>
				</ul>
			</outcomes>

			<p>
				Let's begin by exploring the completing the square analogy and how it relates to the integrating factor method.
			</p>
		</introduction>

		<subsection><title> Reversing the Product Rule </title>
			<introduction>
				<p>
					The integrating factor method relies on rewriting a differential equation so that the left-hand side becomes the 
					<q>derivative of a product</q>. To do this, we need to recognize when an expression 
					<q>can be written as a product rule in reverse</q>.
				</p>

				<p>
					In this subsection, we will:
				</p>

				<p>
					<line>‚úÖ Review the product rule from calculus.</line>
					<line>‚úÖ Practice reversing the product rule on different expressions.</line>
					<line>‚úÖ Apply this idea to transform differential equations into a solvable form.</line>
				</p>
			</introduction>

			<subsubsection><title>The Product Rule: A Quick Review</title>
				<p>
					The product rule states that if <m>f(x)</m> and <m>g(x)</m> are differentiable functions, then:
				</p>

				<men xml:id="product-rule">
					\frac{d}{dx} [ f(x) g(x) ] = f(x) g'(x) + f'(x) g(x).
				</men>

				<p>
					Most calculus problems apply the product rule <q>in the forward direction</q>‚Äîgiven <m>f(x)</m> and <m>g(x)</m>, compute their derivative. However, in the integrating factor method, we must <q>work in reverse</q>. For example, you can rewrite the expression
				</p>

				<me>
					\frac{1}{x} \sin x + \cos x \ln x
				</me>

				<p>
					in terms of the derivative of the product of two functions: <m>\big[\sin x \cdot \ln x \big]'</m>.
				</p>

				<p>
					To get in the mindset of reversing the product rule, let's warm-up with a few forward product rule problems.
				</p>

				<example><title> Forward Product Rule </title>

					<p>
						<statement>
							<p>
								Compute each of the derivatives below.
							</p>
							<me>
								1. \quad \left[x^6 e^{3x}\right]' \hspace{5em} 2. \quad \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{x^3} y(x)\right]
							</me>
						</statement>
					</p>

					<solution>
						<p>
							Using the product rule:
							<ol>
								<li>
									<p>
										<m>\ds
											\left( x^6 e^{3x} \right)' =
											x^6 \cdot 3e^{3x} + 6x^5 e^{3x}
										</m>
									</p>
								</li>
								<li>
									<p>
										<m>\ds\frac{d}{dx}
											\left( e^{x^3} y(x) \right) =
											e^{x^3} y'(x) + 3x^2 e^{x^3} y(x)
										</m>
									</p>
								</li>
							</ol>
						</p>
					</solution>

				</example>

			</subsubsection>

			<subsubsection><title>Recognizing and Reversing the Product Rule</title>

				<p>
					Now, let's try reversing the product rule in the next few examples.
				</p>

				<example><title> Basic Reverse Product Rule </title>

					<p>
						<statement>
							<p>
								Rewrite each expression in the form <m>[f(x) g(x)]'</m>.
								<me>
									1. \quad e^{x}\cos x + e^{x}\sin x \hspace{5em} 2. \quad \frac{e^{x^2}}{x} + 2 x \ln x e^{x^2}
								</me>
							</p>
						</statement>
					</p>

					<solution>
						<p>
							<solution>

								<p>
									One approach is to guess <m>f</m> and <m>g</m> on different terms, then check that the corresponding <m>f'</m> and <m>g'</m> are in the other terms.
								</p>

								<p>
									First, try guessing <m>f</m> and <m>g</m> as follows
								</p>

								<p>
									<me>
										e^x\
										\underset{\Large f}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}\cos x}}}
										+
										\underset{\Large g}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}e^x}}}\
										\sin x
									</me>.
								</p>

								<p>
									To be a valid product rule, <m>f'</m> needs to be <m>\sin x</m>, but our choice of <m>f</m> results in <m>f' = [\cos x]' = -\sin x</m>, so this guess doesn't work.
								</p>

								<p>
									Next, try the labels
								</p>

								<p>
									<me>
										\underset{\Large f}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}e^x}}}\
										\cos x
										+
										e^x\
										\underset{\Large g}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}\sin x}}}
									</me>.
								</p>

								<p>
									Noting that <m>f' = [e^x]' = e^x</m> is the other part of the second term and <m>g' = [\sin x]' = \cos x</m> is in the first term confirms this is a valid product rule. Therefore, we can reverse the product rule as
								</p>

								<me>
									e^x \cos x + e^x \sin x = [e^x \cos x]'
								</me>.


							</solution>

							<solution>

								<p>
									To make this easier, let's separate the terms as
									<me>
										\frac{1}{x} e^{x^2} + \ln x (2 x e^{x^2})
									</me>.
								</p>
								
								<p>
									So, as before, we first set <m>f</m> and <m>g</m>
								</p>

								<me>
									\frac{1}{x}\
									\underset{\Large f}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}e^{x^2}}}}
									+
									\underset{\Large g}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}\ln x}}}\
									2 x e^{x^2}
								</me>,

								<p>
									then verify the derivatives of <m>f</m> and <m>g</m> are in the other terms,
								</p>

								<me>
									f' = \left[e^{x^2}\right]' = 2 x e^{x^2} \quad ‚úÖ \qquad g' = \left[\ln x\right]' = \frac{1}{x} \quad ‚úÖ
								</me>.

								<p>
									Therefore,
								</p>

								<me>
									\frac{e^{x^2}}{x} + 2 x \ln x e^{x^2} = \left[e^{x^2} \ln x\right]'
								</me>.
							</solution>
						</p>
					</solution>
					
				</example>

				<p>
					Here is an example that includes a variable in the expression. This is more like what you will see in a the integrating factor method.
				</p>

				<example xml:id="reversed-product-rule-example"><title> Reversed Product Rule with a Variable </title>

					<p>
						<statement>
							<p>
								Rewrite each expression in the form <m>[f(x) y(x)]'</m>.
								<me>
									1. \quad e^{9x}\frac{dy}{dx} + 9y e^{9x} \hspace{5em} 2. \quad e^{1/x}y' - \frac{e^{1/x}}{x^2}y
								</me>
							</p>
						</statement>
					</p>

					<solution>
						<p>
							<solution>
								<p>
									Observe that <m>dy/dx</m> and <m>y</m> in opposite terms gives you <m>g</m> for free and so <m>f</m> must be the function next to <m>dy/dx</m>. That is,
								</p>

								<me>
									\underset{\Large f}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{e^{9x}}}}\
									\frac{dy}{dx}
									+
									\underset{\Large g}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}y}}}\
									(9 e^{9x})
								</me>.

								<p>
									Clearly, <m>g'</m> is <m>dy/dx</m> in the first term and <m>f' = [e^{9x}]' = 9e^{9x}</m> is the other part in the second term, so we can conclude
								</p>

								<me>
									e^{9x}\frac{dy}{dx} + 9 e^{9x} y = \left[e^{9x} y\right]'
								</me>.

							</solution>

							<solution>
								<p>
									Again, we can set <m>g'</m> to <m>y'</m> for free, so <m>g</m> must be <m>y</m> and <m>f</m> must be <m>e^{1/x}</m>, as shown
								</p>

								<me>
									\underset{\Large f}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{e^{1/x}}}}\
									y'
									+
									\left(-\frac{e^{1/x}}{x^2}\right)\
									\underset{\Large g}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}y}}}
								</me>.

								<p>
									The only thing left to do is verify the derivative of <m>f</m>:
									<me>
										f' = \left[e^{1/x}\right]' = e^{1/x}\left[\frac{1}{x}\right]^\prime = e^{1/x}\left(-\frac{1}{x^2}\right) = -\frac{e^{1/x}}{x^2} \quad ‚úÖ
									</me>.
								</p>

								<p>
									So, we can reverse the product rule as
								</p>

								<me>
									e^{1/x}y' - \frac{e^{1/x}}{x^2}y = \left[e^{1/x} y\right]'
								</me>.

							</solution>
						</p>
					</solution>
					
				</example>
				
				<p>
					These examples are not necessarily skills you need to master, but they are good exercises to help you understand how the integrating factor method works. The next step is to apply these concepts to solving a differential equations. 
				</p>

			
			</subsubsection>

			<subsubsection><title>Why This Matters for Differential Equations</title>

				<p>
					Some differential equations contain expressions similar to those in <xref ref="reversed-product-rule-example"/>. When this is the case, it is typically beneficial to rewrite the equation as a <q>completed</q> derivative. For example, the left hand side of the equation
				</p>

				<men xml:id="reverse-product-rule-DE">
					\ub{e^{2x} \frac{dy}{dx} + 2e^{2x} y}_{\text{looks like a product rule}} = 5e^{2x}
				</men>,

				<p>
					resembles the derivative of <m> [e^{2x}\cdot y] </m>. This allows us to write the equation as
				</p>

				<men xml:id="reverse-product-rule-DE-solution">
					\frac{d}{dx} [ e^{2x} y ] = 5e^{2x}.
				</men>

				<p>
					This is great, since <em>direct integration</em> now applies and the general solution can be found as follows:
				</p>

				<md>
					<mrow>	\int \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{2x} \cdot y \right]\ dx	=\amp \int \frac{5}{2} e^{2x}\ dx	</mrow>
					<mrow>	e^{2x} y											=\amp \frac{5}{2} e^{2x} + c		</mrow>
					<mrow>	y													=\amp \frac{5}{2} + c e^{-2x}		</mrow>
				</md>.

				<example xml:id="reversing-the-product-rule-in-a-DE-example"><title> Reversed Product Rule in a Differential Equation </title>

					<p>
						<statement>
							<p>
								Find the general solution of the equation
								<men xml:id="reversing-the-product-rule-in-a-DE-example-eqn">
									e^{x^4} \frac{dy}{dx} + 4x^3e^{x^4} y = 5x
								</men>
								by rewriting the left-side as a single derivative and then direct integration.
							</p>
						</statement>
					</p>

					<p>
						<solution>

							<p>
								To reverse the product rule on the left-side, set <m>g</m> and <m>f</m> as before
							</p>

							<me>
								\underset{\Large f}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{e^{x^4}}}}\
								\frac{dy}{dx}
								+
								\underset{\Large g}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}y}}}\
								(4x^3 e^{x^4}) \quad \Big(f' = [e^{x^4}]' = 4x^3 e^{x^4} \ ‚úÖ \Big)
							</me>

							<p>
								and so the left side can be written as the derivative <m>\ \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{x^4} y\right]</m>.
							</p>

							<p>
								Now, we update the differential equation and integrate as follows:
							</p>

							<md>
								<mrow>			\frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{x^4} \cdot y \right]	\amp = 5x										</mrow>
								<mrow>	\int \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{x^4} \cdot y \right]\ dx	\amp = \int 5x \ dx								</mrow>
								<mrow>											e^{x^4} y	\amp = \frac{5}{2} x^2 + c						</mrow>
								<mrow>													y	\amp = \frac{5}{2} x^2 e^{-x^4} + c e^{-x^4}	</mrow>
							</md>.

						</solution>
					</p>

				</example>

			</subsubsection>

			<conclusion>

				<p>
					It turns out that writing a first order linear differential equation so that one side is a <q>complete</q> derivative is <em>always possible</em> and is exactly what the integrating factor method is all about. The only problem is that these equations are missing a piece needed to for this approach. In the next section, we will show you how to find and use this missing piece to rewrite any first order linear equations in this way. 
				</p>

				<p>
					<objectives>
						<ul>
							<li>
								<p>
									The product rule is not only used to compute derivatives, but it also can combine multiple functions into a single derivative.
								</p>
							</li>
							<li>
								<p>
									<q>Reversing</q> the product rule within a differential equation is a key step in the <em>integrating factor method</em>.
								</p>
							</li>
							<li>
								<p>
									Once we rewrite one side of a differential equation as a single derivative, it can be solved by <em>direct integration</em>.
								</p>
							</li>
						</ul>
					</objectives>
				</p>

			</conclusion>

		<subsection><title> Completing the Square Analogy </title>
			<introduction>
				<p>
					Solving a differential equation using the integrating factor method is similar to solving a quadratic equation by 
					<q>completing the square</q>. In both cases, we strategically modify the equation by introducing a missing component 
					to rewrite it in a form that is easier to solve.
				</p>

				<p>
					In this subsection, we will:
				</p>

				<p>
					<line>‚úÖ Review completing the square to solve quadratic equations.</line>
					<line>‚úÖ Compare it to completing the derivative to solve a differential equation.</line>
					<line>‚úÖ Motivate how the integrating factor method works through this analogy.</line>
				</p>
				
			</introduction>

			<subsubsection xml:id="quadratic-completing-square"><title>Completing the Square - Quadratic Equations</title>
				<p>
					Consider the quadratic equation:
				</p>

				<men xml:id="quadratic-example">
					x^2 + 6x = -1
				</men>

				<p>
					One way to solve this equation is to move <m>-1</m> to the left-hand side and apply the quadratic formula. However, 
					another approach is to <q>complete the square</q> by strategically adding a number that turns the left-hand side into 
					a perfect square.
				</p>

				<p>
					In this case, the missing piece is <m>\left(\frac{6}{2}\right)^2 = 9</m>, so we add it to both sides:
				</p>

				<me>
					x^2 + 6x + 9 = -1 + 9.
				</me>

				<p>
					Now, the left-hand side can be written as a perfect square:
				</p>

				<men xml:id="perfect-square">
					(x + 3)^2 = 8.
				</men>

				<p>
					Taking the square root of both sides gives you the solution
				</p>

				<me>
					x + 3 = \pm\sqrt{8} \quad \implies \quad x = -3 \pm 2\sqrt{2}
				</me>.

				<p>
					The key idea here is that we added a carefully chosen number to turn the expression into a perfect square, making the equation easier to solve.
				</p>
			</subsubsection>

			<subsubsection xml:id="completing-the-derivative"><title>Completing the Derivative - Differential Equations</title>
				<p>
					The integrating factor method follows a similar idea, but instead of completing the square, we <q>complete the derivative</q>.
					To see this, consider the first-order linear differential equation:
				</p>

				<me>
					\frac{dy}{dx} + 6y = -1.
				</me>

				<p>
					We would like to write the left side as a single derivative, but that is not possible in its current form. However, if we multiply both sides by <m>e^{6x}</m>, we get
				</p>

				<me>
					e^{6x}\left(\frac{dy}{dx} + y\right) = -1\cdot e^{6x} \quad \implies \quad e^{6x}\frac{dy}{dx} + 6e^{6x}y = -e^{6x}.
				</me>

				<p>
					Now, the left-hand side looks like a product rule, so we can complete the derivative by writing it as
				</p>

				<men xml:id="completed-derivative">
					\frac{d}{dx} [e^{6x} y] = -e^{6x}.
				</men>

				<p>
					Just like in completing the square, we identify the missing component and introduce it to simplify the problem. 
					The function <m>e^{6x}</m> that we introduced is called the <term>integrating factor</term>. It plays a role similar to the 
					<m>9</m> we added in the quadratic equation‚Äîit transforms the equation into a form that is easy to solve.
				</p>

	<section><title> Finding the Integrating Factor </title>	
		<introduction>
			<p>
				Before we begin with the next topic, make sure the following points are clear:
			</p>

			<p>
				<line> 1Ô∏è‚É£ A derivative can be <q>completed</q> in <em>ANY</em> first order linear equation.</line>
				<line> 2Ô∏è‚É£ The equation must be multiplied by a function to complete the derivative.</line>
				<line> 3Ô∏è‚É£ The completed derivative is formed as a reverse product rule.</line>
			</p>

			<p>
				These ideas make up the fundamental strategy behind the integrating factor method. In fact, you have already seen examples of this strategy in action. What you haven't seen, however, is how to find the function that completes the derivative for any given first order linear equation. So the next step is to come up with a formula for finding it.
			</p>
		</introduction>

		<subsection><title> A Motivating Example </title>
			<p>
				Recall from the previous section that we encountered the equation:
			</p>

			<men xml:id="if-example-incomplete-eqn">
				\frac{dy}{dx} + 2y = 5
			</men>.

			<p>
				Even though we have already seen which function completes the derivative, assume it is unknown and it call <m>\mu</m>. Our goal is to find <m>\mu</m> as a function of <m>x</m> such that <xref ref="if-example-incomplete-eqn"/> can be rewritten as
			</p>

			<me>
				\frac{d}{dx} \left[\mu(x) y \right] = 5\mu
			</me>

			<p>
				The strategy for determining <m>\mu(x)</m> is visualized in the following diagram
			</p>

			<figure xml:id="if-strategy-1">
				<caption>A visual illustration of how to find <m>\mu(x)</m> given that we want to rewrite the <em>Starting Equation</em> <xref ref="starting-equation"/> as the <em>Goal Equation</em> <xref ref="goal-equation"/>.</caption>
				
				<sidebyside valign="middle" halign="right left" widths="10% 26% 45% 19%">
					<p/>
					<p>
						1Ô∏è‚É£  Multiply by <m>\ds\mu\ \left\lbrace\begin{array}{c}\\\\\\\end{array}\right.</m>
					</p>
					<p>
						<mdn>
							<mrow xml:id="starting-equation">
								\frac{dy}{dx} + 2 y \amp = 5
							</mrow>
							<mrow xml:id="middle-equation-1">
								\mu\frac{dy}{dx} + {\color{blue} \us{\downarrow}{\boxed{ 2\mu }}} y\amp = 5\mu
							</mrow>
						</mdn>
					</p>
					<p/>
				</sidebyside>

				<sidebyside valign="middle" halign="right left" widths="10% 26% 45% 19%">
					<p/>
					<p>
						3Ô∏è‚É£ Condition on <m>\mu</m>
					</p>
					<p>
						<me>
							{\color{blue} \boxed{\frac{d\mu}{dx} = 2\mu}}
						</me>
					</p>
					<p/>
				</sidebyside>

				<sidebyside valign="bottom" halign="right left" widths="10% 26% 45% 19%">
					<p/>
					<p>
						2Ô∏è‚É£ Product Rule <m>\ds\ \left\lbrace\vphantom{\begin{array}{c}\\\\\\\end{array}}\right.</m>
					</p>
					<p>
						<mdn>
							<mrow xml:id="middle-equation-2">	\mu\frac{dy}{dx} + {\color{blue} \os{\uparrow}{\boxed{\frac{d\mu}{dx}}}} y	\amp = 5 \mu </mrow>
							<mrow xml:id="goal-equation">	\frac{d}{dx} \left[\mu y \right]	\amp = 5\mu </mrow>
						</mdn>
					</p>
					<p/>
				</sidebyside>
			</figure>

			<p>
				The steps in <xref ref="if-strategy-1"/> are broken down, in order, as follows:
			</p>
			
			<p>
				<sidebyside widths="2% 6% 92%">
					<p/>
					<p>
						1Ô∏è‚É£
					</p>
					<p>
						Multiply <xref ref="starting-equation"/> by <m>\mu</m> to get <xref ref="middle-equation-1"/>.
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<sidebyside widths="2% 6% 92%">
					<p/>
					<p>
						2Ô∏è‚É£
					</p>
					<p>
						Use the product rule on the left side of <xref ref="goal-equation"/> to get <xref ref="middle-equation-2"/>.
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<sidebyside widths="2% 6% 92%">
					<p/>
					<p>
						3Ô∏è‚É£
					</p>
					<p>
						In order for <xref ref="starting-equation"/> and <xref ref="goal-equation"/> to be equal, <xref ref="middle-equation-1"/> and <xref ref="middle-equation-2"/> must also be equal, which gives the condition equation:
						<men xml:id="condition-equation">
							\frac{d\mu}{dx} = 2\mu
						</men>.
					</p>
				</sidebyside>
			</p>

			<p>	
				This means that the function, <m>\mu</m>, we are looking for must satisfy equation <xref ref="condition-equation"/>, which can be solved by separation of variables as follows:
			</p>

			<md>
				<mrow>	\frac{d\mu}{dx} = 2\mu \quad \implies \quad
						\frac{1}{\mu}\ d\mu			\amp = 2 dx			</mrow>
				<mrow>	\int \frac{1}{\mu}\ d\mu	\amp = \int 2 dx	</mrow>
				<mrow>	\ln|\mu|					\amp = 2x + c		</mrow>
				<mrow>	\mu(x)						\amp = ce^{2x}		</mrow>
			</md>
			
			<p>
				Since we only need one function to reverse the product rule, any non-zero choice for <m>c</m> will work. However, the convention is to always chose <m>c=1</m>. Therefore, the integrating factor for equation <xref ref="if-example-incomplete-eqn"/> is <m>\mu(x) = e^{2x}</m>.
			</p>

			<p>
				To summarize, if we are given the equation
			</p>

			<me>
				\frac{dy}{dx} + 2y = 5,
			</me>

			<p>
				we can multiply both sides by <m>e^{2x}</m> to get
			</p>

			<me>
				e^{2x} \frac{dy}{dx} + 2e^{2x} y = 5e^{2x},
			</me>

			<p>
				which can be rewritten as
			</p>

			<me>
				\frac{d}{dx} \left[ e^{2x} y \right] = 5e^{2x},
			</me>

			<p>
				and solved by direct integration.
			</p>

			<assemblage><title> Deriving the Integrating Factor for Equation <xref ref="if-example-incomplete-eqn"/></title>
				<p>
					<interactive xml:id="if-derivation-interactive" platform="javascript" width="88%" aspect="1.75:1" source="code/if-example-slide-show-1.js" preview="figures/if-strategy-3.png">

					</interactive>
				</p>
			</assemblage>
		</subsection>



		<subsection><title> The General Case </title>
			<p>
				The claim is that if you multiply any first order linear differential equation,			
			</p>

			<me>
				\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x)
			</me>.
			
			<p>
				by some function <m>\mu(x)</m>, you complete the derivative on the left side as
			</p>

			<md>
				<mrow> {\mu \frac{dy}{dx} + \mu P(x) y} \amp = \mu Q(x) </mrow>
				<mrow> \downarrow \qquad\amp </mrow>
				<mrow> {\frac{d}{dx} \left[ \mu(x) y \right]} \amp = \mu Q(x) </mrow>
			</md>.

			<p>
				The question is, <em><q>What must be true for the left sides of the equations to be equal?</q></em>
			</p>

			<p>
				To answer this, set them equal to each other, like so
			</p>

			<me>
				\left[ \mu y \right]^{\prime} = \mu \frac{dy}{dx} + \mu P(x) y
			</me>.

			<p>
				Then, we expand the derivative on the left using the product rule and mark the differences on oppposite sides of the equation:
			</p>

			<me>
				\mu \frac{dy}{dx} + \us{\Large üö©}{\ul{\mu^{\prime}}}\ y = \mu \frac{dy}{dx} + \us{\Large üö©}{\ul{\mu P(x)}}\ y
			</me>.

			<p>
				As you can see, the two sides of the equation are identical, expect for the flagged üö© expressions. For this equation to be valid, these expressions must also be equal. So the answer to the question, above, is the requirement 
			</p>

			<men xml:id="if-sov-eqn">
				\mu^{\prime} = \mu P(x).
			</men>

			<p>
				Just as with our motivating example, this is a differential equation that can be <em>partially</em> solved by separation of variables as follows:
			</p>

			<sidebyside widths="30% 70%" valigns="top middle">
				<p>
					Separate Variables:
				</p>
				<p>
					<me>
						\frac{1}{\mu}\ d\mu = P(x)\ dx
					</me>
				</p>
			</sidebyside>

			<sidebyside widths="30% 70%" valigns="top middle">
				<p>
					Integrate Both Sides:
				</p>
				<p>
					<md>
						<mrow>	\int \frac{1}{\mu}\ d\mu	\amp = \int P(x)\ dx	</mrow>  
						<mrow>	\ln|\mu|					\amp = \int P(x) dx		</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>
			</sidebyside>

			<aside>
				<p>
					<term>üìå Note.</term> The absolute value is dropped since the sign of the integrating factor is taken to be positive by convention.
				</p>
			</aside>

			<sidebyside widths="30% 70%" valigns="middle middle">
				<p>
					Solve for <m>\mu(x)</m>:
				</p>
				<p>
					<me>
						\mu(x) = e\vphantom{\sum}^{{\Large\int} P(x)\ dx}
					</me>.
				</p>
			</sidebyside>

			<assemblage xml:id="integrating-factor"><title> Integrating Factor </title>
				<p>
					The <term>integrating factor</term> for a first-order linear differential equation in standard form
					<me>
						y' + P(x)y = Q(x)
					</me>
					is given by	
					<m>
						\ \ds\mu(x) = e\vphantom{\sum}^{{\Large\int} P(x)\ dx}
					</m>.
				</p>
			</assemblage>
		</subsection>


		<subsection><title> The General Case </title>
			<introduction>
				<p>
					In the previous subsection, we worked through a specific example to find the function <m>\mu(x)</m> that allows us to rewrite a differential equation in a solvable form. Now, we generalize this process to determine the integrating factor for any first-order linear equation.
				</p>

				<p>
					Given a first-order linear differential equation:
				</p>

				<me>
					\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x),
				</me>

				<p>
					our goal is to determine a function <m>\mu(x)</m> such that multiplying through by <m>\mu(x)</m> transforms the equation into:
				</p>

				<me>
					\frac{d}{dx} \big[ \mu(x) y \big] = \mu(x) Q(x).
				</me>

				<p>
					This will allow us to solve for <m>y</m> using direct integration.
				</p>
			</introduction>

			<p>
				To determine <m>\mu(x)</m>, we start by multiplying both sides of the equation by an unknown function <m>\mu(x)</m>:
			</p>

			<me>
				\mu(x) \frac{dy}{dx} + \mu(x) P(x) y = \mu(x) Q(x).
			</me>

			<p>
				For the left-hand side to be a single derivative, <m>\mu(x)</m> must satisfy:
			</p>

			<men xml:id="if-sov-eqn">
				\frac{d}{dx} \big[ \mu(x) y \big] = \mu(x) \frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{d\mu}{dx} y.
			</men>

			<p>
				Comparing this with our transformed equation, we see that the two sides will match if:
			</p>

			<me>
				\frac{d\mu}{dx} = P(x) \mu.
			</me>

			<p>
				This is a <term>separable differential equation</term>, which we solve using integration:
			</p>

			<md>
				<mrow> \frac{1}{\mu} d\mu = P(x) dx </mrow>
				<mrow> \int \frac{1}{\mu} d\mu = \int P(x) dx </mrow>
				<mrow> \ln |\mu| = \int P(x) dx </mrow>
				<mrow> \mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) dx} </mrow>
			</md>

			<p>
				Since we only need one function to complete the derivative, we drop the absolute value and any arbitrary constant. The final formula for the integrating factor is:
			</p>

			<assemblage xml:id="integrating-factor"><title> Integrating Factor </title>
				<p>
					The <term>integrating factor</term> for a first-order linear differential equation in standard form
				</p>

				<me>
					\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x)
				</me>

				<p>
					is given by:
				</p>

				<me>
					\mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) dx}.
				</me>
			</assemblage>

			<p>
				Now that we have a formula for the integrating factor, let's apply it to an example.
			</p>

			<!-- Placeholder for example -->
			<example xml:id="integrating-factor-example">
				<title> Using the Integrating Factor Formula </title>
				<p>[Example content remains unchanged]</p>
			</example>

			<objectives>
				<ul>
					<li>
						<p>Multiplying by the integrating factor <m>\mu(x)</m> transforms a first-order linear equation into a form where the left-hand side is a single derivative.</p>
					</li>
					<li>
						<p>The integrating factor is always given by <m>\mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) dx}</m>, which is found by solving a separable differential equation.</p>
					</li>
					<li>
						<p>Once rewritten in completed derivative form, the equation can be solved using <em>direct integration</em>.</p>
					</li>
				</ul>
			</objectives>
		</subsection>

	<section><title> Integrating Factor Method </title>	
		<introduction>
			<p>
				The <term>Integrating Factor (IF) Method</term> is an essential technique for solving <em>any</em> first-order linear differential equation. Such equations can <em>always</em> be written in the <term>standard form</term> given by
			</p>

			<men xml:id="if-standard-form">
				y' + P(x)\ y = Q(x)
			</men>.

			<theorem xml:id="std-form-explained"><title> <em>1st-Order Linear Standard Form Explained</em> </title>
			
				<statement>
					<p>
						Since a general linear differential equation looks like
						<me>
							a_n(x)y^{(n)} + \cdots + a_2(x)y'' + a_1(x)y' + a_0(x)y = f(x)
						</me>,
						the first-order case (<m>n=1</m>), simplifies to
						<me>
							a_1(x)y' + a_0(x)y = f(x)
						</me>.
						Dividing by the coefficient of <m>y'</m>, and renaming the new coefficients as <m>P</m> and <m>Q</m>, gives the standard form
						<me>
							y' + \us{P(x)}{\ub{\frac{a_0(x)}{a_1(x)}}}y	= \us{Q(x)}{\ub{\frac{f(x)}{a_1(x)}}}	
							\quad \implies \quad
							y' + P(x)y	= Q(x)
						</me>.
					</p>
				</statement>

			</theorem>

			<p>
				Assuming your equation is in this standard form, this method introduces a function, called the <em>integrating factor</em>, which helps <q>complete the derivative</q> in the equation so that direct integration can be used to solve for the unknown function. The general process is summarized in the diagram below: 
			</p>

			<sidebyside valign="middle" margins="2% 10%" widths="10% 3% 66%">
				<p>
					Complete Derivative
				</p>
				<p>
					<me>
						\left\lbrace\vphantom{\begin{array}{c}\\\\\\\\\\\end{array}}\right.
					</me>
				</p>
				<p>
					<md>
						<mrow>
							\frac{dy}{dx} + 2 y = 5 						\,\,\,\quad\qquad 
							\amp \leftarrow \text{Multiply by } e^{2x}
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							e^{2x}\frac{dy}{dx}	+ \ 2e^{2x} y = 5e^{2x}		\quad
							\amp\leftarrow \text{Reverse Product Rule}
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\frac{d}{dx} \left[e^{2x} y \right] = 5e^{2x}	\,\,\qquad
							\amp\leftarrow \text{Direct Integration}
						</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>
			</sidebyside>

			<p>
				In this section, we'll break down this process, guiding you through finding the integrating factor, applying it to the equation, and then solving the equation by direct integration.
			</p>
		</introduction>

		<subsection><title> IF Method Steps </title>
			<p>
				The integrating factor method is a step-by-step process that works for all first-order linear differential equations and is summarized here.
			</p>

			<exploration xml:id="if-method"><title> Integrating Factor (IF) </title>

				<p>
					A differential equation that is first-order and linear can be written as
					<men xml:id="if-standard-eqn">
						\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x) y = Q(x)
					</men>
					and its general solution can be found through the following steps:
				</p>

				<dl width="narrow">
					<li xml:id="if-step-1"><title> Get the Integrating Factor </title>
						<p>
							Identify <m>P(x)</m> and compute the integrating factor
							<me>
								\mu = e\vphantom{\large|}^{\textstyle\int P(x)\ dx}.
							</me>
						</p>
					</li>
					<li xml:id="if-step-2"><title> Complete the Derivative </title>
						<p>
							Multiply both sides of <xref ref="if-standard-eqn"/> by <m>\mu</m> and reverse the product rule on the left side of  <xref ref="if-standard-eqn-with-mu"/> to get <xref ref="if-method-reversed-product-rule"/>.
						</p>
						<p>
							<mdn>
								<mrow xml:id="if-standard-eqn-with-mu">	\mu\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x) \mu y	\amp = Q(x)	\mu	</mrow>
								<mrow xml:id="if-method-reversed-product-rule">	\frac{d}{dx}\left[\mu \cdot y\right]	\amp = Q(x) \mu </mrow>
							</mdn>
						</p>
					</li>
					<li xml:id="if-step-3"><title> Apply Direct Integration </title>
						<p>
							Integrate both sides of equation <xref ref="if-method-reversed-product-rule"/> and solve for <m>y</m> to find the general solution.
						</p>
					</li>
				</dl>
			</exploration>

			<p>
				These three steps‚Äîconstructing the integrating factor, transforming the equation, and solving via integration‚Äîare the foundation of the method. Now, let's explore this process in action with examples.
			</p>
		</subsection>

		<subsection><title> Examples </title>
			<p>
				Let's start with a straightforward example to demonstrate how the integrating factor method works in practice. This example requires minimal manipulation since the equation is already in standard form.
			</p>

			<example xml:id="if-intro-exercise"><title> Minimal Example </title>
			
				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Find the general solution to the equation
						</p>
						<p>
							<me>
								y' + 6y = 1
							</me>.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>

				<p>
					<solution>

						<p>
							This is a first-order linear equation and it is already in standard form, so we proceed with the integrating factor method.
						</p>

						<p>
							<term> <xref ref="if-step-1" text="custom">Step 1: Integrating Factor</xref> </term>
						</p>

						<p>
							Identify <m>P(x)</m> and compute <m>\mu</m>
							<me>
								y' + \os{P}{\boxed{6}}\ y = 1\quad \rightarrow \quad \mu = e^{\int 6\ dx} = e^{6x}
							</me>
							<em>Recall, we can ignore the integration constant for the integrating factor.</em>
						</p>

						<p>
							<term> <xref ref="if-step-2" text="custom">Step 2: Reverse Product Rule</xref> </term>
						</p>

						<p>
							Multiply both sides by <m>e^{6x}</m> and reverse the product rule on the left side.
							<md>
								<mrow> e^{6x}y' + 6e^{6x}y						=\amp\ e^{6x}	</mrow>
								<mrow> \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{6x}\cdot y\right]	=\amp\ e^{6x}	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>

						<p>
							<term> <xref ref="if-step-3" text="custom">Step 3: Direct Integration</xref> </term>
						</p>

						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow> \int \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{6x}\cdot y\right]\ dx	=\amp\ \int e^{6x}\ dx			</mrow>
								<mrow> e^{6x}\cdot y									=\amp\ \frac{1}{6} e^{6x} + c	</mrow>
								<mrow> y												=\amp\ \frac{1}{6} + ce^{-6x}.	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
					</solution>
				</p>

			</example>

			<p>
				Now, let's tackle an equation that requires you to put it into standard form.
			</p>

			<example><title> Not initially in Standard Form </title>

				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Solve the equation
						</p>
						<p>
							<me>x^2y' = 5x^3 + 2x^3y </me>.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>

				<p>
					<solution>

						<p>
							This equation is first-order and linear, but is not in standard form, so we rewrite it as
							<me>
								y' - 2x y = 5x
							</me>
							and now we can apply the integrating factor method.
						</p>

						<p>
							<term> <xref ref="if-step-1" text="custom">Step 1: Integrating Factor</xref> </term>
						</p>

						<p>
							Identify <m>P(x)</m> and compute <m>\mu</m>
							<me>
								y' + \os{P}{\boxed{-2x}} y = 5x \quad \rightarrow \quad \mu = e^{\int -2x\ dx} = e^{-x^2}
							</me>
						</p>

						<p>
							<term> <xref ref="if-step-2" text="custom">Step 2: Reverse Product Rule</xref> </term>
						</p>

						<p>
							Multiply by <m>e^{-x^2}</m> and reverse the product rule on the left.
							<md>
								<mrow> e^{-x^2}y' - 2x e^{-x^2}y				=\amp\ 5xe^{-x^2}	</mrow>
								<mrow> \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{-x^2}\cdot y\right]	=\amp\ 5xe^{-x^2}	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>

						<p>
							<term> <xref ref="if-step-3" text="custom">Step 3: Direct Integration</xref> </term>
						</p>

						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow> \int \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{-x^2}\cdot y\right]dx	=\amp\ \int 5xe^{-x^2}dx		</mrow>
								<mrow> e^{-x^2}\cdot y									=\amp\ -\frac{5}{2}e^{-x^2} + c	</mrow>
								<mrow> y												=\amp\ -\frac{5}{2} + ce^{x^2}	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>

						<solution><title>Verify the Solution</title>

							
							<p>
								We have found the general solution, but it is worth remembering that we can verify our solution by substituting back into the original equation.
							</p>

							<sidebyside>
								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>	\ul{LHS}														</mrow>
										<mrow>	x^2y'															</mrow>
										<mrow>	x^2 \cdot \frac{d}{dx}\left( -\frac{5}{2} + ce^{x^2} \right)	</mrow>
										<mrow>	x^2 \cdot \left( 0 + 2cxe^{x^2} \right)							</mrow>
										<mrow>	2c x^3 e^{x^2}	\quad‚úÖ											</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>

								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>	\ul{RHS}											</mrow>
										<mrow>	5x^3 + 2x^3y										</mrow>
										<mrow>	5x^3 + 2x^3\left( -\frac{5}{2} + ce^{x^2} \right)	</mrow>
										<mrow>	5x^3 - 5x^3+ 2c x^3 e^{x^2}							</mrow>
										<mrow>	2c x^3 e^{x^2}	\quad‚úÖ								</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>
							</sidebyside>
							
							<p>
								The same result on the left &amp; right confirms our solution.
							</p>
						</solution>

					</solution>
				</p>

			</example>

			<p>
				In the next example, we'll work with an equation with a slightly more complex integrating factor.
			</p>

			<example><title> Integrating Factor that contains <m>\ln</m> </title>

				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Find the general solution to the differential equation
						</p>
						<p>
							<me>-\frac{4}{t^2} \cdot M =  t^5 - \frac{1}{t} \cdot\frac{dM}{dt} + 1.</me>
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>

				<p>
					<solution>

						<p>
							This equation is first-order and linear, but is not in standard form, so we rewrite it as
							<md>
								<mrow> \frac{1}{t}\cdot \frac{dM}{dt} - \frac{4}{t^2} \cdot M	\amp =  t^5 + 1	</mrow>
								<mrow> \frac{dM}{dt} + \us{P}{\boxed{-\frac{4}{t}}} \cdot M		\amp = t^6 + t	</mrow>
							</md>
							and now we can apply the integrating factor method.
						</p>

						<p>
							<sidebyside widths="20% 80%" valigns="top middle">
								
								<p>
									Integrating <m>\rightarrow</m> Factor
								</p>

								<p>
									<me>
										\mu = e\vphantom{\large|}^{\int \sfrac{-4}{t}\ dt} = e^{-4\ln t} = e^{\ln t^{-4}} = t^{-4} = \frac{1}{t^4}
									</me>
								</p>

							</sidebyside>
						</p>

						<p>
							<sidebyside widths="20% 80%" valigns="top middle">
								
								<p>
									Reverse <m>\rightarrow</m> Product Rule
								</p>

								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>
											\frac{1}{t^4} \frac{dM}{dt} - \frac{1}{t^4} \frac{4}{t} M
												\amp = \frac{1}{t^4}(t^6 + t)
										</mrow>
										<mrow>
											\frac{d}{dt}\left[ \frac{1}{t^4} M \right]
												\amp = t^2 + t^{-3}
										</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>

							</sidebyside>
						</p>

						<p>
							<sidebyside widths="20% 80%" valigns="top middle">
								
								<p>
									Direct <m>\rightarrow</m> Integration
								</p>

								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>
											\int \frac{d}{dt}\left[ \frac{1}{t^4} M \right]\ dt
												\amp = \int \left(t^2 + t^{-3}\right) dt
										</mrow>
										<mrow>
											\frac{1}{t^4} M
												\amp = \frac{1}{3}t^{3} - \frac{1}{2}t^{-2} + c
										</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>

							</sidebyside>
						</p>

						<p>
							<sidebyside widths="20% 80%" valigns="top middle">
								
								<p>
									General <m>\rightarrow</m> Solution
								</p>

								<p>
									<me>
										M = \frac{1}{3}t^{7} - \frac{1}{2}t^2 + ct^4
									</me>.
								</p>

							</sidebyside>
						</p>

					</solution>
				</p>

			</example>

			<p>
				The next two examples introduce scenarios where more advanced integration techniques are required to determine the solution.
			</p>

			<example>

				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Solve the differential equation:
							<me>x^2 y' - y = 1, \quad x > 0</me>.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>
				
				<p>
					<solution>

						<p>
							We put the equation in standard form to identify <m>P(x)</m> and compute the integrating factor.
						</p>
					
						<p>
							<me>
								y' + \us{P}{\boxed{-\frac{1}{x^2}}}\ y = \frac{1}{x^2} \quad \implies \quad
								\mu(x) = e^{\large \int -\sfrac{1}{x^2} dx} = e^{1/x}
							</me>
						</p>
					
						<p>
							Multiply by <m>e^{1/x}</m> and reverse the product rule on the left side.
							<md>
								<mrow> e^{1/x}y' - \frac{e^{1/x}}{x^2} y	=\amp\ \frac{e^{1/x}}{x^2}	</mrow>
								<mrow> \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{1/x} y\right]	=\amp\ \frac{e^{1/x}}{x^2}	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
					
						<p>
							Next, integrate both sides and use <m>u</m>-substitution on the right side.
							<sidebyside widths="10% 60% 25% 5%" valigns="top top top top">

								<p/>
								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>
											\int \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{1/x} y\right] dx
															=\amp\ \int \frac{e^{1/x}}{x^2} dx \qquad \rightarrow
										</mrow>
										<mrow>	e^{1/x} y	=\amp\ \int -e^u du </mrow>
										<mrow>	e^{1/x} y	=\amp\ -e^u + c		</mrow>
										<mrow>	e^{1/x} y	=\amp\ -e^{1/x} + c	</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>
								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>	u	=\amp\ \frac{1}{x}			</mrow>
										<mrow>	du	=\amp\ -\frac{1}{x^2} dx	</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>
								<p/>

							</sidebyside>
							
							Finally, solve for <m>y</m>:
							<md>
								<mrow> y = -1 + ce^{-1/x}	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>

					</solution>
				</p>

			</example>

			<example>

				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Solve the equation:
							<me> t^2 y' + 2ty = t^3 \ln t </me>.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>

				<p>
					<solution>

						<p>
							We put the equation in standard form to identify <m>P(x)</m> and compute the integrating factor.
						</p>
					
						<p>
							<me>
								y' + \us{P}{\boxed{\frac{2}{t}}}\ y = t \ln t \quad \implies \quad
								\mu(t) = e^{\large \int (\sfrac{2}{t})\, dt} = e^{2 \ln t} = t^2
							</me>
						</p>
					
						<p>
							Multiply by <m>e^{1/x}</m> and reverse the product rule on the left side.
							<md>
								<mrow> t^2 y' + 2t y					=\amp\ t^3 \ln t	</mrow>
								<mrow> \frac{d}{dt} \left[t^2 y\right]	=\amp\ t^3 \ln t	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
					
						<p>
							Now, integrate both sides and use <xref ref="ibp-formula" text="custom">integration by parts</xref> on the right.
						</p>

						<p>
							<sidebyside widths="3% 60% 35% 2%" valigns="top top top top">

								<p/>
								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>
											\int \frac{d}{dt} \big[t^2 y\big] dt =\amp\ \int t^3 \ln t\ dt
										</mrow>
										<mrow>	
											t^2 y	=
											\us{\textcolor{BurntOrange}{ u}}{\us{\textcolor{BurntOrange} \uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{\frac11}\ln t}}}
											\cdot
											\us{\textcolor{BurntOrange}{ v}}{\us{\textcolor{BurntOrange} \uparrow}{\ul{\frac14 t^4}}}
											\amp\ - \int
											\us{\textcolor{BurntOrange}{ v}}{\us{\textcolor{BurntOrange} \uparrow}{\ul{\frac14 t^4}}}
											\cdot
											\us{\textcolor{BurntOrange}{du}}{\us{\textcolor{BurntOrange} \uparrow}{\ul{\frac{1}{t}\ dt}}}
										</mrow>
										<mrow>	t^2 y	= \frac14 t^4 \ln t \amp\ - \frac14 \int  t^3\ dt					</mrow>
										<mrow>	t^2 y	= \frac{t^4}{4} \ln t \amp\ - \frac{t^4}{16} + c						</mrow>
										<mrow>		y	= \frac{t^2}{4} \ln t \amp\ - \frac{t^2}{16} + c\,t^2				</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>
								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>	\small\textcolor{BurntOrange}{				u = \ln t,}	\amp \ \ \small\textcolor{BurntOrange}{v = \frac14t^4}	</mrow>
										<mrow>	\small\textcolor{BurntOrange}{	du = \frac{1}{t}\ dt,}	\amp \ \ \small\textcolor{BurntOrange}{dv = t^3\ dt}	</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>
								<p/>

							</sidebyside>
						</p>

						<p>
							Finally, solve for <m>y</m>:
							<md>
								<mrow>	y = \frac{t^2}{4} \ln t - \frac{t^2}{16} + c\,t^2	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>

					</solution>
				</p>

			</example>

			<p>
				To conclude, we'll examining one more equation that involves trigonometric functions and an initial condition. Luckily, initial conditions are handled in the same way as in the separable equation case: find the general solution first, then apply the initial condition to determine the constant.
			</p>

			<example>

				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Solve the initial value problem.
							<me>
								A = \sec\theta A' - \frac{\theta e^{\large\theta^2 + \sin\theta}}{\cos \theta}, \hspace{1cm} A(0) = -\frac{1}{2}
							</me>.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>

				<p>
					<solution>

						<p>
							Let's put the equation in standard form. After replacing <m>\sec\theta</m> with <m>\frac{1}{\cos\theta}</m>and moving the <m>A'</m> term to the left, we have
						</p>
					
						<p>
							<me>
								\frac{1}{\cos\theta} A' - A = \frac{\theta e^{\large\theta^2 + \sin\theta}}{\cos \theta}
							</me>.
						</p>

						<p>
							Multiplying this by <m>\cos\theta</m> allows us to identify <m>P</m> and compute <m>\mu</m> as
							<me>
								A' + \us{P}{\boxed{- \cos\theta}}\ A = \theta e^{\large \theta^2 + \sin\theta}
								\quad \implies \quad
								\mu = e\vphantom{\large|}^{\large\int -\cos\theta d\theta} = e^{\large -\sin\theta}
							</me>
						</p>

						<p>
							Multiply by <m>e^{1/x}</m> and reverse the product rule on the left side.
							<md>
								<mrow> e^{\large -\sin\theta}A' - \cos\theta e^{\large -\sin\theta}A = \theta e^{\large\theta^2 + \sin\theta}	</mrow>
								<mrow> \frac{d}{d\theta}\left[ e^{\large -\sin\theta}A \right] = \theta e^{\large\theta^2}	</mrow>
							</md>.
						</p>
					
						<p>
							Integrate both sides and use <m>u</m>-substitution on the right side.
						</p>

						<p>
							<sidebyside widths="10% 60% 25% 5%" valigns="top top top top">

								<p/>
								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>
											\int \frac{d}{d\theta}\left[ e^{\large -\sin\theta}A \right] d\theta = \int \theta e^{\large\theta^2} d\theta
										</mrow>
										<mrow>
											e^{\large -\sin\theta}A = \frac{1}{2}e^{\large\theta^2} + c
										</mrow>
										<mrow>
											A = \frac{1}{2}e^{\large\theta^2 + \sin\theta} + ce^{\large\sin\theta}
										</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>
								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>	u	=\amp\ \theta^2			</mrow>
										<mrow>	du	=\amp\ 2\theta\ d\theta	</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>
								<p/>

							</sidebyside>

						</p>

						<p>
							Finally, we apply the initial condition to determine the constant <m>c</m>.
							<md>
								<mrow> -\frac{1}{2} = A(0) =\amp\ \frac{1}{2}e^{\large 0^2 + \sin(0)} + ce^{\large\sin(0)}	</mrow>
								<mrow>  =\amp\ \frac{1}{2}e^0 + ce^0 	</mrow>
								<mrow> =\amp\ \frac{1}{2} + c\quad \implies \quad c = -1	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>

						<p>
							We can then write the particular solution to the initial value problem.
							<me>A(\theta) = \frac{1}{2}e^{\large\theta^2 + \sin\theta} - e^{\large\sin\theta}</me>
						</p>
					</solution>
				</p>

			</example>

			<p>
				The integrating factor method is a versatile tool for solving first-order linear differential equations. By reducing the equation to a form that allows for direct integration, we can tackle a wide variety of problems with confidence. The examples in this chapter illustrate the broad applicability of this technique, and practice with a range of equations will solidify your understanding of how to use it effectively.
			</p>
