<chapter><title> Integrating Factor </title>

	<introduction>
			
			<p>
				This chapter introduces a powerful method for solving first-order linear differential equations known as the <em>Integrating Factor Method</em>. At its core, the method transforms a differential equation into an expression that can be solved through direct integration.
			</p>

			<p>
				To provide some intuition, consider a first-order linear differential equation:
			</p>

			<men xml:id="first-order-std-eqn">
				\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x)
			</men>

			<p>
				The goal is to rewrite this equation so that the left-hand side appears as the derivative of a product:
			</p>

			<men xml:id="di-eqn">
				\frac{d}{dx}[\mu(x) y] = \mu(x) Q(x)
			</men>

			<p>
				Here, <m>\mu(x)</m> is a function‚Äîcalled the <term>integrating factor</term>‚Äîthat we introduce specifically to make this transformation possible. Remarkably, such a function <em>always exists</em> for any first-order linear differential equation.
			</p>

	</introduction>

	<section><title> Completing the Derivative </title>

		<introduction>

			<p>
				Many algebraic equations can be solved by strategically grouping or combining terms. For example, consider the quadratic equation:
			</p>

			<me>
				x^2 + 10x + 25 = 0.
			</me>

			<p>
				One way to solve this equation is by factoring the left-hand side:
			</p>

			<me>
				(x + 5)^2 = 0.
			</me>

			<p>
				This way of grouping is useful because it allows us to solve for <m>x</m> directly by taking a square root.
			</p> 

			<p>
				Certain <term>first-order differential equations</term> benefit from a similar strategy. Instead of grouping algebraic terms into a square, we restructure a differential equation so that one side appears as a <em>single derivative</em>.
			</p>

			<p>
				For example, the equation:
			</p>

			<me>
				\frac{dy}{dx} + 2 y = 5
			</me>

			<p>
				can be rewritten as:
			</p>

			<me>
				\frac{d}{dx} [e^{2x} y] = 5e^{2x}.
			</me>

			<p>
				This transformation is powerful because it allows us to solve for <m>y</m> using <em>direct integration</em>, just as factoring allowed us to solve for <m>x</m> in the quadratic example.
			</p>

			<p>
				For this discussion we will refer to this process as <term>completing the derivative</term>. It consists of two key steps:
			</p>

			<p>
				<line> 1Ô∏è‚É£ <term>Introducing a missing function</term> needed to bundle terms into a derivative.</line>
				<line> 2Ô∏è‚É£ <term>Applying the reverse product rule</term> to construct a combined derivative.</line>
			</p>

			<p>
				This section explores both of these crucial steps. We begin by reviewing how to recognize and reverse the product rule within a differential equation. Then, we introduce cases where an additional function is required before the derivative can be constructed.
			</p>

		</introduction>

		<subsection><title> The Product Rule: A Quick Review </title>

			<p>
				The <term>product rule</term> is a fundamental differentiation technique that describes how to differentiate a product of two functions. If <m>f(x)</m> and <m>g(x)</m> are differentiable, their derivative satisfies:
			</p>

			<me>
				\frac{d}{dx} \left[ f(x) g(x) \right] = f(x) g'(x) + f'(x) g(x).
			</me>

			<p>
				Let's warm up with a few examples to refresh our memory.
			</p>

			<example><title> Forward Product Rule </title>

				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Compute each of the derivatives below.
						</p>
						<me>
							1. \quad \left[x^6 e^{3x}\right]' \hspace{5em} 2. \quad \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{x^3} y(x)\right]
						</me>
					</statement>
				</p>

				<solution>
					<p>
						Using the product rule:
						<ol>
							<li>
								<p>
									<m>\ds
										\left( x^6 e^{3x} \right)' =
										x^6 \cdot 3e^{3x} + 6x^5 e^{3x}
									</m>
								</p>
							</li>
							<li>
								<p>
									<m>\ds\frac{d}{dx}
										\left( e^{x^3} y(x) \right) =
										e^{x^3} y'(x) + 3x^2 e^{x^3} y(x)
									</m>
								</p>
							</li>
						</ol>
					</p>
				</solution>

			</example>

		</subsection>

		<subsection><title>Recognizing and Reversing the Product Rule</title>

			<p>
				Normally, we apply the product rule <em>in the forward direction</em>, starting with a product and differentiating it into separate terms. However, when solving differential equations, we often need to work <em>in reverse</em>‚Äîthat is, given a sum of terms, we determine whether they originate from a product rule derivative. This process is crucial in the integrating factor method, where we aim to rewrite an equation so that one side is a derivative of a product.
			</p>

			<p>
				For example, the expression:
			</p>

			<me>
				\frac{1}{x} \sin x + \cos x \ln x
			</me>

			<p>
				can be rewritten as:
			</p>

			<me>
				\left[ \sin x \cdot \ln x \right]'.
			</me>

			<p>
				The product rule expands the derivative of a product into two distinct terms:
			</p>

			<me>
				\frac{d}{dx} \left[ f \cdot g \right] = \ul{f \cdot g'} + \ul{f' \cdot g}.
			</me>

			<p>
				Recognizing when a product rule can be reversed involves:
			</p>

			<ol>
				<li>
					<p>
						Identifying two terms that could correspond to <m>f \cdot g'</m> and <m>f' \cdot g</m>.
					</p>
				</li>
				<li>
					<p>
						Choose candidate functions for <m>f</m> and <m>g</m> in separate terms.
					</p>
				</li>
				<li>
					<p>
						Using your choices, test that <m>[f \cdot g]'</m> produces the original terms.
					</p>
				</li>
			</ol>

			<p>
				Let's practice these steps with a few examples.
			</p>

			<example><title> Basic Reverse Product Rule (ver 1) </title>

				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Rewrite each expression in the form <m>[f(x) g(x)]'</m>.
							<me>
								1. \quad e^{x}\cos x + e^{x}\sin x \hspace{5em} 2. \quad \frac{e^{x^2}}{x} + 2 x \ln x e^{x^2}
							</me>
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>

				<solution>
					<p>
						<solution>

							<p>
								First, select an <m>f</m> and <m>g</m> in different terms, like so
							</p>

							<p>
								<me>
									e^x\
									\underset{\Large f}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}\cos x}}}
									+
									\underset{\Large g}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}e^x}}}\
									\sin x
								</me>.
							</p>

							<p>
								For this to work, <m>f'</m> should be <m>\sin x</m>, next to our choosen <m>g</m>. However,
								<me>
									f' = [\cos x]' = -\sin x
								</me>
							</p>

							<p>
								So this labeling doesn't work and we instead try the labels
							</p>

							<p>
								<me>
									\underset{\Large f}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}e^x}}}\
									\cos x
									+
									e^x\
									\underset{\Large g}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}\sin x}}}
								</me>.
							</p>

							<p>
								Noting that <m>f' = [e^x]' = e^x</m> is the other part of the second term and <m>g' = [\sin x]' = \cos x</m> is in the first term confirms this is a valid product rule. Therefore, we can reverse the product rule as
							</p>

							<me>
								e^x \cos x + e^x \sin x = [e^x \cos x]'
							</me>.


						</solution>

						<solution>

							<p>
								To make this easier, let's separate the terms as
								<me>
									\frac{1}{x} e^{x^2} + \ln x (2 x e^{x^2})
								</me>.
							</p>
							
							<p>
								So, as before, we first set <m>f</m> and <m>g</m>
							</p>

							<me>
								\frac{1}{x}\
								\underset{\Large f}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}e^{x^2}}}}
								+
								\underset{\Large g}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}\ln x}}}\
								2 x e^{x^2}
							</me>,

							<p>
								then verify the derivatives of <m>f</m> and <m>g</m> are in the other terms,
							</p>

							<me>
								f' = \left[e^{x^2}\right]' = 2 x e^{x^2} \quad ‚úÖ \qquad g' = \left[\ln x\right]' = \frac{1}{x} \quad ‚úÖ
							</me>.

							<p>
								Therefore,
							</p>

							<me>
								\frac{e^{x^2}}{x} + 2 x \ln x e^{x^2} = \left[e^{x^2} \ln x\right]'
							</me>.
						</solution>
					</p>
				</solution>
				
			</example>

			<example><title> Basic Reverse Product Rule </title>

				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Rewrite each expression in the form <m>[f(x) g(x)]'</m>.
							<me>
								1. \quad e^{x}\cos x + e^{x}\sin x \hspace{5em} 2. \quad \frac{e^{x^2}}{x} + 2 x \ln x e^{x^2}
							</me>
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>

				<solution>
					<p>
						<solution>

							<p>
								First, select an <m>f</m> and <m>g</m> in different terms, like so
							</p>

							<p>
								<me>
									e^x\
									\underset{\Large f}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}\cos x}}}
									+
									\underset{\Large g}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}e^x}}}\
									\sin x
								</me>.
							</p>

							<p>
								Based on these choices, we test <m>[f\cdot g]'</m>,
							</p>

							<me>
								[e^x \cos x]' = e^x \cos x + e^x (-\sin x) \neq e^x \cos x + e^x \sin x \quad ‚ùå 
							</me>
							
							<p>
								and see that this choice doesn't work. Switching our labels to
							</p>

							<me>
								\underset{\Large f}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}e^x}}}\
								\cos x
								+
								e^x\
								\underset{\Large g}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}\sin x}}}
							</me>,

							<p>
								identifies the correct <m>f</m> and <m>g</m>, since
							</p>

							<me>
								[e^x \sin x]' = e^x \sin x + e^x \cos x = e^x \cos x + e^x \sin x \quad ‚úÖ
							</me>.

							<p>
								Therefore, 
								<me>
									e^x \cos x + e^x \sin x = [e^x \cos x]' \vphantom{\frac11}
								</me>.
							</p>

						</solution>

						<solution>
							
							<p>
								First, rearrange the terms a bit and set <m>f</m> and <m>g</m>
							</p>

							<me>
								\frac{1}{x}\cdot
								\underset{\Large f}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}\frac{e^{x^2}}{1}}}}
								+ 2 x 
								\underset{\Large g}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}\ln x}}}\
								e^{x^2}
							</me>.

							<p>
								Next, verify the derivative of <m>f \cdot g</m>,
							</p>

							<me>
								\left[e^{x^2}\ln x\right]' = e^{x^2}\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) + e^{x^2}(2 x) \ln x \os{‚úÖ}{=} \frac{e^{x^2}}{x} + 2 x \ln x e^{x^2}
							</me>.

							<p>
								Therefore,
							</p>

							<me>
								\frac{e^{x^2}}{x} + 2 x \ln x e^{x^2} = \left[e^{x^2} \ln x\right]'
							</me>.
						</solution>
					</p>
				</solution>
				
			</example>

			<p>
				The next example involves an expression that contains a variable, making it more relevant to differential equations.
			</p>

			<example xml:id="reversed-product-rule-example"><title> Reversed Product Rule with a Variable </title>

				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Rewrite each expression in the form <m>[f(x) y(x)]'</m>.
							<me>
								1. \quad e^{9x}\frac{dy}{dx} + 9y e^{9x} \hspace{5em} 2. \quad e^{1/x}y' - \frac{e^{1/x}}{x^2}y
							</me>
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>

				<solution>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								Since <m>dy/dx</m> and <m>y</m> are in separate terms, setting <m>g</m> to <m>y</m> gives you <m>g'</m> and <m>f</m> for free since
							</p>

							<me>
								\underset{\Large f}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{e^{9x}}}}\
								\underset{\Large g'}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}\frac{dy}{dx}}}}
								+
								\underset{\Large g}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}y}}}\
								(9 e^{9x})
							</me>.

							<p>
								The only thing you need to check is
								<me>
									f' = [e^{9x}]' = 9e^{9x} \quad ‚úÖ
								</me>
								which is sitting right next to <m>g</m>. Therefore, we have
							</p>

							<me>
								e^{9x}\frac{dy}{dx} + 9 e^{9x} y = \left[e^{9x} y\right]'
							</me>.

						</solution>

						<solution>

							<p>
								Again, set <m>g</m> to <m>y</m> and the rest fall into place as
							</p>

							<me>
								\underset{\Large f}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{e^{1/x}}}}\
								\underset{\Large g'}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}y'}}}
								+
								\left(-\frac{e^{1/x}}{x^2}\right)\
								\underset{\Large g}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}y}}}
							</me>.

							<p>
								The only thing left to do is verify the derivative of <m>f</m>:
								<me>
									f' = \left[e^{1/x}\right]' = e^{1/x}\left[\frac{1}{x}\right]^\prime = e^{1/x}\left(-\frac{1}{x^2}\right) = -\frac{e^{1/x}}{x^2} \quad ‚úÖ
								</me>.
							</p>

							<p>
								So, we can reverse the product rule as
							</p>

							<me>
								e^{1/x}y' - \frac{e^{1/x}}{x^2}y = \left[e^{1/x} y\right]'
							</me>.

						</solution>
					</p>
				</solution>
				
			</example>
			
			<p>
				While reversing the product rule is not standard exercise, they are important for developing the intuition needed to understand the integrating factor method, as we will soon see.
			</p>

		</subsection>

		<subsection><title> A Complete Product Rule </title>

			<p>
				Now that you have seen how to combine two terms into a single derivative by reversing the product rule, you are ready to see how this could help you solve a differential equation. Suppose you are given the following equation
			</p>

			<men xml:id="reverse-product-rule-DE">
				\ub{e^{2x} \frac{dy}{dx} + 2e^{2x} y}_{\text{expanded product rule?}} = 5e^{2x}.
			</men>

			<p>
				Seeing the two terms with a derivative suggests that it may be possible to apply a reverse product rule to the left side. In fact, it can and it is not hard to verify that equation <xref ref="reverse-product-rule-DE"/> is equivalent to
			</p>

			<men xml:id="reverse-product-rule-DE-solution">
				\frac{d}{dx} [ e^{2x} y ] = 5e^{2x}.
			</men>

			<p>
				This transformation is powerful because it allows us to solve the equation through direct integration, as illustrated in the next example.
			</p>

			<example xml:id="reversing-the-product-rule-in-a-DE-example"><title> Reversed Product Rule in a Differential Equation </title>

				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Find the general solution of the equation
							<men xml:id="reversing-the-product-rule-in-a-DE-example-eqn">
								e^{x^4} \frac{dy}{dx} + 4x^3e^{x^4} y = 5x
							</men>
							by rewriting the left-side as a single derivative and then direct integration.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>

				<p>
					<solution>

						<p>
							To reverse the product rule on the left-side, set <m>g</m> to <m>y</m>, as before, to get 
						</p>

						<me>
							\underset{\Large f}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{e^{x^4}}}}\
							\underset{\Large g'}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}\frac{dy}{dx}}}}
							+
							\underset{\Large g}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}y}}}\
							(4x^3 e^{x^4})
						</me>

						<p>
							and checking <m>\ f' = [e^{x^4}]' = 4x^3 e^{x^4}\ </m> confirms that the left side can be combined as a single derivative:
						</p>

						<me>
							e^{x^4} \frac{dy}{dx} + 4x^3e^{x^4} y = \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{x^4} y\right]
						</me>.

						<p>
							Now, we update the differential equation <xref ref="reversing-the-product-rule-in-a-DE-example-eqn"/> and integrate as follows:
						</p>

						<md>
							<mrow>			\frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{x^4} \cdot y \right]	\amp = 5x										</mrow>
							<mrow>	\int \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{x^4} \cdot y \right]\ dx	\amp = \int 5x \ dx								</mrow>
							<mrow>											e^{x^4} y	\amp = \frac{5}{2} x^2 + c						</mrow>
							<mrow>													y	\amp = \frac{5}{2} x^2 e^{-x^4} + c e^{-x^4}	</mrow>
						</md>.

					</solution>
				</p>

			</example>

			<p>
				This example shows a differential equation that conveniently had all the pieces it needed to reverse the product rule. Unfortunately, equations are rarely presented in such a complete form and an additional component must be provided before an appoach like this will work. 
			</p>

			<p>
				To help us see how adding something to an equation can help us alter its form and solve it, we will first look at an analogy from algebra in the next section.
			</p>

		</subsection>

		<subsection><title> An Analogy: Completing the Square </title>

			<p>
				Suppose you wanted to solve the following quadratic equation
			</p>

			<men xml:id="quadratic-example">
				x^2 + 6x = -1.
			</men>

			<p>
				You could solve it using the quadratic formula. However, another approach is to <q>complete the square</q> by strategically placing a number on the left side which turns it into a perfect square. Following the rule from <q>completing the square</q>, we know <m>\left(\frac{6}{2}\right)^2 = 9</m> does the trick, so we add it to both sides to get
			</p>

			<me>
				\ub{\os{{\large\text{incomplete}}}{\ob{x^2 + 6x}^{\large\text{square}}} + 9}_{\large\text{complete square}} = -1 + 9.
			</me>

			<p>
				Completing the square gets it name because it tells you the missing piece needed to factor our equation into the form
			</p>

			<men xml:id="perfect-square">
				(x + 3)^2 = 8.
			</men>

			<p>
				Now, taking a square root releases <m>x</m>, leading directly to the solutions
			</p>

			<me>
				x + 3 = \pm\sqrt{8} \quad \implies \quad x = -3 \pm 2\sqrt{2}.
			</me>

			<p>
				Just as completing the square tells you that <m>9</m> is the missing piece that makes a <q>perfect</q> square in a quadratic equation, the integrating factor method tells you the missing piece you need to construct a <q>perfect</q> derivative in a differential equation.
			</p>

		</subsection>

		<subsection><title> An Incomplete Product Rule </title>

			<p>
				As mention to in the previous sections, most first-order linear differential equations must be modified before its terms can be combined into a single derivative. To make sense of this, we will look at a concrete example using the differential equation
			</p>

			<sidebyside valign="middle" widths="5% 15% 5% 45% 30%">
				<p/>
				<p>
					incomplete derivative
				</p>
				<p>
					<me>
						\rightarrow
					</me>
				</p>
				<p>
					<me>
						\frac{dy}{dx} + 6y = -1
					</me>.
				</p>
				<p/>
			</sidebyside>

			<p>
				In this form, the left side resembles an expanded product rule, but if you attempt to reverse it you will see that no choice of <m>f</m> and <m>g</m> will work. However, if you multiply both sides by <m>e^{6x}</m>, the equation becomes
			</p>

			<sidebyside valign="middle" widths="5% 15% 5% 45% 30%">
				<p/>
				<p>
					complete derivative
				</p>
				<p>
					<me>
						\rightarrow
					</me>
				</p>
				<p>
					<me>
						e^{6x}\frac{dy}{dx} + 6e^{6x}y = -e^{6x}
					</me>.
				</p>
				<p/>
			</sidebyside>

			<p>
				The inclusion of <m>e^{6x}</m> provides the missing piece needed to reverse a product rule and update the equation as
			</p>

			<sidebyside valign="middle" widths="5% 15% 5% 45% 30%">
				<p/>
				<p>
					perfect derivative
				</p>
				<p>
					<me>
						\rightarrow
					</me>
				</p>
				<p>
					<me>
						\frac{d}{dx} [e^{6x} y] = -e^{6x}
					</me>.
				</p>
				<p/>
			</sidebyside>

			<p>
				With the left side as a single derivative, we can integrate both sides to find <m>y</m>.
			</p>

			<theorem><title> Analogy: A Side-by-Side Comparison </title>
				<statement>
					<p>
						The table below compares completing the square to the integrating factor method, highlighting their similarities:
						<tabular halign="center">
							<col right="minor"/>
							<col right="minor"/>
							<row bottom="minor">
								<cell/>
								<cell>Completing the Square</cell>
								<cell>
									<line>Integrating Factor Method</line>
									<line>(Completing the Derivative)</line>
								</cell>
							</row>
							<row><cell/><cell/><cell/></row>
							<row><cell/><cell/><cell/></row>
							<row>
								<cell>Goal</cell>
								<cell>Solve for <m>x</m></cell>
								<cell>Solve for <m>y</m></cell>
							</row>
							<row><cell/><cell/><cell/></row>
							<row><cell/><cell/><cell/></row>
							<row>
								<cell>Equation</cell>
								<cell><m>x^2 + 6x = -1</m></cell>
								<cell><m>\ds\frac{dy}{dx} + 6y = -1</m></cell>
							</row>
							<row><cell/><cell/><cell/></row>
							<row><cell/><cell/><cell/></row>
							<row>
								<cell>
									<line>Missing</line>
									<line>Piece</line>
								</cell>
								<cell><line><m>9</m></line><line><em>(added onto both sides)</em></line></cell>
								<cell><line><m>e^{6x}</m></line><line><em>(multiplied onto both sides)</em></line></cell>
							</row>
							<row><cell/><cell/><cell/></row>
							<row><cell/><cell/><cell/></row>
							<row>
								<cell>
									<line>Modified</line>
									<line>Equation</line>
								</cell>
								<cell><m>x^2 + 6x + \boxed{9} = -1 + \boxed{9}</m></cell>
								<cell><m>\ds\boxed{e^{6x}}\frac{dy}{dx} + 6\boxed{e^{6x}}y = -1 \boxed{e^{6x}}</m></cell>
							</row>
							<row><cell/><cell/><cell/></row>
							<row><cell/><cell/><cell/></row>
							<row>
								<cell>
									<line>Completed</line>
									<line>Equation</line>
								</cell>
								<cell><m>(x + 3)^2 = 8</m></cell>
								<cell><m>\ds\frac{d}{dx} [e^{6x} y] = -e^{6x}</m></cell>
							</row>
						</tabular>
					</p>
				</statement>
			</theorem>

		</subsection>

		<conclusion>

			<p>
				In sum, solving a differential equation using the integrating factor method is similar to solving a quadratic equation by 
				<q>completing the square</q>. In both cases, we strategically augment the equations by introducing a missing component 
				to rewrite it in a form that is easier to solve.
			</p>

			<p>
				In the next section, we will learn that this missing factor is known as the <term>integrating factor</term> and we will derive a general formula for finding it.
			</p>

		</conclusion>

	</section>

	<section><title> Finding the Integrating Factor </title>

		<introduction>

			<p>
				In the previous section, we saw that rewriting a first-order linear differential equation so that one side is a <q>completed</q> derivative allows us to solve it using direct integration. However, many equations do not initially have this form.
			</p>

			<p>
				The key to overcoming this issue is finding a function‚Äîcalled the <term>integrating factor</term>‚Äîthat, when multiplied onto the equation, transforms it into a single derivative. That is, given the standard form:
			</p>

			<men xml:id="if-standard-form00">
				\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x) y = Q(x),
			</men>

			<p>
				we seek a function <m>\mu(x)</m> such that:
			</p>

			<men xml:id="if-completed-derivative">
				\frac{d}{dx} [\mu(x) y] = \mu(x) Q(x).
			</men>

			<p>
				This section focuses on systematically determining <m>\mu(x)</m> for any first-order linear differential equation. We begin with an example that illustrates the core idea, then generalize the approach to derive a general formula.
			</p>

			<p>
				By the end of this section, you will be able to:
			</p>

			<p>
				<line> 1Ô∏è‚É£ Recognize when a first-order linear equation requires an integrating factor.</line>
				<line> 2Ô∏è‚É£ Use a step-by-step approach to determine <m>\mu(x)</m>.</line>
				<line> 3Ô∏è‚É£ Understand why the integrating factor method always works.</line>
			</p>

			<p>
				Before moving on, remember these key points:
			</p>

			<p>
				<line> ‚úÖ A derivative can be <q>completed</q> in <em>any</em> first-order linear equation.</line>
				<line> ‚úÖ The equation must be multiplied by a function to complete the derivative.</line>
				<line> ‚úÖ The completed derivative is formed by reversing the product rule.</line>
			</p>

			<p>
				Now, let's explore how to find the integrating factor step by step.
			</p>

		</introduction>

		<subsection><title> A Motivating Example </title>
			
			<introduction>
				<p>
					In the previous section, we saw that certain first-order linear differential equations can be rewritten so that the left-hand side is a single derivative. However, most equations do not initially have this structure. Instead, they are missing a crucial function that allows us to apply the <em>product rule in reverse</em>.
				</p>

				<p>
					The goal now is to illustrate how to determine this missing function‚Äîknown as the <term>integrating factor</term>‚Äîso that we can rewrite one of the sides as a single derivative.
				</p>

				<p>
					Consider the equation:
				</p>

				<men xml:id="if-example-incomplete-eqn">
					\frac{dy}{dx} + 2y = 5.
				</men>

				<p>
					The claim is that the left side of this equation is currently an incomplete product rule that can be completed and reversed by multiplying a function <m>\mu</m> onto both sides of the equation. In short, this allows us to rewrite <xref ref="if-example-incomplete-eqn"/> as
				</p>

				<me>
					\frac{d}{dx} \left[\mu y \right] = 5\mu.
				</me>

				<p>
					However, the question still remains: <em>Which function, <m>\mu</m>, does this?</em> Let's find out.
				</p>

			</introduction>

			<subsubsection><title> Finding <m>\mu(x)</m>: A Step-by-Step Breakdown </title>

		<subsection><title> The General Case </title>

			<introduction>

				<p>
					We are now ready to determine the general formula for <m>\mu</m> given any first-order linear differential equation in the standard form,
				</p>

				<men xml:id="if-general-standard-form">
					\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x).
				</men>

				<p>
					Again, we seek a function, <m>\mu</m>, that we multiply onto this equation to transform it into
				</p>

				<men xml:id="if-combined-form">
					\frac{d}{dx} \left[ \mu y \right] = \mu Q(x).
				</men>

			</introduction>

			<subsubsection><title> Deriving the Integrating Factor </title>

				<p>
					As with the motivating example, we start by multiplying both sides of the differential equation by an unknown function <m>\mu</m>:
				</p>

				<men xml:id="if-mu-standard-form">
					\mu \frac{dy}{dx} + \mu P(x) y = \mu Q(x).
				</men>

				<p>
					The left side can only turn into <m>\frac{d}{dx} \left[ \mu y \right]</m>, if it is also equal to the expanded form of this derivative. In particular, we need:
				</p>

				<me>
					\ob{
						\mu \frac{dy}{dx} + \us{\Large üö©}{\ul{\mu P(x)}}\ y
					}^{\large\text{left-side of }<xref ref="if-mu-standard-form"/>} = 
					\ob{
						\mu \frac{dy}{dx} + \us{\Large üö©}{\ul{\frac{d\mu}{dx}}}\ y
					}^{\large\text{expanded }\frac{d}{dx} \left[ \mu y \right]} 
				</me>.

				<p>
					As you can see, the two sides are identical, expect for the flagged üö© expressions. So, to rewrite the left side of <xref ref="if-mu-standard-form"/> as the combined derivative, <m>\frac{d}{dx} \left[ \mu y \right]</m>, the <m>\mu</m> that works must also satisfy
				</p>

				<men xml:id="if-sov-eqn">
					\frac{d\mu}{dx} = \mu P(x).
				</men>

				<p>
					Therefore, <m>\mu</m>, is a solution to <xref ref="if-sov-eqn"/>, which we can solve using separation of variables, as follows:
				</p>

				<sidebyside widths="30% 70%" valigns="top middle">
					<p>
						Separate Variables:
					</p>
					<p>
						<me>
							\frac{1}{\mu}\ d\mu = P(x)\ dx
						</me>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<sidebyside widths="30% 70%" valigns="top middle">
					<p>
						Integrate Both Sides:
					</p>
					<p>
						<md>
							<mrow>	\int \frac{1}{\mu}\ d\mu	\amp = \int P(x)\ dx	</mrow>
							<mrow>	\ln|\mu|					\amp = \int P(x) dx		</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<aside>
					<p>
						<term>üìå Note.</term> The absolute value is dropped since the sign of the integrating factor is taken to be positive by convention.
					</p>
				</aside>

				<sidebyside widths="30% 70%" valigns="middle middle">
					<p>
						Solve for <m>\mu(x)</m>:
					</p>
					<p>
						<me>
							\mu(x) = e\vphantom{\sum}^{{\Large\int} P(x)\ dx}
						</me>.
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<assemblage xml:id="integrating-factor-general-formula"><title> Integrating Factor: General Formula </title>
					<p>
						The <term>integrating factor</term> for a first-order linear differential equation in standard form
						<me>
							y' + P(x)y = Q(x)
						</me>
						is given by	
						<m>
							\ \ds\mu(x) = e\vphantom{\sum}^{{\Large\int} P(x)\ dx}
						</m>.
					</p>
				</assemblage>

				<p>
					Now that we have derived the general formula, let's apply it to a few examples.
				</p>

			</subsubsection>

			<subsubsection><title> Examples of Integrating Factors </title>
				
				<example xml:id="integrating-factor-basic-example"><title> Basic Integrating Factor </title>

					<p>
						<statement>
							<p>
								Find the integrating factor <m>\mu(x)</m> for the differential equation:
							</p>

							<me>
								\frac{dy}{dx} + 4y = x.
							</me>
						</statement>
					</p>
				
					<solution>
						<p>
							The equation is in standard form with <m>P(x) = 4</m>. Using the formula:
						</p>

						<me>
							\mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) dx} = e^{\int 4 dx} = e^{4x}
						</me>.

						<p>
							Thus, the integrating factor is <m>e^{4x}</m>.
						</p>
					</solution>
				</example>

				<example xml:id="integrating-factor-log-example"><title> Integrating Factor with a Log Function </title>

					<p>
						<statement>
							<p>
								Determine the integrating factor <m>\mu(x)</m> for the differential equation:
							</p>

							<me>
								\frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{2}{x} y = x^3, \quad x > 0.
							</me>
						</statement>
					</p>

					<solution>
						<p>
							The equation is in standard form with <m>P(x) = \frac{2}{x}</m>. Using the formula:
						</p>

						<me>
							\mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) dx} = e^{\int \frac{2}{x} dx}.
						</me>

						<p>
							Evaluating the integral:
						</p>

						<me>
							\mu(x) = e^{2 \ln x} = x^2.
						</me>

						<p>
							Thus, the integrating factor is <m>x^2</m>.
						</p>
					</solution>
				</example>

				<example xml:id="integrating-factor-trig-example"><title> Integrating Factor with a Trigonometric Function </title>

					<p>
						<statement>
							<p>
								Compute the integrating factor <m>\mu(x)</m> for the differential equation:
							</p>

							<me>
								\frac{dy}{dx} + \tan x \cdot y = \cos x.
							</me>
						</statement>
					</p>

					<solution>
						<p>
							The equation is in standard form with <m>P(x) = \tan x</m>. Using the formula:
						</p>

						<me>
							\mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) dx} = e^{\int \tan x dx}.
						</me>

						<p>
							Evaluating the integral:
						</p>

						<me>
							\mu(x) = e^{\ln | \sec x |} = \sec x.
						</me>

						<p>
							Thus, the integrating factor is <m>\sec x</m>.
						</p>
					</solution>
				</example>

			</subsubsection>

			<conclusion>
			
				<p>
					In this section, we derived the general formula for the integrating factor and demonstrated how to compute it with a few examples. The key takeaway is that the integrating factor always takes the form
				</p>

				<me>
					\mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) dx}
				</me>,

				<p>
					and once we have it, we can multiply it onto our equation to rewrite the left-hand side as a single derivative. In the next section, we will apply this approach step-by-step to solve first-order linear differential equations.
				</p>

			</conclusion>

	<section><title> Integrating Factor Method </title>	
		
		<introduction>

			<p>
				A Quick Recap. The Integrating Factor Story so far ...
			</p>

			<p>
				<sidebyside widths="5% 5% 90%" valign="top">
					<p/><p>üìå</p>
					<p>Multiplying by the integrating factor <m>\mu(x)</m> transforms a first-order linear equation into a form where the left-hand side is a single derivative.</p>
				</sidebyside>
				<sidebyside widths="5% 5% 90%" valign="top">
					<p/><p>üìå</p>
					<p>The integrating factor is always given by <m>\mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) dx}</m>, which is found by solving a separable differential equation.</p>
				</sidebyside>
				<sidebyside widths="5% 5% 90%" valign="top">
					<p/><p>üìå</p>
					<p>Once rewritten in completed derivative form, the equation can be solved using <em>direct integration</em>.</p>
				</sidebyside>
			</p>

			<p>
				The <term>Integrating Factor (IF) Method</term> is an essential technique for solving <em>any</em> first-order linear differential equation. Such equations can <em>always</em> be written in the <term>standard form</term> given by
			</p>

			<men xml:id="if-standard-form">
				y' + P(x)\ y = Q(x)
			</men>.

			<theorem xml:id="std-form-explained"><title> <em>1st-Order Linear Standard Form Explained</em> </title>
			
				<statement>
					<p>
						Since a general linear differential equation looks like
						<me>
							a_n(x)y^{(n)} + \cdots + a_2(x)y'' + a_1(x)y' + a_0(x)y = f(x)
						</me>,
						the first-order case (<m>n=1</m>), simplifies to
						<me>
							a_1(x)y' + a_0(x)y = f(x)
						</me>.
						Dividing by the coefficient of <m>y'</m>, and renaming the new coefficients as <m>P</m> and <m>Q</m>, gives the standard form
						<me>
							y' + \us{P(x)}{\ub{\frac{a_0(x)}{a_1(x)}}}y	= \us{Q(x)}{\ub{\frac{f(x)}{a_1(x)}}}	
							\quad \implies \quad
							y' + P(x)y	= Q(x)
						</me>.
					</p>
				</statement>

			</theorem>

			<p>
				Once in standard form, this method determines a function, called the <em>integrating factor</em>, which <q>completes the derivative</q> on the left side of the equation so that direct integration can be used to solve for the unknown function. The complete process using a concrete example is summarized in the diagram below: 
			</p>

			<sidebyside valign="middle" margins="2% 10%" widths="10% 3% 66%">
				<p>
					Complete Derivative
				</p>
				<p>
					<me>
						\left\lbrace\vphantom{\begin{array}{c}\\\\\\\\\\\end{array}}\right.
					</me>
				</p>
				<p>
					<md>
						<mrow>
							\frac{dy}{dx} + 2 y = 5 						\,\,\,\quad\qquad 
							\amp \leftarrow \text{Multiply by } e^{2x}
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							e^{2x}\frac{dy}{dx}	+ \ 2e^{2x} y = 5e^{2x}		\quad
							\amp\leftarrow \text{Reverse Product Rule}
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\frac{d}{dx} \left[e^{2x} y \right] = 5e^{2x}	\,\,\qquad
							\amp\leftarrow \text{Direct Integration}
						</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>
			</sidebyside>

			<p>
				In this section, we'll break down this process, guiding you through finding the integrating factor, applying it to the equation, and then solving the equation by direct integration.
			</p>

		</introduction>

		<subsection><title> IF Method Steps </title>

			<p>
				The integrating factor method is a step-by-step process that works for all first-order linear differential equations and is summarized here.
			</p>

			<exploration xml:id="if-method"><title> Integrating Factor (IF) </title>

				<p>
					A differential equation that is first-order and linear can be written as
					<men xml:id="if-standard-eqn">
						\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x) y = Q(x)
					</men>
					and its general solution can be found through the following steps:
				</p>

				<dl width="narrow">
					<li xml:id="if-step-1"><title> Get the Integrating Factor </title>
						<p>
							Identify <m>P(x)</m> and compute the integrating factor
							<me>
								\mu = e\vphantom{\large|}^{\textstyle\int P(x)\ dx}.
							</me>
						</p>
					</li>
					<li xml:id="if-step-2"><title> Complete the Derivative </title>
						<p>
							Multiply both sides of <xref ref="if-standard-eqn"/> by <m>\mu</m> and reverse the product rule on the left side of <xref ref="if-standard-eqn-with-mu"/> to get <xref ref="if-method-reversed-product-rule"/>.
						</p>
						<p>
							<mdn>
								<mrow xml:id="if-standard-eqn-with-mu">	\mu\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x) \mu y	\amp = Q(x)	\mu	</mrow>
								<mrow xml:id="if-method-reversed-product-rule">	\frac{d}{dx}\left[\mu \cdot y\right]	\amp = Q(x) \mu </mrow>
							</mdn>
						</p>
					</li>
					<li xml:id="if-step-3"><title> Apply Direct Integration </title>
						<p>
							Integrate both sides of equation <xref ref="if-method-reversed-product-rule"/> and solve for <m>y</m> to find the general solution.
						</p>
					</li>
				</dl>
			</exploration>

			<p>
				These three steps‚Äîconstructing the integrating factor, transforming the equation, and solving via integration‚Äîare the foundation of the method. Now, let's explore this process in action with examples.
			</p>

		</subsection>

		<subsection><title> Examples </title>

			<p>
				Let's start with a straightforward example to demonstrate how the integrating factor method works in practice. This example requires minimal manipulation since the equation is already in standard form.
			</p>

			<example xml:id="if-intro-exercise"><title> Minimal Example </title>
			
				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Find the general solution to the equation
						</p>
						<p>
							<me>
								y' + 6y = 1
							</me>.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>

				<p>
					<solution>

						<p>
							This is a first-order linear equation and it is already in standard form, so we proceed with the integrating factor method.
						</p>

						<p>
							<term> <xref ref="if-step-1" text="custom">Step 1: Integrating Factor</xref> </term>
						</p>

						<p>
							Identify <m>P(x)</m> and compute <m>\mu</m>
							<me>
								y' + \os{P}{\boxed{6}}\ y = 1\quad \rightarrow \quad \mu = e^{\int 6\ dx} = e^{6x}
							</me>
							<em>Recall, we can ignore the integration constant for the integrating factor.</em>
						</p>

						<p>
							<term> <xref ref="if-step-2" text="custom">Step 2: Reverse Product Rule</xref> </term>
						</p>

						<p>
							Multiply both sides by <m>e^{6x}</m> and reverse the product rule on the left side.
							<md>
								<mrow> e^{6x}y' + 6e^{6x}y						=\amp\ e^{6x}	</mrow>
								<mrow> \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{6x}\cdot y\right]	=\amp\ e^{6x}	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>

						<p>
							<term> <xref ref="if-step-3" text="custom">Step 3: Direct Integration</xref> </term>
						</p>

						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow> \int \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{6x}\cdot y\right]\ dx	=\amp\ \int e^{6x}\ dx			</mrow>
								<mrow> e^{6x}\cdot y									=\amp\ \frac{1}{6} e^{6x} + c	</mrow>
								<mrow> y												=\amp\ \frac{1}{6} + ce^{-6x}.	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
					</solution>
				</p>

			</example>

			<p>
				Now, let's tackle an equation that requires you to put it into standard form.
			</p>

			<example><title> Not initially in Standard Form </title>

				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Solve the equation
						</p>
						<p>
							<me>x^2y' = 5x^3 + 2x^3y </me>.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>

				<p>
					<solution>

						<p>
							This equation is first-order and linear, but is not in standard form, so we rewrite it as
							<me>
								y' - 2x y = 5x
							</me>
							and now we can apply the integrating factor method.
						</p>

						<p>
							<term> <xref ref="if-step-1" text="custom">Step 1: Integrating Factor</xref> </term>
						</p>

						<p>
							Identify <m>P(x)</m> and compute <m>\mu</m>
							<me>
								y' + \os{P}{\boxed{-2x}} y = 5x \quad \rightarrow \quad \mu = e^{\int -2x\ dx} = e^{-x^2}
							</me>
						</p>

						<p>
							<term> <xref ref="if-step-2" text="custom">Step 2: Reverse Product Rule</xref> </term>
						</p>

						<p>
							Multiply by <m>e^{-x^2}</m> and reverse the product rule on the left.
							<md>
								<mrow> e^{-x^2}y' - 2x e^{-x^2}y				=\amp\ 5xe^{-x^2}	</mrow>
								<mrow> \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{-x^2}\cdot y\right]	=\amp\ 5xe^{-x^2}	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>

						<p>
							<term> <xref ref="if-step-3" text="custom">Step 3: Direct Integration</xref> </term>
						</p>

						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow> \int \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{-x^2}\cdot y\right]dx	=\amp\ \int 5xe^{-x^2}dx		</mrow>
								<mrow> e^{-x^2}\cdot y									=\amp\ -\frac{5}{2}e^{-x^2} + c	</mrow>
								<mrow> y												=\amp\ -\frac{5}{2} + ce^{x^2}	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>

						<solution><title>Verify the Solution</title>
							
							<p>
								We have found the general solution, but it is worth remembering that we can verify our solution by substituting back into the original equation.
							</p>

							<sidebyside>
								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>	\ul{LHS}														</mrow>
										<mrow>	x^2y'															</mrow>
										<mrow>	x^2 \cdot \frac{d}{dx}\left( -\frac{5}{2} + ce^{x^2} \right)	</mrow>
										<mrow>	x^2 \cdot \left( 0 + 2cxe^{x^2} \right)							</mrow>
										<mrow>	2c x^3 e^{x^2}	\quad‚úÖ											</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>

								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>	\ul{RHS}											</mrow>
										<mrow>	5x^3 + 2x^3y										</mrow>
										<mrow>	5x^3 + 2x^3\left( -\frac{5}{2} + ce^{x^2} \right)	</mrow>
										<mrow>	5x^3 - 5x^3+ 2c x^3 e^{x^2}							</mrow>
										<mrow>	2c x^3 e^{x^2}	\quad‚úÖ								</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>
							</sidebyside>
							
							<p>
								The same result on the left &amp; right confirms our solution.
							</p>
						</solution>

					</solution>
				</p>

			</example>

			<p>
				In the next example, we'll work with an equation with a slightly more complex integrating factor.
			</p>

			<example><title> Integrating Factor that contains <m>\ln</m> </title>

				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Find the general solution to the differential equation
						</p>
						<p>
							<me>-\frac{4}{t^2} \cdot M = t^5 - \frac{1}{t} \cdot\frac{dM}{dt} + 1.</me>
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>

				<p>
					<solution>

						<p>
							This equation is first-order and linear, but is not in standard form, so we rewrite it as
							<md>
								<mrow> \frac{1}{t}\cdot \frac{dM}{dt} - \frac{4}{t^2} \cdot M	\amp = t^5 + 1	</mrow>
								<mrow> \frac{dM}{dt} + \us{P}{\boxed{-\frac{4}{t}}} \cdot M		\amp = t^6 + t	</mrow>
							</md>
							and now we can apply the integrating factor method.
						</p>

						<p>
							<sidebyside widths="20% 80%" valigns="top middle">
								
								<p>
									Integrating <m>\rightarrow</m> Factor
								</p>

								<p>
									<me>
										\mu = e\vphantom{\large|}^{\int \sfrac{-4}{t}\ dt} = e^{-4\ln t} = e^{\ln t^{-4}} = t^{-4} = \frac{1}{t^4}
									</me>
								</p>

							</sidebyside>
						</p>

						<p>
							<sidebyside widths="20% 80%" valigns="top middle">
								
								<p>
									Reverse <m>\rightarrow</m> Product Rule
								</p>

								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>
											\frac{1}{t^4} \frac{dM}{dt} - \frac{1}{t^4} \frac{4}{t} M
												\amp = \frac{1}{t^4}(t^6 + t)
										</mrow>
										<mrow>
											\frac{d}{dt}\left[ \frac{1}{t^4} M \right]
												\amp = t^2 + t^{-3}
										</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>

							</sidebyside>
						</p>

						<p>
							<sidebyside widths="20% 80%" valigns="top middle">
								
								<p>
									Direct <m>\rightarrow</m> Integration
								</p>

								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>
											\int \frac{d}{dt}\left[ \frac{1}{t^4} M \right]\ dt
												\amp = \int \left(t^2 + t^{-3}\right) dt
										</mrow>
										<mrow>
											\frac{1}{t^4} M
												\amp = \frac{1}{3}t^{3} - \frac{1}{2}t^{-2} + c
										</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>

							</sidebyside>
						</p>

						<p>
							<sidebyside widths="20% 80%" valigns="top middle">
								
								<p>
									General <m>\rightarrow</m> Solution
								</p>

								<p>
									<me>
										M = \frac{1}{3}t^{7} - \frac{1}{2}t^2 + ct^4
									</me>.
								</p>

							</sidebyside>
						</p>

					</solution>
				</p>

			</example>

			<p>
				The next two examples introduce scenarios where more advanced integration techniques are required to determine the solution.
			</p>

			<example>

				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Solve the differential equation:
							<me>x^2 y' - y = 1, \quad x > 0</me>.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>
				
				<p>
					<solution>

						<p>
							We put the equation in standard form to identify <m>P(x)</m> and compute the integrating factor.
						</p>
					
						<p>
							<me>
								y' + \us{P}{\boxed{-\frac{1}{x^2}}}\ y = \frac{1}{x^2} \quad \implies \quad
								\mu(x) = e^{\large \int -\sfrac{1}{x^2} dx} = e^{1/x}
							</me>
						</p>
					
						<p>
							Multiply by <m>e^{1/x}</m> and reverse the product rule on the left side.
							<md>
								<mrow> e^{1/x}y' - \frac{e^{1/x}}{x^2} y	=\amp\ \frac{e^{1/x}}{x^2}	</mrow>
								<mrow> \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{1/x} y\right]	=\amp\ \frac{e^{1/x}}{x^2}	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
					
						<p>
							Next, integrate both sides and use <m>u</m>-substitution on the right side.
							<sidebyside widths="10% 60% 25% 5%" valigns="top top top top">

								<p/>
								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>
											\int \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{1/x} y\right] dx
															=\amp\ \int \frac{e^{1/x}}{x^2} dx \qquad \rightarrow
										</mrow>
										<mrow>	e^{1/x} y	=\amp\ \int -e^u du </mrow>
										<mrow>	e^{1/x} y	=\amp\ -e^u + c		</mrow>
										<mrow>	e^{1/x} y	=\amp\ -e^{1/x} + c	</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>
								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>	u	=\amp\ \frac{1}{x}			</mrow>
										<mrow>	du	=\amp\ -\frac{1}{x^2} dx	</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>
								<p/>

							</sidebyside>
							
							Finally, solve for <m>y</m>:
							<md>
								<mrow> y = -1 + ce^{-1/x}	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>

					</solution>
				</p>

			</example>

			<example>

				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Solve the equation:
							<me> t^2 y' + 2ty = t^3 \ln t </me>.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>

				<p>
					<solution>

						<p>
							We put the equation in standard form to identify <m>P(x)</m> and compute the integrating factor.
						</p>
					
						<p>
							<me>
								y' + \us{P}{\boxed{\frac{2}{t}}}\ y = t \ln t \quad \implies \quad
								\mu(t) = e^{\large \int (\sfrac{2}{t})\, dt} = e^{2 \ln t} = t^2
							</me>
						</p>
					
						<p>
							Multiply by <m>e^{1/x}</m> and reverse the product rule on the left side.
							<md>
								<mrow> t^2 y' + 2t y					=\amp\ t^3 \ln t	</mrow>
								<mrow> \frac{d}{dt} \left[t^2 y\right]	=\amp\ t^3 \ln t	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
					
						<p>
							Now, integrate both sides and use <xref ref="ibp-formula" text="custom">integration by parts</xref> on the right.
						</p>

						<p>
							<sidebyside widths="3% 60% 35% 2%" valigns="top top top top">

								<p/>
								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>
											\int \frac{d}{dt} \big[t^2 y\big] dt =\amp\ \int t^3 \ln t\ dt
										</mrow>
										<mrow>	
											t^2 y	=
											\us{\textcolor{BurntOrange}{ u}}{\us{\textcolor{BurntOrange} \uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{\frac11}\ln t}}}
											\cdot
											\us{\textcolor{BurntOrange}{ v}}{\us{\textcolor{BurntOrange} \uparrow}{\ul{\frac14 t^4}}}
											\amp\ - \int
											\us{\textcolor{BurntOrange}{ v}}{\us{\textcolor{BurntOrange} \uparrow}{\ul{\frac14 t^4}}}
											\cdot
											\us{\textcolor{BurntOrange}{du}}{\us{\textcolor{BurntOrange} \uparrow}{\ul{\frac{1}{t}\ dt}}}
										</mrow>
										<mrow>	t^2 y	= \frac14 t^4 \ln t \amp\ - \frac14 \int t^3\ dt					</mrow>
										<mrow>	t^2 y	= \frac{t^4}{4} \ln t \amp\ - \frac{t^4}{16} + c						</mrow>
										<mrow>		y	= \frac{t^2}{4} \ln t \amp\ - \frac{t^2}{16} + c\,t^2				</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>
								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>	\small\textcolor{BurntOrange}{				u = \ln t,}	\amp \ \ \small\textcolor{BurntOrange}{v = \frac14t^4}	</mrow>
										<mrow>	\small\textcolor{BurntOrange}{	du = \frac{1}{t}\ dt,}	\amp \ \ \small\textcolor{BurntOrange}{dv = t^3\ dt}	</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>
								<p/>

							</sidebyside>
						</p>

						<p>
							Finally, solve for <m>y</m>:
							<md>
								<mrow>	y = \frac{t^2}{4} \ln t - \frac{t^2}{16} + c\,t^2	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>

					</solution>
				</p>

			</example>

			<p>
				To conclude, we'll examining one more equation that involves trigonometric functions and an initial condition. Luckily, initial conditions are handled in the same way as in the separable equation case: find the general solution first, then apply the initial condition to determine the constant.
			</p>

			<example>

				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Solve the initial value problem.
							<me>
								A = \sec\theta A' - \frac{\theta e^{\large\theta^2 + \sin\theta}}{\cos \theta}, \hspace{1cm} A(0) = -\frac{1}{2}
							</me>.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>

				<p>
					<solution>

						<p>
							Let's put the equation in standard form. After replacing <m>\sec\theta</m> with <m>\frac{1}{\cos\theta}</m>and moving the <m>A'</m> term to the left, we have
						</p>
					
						<p>
							<me>
								\frac{1}{\cos\theta} A' - A = \frac{\theta e^{\large\theta^2 + \sin\theta}}{\cos \theta}
							</me>.
						</p>

						<p>
							Multiplying this by <m>\cos\theta</m> allows us to identify <m>P</m> and compute <m>\mu</m> as
							<me>
								A' + \us{P}{\boxed{- \cos\theta}}\ A = \theta e^{\large \theta^2 + \sin\theta}
								\quad \implies \quad
								\mu = e\vphantom{\large|}^{\large\int -\cos\theta d\theta} = e^{\large -\sin\theta}
							</me>
						</p>

						<p>
							Multiply by <m>e^{1/x}</m> and reverse the product rule on the left side.
							<md>
								<mrow> e^{\large -\sin\theta}A' - \cos\theta e^{\large -\sin\theta}A = \theta e^{\large\theta^2 + \sin\theta}	</mrow>
								<mrow> \frac{d}{d\theta}\left[ e^{\large -\sin\theta}A \right] = \theta e^{\large\theta^2}	</mrow>
							</md>.
						</p>
					
						<p>
							Integrate both sides and use <m>u</m>-substitution on the right side.
						</p>

						<p>
							<sidebyside widths="10% 60% 25% 5%" valigns="top top top top">

								<p/>
								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>
											\int \frac{d}{d\theta}\left[ e^{\large -\sin\theta}A \right] d\theta = \int \theta e^{\large\theta^2} d\theta
										</mrow>
										<mrow>
											e^{\large -\sin\theta}A = \frac{1}{2}e^{\large\theta^2} + c
										</mrow>
										<mrow>
											A = \frac{1}{2}e^{\large\theta^2 + \sin\theta} + ce^{\large\sin\theta}
										</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>
								<p>
									<md>
										<mrow>	u	=\amp\ \theta^2			</mrow>
										<mrow>	du	=\amp\ 2\theta\ d\theta	</mrow>
									</md>
								</p>
								<p/>

							</sidebyside>

						</p>

						<p>
							Finally, we apply the initial condition to determine the constant <m>c</m>.
							<md>
								<mrow> -\frac{1}{2} = A(0) =\amp\ \frac{1}{2}e^{\large 0^2 + \sin(0)} + ce^{\large\sin(0)}	</mrow>
								<mrow> =\amp\ \frac{1}{2}e^0 + ce^0 	</mrow>
								<mrow> =\amp\ \frac{1}{2} + c\quad \implies \quad c = -1	</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>

						<p>
							We can then write the particular solution to the initial value problem.
							<me>A(\theta) = \frac{1}{2}e^{\large\theta^2 + \sin\theta} - e^{\large\sin\theta}</me>
						</p>
					</solution>
				</p>

			</example>

			<p>
				The integrating factor method is a versatile tool for solving first-order linear differential equations. By reducing the equation to a form that allows for direct integration, we can tackle a wide variety of problems with confidence. The examples in this chapter illustrate the broad applicability of this technique, and practice with a range of equations will solidify your understanding of how to use it effectively.
			</p>

		</subsection>
