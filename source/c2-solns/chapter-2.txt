<section label="what-is-a-solution">
	<title>What is a Solution?</title>

	<introduction>
		<sidebyside width="90%" margins="0% 10%">
		<p>
			<aside><title>üéß Listen</title><p><audio source="audio/readings/c2/solution-basics-intro.mp3" width="100%" /></p></aside>
			A central goal in differential equations is learning how to find their solutions. In fact, most problems you'll encounter begin with a familiar prompt:
		</p>
		</sidebyside>

		<p>
			<blockquote>
				<em><q>Find the solution to the following differential equation.</q></em>
			</blockquote>
			This "solve and move on" mindset can sometimes obscure a deeper, more important question:
			<blockquote>
				<em><q>What does it actually mean to be a solution to a differential equation?</q></em>
			</blockquote>
		</p>

		<p>
			A solution to a differential equation is not just the outcome of a sequence of mathematical steps; it is a function that fulfills all the criteria of the equation. This section aims to provide a clearer understanding of what it means to be a solution to a differential equation, as well as methods for verifying and visualizing them.
		</p>

		<sidebyside width="90%" margins="0% 10%">
		<p>
			<aside><title>üéß Listen</title><p><audio source="audio/readings/c2/what-is-a-solution-intro.mp3" width="100%" /></p></aside>
			In algebra, a solution is any <em>value</em> that <q>satisfies</q> the algebraic equation. In contrast, a solution to a differential equation is any <em>function</em> that <q>satisfies</q> the differential equation. So, understanding what it means to satisfy a differential equation is the first step toward understanding its solutions.
		</p>
		</sidebyside>
	</introduction>

	<paragraphs label="Satisfying-an-Algebraic-Equation">
		<title>Satisfying an Algebraic Equation</title>
		<sidebyside width="90%" margins="0% 10%">
		<p>
			<aside><title>üéß Listen</title><p><audio source="audio/readings/c2/satisfying-an-ae.mp3" width="100%" /></p></aside>
			In algebra, we say that a value <em>satisfies</em> an equation if, when we substitute that value in place of the variable, it makes both sides of the equation equal. This gives a true statement, like <m>0=0</m> or <m>1=1</m>.
		</p>
		</sidebyside>

		<p>
			For example, consider the equation:
			<me>
				y^3 = 3y + 2.
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			Let's check whether <m>y = 2</m> and <m>y = 0</m> are solutions by substituting them into the equation.
		</p>

		<p>
			First, try <m>y = 2</m>.
			<md>
				<mrow> (2)^3	\amp = 3(2) + 2 </mrow>
				<mrow> 8		\amp = 6 + 2 </mrow>
				<mrow> 8		\amp = 8 </mrow>
			</md>
			That's a true statement, so <m>y = 2</m> satisfies the equation.
		</p>

		<p>
			Now check <m>y = 0</m>.
			<md>
				<mrow> (0)^3	\amp = 3(0) + 2 </mrow>
				<mrow> 0		\amp = 0 + 2 </mrow>
				<mrow> 0		\amp \ne 2 </mrow>
			</md>
			This is false, so <m>y = 0</m> does not satisfy the equation.
		</p>

		<p>
			To summarize: <m>y = 2</m> is a solution to the equation, while <m>y = 0</m> is not.
		</p>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs xml:id="satisfying-a-de" label="Satisfying-a-Differential-Equation">
		<title>Satisfying a Differential Equation</title>
		<sidebyside width="90%" margins="0% 10%">
		<p>
			<aside><title>üéß Listen</title><p><audio source="audio/readings/c2/satisfying-a-de.mp3" width="100%" /></p></aside>
			In differential equations, solutions are <em>functions</em> rather than single values. To verify a solution, we substitute the function into the dependent variable in the equation. If both sides simplify to the same expression, then the function is a solution.
		</p>
		</sidebyside>

		<p>
			For instance, consider the differential equation:
		</p>

		<me>
			y' = 3y.
		</me>

		<p>
			To check whether <m>y = x^3</m> or <m>y = e^{3x}</m> is a solution, we substitute each into the equation and compare both sides.
		</p>

		<p>
			First, try <m>y = x^3</m>.
		</p>

		<md>
			<mrow> \left[x^3\right]'	\amp = 3(x^3) </mrow>
			<mrow> 3x^2					\amp \ne 3x^3 </mrow>
		</md>

		<p>
			Since these are not the same, <m>y = x^3</m> does not satisfy the equation.
		</p>

		<p>
			Now try <m>y = e^{3x}</m>.
		</p>

		<md>
			<mrow> \left[e^{3x}\right]'	\amp = 3(e^{3x}) </mrow>
			<mrow> 3e^{3x}				\amp = 3e^{3x} </mrow>
		</md>

		<p>
			Since both sides match, this is a true statement and <m>y = e^{3x}</m> is a solution.
		</p>

		<p>
			Here, a <q>true statement</q> means both sides simplify to the same function. When we substitute <m>y = x^3</m>, we get different expressions. But when we substitute <m>y = e^{3x}</m>, both sides match. Technically, we could rewrite <m>3e^{3x} = 3e^{3x}</m> as <m>0 = 0</m> by subtracting one side from the other, but it's enough to see that the functions match directly.
		</p>

	<paragraphs label="Verifying-Solutions"><title>Verifying Solutions</title>
		<example><title>A Warm-Up</title>

			<statement>
				<p>
					Verify if <m>y = 2x^2</m> is a solution to <m>\quad xy' - 2x^2 = y</m>.
				</p>
			</statement>
			
			<solution>
				<p>
					We'll move all terms to the left side to simplify the process:
					<me>xy' - 2x^2 - y = 0</me>.
				</p>

				<p>
					Now substitute in <m>y = 2x^2</m> to the left and show that it simplies to <m>0</m>:
					<md>
						<mrow> xy' - 2x^2 - y	\amp = x\left( 2x^2 \right)' - 2x^2 - 2x^2 </mrow>
						<mrow>					\amp = 4x^2 - 2x^2 - 2x^2 </mrow>
						<mrow>					\amp = 0	\quad‚úÖ </mrow>
					</md>
					So <m>y = 2x^2</m> is a solution to <m>xy' - 2x^2 = y</m>.
				</p>
			</solution>
		</example>

		<example><title>A Second Order Equation</title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Verify if <m>P = \sin t</m> is a solution to <m>\quad 2P'' + P = \sin t</m>.
				</p>
			</statement>

			<solution>
				<p>
					Since <m>P''</m> appears, compute the derivatives first:
					<me>	
						P = \sin t, \quad P' = \cos t, \quad P'' = -\sin t
					</me>
				</p>

				<p>
					Now rewrite the equation with all terms on one side and substitute:
					<md>
						<mrow> 2P'' + P - \sin t	\amp = 	2(-\sin t) + \sin t - \sin t </mrow>
						<mrow>						\amp = -2\sin t + \cancel{\sin t} - \cancel{\sin t} </mrow>
						<mrow>						\amp = -2\sin t \ne 0 \quad‚ùå </mrow>
					</md>
					So <m>P = \sin t</m> is <term>not</term> a solution to <m>2P'' + P = \sin t</m>.
				</p>
			</solution>
		</example>

		<p>
			You can also verify solutions that include constants, as the next examples show.
		</p>

		<example xml:id="example-what-is-a-solution-1"><title>Verifying Solutions with a Common Form</title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					<statement>
						<p>
							Show that the functions
							<me>y = e^{2x},\quad y = -5e^{2x},\quad y = \pi e^{2x},\quad y = 0</me>
							are all solutions to the differential equation
							<me>y' - 2y = 0</me>.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</p>
			</statement>
			<solution>
				<p>
					Rather than verify each function separately, notice that they all are of the form <m>y = c e^{2x}</m>, where <m>c</m> is a constant. Let's verify the general case.
				</p>
				
				<p>
					Substitute <m>y = c e^{2x}</m> into the equation:
					<me>
						y' - 2y = (c e^{2x})' - 2(c e^{2x})	= 2c e^{2x} - 2c e^{2x}	= 0 \quad‚úÖ
					</me>
					So <m>y = c e^{2x}</m> is a solution for any constant <m>c</m>. In particular, this includes the functions
					<me>
						y = e^{2x},\quad y = -5e^{2x},\quad y = \pi e^{2x},\quad y = 0
					</me>
					for the constants <m>c = 1, -5, \pi, 0</m> respectively.
				</p>
			</solution>
		</example>

		<example xml:id="example-what-is-a-solution-2"><title>Verifying a Solution with Two Constants</title>

			<p>
				<statement>
					<p>
						Verify that <m>y = c_1 e^{-x} + c_2 e^{x} + x^2 + 2</m> is a solution to
						<me>y'' - y = -x^2</me>.
					</p>
				</statement>
			</p>

			<p>
				<solution>

					<p>
						Move all terms to one side:
						<me>y'' - y + x^2 = 0</me>
					</p>
			
					<p>
						Now substitute <m>y = c_1 e^{-x} + c_2 e^{x} + x^2 + 2</m>:
						<md>
							<mrow> y'' - y + x^2 \amp = \left( c_1 e^{-x} + c_2 e^{x} + x^2 + 2 \right)'' - (c_1 e^{-x} + c_2 e^{x} + x^2 + 2) + x^2 </mrow>
							<mrow> \amp = \left( -c_1 e^{-x} + c_2 e^{x} + 2 \right)' - c_1 e^{-x} - c_2 e^{x} - x^2 - 2 + x^2 </mrow>
							<mrow> \amp = c_1 e^{-x} + c_2 e^{x} + 0 - c_1 e^{-x} - c_2 e^{x} - 2 + 0 </mrow>
							<mrow> \amp = 0 \quad‚úÖ </mrow>
						</md>
						Thus, <m>y = c_1 e^{-x} + c_2 e^{x} + x^2 + 2</m> is a solution to <m>y'' - y = -x^2</m>.
					</p>

				</solution>
			</p>

		</example>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs label="what-is-a-solution-wrapup">
		<title>üóùÔ∏è Key Takeaways</title>
		<p>
			<aside><title>üéß Listen</title><p><audio source="audio/readings/c2/what-is-a-solution-wrapup.mp3" width="100%" /></p></aside>
			<ul marker="square">
				<li>
					In differential equations, a solution is a function that satisfies the equation. This means that when you plug it into the dependent variable, both sides simplify to the same expression.
				</li>
				<li>
					To verify a solution, take any necessary derivatives, substitute into the equation, and simplify. If both sides match, or reduce to something like <m>0 = 0</m>, then the function is a solution.
				</li>
			</ul>

<section label="solution-types-initial-conditions">
	<title>Solution Types &amp; Initial Conditions</title>

	<introduction>
		<sidebyside width="90%" margins="0% 10%">
			<p>
				<aside><title>üéß Listen</title><p><audio source="audio/readings/c2/solution-types.mp3" width="100%" /></p></aside>
				You now know that a solution to a differential equation is a function that satisfies the equation. But <xref ref="example-what-is-a-solution-1"/> shows that a single differential equation can actually have many solutions. That example shows there can be infinitely many solutions, one for each value of the constant <m>c</m> in <m>y = c e^{2x}</m>.
			</p>
		</sidebyside>

		<p>
			To make sense of all these solutions, we classify solutions into three key categories: <term>general solution</term>, <term>particular solution</term>, and <term>family of solutions</term>. Understanding the distinctions between these types is an important part of solving differential equations.
		</p>
	</introduction>

	<paragraphs label="solution-type-analogy">
		<title>An Analogy</title>
		<p>
			To help clarify the differences between these types of solutions, let's use an analogy.
		</p>

		<p>
			Imagine a family of solutions as a collection of all possible dates in a year. The general solution is like the format for writing dates, such as <c>mm/dd/yyyy</c>. Each particular solution is like a specific date, such as <c>10/04/2039</c>. The family of solutions would be the entire list of all dates in this format.
		</p>

		<p>
			Of course, solutions to differential equations aren't dates, but the analogy highlights the idea: the general solution gives the structure, particular solutions are specific instances, and the family includes them all.
		</p>

	<paragraphs label="solution-types">
		<title>Solution Types</title>
		<p>
			To make sense of all the possible solutions, we classify solutions into three key categories:
		</p>

		<dl>
			<li><title>General solution:</title>
				<p>
					This includes one or more arbitrary constants, like <m>c</m> in <m>y = c e^{2x}</m>. You can think of it as a template from which specific solutions can be generated.
				</p>
			</li>
			<li><title>Particular solution:</title>
				<p>
					A particular solution results from assigning a specific value to each arbitrary constant in the general solution. For example, if <m>c = 5</m>, then <m>y = 5 e^{2x}</m>.
				</p>
			</li>
			<li><title>Family of solutions:</title>
				<p>
					A family of solutions is the complete collection of particular solutions generated by varying the constants in the general solution.
				</p>
			</li>
		</dl>

		<p>
			Solving a differential equation usually starts with the <em>general solution</em>, which includes one or more arbitrary constants. Remember, each constant can take on any value, which leads to infinitely many possible <em>particular solutions</em>.
		</p>

		<warning label="not-all-solutions-with-constants-are-general-solutions">
			<title>Not All Solutions with Constants Are General Solutions ‚ö†Ô∏è</title>
			<p>
				Keep in mind, solutions with arbitrary constants are not general solutions by default. For example, both of the functions
				<me>
					y = \frac{1}{2}x^2 + c_1 x \quad \text{and} \quad y = \frac{1}{2}x^2 + c_1 x + c_2
				</me>
				are solutions to the equation <m>y'' = 1</m>, but only the second is general solution. The first is a special case of the second, obtained when <m>c_2 = 0</m>, so it can be the general solution.
			</p>
		</warning>

	<paragraphs label="initial-conditions-ivps">
		<title>Initial Conditions &amp; Initial-Value Problems (IVPs)</title>
		<p>
			In real-world problems, we often narrow down the set of possible solutions by specifying <term>initial conditions</term>, known values of the solution or its derivatives at a specific point. A differential equation paired with initial conditions is called an <term>initial-value problem</term> (IVP). If enough information is provided, the IVP gives a unique particular solution that satisfies both the equation and the conditions.
		</p>

		<p label="initial-conditions-ivps-chkpt-bundle">
			<task label="chkpt-initial-conditions-ivps-1">
				<title> üìñ‚ùì Initial Condition Meaning</title>

					</p>
					<p>

					</p>
				</statement>

							</p>
						</feedback>
					</choice>
					<choice>
						<statement>
							<p>
								The starting value of <m>x</m>.
							</p>
						</statement>
						<feedback>

							</p>
						</feedback>
					</choice>

							</p>
						</statement>

							</p>
						</feedback>
					</choice>

			</task>
			<task label="chkpt-initial-conditions-ivps-2">
				<title> üìñ‚ùì Is it an IVP</title>
				<statement>
					<p>
						The differential equation
						<me>
							\frac{dy}{dx} = 2xy - 6x
						</me>
						is an example of an initial-value problem.
					</p>
				</statement>
				<choices randomize="yes">
					<choice>
						<statement>True</statement>
						<feedback>
							<p>

						<statement>False</statement>
						<feedback>

		<p>
			For example, suppose an object is in free fall under constant acceleration <m>32\ \mathrm{ft/s^2}</m>. If <m>h(t)</m> is its height above the ground, then:
			<me>
				h''(t) = -32.
			</me>
			The general solution is:
			<me>
				h(t) = c_1 + c_2t - 16t^2.
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			This solution models any object falling under gravity. To describe a specific situation, we apply two initial conditions:
		</p>

		<ul marker="square">
			<li>The object is dropped from <m>100\ \mathrm{ft}</m>.</li>
			<li>It is dropped from rest with an initial velocity of zero.</li>
		</ul>
	
		<p>
			In terms of <m>h(t)</m>, these conditions become:
			<me>
				h(0) = 100, \quad h'(0) = 0.
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			Now we substitute the first condition into <m>h(t)</m> and the second into <m>h'(t) = c_2 - 32t</m>:
		</p>
		
		<sidebyside widths="26% 50% 16%" margins="4% 4%">
			<p><m>h(0) = 100:</m></p>
			<p><m>100 = h(0) = c_1 + c_2(0) - 16(0)^2</m></p>
			<p><m>\Rightarrow c_1 = 100</m></p>
		</sidebyside>

		<sidebyside widths="26% 50% 16%" margins="4% 4%">
			<p><m>h'(0) = 0:</m></p>
			<p><m>0 = h'(0) = c_2 - 32(0)</m></p>
			<p><m>\Rightarrow c_2 = 0</m></p>
		</sidebyside>

		<p>
			The resulting particular solution is:
			<me>
				h(t) = 100 - 16t^2,
			</me>
			which models an object dropped specifically from <m>100\ \mathrm{ft}</m> with no initial velocity.
		</p>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs label="from-general-to-particular">
		<title>From General Solutions to Particular Solutions</title>

		<p>
			To find a particular solution, we start with a general solution and apply given initial conditions to determine the values of any constants. The following examples illustrate this process.
		</p>

		<example xml:id="example-initial-conditions-1" label="solving-for-a-constant">
			<title>Solving for a Constant</title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Find the particular solution to the initial value problem:
					<me xml:id="eqn-initial-conditions-1">
						\frac{dy}{dx} = 2xy - 6x, \quad y(0) = 2
					</me>
					given that the general solution is:
					<me>
						y = ce^{x^2} + 3.
					</me>
				</p>
			</statement>

			<solution>
				<p>
					The initial condition <m>y(0) = 2</m> tells us that <m>y</m> must equal 2 when <m>x = 0</m>. Substituting into the general solution:
					<md>
						<mrow> y =  ce^{x^2} + 3 \quad \overset{y\ =\ 2,\ x\ =\ 0}{\Rightarrow} \quad	2	\amp =  ce^{0^2} + 3	</mrow>
						<mrow>							 												2	\amp =  c + 3		</mrow>
						<mrow>																			c	\amp =  -1			</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>
				<p>
					Thus, the particular solution is:
					<me>
						y = -e^{x^2} + 3.
					</me>
				</p>
			</solution>
		</example>

		</p>

		<example label="Two-Initial-Conditions">
			<title>Two Initial Conditions</title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Verify that the function
					<me>
						y = c_1 e^{-3t} + c_2 e^{4t}
					</me>
					is a solution to
					<me>
						y'' - y' - 12y = 0,
					</me>
					and then find the particular solution satisfying:
					<me>
						y(0) = 4, \quad y'(0) = -5.
					</me>
				</p>
			</statement>

			<solution><title>Verifying the General Solution</title>
				<p>
					Compute the derivatives:
					<md>
						<mrow> y \amp = c_1 e^{-3t} + c_2 e^{4t} </mrow>
						<mrow> y' \amp = -3c_1 e^{-3t} + 4c_2 e^{4t} </mrow>
						<mrow> y'' \amp = 9c_1 e^{-3t} + 16c_2 e^{4t} </mrow>
					</md>
				</p>

				<p>
					Substitute into the equation:
					<md>
						<mrow>
							y'' - y' - 12y \amp = \left(9c_1 e^{-3t} + 16c_2 e^{4t}\right)
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp\ - \left(-3c_1 e^{-3t} + 4c_2 e^{4t}\right) - 12\left(c_1 e^{-3t} + c_2 e^{4t}\right)
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = 0
						</mrow>
					</md>
					So <m>y(t) = c_1 e^{-3t} + c_2 e^{4t}</m> is indeed a solution.
				</p>
			</solution>

			<solution><title>Finding the Particular Solution</title>
				<p>
					Apply the initial conditions:
				</p>

				<sidebyside>
					<p>
						<md>
							<mrow> y(0) \amp = c_1 + c_2 = 4 </mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
					<p>
						<md>
							<mrow> y'(0) \amp = -3c_1 + 4c_2 = -5 </mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<p>
					Solve the system:
					<md>
						<mrow> c_1 + c_2 \amp = 4 </mrow>
						<mrow> -3c_1 + 4c_2 \amp = -5 </mrow>
					</md>
					Substituting and solving yields:
					<me>
						c_1 = 3, \quad c_2 = 1.
					</me>
				</p>

				<p>
					The particular solution is:
					<me>
						y = 3e^{-3t} + e^{4t}.
					</me>
				</p>
			</solution>
		</example>

	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs label="solution-types-initial-conditions-wrapup">
		<title>üóùÔ∏è Key Takeaways</title>
		<p>
			<aside><title>üéß Listen</title><p><audio source="audio/readings/c1/template.mp3" width="100%" /></p></aside>
			<ul marker="square">
				<li>
					A differential equation can have many solutions, often infinitely many, because the constants in the solution can take on any value.
				</li>
				<li>
					A <em>general solution</em> includes one or more arbitrary constants and captures all possible solutions in a single form.
				</li>
				<li>
					A <em>particular solution</em> comes from assigning specific values to those constants.
				</li>
				<li>
					A <em>family of solutions</em> is the complete set of particular solutions generated by varying the constants in the general solution.
				</li>
				<li>
					<em>Initial conditions</em> give known values for the solution or its derivatives.
				</li>
				<li>
					When a differential equation is paired with initial conditions, we call it an <em>initial value problem</em> (IVP).
				</li>
				<li>
					When we apply these conditions, we can determine the constants in the general solution, resulting in a unique <em>particular solution</em>.
				</li>
			</ul>
		</p>
	</paragraphs>

<section label="direct-integration">
	<title>Direct Integration</title>

	<introduction>
		
		<p>
			Solving a differential equation might sound like a new challenge, but you've actually done it before, in calculus! Any time you find an antiderivative, you're solving a differential equation.
		</p>

		<p>
			In this section, we'll reframe antiderivatives as solutions to differential equations and use this perspective to introduce our first method: direct integration. By the end, you'll be able to find general and particular solutions to simple differential equations using integration.
		</p>
	
	</introduction>

	<task label="review-direct-integration-1">

			</choice>

				<feedback>Saying <m>y</m> is the antiderivative of <m>x^3 - 7</m> is the same thing as saying <q>when you take the derivative of <m>y</m> you shoud get <m>x^3 - 7</m></q></feedback>
			</choice>

	<paragraphs label="antiderivatives">
		<title>üì∞ Antiderivatives</title>

		<p>
			You might not realize it, but solving some differential equations is a skill you already have! For instance, computing the antiderivative of <m>x^2</m> means you're finding a function <m>y</m> whose derivative is <m>x^2</m>. In other words:
		</p>

		<blockquote>
			<p>
				<q>Find <m>y</m> such that the derivative of <m>y</m> is <m>x^2</m>.</q>
			</p>
		</blockquote>

		<p>
			This is exactly the same as solving the differential equation:
		</p>

		<blockquote>
			<p>
				<q>Find <m>y</m> such that <m>\ds \frac{dy}{dx} = x^2</m>.</q>
			</p>
		</blockquote>

		<p>
			To solve this, we integrate both sides with respect to <m>x</m>:
		</p>

		<md>
			<mrow> \int\frac{d}{dx}[y]\ dx \amp = \int x^2\ dx </mrow>
			<mrow> y + c_1 \amp = \frac{1}{3} x^3 + c_2 </mrow>
			<mrow> y \amp = \frac{1}{3} x^3 + (c_2 - c_1) = \frac{1}{3} x^3 + c </mrow>
		</md>
		
		<p>
			The constant <m>c = c_2 - c_1</m> absorbs the difference between the two integration constants. This is a standard simplification when solving differential equations.
		</p>

		<me>
			y = \frac{1}{3}x^3 + c
		</me>

		<task label="chkpt-antiderivatives-2">
			<title> üìñ‚ùì When it Comes to Constants, Less is More</title>
			<statement>Combining constants is a common practice in differential equations.</statement>
			<choices randomize="yes">
				<choice correct="yes">
					<statement> True</statement>
					<feedback>Correct! Combining constants is an easy way to simplify a solution and is a standard practice in differential equations.</feedback>
				</choice>
				<choice>
					<statement>False</statement>
					<feedback>Incorrect, revisit the examples above.</feedback>
				</choice>
			</choices>
		</task>

		<p>
			Now let's look at a slightly more involved example.
		</p>
		
		<example><title>Isolate <m>y</m></title>
			<p>
				<statement>
					<p>
						Find the function, <m>y</m>, such that
						<me>
							2y' - 4\sin x = 2
						</me>.
					</p>
				</statement>
			</p>

			<solution>
				<p>
					This is another antiderivative problem where we first have to isolate <m>y'</m> before integrating, like so
				</p>
				
				<me>
					y' = 1 + 2 \sin x
				</me>.
				
				<p>
					This equation now says <q>The unknown <m>y</m> is the function whose derivative is <m>1 + 2 \sin x</m></q> or, in other words,
				</p>

				<blockquote>
					<p>
						<q>The unknown <m>y</m> is the antiderivative of <m>1 + 2 \sin x</m></q>.
					</p>
				</blockquote>

				<p>
					Instead of writing down the antiderivative as our solution, let's take the differential equations approach and integrate both sides of the equation with respect to <m>x</m>
				</p>
				
				<md>
					<mrow> \int y'\ dx 	\amp = \int \left(1 + 2 \sin x\right) \ dx 				</mrow>
					<mrow> 	y + c_1 	\amp = x - 2 \cos x + c_2 								</mrow>
					<mrow> 			y 	\amp = x - 2 \cos x + \boxed{c_2 - c_1}\leftarrow c		</mrow>
					<mrow> 		y(x)	\amp  = x - 2 \cos x + c 								</mrow>.
				</md>
				
				<p>
					As before, the constants of integration are combined into a single constant <m>c</m>. This practice of merging constants is standard in solving differential equations and simplifies the final result.
				</p>

			</solution>
		
		</example>

		<p>
			In summary, solving simple differential equations often reduces to finding the antiderivative of a function. By integrating both sides and simplifying constants, you can find the general solution.
		</p>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs label="solving-via-direct-integration">
		<title>üì∞ Solving Differential Equations via Direct Integration</title>

		<p>
			We've seen that when you can isolate the derivative of <m>y</m> in the form:
			<me>
				\frac{d}{dx}[y] = f(x)
			</me>
			you can solve the equation by integrating both sides. This idea also applies to more general equations where the derivative involves both <m>x</m> and <m>y</m>:
			<me>
				\frac{d}{dx}\left[g(x,y)\right] = f(x)
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			Here, <m>f(x)</m> must depend only on <m>x</m>, and <m>g(x,y)</m> may involve both variables. This structure allows us to apply our first solution technique: <em>direct integration</em>.
		</p>

		<exploration xml:id="direct-integration">
			<title>Direct Integration</title>
			<p>
				To solve a differential equation of the form
				<men xml:id="direct-integration-equation">
					\frac{d}{dx}\left[g(x,y)\right] = f(x)
				</men>,
				follow these steps:
				<dl>
					<li xml:id="di-step-1"><title>Integrate Both Sides</title>
						<p>
							Apply integration with respect to <m>x</m> to eliminate the derivative.
						</p>
					</li>
					<li xml:id="di-step-2"><title>Solve for <m>y</m></title>
						<p>
							After integration, isolate <m>y</m> to get the general solution.
						</p>
					</li>
				</dl>
			</p>
		</exploration>

		<p label="direct-integration-chkpt-bundle">
			<task label="chkpt-direct-integration-2">

		<p>
			Consider the following examples to see how this approach applies to some more interesting problems.
		</p>

		<example xml:id="direct-integration-example-1"><title>Integrate both sides to Recover <m>y</m></title>
		
			<p>
				<statement>
					<p>
						Find the particular solution to the differential equation
						<me>
							\frac{d}{dx}\left[5x \cdot y\right] = \frac{1}{x^2} , \quad y\left(1\right) = -4
						</me>.
					</p>
				</statement>
			</p>
			<solution>

				<p>
					First, observe that this problem is in the form
					<me>
						\frac{d}{dx}\left[g(x,y)\right] = f(x)
					</me>
					where
					<me>
						g(x,y) = 5x \cdot y \quad \text{ and } \quad f(x) = \frac{1}{x^2}
					</me>.
				</p>
				<p>
					So, we can solve for <m>y</m> using direct integration:
				</p>

				<p>
					<term> <xref ref="di-step-1" text="custom">Step 1: Integrate both Sides</xref> </term>
				</p>

				<p>
					Integrating both sides with respect to <m>x</m>, we get
					<md>
						<mrow> \int \frac{d}{dx}\left[5x \cdot y\right]\ dx \amp = \int x^{-2}\ dx </mrow>
						<mrow> 5x \cdot y + c_1 \amp = -x^{-1} + c_2 </mrow>
					</md>
				</p>

				<p>
					<term> <xref ref="di-step-2" text="custom">Step 2: Isolate the Solution</xref> </term>
				</p>

				<p>
					Now that the dependent variable <m>y</m> is free from the derivative, we can isolate it to find the general solution, as follows
					<md>
						<mrow>  y \amp = \frac{1}{5x}\Big(-\frac{1}{x} + \overset{=\ c}{\overbrace{c_2 - c_1}}\Big) </mrow>
						<mrow>  y \amp = \frac{1}{5x}\left(-\frac{1}{x} + c\right) \quad \leftarrow \ \text{general solution}</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>

				<p>
					Since we are given the condition <m>y\left(1\right) = -4</m>, we need to find <m>c</m>.
					<md>
						<mrow>  -4	\amp = \frac{1}{5}\left(-1 + c\right)\qquad \leftarrow \small\text{ multiply both sides by } 5	</mrow>
						<mrow>  -20	\amp = -1 + c \quad \implies \quad c = -19	</mrow>
					</md>
					Therefore, the particular solution (for <m>c=-19</m>) is
					<me>
						y = \frac{1}{5x}\left(-\frac{1}{x} - 19\right)
					</me>.
				</p>

			</solution>

		</example>

		<example xml:id="direct-integration-example-2"><title>Isolate the Derivative before Integrating</title>
			<p>
				<statement>
					<p>
						Compute the general solution of the differential equation
						<me>
							\frac{1}{\cos x}\frac{d}{dx}\Big[y\sin(2x)\Big] - 1 = \sec x
						</me>
					</p>
				</statement>
			</p>
			<solution>
				<p>
					First, we get the derivative by itself on the left-hand side
					<aside><title>‚òùÔ∏è Recall the Identity</title>
						<p>
							<me>
								\sec x = \frac{1}{\cos x}
							</me>
						</p>
					</aside>
					<md>
						<mrow>
							\frac{d}{dx}\Big[ y \sin(2x) \Big] \amp = \cos x\left( 1 + \frac{1}{\cos x} \right)
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\frac{d}{dx}\Big[
								\us{\textcolor{BurntOrange}{g(x,y)}}{\us{\textcolor{BurntOrange}{\uparrow}}{\ul{y\sin(2x)}}}
							\Big] \amp = \us{\textcolor{BurntOrange}{f(x)}}{\us{\textcolor{BurntOrange}{\uparrow}}{\ul{\cos x + 1}}}
						</mrow>
					</md>
					Now, we integrate by sides with respect to <m>x</m>, releasing <m>y</m> from the derivative and allowing us to isolate the general solution.
					<md>
						<mrow>	\int \frac{d}{dx}\Big[y\sin(2x)\Big]dx	\amp = \int \left(\cos x + 1\right) \ dx	</mrow>
						<mrow>							y\sin(2x) + c_1	\amp = \sin x + x + c_2						</mrow>
						<mrow>										y	\amp = \frac{\sin x + x + c}{\sin(2x)}
																					\qquad (c = c_2 - c_1)
						</mrow>
					</md>.
				</p>
			</solution>

		</example>

		<p>
			Direct integration is one of the simplest techniques for solving differential equations, provided the structure allows it. If you can isolate a derivative of <m>y</m> or an expression involving <m>y</m> and the right-hand side depends only on <m>x</m>, you're in business. Just integrate both sides and solve for <m>y</m>.
		</p>

		<p>
			This approach highlights how closely tied differential equations are to the core ideas of calculus.
		</p>
	</paragraphs>
