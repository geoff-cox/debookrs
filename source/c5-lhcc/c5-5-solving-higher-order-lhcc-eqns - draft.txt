<section><title> Solving High-Order LHCC Equations </title>
  
  <introduction>
    <p>
      As the previous section demonstrated, constructing the general solution of a LHCC equation is fairly straightforward given the roots of the characteristic equation. However, solving <em>high-degree polynomial equations</em> can be quite challenging. While there are strategies to tackle this by hand, sometimes computational tools are necessary to obtain the solutions. Below, we will outline some common approaches
    </p>
  </introduction>

  <paragraphs label="factoring">
    <title> Factoring </title>
    <p>
      Certain types of high-degree polynomials can be factored relatively easily using factoring methods from algebra. Some of the standard techniques that we will use are outlined below.
    </p>

    <assemblage xml:id="factoring-techniques"><title> ðŸ§® Factoring Techniques </title>
      <p>
        <dl>
          <li><title> <m>\textbf{Common Factor}</m> </title>
            <p>
              <md>
                <mrow> ab + ac + ad = a(b+c+d)  </mrow>
              </md>
            </p>
          </li>
          <li><title> <m>\textbf{Grouping}</m> </title>
            <p>
              <md>
                <mrow> ab + ac + bd + cd  \amp = a(b+c) + d(b+c)  </mrow>
                <mrow>            \amp = (a+d)(b+c)    </mrow>
              </md>
            </p>
          </li>
          <li><title> <m>\textbf{Difference of Squares}</m> </title>
            <p>
              <me> a^2 - b^2 = (a+b)(a-b) </me>
            </p>
          </li>
          <li><title> <m>\textbf{Difference of Cubes}</m> </title>
            <p>
              <me> a^3 - b^3 = (a-b)(a^2+ab+b^2)</me>
            </p>
          </li>
          <li><title> <m>\textbf{Sum of Cubes}</m> </title>
            <p>
              <me> a^3 + b^3 = (a+b)(a^2-ab+b^2)</me>
            </p>
          </li>
        </dl>
      </p>
    </assemblage>
    
    <p>
      The next example demonstrate how these factoring techniques can be used to solve high-degree characteristic equations.
    </p>

    <example><title> Finding Characteristic Solutions with Factoring </title>
      <p>
        Find the general solution for each of the higher-order LHCC equations by factoring their characteristic equations.
        <ol>
          <li> <m> 4 y'' - 9 = 0 </m> </li>
          <li> <m> y^{(5)} - 4 y''' = 0 </m> </li>
          <li> <m> y^{(4)} - 25 = 0 </m> </li>
          <li> <m> y^{(4)} - 5y'' + 4 = 0 </m> </li>
          <li> <m> y^{(4)} - 6y''' + 9y'' = 0 </m> </li>
          <li> <m> y^{(5)} - 4 y''' + 8 y'' + 48 = 0 </m> </li>
        </ol>
      </p>

      <solution>
        <p>
          For each equation, we list the characteristic equation, the factoring steps, the solutions, and the general solution.
        </p>

        <ol>
          <li>
            <p>
              <m>4 y'' - 9 = 0</m>
              <sidebyside widths="65% 35%" valign="bottom">
                <p>
                  <md>
                    <mrow>  4 r^2 - 9      \amp = 0  </mrow>
                    <mrow>  (2 r + 3)(2 r - 3)  \amp = 0  </mrow>
                  </md>
                </p>
                <p>
                  difference of squares
                </p>
              </sidebyside>
              <md>
                <mrow> r = -\frac{3}{2},\ \frac{3}{2} \quad \implies \quad 
                  \boxed{\ y = c_1 e^{\large -\frac{3}{2}x} + c_2 e^{\large \frac{3}{2}x}\vphantom{\int}\ }
                </mrow>
              </md>
            </p>
            <p/>
          </li>

          <li>
            <hrule/>
            <p>
              <m>y^{(5)} - 4 y''' = 0</m>
              <sidebyside widths="65% 35%" valign="bottom">
                <p>
                  <md>
                    <mrow> r^5 - 4 r^3 \amp = 0</mrow>
                    <mrow> r^3(r^2 - 4) \amp = 0</mrow>
                    <mrow> r^3(r + 2)(r - 2) \amp = 0</mrow>
                  </md>
                </p>
                <p>
                  common factor &amp;  difference of squares
                </p>
              </sidebyside>
              <md>
                <mrow> r = \ub{\ 0\ ,\ 0\ ,\ 0\ }_{\text{repeated}}, -2,\ 2 </mrow>
                <mrow> \boxed{\ y = c_1 + c_2 x + c_3 x^2 + c_4 e^{-2x} + c_5 e^{2x}\vphantom{\int}\ } </mrow>
              </md>
            </p>
            <p/>
          </li>

          <li>
            <p>
              <m>y^{(4)} - 25 = 0</m>
              <sidebyside widths="65% 35%" valign="bottom">
                <p>
                  <md>
                    <mrow> r^4 - 25 \amp = 0</mrow>
                    <mrow> (r^2 + 5)(r^2 - 5) \amp = 0</mrow>
                    <mrow> (r^2 + 5)(r - \sqrt{5})(r + \sqrt{5}) \amp = 0</mrow>
                  </md>
                </p>
                <p>
                  difference of squares
                </p>
              </sidebyside>
              <md>
                <mrow> r = \pm i\sqrt{5},\ -\sqrt{5},\ \sqrt{5} </mrow>
                <mrow> 
                  \boxed{\ y = c_1\cos\left(\sqrt{5}x\right) + c_2\sin\left(\sqrt{5}x\right) 
                  + c_3 e^{\sqrt{5}x} + c_4 e^{-\sqrt{5}x}\vphantom{\int}\ }
                </mrow>
              </md>
            </p>
            <p/>
          </li>

          <li>
            <p>
              <m>y^{(4)} - 5y'' + 4 = 0</m>
              <sidebyside widths="65% 35%" valign="middle">
                <p>
                  <md>
                    <mrow> r^4 - 5r^2 + 4 \amp = 0</mrow>
                    <mrow> u^2 - 5u + 4 \amp = 0</mrow>
                    <mrow> (u - 4)(u + 1) \amp = 0</mrow>
                    <mrow> (r^2 - 4)(r^2 + 1) \amp = 0</mrow>
                    <mrow> (r - 2)(r + 2)(r^2 + 1) \amp = 0</mrow>
                  </md>
                </p>
                <p>
                  <line>  Let <m>u = r^2</m>      </line>
                  <line>  Factor in terms of <m>u</m>      </line>
                  <line>  Replace <m>u</m> with <m>r^2</m>  </line>
                  <line>  difference of squares      </line>
                </p>
              </sidebyside>
              <md>
                <mrow> r = -2,\ 2,\ \pm i </mrow>
                <mrow> \boxed{\ y = c_1 e^{-2x} + c_2 e^{2x} + c_3 \cos x + c_4 \sin x\vphantom{\int}\ } </mrow>
              </md>
            </p>
            <p/>
          </li>

          <li>
            <p>
              <m>y^{(4)} - 6y''' + 9y'' = 0</m>
              <sidebyside widths="70% 5% 25%" valign="middle">
                <p>
                  <md>
                    <mrow> r^4 - 6r^3 + 9r^2 \amp = 0</mrow>
                    <mrow> r^2(r^2 - 6r + 9) \amp = 0</mrow>
                    <mrow> r^2(r - 3)^2 \amp = 0</mrow>
                  </md>
                </p>
                <p/>
                <p>
                  grouping
                </p>
              </sidebyside>
              <md>
                <mrow> r = \ub{\ 0\ ,\ 0\ }_{\text{repeated}},\ \ub{\ 3\ ,\ 3\ }_{\text{repeated}}</mrow>
                <mrow> \boxed{\ y = c_1 + c_2 x + c_3 e^{3x} + c_4 x e^{3x}\vphantom{\int}\ } </mrow>
              </md>
            </p>
            <p/>
          </li>

          <li>
            <p>
              <m>y^{(5)} - 4 y''' + 8 y'' + 48 = 0</m>
              <sidebyside widths="70% 5% 25%" valign="middle">
                <p>
                  <md>
                    <mrow> r^5 - 4 r^3 + 8 r^2 + 48 \amp = 0</mrow>
                    <mrow> r^3(r^2 - 4) + 8(r^2 - 4) \amp = 0</mrow>
                    <mrow> (r^3 + 8)(r^2 - 4) \amp = 0</mrow>
                    <mrow> (r + 2)(r^2 - 2r + 4)(r + 2)(r - 2) \amp = 0</mrow>
                  </md>
                </p>
                <p/>
                <p>
                  grouping &amp; sum of cubes
                </p>
              </sidebyside>
              <me>
                r = \ub{-2\ ,\ -2\ }_{\text{repeated}},\ 2,\ 1 \pm i \sqrt{3}
              </me>
              <p>
                <md>
                  <mrow>
                    \small\boxed{y = c_1 e^{\large -2x} + c_2 x e^{\large -2x} + c_3 e^{\large 2x} 
                    + e^{\large x}\left(c_4\cos(\sqrt{3}x) + c_5\sin(\sqrt{3}x)\right)\vphantom{\int}} 
                  </mrow>
                </md>
              </p>
            </p>
          </li>
        </ol>
      </solution>

  <paragraphs label="rational-root-theorem">
    <title> Rational Root Theorem </title>
    <p>
      The <em>Rational Root Theorem</em> helps identify possible rational solutions by considering factors of the constant and leading coefficients. If a rational solution is found, polynomial division can reduce the equation to a lower-degree polynomial. While this method is powerful and can handle many problems, it is often quite tedius and its multi-step process makes it prone to human error. 
    </p>
  </paragraphs>

  <paragraphs label="technology">
    <title> Technology </title>
    <p>
      When facing a high-order charateristic equation that cannot be factored, a good strategy is to utilize technology to solve these tyes of problems. Many software tools are available and you are encouraged to use any that you are familiar with.
    </p>

    <p>
      The next example demonstrates how to use computational tools to factor a high-degree polynomial.
    </p>

    <example><title> Using a Factoring Tool </title>
      <p>
        <statement>
          <p>
            Find the general solution to the LHCC equations
            <ol>
              <li> <m> y''' - 6y'' + 11y' - 6y = 0 </m> </li>
              <li> <m> 2 y^{(4)} + 9 y''' + y'' - 21 y' + 9 y  = 0 </m> </li>
            </ol>
            using the factoring tool below.
          </p>
        </statement>
      </p>
      
      <corollary xml:id="factoring-tool">
        <title> Polynomial Factoring Calculator </title>
        <figure xml:id="figure-pp">
          <caption>
            Type in a polynomial as a function of <m>r</m>. Use <q><m>*</m></q> for multiplication and <q>^</q> for exponents. Note: The tool only displays factors for integer or fractional (rational) roots.
          </caption>
          <interactive xml:id="poly-factor" platform="sage" width="100%" aspect="7:2">
            <slate surface="sage">
              r = var('r')
              r = PolynomialRing(RationalField(), 'r').gen()
              F = 'r^2 - 2*r - 8'
              P = r^2 - 2*r - 8
              @interact
              def _(ce = input_box(P, label="  Polynomial:", type=str, width=30)):
                ce_poly = sage_eval('lambda r: ' + ce)
                pretty_print( html( r'Polynomial: $ %s $'%latex(ce_poly(r) ) ) )
                pretty_print( html( r'Factored Polynomial: $ %s $'%latex(ce_poly(r).factor()) ) )
            </slate>
          </interactive>
        </figure>
      </corollary>

      <solution>
        <p>
          <ol>
            <li>
              <p>
                First, write down the characteristic equation:
                <md>
                  <mrow> r^3 - 6r^2 + 11r - 6 = 0 </mrow>
                </md>.
                Plugging this into to the factoring tool shows that it factors as <m> (r-3)(r-2)(r-1) </m>.  Solving for <m>r</m>, we get
                <md>
                  <mrow> r = 1, \quad r = 2, \quad r = 3 </mrow>
                </md>.
                Therefore, the general solution is:
                <me> y = c_1 e^{x} + c_2 e^{2x} + c_3 e^{3x} </me>.
              </p>
            </li>
            <li>
              <p>
                The characteristic equation is:
                <me>
                  9 r^5 - 4 r^4 + r + 10 = 0
                </me>
                which is challenging to solve by hand. However, typing it into factoring tool above shows that it can be factored as
                <me>
                  2\left(r+3\right)^2 \left(r-1\right) \left(r-\frac12\right) = 0
                </me>,
                which gives the solutions
                <me>
                  r = -3, \quad r = -3, \quad r = 1, \quad r = \frac12
                </me>.
                Therefore, the general solution is:
                <me>
                  y = c_1 e^{-3x} + c_2x e^{-3x} + c_3 e^{x} + c_4 e^{\frac12 x}
                </me>.
              </p>
            </li>
          </ol>
        </p>
      </solution>
    </example>
    
    <p>
      If you want the solutions to a characteristic equation without factoring it, you can also approximate the solutions directly using a numerical solver, as shown in the next example.
    </p>
    
    <example><title> Approximating the Characteristic Solutions </title>
      <p>
        <statement>
          <p>
            Consider the fifth-order LHCC equation:
            <me> y^{(5)} - 4y''' + 3y'' - y = 0 </me>.
          </p>
        </statement>
      </p>
      
      <solution>
        <p>
          The characteristic equation is:
        </p>

        <me>
          r^5 - 4r^3 + 3r^2 - 1 = 0
        </me>.

        <p>
          Solving this polynomial equation analytically is challenging, so we use a numerical solver (<url href="https://www.wolframalpha.com/input?i=r%5E5-4*r%5E3%2B3*r%5E2-1">Wolfram Alpha</url>). This equation has five roots. Three are real:
        </p>

        <me> r_1 \approx -2.29, \quad r_2 \approx -0.46, \quad r_3 \approx 1.52, </me>

        <p>
          and two are complex:
        </p>

        <me> r_4 \approx 0.61 + 0.5i, \quad r_5 \approx 0.61 - 0.5i </me>.

        <p>
          The general solution, incorporating these roots, is:
        </p>

        <md>
          <mrow>  y = c_1 e^{-2.29x}  +\amp\ c_2 e^{-0.46x} + c_3 e^{1.52x}        </mrow>
          <mrow>            +\amp\ e^{0.61x} (c_4 \cos(0.5x) + c_5 \sin(0.5x))  </mrow>
        </md>.
      </solution>
    </example>
  </paragraphs>

  <p>
    Regardless of the method you choose to find the roots, the general solution is relatively easy to construct once you have them.
  </p>
