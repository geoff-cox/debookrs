<part><title> Linear Constant Coefficient (LCC) Differential Equations </title>

	<introduction>

		<p>
			As we delve deeper into the study of differential equations, we transition from first-order equations to those involving second derivatives and higher. These higher-order equations are essential for modeling complex systems in physics, engineering, and applied mathematics. 
		</p>

		<p>
			In this part of the text, we focus on a particularly important class of differential equations known as <term>linear differential equations with constant coefficients</term>. We will learn to solve these equations using various methods, including the characteristic equation, undetermined coefficients, and variation of parameters. By mastering these techniques, you will develop a robust toolkit for solving a wide range of higher-order linear differential equations
		</p>

		<p>
			We will split our approach into two subcases based on whether the equation we are solving has a constant term of zero (homogeneous) or a non-zero constant term (nonhomogeneous).
		</p>

	</introduction>

	<chapter><title> Homogeneous Equations </title>

		<introduction>
				
			<p>
				As you might expect, solving differential equations becomes more challenging as the order of the derivative increases. However, <term>linear homogeneous differential equations with constant coefficients</term> (LHCC equations) are the exception since they are not only approachable, but also generalize well across many different disciplines in science and engineering.
			</p>

			<p>
				We will begin by identifying what makes an equation linear, homogeneous, and constant coefficient, ensuring a clear understanding of the terminology and structure. From there, we will develop systematic methods to find general solutions, leveraging the characteristic equation to determine the form of solutions. By the end of this chapter, you will have a solid grasp of how to approach and solve LHCC equations, preparing you for further exploration into nonhomogeneous cases and applied problem-solving.
			</p>

		</introduction>

		<section><title> Introduction to LHCC Equations </title>

			<introduction>
				<p>
					Before learning the methods that solve Linear Homogeneous Constant Coefficient (LHCC) differential equations, it is important to be able to identify these equation and have some intuition about the type of solutions that satisfy them.
				</p>

				<p>
					In this section, we will develop the fundamentals of LHCC equations, starting with a break down of their key structural components‚Äî<em>linear</em>, <em>homogeneous</em>, and <em>constant coefficients</em>. From there, we will examine why <em>exponential functions</em> naturally arise as solutions and why other function types do not. Finally, we will introduce the <em>characteristic equation</em>, a powerful algebraic tool that allows us to extract the specific exponential solutions that satisfy these equations.
				</p>
			</introduction>

			<subsection><title> Identifying LHCC Equations </title>

				<introduction>
					<p>
						To solve a differential equation effectively, we must first determine its structural properties. The term "LHCC" highlights three key characteristics: <term>linearity</term>, <term>homogeneity</term>, and <term>constant coefficients</term>. Understanding these properties is crucial because they dictate both the nature of the solutions and the methods used to obtain them.
					</p>

					<p>
						In this subsection, we break down each component of an LHCC equation, providing clear definitions and illustrative examples. By the end, you will be equipped with the ability to classify differential equations based on these properties, distinguishing LHCC equations from other types.
					</p>
				</introduction>

				<subsubsection><title> The <q>L</q> in LHCC: Linearity </title>
					
					<p>
						Recall, a differential equation is considered <term>linear</term> if it only contains linear terms. In other words, the dependent variable and all its derivatives appear only to the first power and are not multiplied or composed with each other. In contrast, an equation is <term>nonlinear</term> if it contains terms such as <m>y^2</m>, <m>(y')^3</m>, <m>\sin(y)</m>, or <m>y \cdot y''</m>. 
					</p>

					<p>
						The general form of an <m>n</m>-th order linear differential equation is:
					</p>

					<men xml:id="linear-equation-w-coefficents">
						\us{	\large\text{of } y^{(n)}	}{
						\us{	\large\text{coefficient }	}{ 
								\us{\uparrow}{ {\color{BurntOrange} \ul{a_n(x)}	}}\ y^{(n)}
						}}	+ \cdots +
						\us{	\large\text{of } y''		}{
						\us{	\large\text{coefficient }	}{
								\us{\uparrow}{ {\color{BurntOrange} \ul{a_2(x)}	}}\ y''
						}}	+
						\us{	\large\text{of } y'			}{
						\us{	\large\text{coefficient }	}{
								\us{\uparrow}{ {\color{BurntOrange} \ul{a_1(x)}	}}\ y'
						}}	+
						\us{	\large\text{of } y			}{
						\us{	\large\text{coefficient }	}{
								\us{\uparrow}{ {\color{BurntOrange} \ul{a_0(x)}	}}\ y
						}}	=
						\us{	\large\text{term}			}{ 
						\us{	\large\text{constant }		}{
								\us{\uparrow}{ {\color{BurntOrange} \ul{f(x)}	}}
						}}
					</men>

					<p>
						where the coefficients, <m>a_n(x), a_{n-1}(x), \dots, a_1(x), a_0(x)</m> and the constant term, <m>f(x)</m>, are functions of the independent variable <m>x</m>. <m>f(x)</m> is also commonly known as the <term>forcing function</term> or <term>nonhomogeneous term</term>.
					</p>

					<aside>
						<p>
							üòï = nonlinear term
						</p>
					</aside>

					<p>
						Examples of <m>\quad</m> 
						<xref ref="linear-equation-examples" text="custom">		üëâ Linear Equations		</xref> <m>\quad</m> 
						<xref ref="nonlinear-equation-examples" text="custom">	üëâ Nonlinear Equations	</xref>
					</p>

				</subsubsection>

				<subsubsection><title> The <q>H</q> in LHCC: Homogeneous </title>

					<p>
						A linear differential equation is classified as <term>homogeneous</term> if its right-hand side (the constant term <m>f(x)</m>) is zero. Otherwise, it is classified as <term>nonhomogeneous</term>.
					</p>

					<p>
						For example, the following equations are homogeneous
					</p>

					<me>
						x y'' - \cos x y' + 6y = 0, \qquad y''' + 2y'' = y'
					</me>

					<p>
						since the constant terms are zero in each case. The equations
					</p>

					<me>
						y'' + 4xy = \boxed{x}, \qquad y' + \boxed{e^x} = 0
					</me>
					
					<p>
						are nonhomogeneous because the (boxed) contant terms are not zero.
					</p>

				</subsubsection>

				<subsubsection><title> The <q>CC</q> in LHCC: Constant Coefficients </title>
					
					<aside>
						<p>
							‚òùÔ∏è <term>Remember!</term> Constant terms are not coefficients.
						</p>
					</aside>

					<p>
						A linear differential equation has <term>constant coefficients</term> if all coefficient functions <m>a_n, a_{n-1}, \dots, a_0</m> are constants rather than functions of <m>x</m>.
					</p>

					<p>
						Some linear differential equations with constant coefficients are
					</p>

					<me>
						2y'' - 3y' + 5y = 0, \qquad y''' + 4y'' - 2y' + 7y = e^x
					</me>,

					<p>
						and here are some equations with (boxed) non-constant coefficients:
					</p>

					<me>
						\boxed{3^x}\ y'' + \boxed{2x}\ y' + y = 0, \qquad y'' + y' + \boxed{\ln x}\ y = \sin x
					</me>.

				</subsubsection>
					
				<p>
					Combining these ideas, we arrive at the formal definition of an LHCC equation.
				</p>
				
				<definition xml:id="def-lhcc"><title> LHCC Equation </title>
					<statement>
						<p>
							A linear homogeneous differential equation with constant coefficients (LHCC) is of the form:
						</p>
						<men xml:id="lhcc-equation">
							a_n\ y^{(n)} + \cdots + a_2\ y'' + a_1\ y' + a_0\ y = 0
						</men>
						<p>
							where <m>a_n,\ a_{n-1},\ \ldots,\ a_2,\ a_1,\ a_0</m> are constants.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</definition>
				
				<p>
					The following equations are examples of LHCC equations:
				</p>

				<me>
					2y'' - 3y' + 5y = 0, \qquad \frac{d^2s}{dt^2} + \frac{ds}{dt} - 4s = 0 \qquad u'' = u
				</me>.

				<conclusion>
					<p>
						With this understanding, we are now ready to explore how to find solutions to LHCC equations. The next section introduces an 
						intuitive approach to understanding what the solutions to these equations look like.
					</p>
				</conclusion>

			</subsection>

			<subsubsection><title> Fundamental Solutions </title>

				<introduction>
					<p>
						When encountering a new type of differential equation, a natural question arises: <em>What kind of functions make sense to plug in for <m>y</m>?</em> The structure of the equation itself often provides clues as to which functions are likely to satisfy it. 
					</p>
				</introduction>

				<p>
					One way to identify suitable solutions is with the idea of <xref ref="like-terms" text="custom"><term>like-terms</term></xref>. In algebra, like-terms share the same variable parts and can be simplified as a single term when added. For example,
				</p>

				<men xml:id="like-terms-combining">
					\ul{5\cos x} + \ul{\ul{2x^2}} - \ul{\cos x} + \ul{\ul{3x^2}} = 4 \cos x + 5x^2
				</men>.
				
				<p>
					To see how this helps us find valid solutions, consider the LHCC equation:
				</p>

				<me>
					y' - 2y = 0
				</me>.

				<p>
					A function of is a solution to this equation, if you plug it in for <m>y'</m> and <m>y</m> and everything simplifies to <m>0 = 0</m>. This only happens when all the terms on the left cancel out, meaning they simplify as <em>like-terms</em> where the coefficients add up to zero.
				</p>

				<p>
					This implies that any function we plug in for <m>y</m>, must result in both <m>y'</m> and <m>2y</m> being like-terms. Otherwise, the left side could never simplify to zero. This leads to the next important question:
				</p>

				<blockquote>
					<p>
						<q>Which types of functions are like-terms with their own differatives?</q>
					</p>
				</blockquote>

				<p>
					You may recall from calculus that the derivative of an exponential function is <em>another exponential function</em>. For example, the derivative of <m>e^{7x}</m> is <m>7e^{7x}</m>, which are like-terms. This property makes exponentials good candidates for solutions to LHCC equations. To see this more concretely, let's substitute different function types into our equation and see what kind of terms they produce.
				</p>

				<sidebyside widths="28% 5% 32% 5% 30%">
					<p>
						<em>Case 1:</em> <m>\quad y = x^2</m>
						<md>
							<mrow> \left[x^2\right]' - 2 \left[x^2\right] = 0\amp </mrow>
							<mrow> \us{\large\text{don't cancel}}{\ub{2x - 2x^2}_{\large\text{not like-terms}}} = 0\amp </mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
					<p/>
					<p>
						<em>Case 2:</em> <m>\quad y = \ln x</m>
						<md>
							<mrow> \left[2\ln x\right]' - 2\left[\ln x\right] = 0\amp </mrow>
							<mrow> \us{\large\text{don't cancel}}{\ub{\frac{2}{x} - 2\ln x}_{\large\text{not like-terms}}} = 0\amp </mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
					<p/>
					<p>
						<em>Case 3:</em> <m>\quad y = e^{2x}</m>
						<md>
							<mrow> \left[e^{2x}\right]' - 2 \left[e^{2x}\right] = 0\amp </mrow>
							<mrow> \us{\large\text{do cancel}}{\ub{2e^{2x} - 2e^{2x}}_{\large\text{like-terms}}} = 0\amp </mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<p>
					This shows that <em>only</em> the exponential function <m>y = e^{2x}</m> produces like-terms that fully cancel out when plugged in for <m>y</m>. The next example shows that a similar pattern holds for second-order equations as well.
				</p>

				<example xml:id="lhcc-soln-example-1"><title> Multiple Exponential Solutions </title>
				
					<p>
						<statement>
							<p>
								Verify that <m>y = e^{4x}</m> and <m>y = e^{-x}</m> are solutions to the differential equation
							</p>
							<me>
								y'' - 3y' - 4y = 0
							</me>,
							<p>
								but <m>y = x^4</m>, <m>y=\ln x</m> and <m>y=e^{3x}</m> are not.
							</p>
						</statement>
					</p>
					<solution>
						<p>
							First, let's check the first two exponential functions:
						</p>
						<sidebyside widths="20% 80%">
							<p>
								<me>
									y=e^{4x}:
								</me>
							</p>
							<p>
								<md>
									<mrow> 	[e^{4x}]'' - 3\left[e^{4x}\right]' - 4\left[e^{4x}\right]	=\amp\ 0	</mrow>
									<mrow> 	
										\us{\large\text{like-terms simplify to }0}{\ub{
											16e^{4x} - 12e^{4x} - 4e^{4x}	
										}}
										=\amp\ 0	
									</mrow>
									<mrow> 	0	=\amp\ 0	\ \ ‚úÖ </mrow>
								</md>
							</p>
						</sidebyside>
						<sidebyside widths="20% 80%">
							<p>
								<me>
									y=e^{-x}:
								</me>
							</p>
							<p>
								<md>
									<mrow> 	[e^{-x}]'' - 3\left[e^{-x}\right]' - 4\left[e^{-x}\right]	=\amp\ 0	</mrow>
									<mrow> 	
										\us{\large\text{like-terms simplify to }0}{\ub{
											e^{-x} + 3e^{-x} - 4e^{-x}

						</sidebyside>
						<p>
							As you can see, plugging in exponential functions produces like-terms that cancel out and simplify to zero.
						</p>
						<p>
							Let's see what happens when we plug in non-exponential functions:
						</p>
						<sidebyside widths="20% 80%">
							<p>
								<me>
									y=x^4:
								</me>
							</p>
							<p>
								<md>

											12x^2 - 12x^3 - 4x^4

								</md>
							</p>
						</sidebyside>
						<sidebyside widths="20% 80%">
							<p>
								<me>
									y=\ln x:
								</me>
							</p>
							<p>
								<md>
									<mrow> 	[\ln x]'' - 3\left[\ln x\right]' - 4\left[\ln x\right]	=\amp\ 0	</mrow>
									<mrow> 	
										\us{\large\text{not like-terms, do not combine}}{\ub{
											-x^{-2} - 3x^{-1} - 4\ln x

									</mrow>

							</p>

							In this case, the non-exponential functions produce non-like-terms that do not simplify and are not solutions.
						</p>
						<p>
							Finally, let's check the exponential function, <m>y = e^{3x}</m>:
						</p>
						<sidebyside widths="20% 80%">
							<p>

								</me>

							<p>

									<mrow>

											9e^{3x} - 9e^{3x} - 4e^{3x}	
										}}

									</mrow>
									<mrow> 	- 4e^{3x}	=\amp\ 0	\ \ ‚ùå </mrow>

							</p>
						</sidebyside>
						<p>
							In this last case, although we get like-terms, they do not fully cancel and so <m>y=e^{3x}</m> is not a solution.
						</p>
					</solution>
				</example>

				<conclusion>
					<p>
						These examples provide strong evidence that exponentials are a natural choice for solutions to LHCC equations. It also shows that not all exponential functions work, only those that result in coefficients that sum to zero. In the next section, we will introduce you to the <em>characteristic equation</em>, a tool that tells us which of the exponential functions are the correct ones.
					</p>

			<subsubsection><title> The Characteristic Equation </title>

				<introduction>
					<p>
						So far, we have established that the best candidates for the solutions of <em>Linear Homogeneous Constant Coefficient (LHCC) equations</em> are <em>exponential functions</em>, but we don't know which ones work and how many there are. To determine this, we need the <em>characteristic equation</em>, which we will cover in this subsection.
					</p>
				</introduction>

				<p>
					To motivate the characteristic equation, let's start with the simple first-order LHCC equation:
				</p>

				<me>
					y' - 5y = 0.
				</me>

				<p>
					We assume the solution is an exponential, of the form <m>y = e^{rx}</m>, where <m>r</m> is a constant to be determined. Substituting this into the equation, gives
				</p>

				<md>
					<mrow>	\left[e^{rx}\right]' - 5 e^{rx}	\amp = 0	</mrow>
					<mrow>	r e^{rx} - 5 e^{rx}				\amp = 0	</mrow>
					<mrow>	(r - 5) e^{rx} 					\amp = 0	</mrow>
				</md>.

				<p>
					Since <m>e^{rx}</m> is never zero, we must have: <m>\quad r - 5 = 0\ \Rightarrow\ r = 5</m>.
				</p>

				<p>
					Thus, the fundamental solution is <m>y = e^{5x}</m> and the equation <m>r - 5 = 0</m>, which determines <m>r</m>, is the <em>characteristic equation</em> for this LHCC equation.
				</p>

				<p>
					Now, let's extend this idea to a higher-order equation. Consider the second-order equation:
				</p>

				<me>
					y'' - 3y' - 4y = 0.
				</me>

				<p>
					Again, assuming, <m>y = e^{rx}</m>, we substitute in <m>y</m>, <m>y'</m>, and <m>y''</m> to obtain
				</p>

				<md>
					<mrow>	(r^2 e^{rx}) - 3 (r e^{rx}) - 4 (e^{rx})	\amp = 0	</mrow>
					<mrow>	(r^2 - 3r - 4) e^{rx}						\amp = 0	</mrow>
				</md>

				<p>
					As before, this leads to the <em>characteristic equation</em>, given by
				</p>

				<me>
					r^2 - 3r - 4 = 0.
				</me>

				<p>
					Solving for <m>r</m>, we get
				</p>

				<md>
					<mrow> (r - 4)(r + 1) = 0 \amp </mrow>
					<mrow> r = 4, \quad r = -1. \amp </mrow>
				</md>

				<p>
					and finally the fundamental solutions are
				</p>

				<me>
					y_1 = e^{4x}, \quad y_2 = e^{-x}.
				</me>

				<p>
					As you can see, the structure of exponential functions naturally lead to a characteristic equation that we can solve to determine each exponential solution. The formal definition of the characteristic equation is given next.
				</p>

				<definition xml:id="def-characteristic-equation"><title> The Characteristic Equation </title>
					<statement>
						<p>
							The <term>characteristic equation</term> of an <m>n</m>-th order LHCC equation
						</p>

						<me>
							a_n\ y^{(n)} + a_{n-1}\ y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_1\ y' + a_0\ y = 0
						</me>

						<p>
							is the <m>n</m>-th degree polynomial
						</p>

						<me>
							a_n\ r^n + a_{n-1}\ r^{n-1} + \cdots + a_1 r + a_0 = 0
						</me>.

						<p>
							The solutions <m>r_1, r_2, \dots, r_n</m> to this polynomial determine the fundamental exponential solutions
							<fn>
								<p>
									Later, we will see that it is possible for a solutions to have the form
									<me>
										y_i = x^k e^{r_i\ x}
									</me>,
									where <m>k</m> depends on how many times <m>r_i</m> repeats as a solution to the characteristic equation.
								</p>
							</fn>
							<me>
								y_1=e^{r_1 x},\ y_2=e^{r_2 x},\ \dots,\ y_n=e^{r_n x}
							</me>.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</definition>

				<theorem xml:id="characteristic-eqn-details"><title> Where does the Characteristic Equation come from? </title>
					<p>
						To find the characteristic equation, we substitute <m>y = e^{rx}</m> into the <m>n</m>-th order <xref ref="lhcc-equation" text="custom">LHCC equation</xref> and solve for <m>r</m>. Doing this gives us
						<md>
							<mrow>	
								a_n\ \left[e^{rx}\right]^{(n)} + \cdots + a_2\ \left[e^{rx}\right]'' + a_1\ \left[e^{rx}\right]' + a_0\ e^{rx} =\amp\ 0
							</mrow>
							<mrow>	a_n\ r^n e^{rx} + \cdots + a_2\ r^2 e^{rx} + a_1\ r e^{rx} + a_0\ e^{rx} =\amp\ 0 </mrow>
							<mrow>	\left(a_n\ r^n + \cdots + a_2\ r^2 + a_1\ r + a_0\right)e^{rx}  =\amp\ 0 </mrow>
						</md>.
						Since <m>e^{rx}</m> is never zero, we must have
						<me>
							a_n\ r^n + \cdots + a_2\ r^2 + a_1\ r + a_0 = 0
						</me>
						which is an <m>n</m>-th order polynomial in <m>r</m>.
					</p>
				</theorem>

				<example><title> Characteristic Equation &amp; Exponential Solutions </title>
					<statement>
						<p>
							For each of the following equations, determine the characteristic equation and the fundamental exponential solutions.
						</p>
						<p>
							<sidebyside widths="40% 60%">
								<p>
									<line>	1.	<m>\ds\ 5y' + 6y = 0</m>	</line>
									<line>		<m></m>						</line>
								</p>
								<p>
									<solution>
										<p>
											Assuming <m>y = e^{rx}</m>, we have
										</p>
										<md>
											<mrow>	5\left[e^{rx}\right]' + 6\left[e^{rx}\right] = 0	\amp </mrow>
											<mrow>	5r e^{rx} + 6 e^{rx} = 0							\amp </mrow>
											<mrow>	(5r + 6) e^{rx} = 0									\amp </mrow>
										</md>.
										<p>
											Thus, the characteristic equation is
										</p>
										<me>
											5r + 6 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad r = -\frac{6}{5}
										</me>,
										<p>
											and the fundamental solution is
										</p>
										<me>
											y = e^{-\frac{6}{5}x}.
										</me>
									</solution>
								</p>
							</sidebyside>
							<sidebyside widths="40% 60%">
								<p>
									<line>	2.	<m>\ds\ y'' - 4y = 0</m>	</line>
									<line>		<m></m>						</line>
								</p>
								<p>
									<solution>
										<p>
											Assuming <m>y = e^{rx}</m>, we have
										</p>
										<md>
											<mrow>	r^2 e^{rx} - 4 e^{rx} = 0	\amp </mrow>
											<mrow>	(r^2 - 4) e^{rx} = 0		\amp </mrow>
										</md>
										<p>
											Thus, the characteristic equation is
										</p>
										<md>
											<mrow>	r^2 -	\amp 4 = 0								</mrow>
											<mrow>			\amp \Rightarrow\ (r - 2)(r + 2) = 0	</mrow>
										</md>
										<p>
											and the fundamental solutions are
										</p>
										<me>
											y_1 = e^{2x}, \quad y_2 = e^{-2x}.
										</me>
									</solution>
								</p>
							</sidebyside>
							<sidebyside widths="40% 60%">
								<p>
									<line>	3.	<m>\ds\ y''' - 6y'' + y' - 6y = 0</m>	</line>
									<line>		<m></m>									</line>
								</p>
								<p>
									<solution>
										<p>
											Assuming <m>y = e^{rx}</m>, we have
										</p>
										<md>
											<mrow>	r^3 e^{rx} - 6r^2 e^{rx} + r e^{rx} - 6 e^{rx} = 0	\amp </mrow>
											<mrow>	(r^3 - 6r^2 + r - 6) e^{rx} = 0						\amp </mrow>
										</md>
										<p>
											Thus, the characteristic equation is
										</p>
										<me>
											r^3 - 6r^2 + r - 6 = 0
										</me>.
										<p>
											We can factor this as
										</p>
										<md>
											<mrow>	r^2(r - 6) + (r - 6) = 0	\amp </mrow>
											<mrow>	(r^2 + 1)(r - 6) = 0		\amp </mrow>
											<mrow>	r = -i,\ r = i,\ r = 6		\amp </mrow>
										</md>.
										<p>
											Therefore, the fundamental solutions are
										</p>
										<me>
											y_1 = e^{-ix}, \quad y_2 = e^{ix}, \quad y_3 = e^{6x}.
										</me>
									</solution>
								</p>
							</sidebyside>
						</p>
					</statement>
				</example>
				
				<p>

					This last example demostrates that, in general, an <m>n</m>-th order LHCC equation has <m>n</m> exponential solutions of the form <m>e^{rx}</m>, where <m>r</m> is a solution (real or complex) to the <m>n</m>-th degree characteristic equation.
				</p>

				<p>
					Now that we understand how to systematically find the fundamental exponential solutions, the last and easiest step is to construct the <em>general solution</em> by combining these solutions appropriately.
				</p>

		<section><title> Solving LHCC Equations </title>

			<introduction>
				<p>
					Now that we understand the structure and solutions associated with <em>Linear Homogeneous Constant Coefficient (LHCC) equations</em>, our final goal is to develop systematic ways to obtain their general solutions. Fortunately, LHCC solutions consist exclusively of exponential function types. By understanding the role of <em>exponential functions</em> in these equations, we can construct the general solution by combining the fundamental solutions that satisfy the equation.
				</p>

				<p>
					In this section, we will show how <em>general solutions</em> to LHCC equaions are built by employing the <em>superposition principle</em>. Additionally, we will investigate how the nature of the solutions to the <em>characteristic equation</em> affects the final form of the general solution.
				</p>
			</introduction>

			<subsection><title> The General Solution </title>

				<introduction>

					<p>
						We are now ready to discuss how to form the general solution to a Linear Homogeneous Constant Coefficient (LHCC) equation. Before we do, let's review everything we know about LHCC equations up to this point.
					</p>

					<sidebyside widths="14% 86%">
						<p>
							‚úèÔ∏è <term>FACT 1</term>
						</p>
						<p>
							An <m>n</m>-th order of LHCC equation has the form
							<me>
								a_n\ y^{(n)} + a_{n-1}\ y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_2\ y'' + a_1\ y' + a_0\ y = 0
							</me>,
							where <m>a_n,\ a_{n-1},\ \ldots,\ a_2,\ a_1,\ a_0</m> are constants.
						</p>
					</sidebyside>

					<sidebyside widths="14% 86%">
						<p>
							‚úèÔ∏è <term>FACT 2</term>
						</p>
						<p>
							The <em>characteristic equation</em> of the LHCC equation is given by
							<me>
								a_n\ r^n + a_{n-1}\ r^{n-1} + \cdots + a_2\ r^2 + a_1\ r + a_0 = 0
							</me>,
							and has <m>n</m> solutions: <m>\ r_1,\ r_2,\ \dots,\ r_n</m> (real or complex).
						</p>
					</sidebyside>

					<sidebyside widths="14% 86%">
						<p>
							‚úèÔ∏è <term>FACT 3</term>
						</p>
						<p>
							The <em>fundamental solutions</em> to the LHCC equation are given by
							<me>
								y_1 = e^{r_1 x},\quad y_2 = e^{r_2 x},\quad \dots,\quad y_n = e^{r_n x},
							</me>
							where <m>r_1,\ r_2,\ \dots,\ r_n</m> are the solutions to the <em>characteristic equation</em>.
						</p>
					</sidebyside>

					<p>
						Now, we will explore how to combine these fundamental solutions to construct the <em>general solution</em> to any LHCC equation. This will allow us to capture all possible solutions to the differential equation, ensuring that we do not miss any.
					</p>

				</introduction>

				<p>
					In addition to these facts, the last thing that we need to start building the general solution is an important concept known as the <em>superposition principle</em>. 
				</p>

				<definition xml:id="superposition-principle"><title> Superposition Principle </title>
					<statement>
						<p>
							If <m>y_1, y_2, \dots, y_n</m> are solutions to a homogeneous linear differential equation, then any combination:
						</p>

						<me>
							y = c_1\ y_1 + c_2\ y_2 + \dots + c_n\ y_n
						</me>

						<p>
							is also a solution for any constants <m>c_1, c_2, \dots, c_n</m>.
						</p>
					</statement>
				</definition>

				<p>
					With this result and the above facts in hand, we can now construct the general solution to any LHCC equation as the sum of all its fundamental solutions. This gives us our fourth and final fact:
				</p>

				<sidebyside widths="14% 86%">
					<p>
						‚úèÔ∏è <term>FACT 4</term>
					</p>
					<p>
						For an <m>n</m>-th order LHCC equation, the general solution is
						<me>
							y = c_1\ y_1 + c_2\ y_2 + \cdots + c_n\ y_n,
						</me>
						where <m>c_1</m>, <m>c_2</m>, <m>\dots</m>, <m>c_n</m> are arbitrary constants and <m>y_1</m>, <m>y_2</m>, <m>\dots</m>, <m>y_n</m> are the fundamental solutions.
					</p>
				</sidebyside>

				<example><title> LHCC Equation General Solution </title>

					<p>
						<statement>
							<p>
								Find the general solution to each of the equation below.
							</p>
							<p>
								<sidebyside widths="40% 60%">
									<p>
										<line>	1.	<m>\ds\ y'+\pi\ y = 0</m>	</line>
										<line>		<m></m>						</line>
									</p>
									<p>
										<solution>
											<p>
												The characteristic equation
											</p>
											<me>
												r + \pi = 0
											</me>,
											<p>
												has the solution <m>r = -\pi</m>. So, the equation has one fundamental solution of the form
											</p>
											<me>
												y_1 = e^{-\pi x}
											</me>.
											<p>
												By the superposition principle, the general solution is
											</p>
											<me>
												y = c_1 e^{-\pi x}
											</me>.
										</solution>
									</p>
								</sidebyside>
								<sidebyside widths="40% 60%">
									<p>
										<line>	2.	<m>\ds\ \omega'' - 7\omega' = 0</m>	</line>
										<line>		<m></m>						</line>
									</p>
									<p>
										<solution>
											<p>
												The characteristic equation
											</p>
											<me>
												r^2 - 7r = 0
											</me>,
											<p>
												has the solutions <m>r = 0,\ 7</m>. So, the fundamental solutions are
											</p>
											<me>
												\omega_1 = e^{0x}, \quad \omega_2 = e^{7x}
											</me>.
											<p>
												By the superposition principle, the general solution is
											</p>
											<me>
												\omega = c_1\ e^{0x} + c_2\ e^{7x} = c_1 + c_2\ e^{7x}
											</me>.
										</solution>
									</p>
								</sidebyside>
								<sidebyside widths="40% 60%">
									<p>
										<line>	3.	<m>\ds\ 2\frac{d^{3}M}{dt^{3}} - 6\frac{dM}{dt} = 0</m>	</line>
										<line>		<m></m>									</line>
									</p>
									<p>
										<solution>
											<p>
												The characteristic equation
											</p>
											<me>
												2r^3 - 6r = 0
											</me>,
											<p>
												can be factored as
											</p>
											<md>
												<mrow>	2r(r^2 - 3) = 0	\amp </mrow>
											</md>.
											<p>
												So, <m>r = 0,\ \sqrt{3},\ -\sqrt{3}\ </m> and the fundamental solutions are
											</p>
											<me>
												e^{0t}, \qquad e^{\sqrt{3}t}, \qquad e^{-\sqrt{3}t}
											</me>.
											<p>
												By the superposition principle, the general solution is
											</p>
											<me>
												M(t) = c_1 + c_2 e^{\sqrt{3}t} + c_3 e^{-\sqrt{3}t}
											</me>.
										</solution>
									</p>
								</sidebyside>
							</p>
						</statement>
					</p>
				</example>

				<conclusion>
					<p>
						These examples illustrate how the general solution of an LHCC equation is created given the exponential functions that satisfy the equation. While the method is fairly straight forward, there are still a few details that need to be addressed. In particular, we will investigate what to do if the characteristic equations has <em>repeated roots</em> and how to handle high order LHCC equations.
					</p>

			<subsection><title> 2nd-Order Equations </title>
				
				<introduction>
					<p>
						Now that we've mastered first-order LHCC equations, it's time to explore second-order LHCC equations. These are slightly more complex but follow a similar approach. We'll see how the characteristic equation helps us find solutions and look at the different cases that arise depending on the nature of the roots.
					</p>
				</introduction>

				<p>
					In general, for any second-order LHCC equation of the form:
					<men xml:id="second-order-lhcc-de"> a y'' + b y' + c y = 0 </men>
					The characteristic equation is:
					<men xml:id="second-order-lhcc-characteristic-equation"> a r^2 + b r + c = 0 </men>
					This quadratic equation will have two solutions, which could be either real or complex. The general solution depends on the nature of these solutions, and we will now explore the different  cases. Before moving on, we highly recommend taking a minute to review some <xref ref="solving-the-quadratic-equation" text="custom">concepts related to solving quadratic equations</xref>.
				</p>

				<p>
					Let <m>r_1</m> and <m>r_2</m> be the solutions to the characteristic equation. When <m>r_1 \ne r_2</m>, the general solution is:
				</p>

				<men xml:id="second-order-lhcc-solution-r1-notequalto-r2"> 
					y = c_1\, e^{r_1 x} + c_2\, e^{r_2 x}
				</men>

				<p>
					However, if <m>r_1 = r_2</m>, the terms combine into like-terms,
				</p>

				<me>
					y = c_1\, e^{r_1 x} + c_2\, e^{r_2 x} = \color{blue}\us{ \large \text{like-terms}}{\us{\nwarrow}{\ul{c_1\, e^{r_1 x}}} + \us{\nearrow}{\ul{c_2\, e^{r_1 x}}}} = (\us{C}{\ub{c_1 + c_2}})\, e^{r_1 x}
				</me>,

				<p>
					which is an incomplete solution. To fix this, we multiply the second term by <m>x</m>, resulting in the correct general solution:
				</p>

				<me>
					y = c_1\, e^{r_1 x} + c_2\, x e^{r_1 x}
				</me>.

				<p>
					The following explains how <xref ref="lhcc-case-3" text="title"/> comes directly from <xref ref="lhcc-case-1" text="title"/>.
				</p>

				<p>
					Since <m>r_1</m> and <m>r_2</m> are complex, they can be written as
					<me>
						r_1 = \alpha + \beta i \quad \text{and} \quad r_2 = \alpha - \beta i
					</me>.
					Substituting these into <xref ref="second-order-lhcc-solution-r1-notequalto-r2"/>, using <xref ref="eulers-formula" text="title"/>, the <xref ref="evenodd-cos" text="custom"> even property of cosine </xref> and <xref ref="evenodd-sin" text="custom"> odd property of sine </xref>, we can rewrite the general solution as
					<md>
						<mrow> y\  &amp;= c_1 e^{(\alpha + \beta i) x} + c_2 e^{(\alpha - \beta i) x} </mrow>
						<mrow> &amp;= c_1 e^{\alpha x} e^{i \beta x} + c_2 e^{\alpha x} e^{-i \beta x} </mrow>
						<mrow> &amp;= e^{\alpha x} \Big{[}c_1 e^{i \beta x} + c_2 e^{-i \beta x}\Big{]} </mrow>
						<mrow> &amp;= e^{\alpha x} \Big{[}c_1 (\cos(\beta x) + i\sin(\beta x)) + c_2 (\cos(-\beta x) + i\sin(-\beta x))\Big{]} </mrow>
						<mrow> &amp;= e^{\alpha x} \Big{[}c_1 \cos(\beta x) + i c_1 \sin(\beta x) + c_2 \cos(-\beta x) + i c_2 \sin(-\beta x)\Big{]} </mrow>
						<mrow> &amp;= e^{\alpha x} \Big{[}c_1 \cos(\beta x) + i c_1 \sin(\beta x) + c_2 \cos(\beta x) - i c_2 \sin(\beta x)\Big{]} </mrow>
						<mrow> &amp;= e^{\alpha x} \Big{[}(\us{c_1}{\ub{c_1 + c_2}}) \cos(\beta x) + \us{c_2}{\ub{i (c_1 - c_2)}} \sin(\beta x)\Big{]} </mrow>
						<mrow> &amp;= e^{\alpha x} (c_1 \cos(\beta x) + c_2 \sin(\beta x)) </mrow>
					</md>.
				</p>

				<p>
					Although, the above explanation covers the situation when <m>r_1</m> and <m>r_2</m> are equal or not, we will split the <m>r_1 \ne r_2</m> case into <xref ref="lhcc-case-3" text="custom">two cases that treat real or complex values separately</xref>. The three cases are summarized below.
				</p>

				<assemblage><title> ‚ú≥Ô∏è Summary of Cases </title>
					<p>
						Let <m>r_1</m> and <m>r_2</m> be the solutions of the characteristic equation associated with the second-order LHCC equation. The general solution is:
						<dl width="wide">
							<li xml:id="lhcc-case-1"><title> Case 1 (real &amp; <m>\ne</m>) </title>
								<m>
									\ds y = c_1 e^{r_1 x} + c_2 e^{r_2 x} \quad (r_1 \ne r_2)
								</m>
							</li>
							<li xml:id="lhcc-case-2"><title> Case 2 (real &amp; <m>=</m>) </title>
								<m>
									\ds y = (c_1 + c_2 x) e^{r_1 x} \quad (r_1 = r_2)
								</m>
							</li>
							<li xml:id="lhcc-case-3"><title> Case 3 (complex) </title>
								<line>
									<m>
										\ds y = e^{\alpha x} (c_1 \cos(\beta x) + c_2 \sin(\beta x))
									</m>
								</line>
								<line>
									<m>
										\ds (r = \alpha \pm i\beta \implies r_1 = \alpha + i\beta, \ r_2 = \alpha - i\beta)
									</m>
								</line>
							</li>
						</dl>
					</p>
				</assemblage>

				<p>
					Let's now practice solving some second-order LHCC equations.
				</p>

				<example>

					<p>
						Find the general solutions for each LHCC equation.
						<sidebyside widths="30% 70%">
							<p>
								<m> y'' - 5y' + 6y = 0 </m>
							</p>
							<p>
								<solution>
									<p>
										First, write down and solve the characteristic equation:
										<md>
											<mrow> r^2 - 5r + 6 =\amp\ 0 </mrow>
											<mrow> (r-2)(r-3) =\amp\ 0 </mrow>
											<mrow> r_1 = 2 \quad \text{and} \quad r_2 =\amp\ 3 </mrow>
										</md>.
										Since <m>r_1</m> and <m>r_2</m> are real and <m>r_1 \ne r_2</m>, we are in <xref ref="lhcc-case-1" text="custom">Case 1</xref>. Therefore, the general solution is:
										<me> y = c_1 e^{2x} + c_2 e^{3x} </me>.
									</p>
								</solution>
							</p>
						</sidebyside>
					</p>
					
					<p>
						<sidebyside widths="30% 70%">
							<p>
								<m> y'' + 4y' + 4y = 0 </m>
							</p>

							<p>
								<solution>
									<p>
										The characteristic equation is:
										<md>
											<mrow> r^2 + 4r + 4 =\amp\ 0 </mrow>
											<mrow> (r+2)(r+2) =\amp\ 0 </mrow>
											<mrow> r_1 = -2 \quad \text{and} \quad r_2 =\amp\ -2 \quad \leftarrow <xref ref="lhcc-case-2" text="custom">Case 2</xref> </mrow>
										</md>.
										Therefore, the general solution is:
										<me> y = (c_1 + c_2 x) e^{-2x} </me>.
									</p>
								</solution>
							</p>
						</sidebyside>
					</p>

					<p>
						<sidebyside widths="30% 70%">
							<p>
								<m> y'' + 2y' + 5y = 0 </m>
							</p>
					
							<p>
								<solution>
									<p>
										The characteristic equation in this problem is:
										<md>
											<mrow> r^2 + 2r + 5 = 0\amp </mrow>
											<mrow> r = \us{\alpha}{\boxed{-1}} \pm \us{\beta}{\boxed{2}}i \amp\ \quad \leftarrow <xref ref="lhcc-case-3" text="custom">Case 3</xref> </mrow>
										</md>.
										Here, <m>\alpha = -1</m> and <m>\beta = 2</m>. So the general solution is:
										<me> y = e^{-x} (c_1 \cos(2x) + c_2 \sin(2x)) </me>.
									</p>
								</solution>
							</p>
						</sidebyside>
					</p>
				</example>

			<subsection><title> Higher-Order Equations </title>

					<p>

					</p>
				</introduction>

								<line><em>(5th order)</em></line>
							</cell>
							<cell><me>c_1\sin t + c_2\cos t + (c_3t + c_4)e^{\pi t} + c_5e^{5t}</me></cell>
						</row>
					</tabular>
				</table>

				<p>
					As the table demonstrates, constructing the general solution of an LHCC equation is systematic once you have the roots of the characteristic equation. However, solving higher-order polynomial equations by hand can be difficult. The next example shows a few algebra techniques you can use to handle higher degree equations.
				</p>

				<example>

					<p>
						Solve the following characteristic equations.
						<ol>
							<li> <m> 4 r^2 - 9 = 0 </m> </li>
							<li> <m> r^3 + 3 r^2 - 4 r = 0 </m> </li>
							<li> <m> r^5 + 10 r^4 = 0 </m> </li>
							<li> <m> r^5 - 4 r^3 = 0 </m> </li>
							<li> <m> r^4 - 25 = 0 </m> </li>
							<li> <m> r^4 - 5r^2 + 4 = 0 </m> </li>
						</ol>
					</p>

					<solution>
						<p>
							Note: DoS = Difference of Squares.
							<ol>
								<li>
									<p>
										<tabular>
											<row>
												<cell><m> 4 r^2 - 9 = 0 </m></cell>
												<cell> <m>\leftarrow</m> DoS </cell>
											</row>
											<row>
												<cell><m> (2 r + 3)(2 r - 3) = 0 </m></cell>
												<cell/>
											</row>
											<row>
												<cell><m> 2 r + 3 = 0, \quad 2 r - 3 = 0 </m></cell>
												<cell></cell>
											</row>
											<row>
												<cell><m> \ds r_1 = -\frac32, \quad r_2 = \frac32 </m></cell>
												<cell></cell>
											</row>
										</tabular>
									</p>
								</li>

								<li>
									<p>
										<tabular>
											<row>
												<cell><m> r^3 + 3 r^2 - 4 r = 0 </m></cell>
												<cell> <m>\leftarrow r </m> common </cell>
											</row>
											<row>
												<cell><m> r\ (r^2 + 3 r - 4) = 0 </m></cell>
												<cell/>
											</row>
											<row>
												<cell><m> r\ (r + 4)(r - 1) = 0 </m></cell>
												<cell></cell>
											</row>
											<row>
												<cell><m> \ds r_1 = 0, \quad r_2 = -4, \quad r_3 = 1 </m></cell>
												<cell></cell>
											</row>
										</tabular>
									</p>
								</li>
								
								<li>
									<p>
										<tabular>
											<row>
												<cell><m> r^5 + 10 r^4 = 0 </m></cell>
												<cell> <m>\leftarrow r^4 </m> common </cell>
											</row>
											<row>
												<cell><m> r^4\ (r + 10) = 0 </m></cell>
												<cell/>
											</row>
											<row>
												<cell><m> \ds r_1 = 0\ (\text{4 repeats}), \quad r_2 = -10 </m></cell>
												<cell></cell>
											</row>
										</tabular>
									</p>
									<p>
										<em> Technically, <m>r^4 = (r-0)^4</m> and represents 4 repeated factors. </em>
									</p>
								</li>

								<li>
									<p>
										<tabular>
											<row>
												<cell><m> r^5 - 4 r^3 = 0 </m></cell>
												<cell> <m>\leftarrow</m> common </cell>
											</row>
											<row>
												<cell><m> r^3\ (r^2  - 4) = 0 </m></cell>
												<cell> <m>\leftarrow</m> DoS </cell>
											</row>
											<row>
												<cell><m> r^3\ (r + 2)(r - 2) = 0 </m></cell>
												<cell/>
											</row>
											<row>
												<cell><m> \ds r_1 = 0\ (\text{3 repeats}), \quad r_2 = -2, \quad r_3 = 2 </m></cell>
												<cell></cell>
											</row>
										</tabular>
									</p>
								</li>

								<li>
									<p>
										<tabular>
											<row>
												<cell><m> r^4 - 25 = 0 </m></cell>
												<cell> <m>\leftarrow</m> DoS </cell>
											</row>
											<row>
												<cell><m> (r^2 + 5)(r^2  - 5) = 0 </m></cell>
												<cell> <m>\leftarrow</m> DoS </cell>
											</row>
											<row>
												<cell><m> (r^2 + 5)(r - \sqrt{5})(r + \sqrt{5}) = 0 </m></cell>
												<cell/>
											</row>
											<row>
												<cell><m> r^2 + 5 = 0, \quad r - \sqrt{5} = 0, \quad r + \sqrt{5} = 0 </m></cell>
												<cell></cell>
											</row>
											<row>
												<cell><m> \ds r_1 = -i\sqrt{5}, \quad r_2 = i\sqrt{5}, \quad r_3 = -\sqrt{5}, \quad r_4 = \sqrt{5} </m></cell>
												<cell></cell>
											</row>
										</tabular>
									</p>
								</li>

								<li>
									<p>
										<md>
											<mrow> r^4 - 5r^2 + 4 = 0 </mrow>
										</md>.
										Let <m>u = r^2</m>, then we can rewrite the equation as:
										<md>
											<mrow> u^2 - 5u + 4 = 0 </mrow>
										</md>.
										Solving for <m>u</m>, we get:
										<md>
											<mrow> u = 1, \quad u = 4 </mrow>
										</md>.
										Thus, <m>r^2 = 1</m> gives <m>r = \pm 1</m>, and <m>r^2 = 4</m> gives <m>r = \pm 2</m>. The general solution is:
										<me> y = c_1 e^{x} + c_2 e^{-x} + c_3 e^{2x} + c_4 e^{-2x} </me>.
									</p>
								</li>
							</ol>
						</p>
					</solution>
				</example>

				<p>
					These factoring techniques useful, but they only apply to specific forms of polynomials and it is unlikely that you will be lucky enough to encounter such forms in practice. Therefore, it is reasonable to use technology to aid you with these tyes of problems. Many software tools are available and you are encouraged to use any that you are familiar with.
				</p>

				<example>

					<p>
						Find the general solution to the LHCC equations
						<ol>
							<li> <m> y''' - 6y'' + 11y' - 6y = 0 </m> </li>
							<li> <m> 2 y^{(4)} + 9 y''' + y'' - 21 y' + 9 y  = 0 </m> </li>
						</ol>
						using the <xref ref="factoring-tool" text="title"/>.
					</p>

					<theorem xml:id="factoring-tool"><title> Polynomial Factoring Calculator </title>
						<figure xml:id="figure-pp">
							<caption>Type in any polynomial of <m>r</m>. Use <q><m>*</m></q> for multiplication. If the polynomial has rational roots, it will show the factors.</caption>
							<interactive xml:id="poly-factor" platform="sage" width="86%" aspect="3.2:1">
								<slate surface="sage">
									r = var('r')
									r = PolynomialRing(RationalField(), 'r').gen()
									F = 'r^2 - 2*r - 8'
									P = r^2 - 2*r - 8
									@interact
									def _(ce = input_box(P, label="	Polynomial:", type=str, width=30)):
										ce_poly = sage_eval('lambda r: ' + ce)
										pretty_print( html( r'Polynomial: $ %s $'%latex(ce_poly(r) ) ) )
										pretty_print( html( r'Factored Polynomial: $ %s $'%latex(ce_poly(r).factor()) ) )
								</slate>
							</interactive>
						</figure>
					</theorem>

					<solution>
						<p>
					<ol>
							<li>
								<p>
									First, write down the characteristic equation:
									<md>
										<mrow> r^3 - 6r^2 + 11r - 6 = 0 </mrow>
									</md>.
									Plugging this into to the factoring tool shows that it factors as <m> (r-3)(r-2)(r-1) </m>.	Solving for <m>r</m>, we get
									<md>
										<mrow> r = 1, \quad r = 2, \quad r = 3 </mrow>
									</md>.
									Therefore, the general solution is:
									<me> y = c_1 e^{x} + c_2 e^{2x} + c_3 e^{3x} </me>.
								</p>
							</li>
							<li>
								<p>
									The characteristic equation is:
									<me>
										9 r^5 - 4 r^4 + r + 10 = 0
									</me>
									which is challenging to solve by hand. However, typing it into factoring tool above shows that it can be factored as
									<me>
										2\left(r+3\right)^2 \left(r-1\right) \left(r-\frac12\right) = 0
									</me>,
									which gives the solutions
									<me>
										r = -3, \quad r = -3, \quad r = 1, \quad r = \frac12
									</me>.
									Therefore, the general solution is:
									<me>
										y = (c_1x + c_2) e^{-3x} + c_3 e^{x} + c_4 e^{\frac12 x}
									</me>.
								</p>
							</li>
						</ol>
						
						</p>
					</solution>
				</example>
				
				<p>
					Besides factoring software, another options is to use numerical solvers. The next example illustrates this.
				</p>
				
				<example>
					<p>
						Consider the fifth-order LHCC equation:
						<me> y^{(5)} - 4y''' + 3y'' - y = 0 </me>.
					</p>
					<solution>
						<p>
							The characteristic equation is:
							<me> r^5 - 4r^3 + 3r^2 - 1 = 0 </me>.
							Solving this polynomial equation analytically is challenging, so we use a numerical solver (<url href="https://www.wolframalpha.com/input?i=r%5E5-4*r%5E3%2B3*r%5E2-1">Wolfram Alpha</url>). This equation has five roots. Three are real:
							<me> r_1 \approx -2.29, \quad r_2 \approx -0.46, \quad r_3 \approx 1.52, </me>
							and two are complex:
							<me> r_4 \approx 0.61 + 0.5i, \quad r_5 \approx 0.61 - 0.5i </me>.
							The general solution, incorporating these roots, is:
							<md>
								<mrow> y = c_1 e^{-2.29x} + c_2 e^{-0.46x}	+\amp\ c_3 e^{1.52x}								</mrow>
								<mrow> 										+\amp\ e^{0.61x} (c_4 \cos(0.5x) + c_5 \sin(0.5x))	</mrow>
							</md>.
							<me>y = c_1e^{-2.29t} + c_2e^{-0.46t} + c_3e^{1.52t} + e^{0.61t}(c_4\cos(0.5t) + c_5\sin(0.5t))</me>.
						</p>
					</solution>
				</example>

				<conclusion>
					<p>
						By recognizing the nature of the roots and utilizing appropriate technology for solving polynomial equations, we can tackle higher-order LHCC equations with confidence.
					</p>
				</conclusion>
