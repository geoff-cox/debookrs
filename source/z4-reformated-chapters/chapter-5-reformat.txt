</overview>

<core-narrative>

  <section label="slope-fields">
    <title>Slope Fields</title>
    <overview>Introduces slope fields and shows how they depict the <q>direction map</q> for all solutions of a first-order DE.</overview>

    <examples>
      <example role="slope-computation">
        <title>Quick Slope Check</title>
        <statement>Given y' – ty = 2 – t², compute the slope at (t,y) = (–1,6).</statement>
      </example>
    </examples>

    <section-summary>
      <ul>
        <li>A slope field visualizes the slope each solution must take at every point.</li>
        <li>Each arrow shows the local <q>direction</q> of solution curves.</li>
        <li>Computer-generated slope fields reveal the complete picture at a glance.</li>
      </ul>
    </section-summary>
  </section>

  <section label="autonomous-eqns">
    <title>Autonomous Equations</title>
    <overview>Defines autonomous equations (y' = f(y)) and shows their horizontal <q>stripe</q> structure in slope fields.</overview>

    <examples>
      <example role="autonomous-slope">
        <title>Autonomous Stripe Pattern</title>
        <statement>Explain why slope fields for y' = y² – 1 form horizontal stripes.</statement>
      </example>
    </examples>

    <section-summary>
      <ul>
        <li>Autonomous means no explicit t in the equation — slopes depend only on y.</li>
        <li>Horizontal stripes in the slope field show this dependence.</li>
        <li>Solutions shift horizontally but keep the same shape.</li>
      </ul>
    </section-summary>
  </section>

  <section label="equilibrium-solns">
    <title>Equilibrium Solutions</title>
    <overview>Focuses on the constant solutions where y' = 0 and the system <q>stops.</q></overview>

    <examples>
      <example role="equilibrium-find">
        <title>Finding Equilibria</title>
        <statement>For y' = 1 – y², solve for constant solutions y(t) = c.</statement>
      </example>
    </examples>

    <section-summary>
      <ul>
        <li>Equilibrium solutions are constant solutions where f(y) = 0.</li>
        <li>In slope fields, they appear as rows of horizontal segments.</li>
        <li>Other solutions flow toward or away from these lines.</li>
      </ul>
    </section-summary>
  </section>

  <section label="classifying-equilibria">
    <title>Classifying Equilibrium Solutions</title>
    <overview>Introduces sinks, sources, and semi-stable nodes and explains how to classify equilibria using phase lines or linearization.</overview>

    <examples>
      <example role="phase-line-demo">
        <title>Phase Line Example</title>
        <statement>Draw a phase line for y' = y² – 4y and classify equilibria.</statement>
      </example>
      <example role="linearization-demo">
        <title>Linearization Method</title>
        <statement>Use f'(y) at equilibrium points to classify stability for y' = y² – 4y + 3.</statement>
      </example>
    </examples>

    <section-summary>
      <ul>
        <li>Stable (sink): nearby solutions move toward the equilibrium.</li>
        <li>Unstable (source): nearby solutions move away.</li>
        <li>Semi-stable (node): attracts on one side, repels on the other.</li>
        <li>Phase lines compress slope field info into a simple vertical diagram.</li>
        <li>Linearization uses f'(y) to quickly classify stability.</li>
      </ul>
    </section-summary>
  </section>

  <section label="parameter-analysis">
    <title>Parameter Analysis</title>
    <overview>Explores how changing parameters affects equilibria and stability, introducing bifurcation diagrams.</overview>

    <examples>
      <example role="bifurcation-demo">
        <title>Saddle-Node Bifurcation</title>
        <statement>Analyze x' = μ – x² to find equilibria and sketch the bifurcation diagram.</statement>
      </example>
    </examples>

    <section-summary>
      <ul>
        <li>Parameters often control the number and type of equilibria.</li>
        <li>Bifurcations mark qualitative changes in behavior.</li>
        <li>Bifurcation diagrams show how equilibria and stability shift with parameters.</li>
      </ul>
    </section-summary>
  </section>
