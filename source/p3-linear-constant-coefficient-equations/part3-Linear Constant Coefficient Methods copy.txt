<part><title> Linear Constant Coefficient Methods </title>
	<introduction>
		<p>
			As we delve deeper into the world of differential equations, it's time to expand our solution techniques beyond first-order equations. Higher-order linear differential equations, those involving second derivatives or higher, are fundamental in modeling more complex systems in physics, engineering, and other fields. 
		</p>

		<p>
			In this chapter, we will explore powerful methods for solving these equations, starting with the characteristic equation for linear homogeneous equations with constant coefficients. We'll then move on to techniques like undetermined coefficients and variation of parameters, which provide us with tools to tackle non-homogeneous equations. By mastering these methods, you'll gain a robust toolkit for solving a wide range of higher-order linear differential equations.
		</p>
	</introduction>

	<chapter><title> Homogeneous </title>
		<introduction>
			<p>
				As you might expect, solving differential equations becomes more challenging as the order of the derivative increases. But don't worry! There's a class of higher-order equations that are not only approachable but also serve as a gateway to more advanced topics. These are known as linear homogeneous differential equations with constant coefficients, or LHCC for short.
			</p>
			
			<p>
				In this chapter, we will learn how to identify LHCC equations and explore a method to solve them by connecting them to a polynomial equation, called the <em>characteristic equation</em>. By the end of this section, you'll see how solving higher-order equations can be as simple as finding the roots of a polynomial.
			</p>
		</introduction>

		<section xmlns:xi="http://www.w3.org/2001/XInclude"><title> Classification </title>
			<p>
				Now that we've tackled some foundational techniques for solving differential equations, it's time to introduce a new type: linear homogeneous differential equations with constant coefficients, or LHCC equations for short. But how do we recognize them? Let's break down each part of the term <em>linear</em>, <em>homogeneous</em>, and <em>constant coefficients</em> to understand these equations fully.
			</p>
			
			<p>
				First, recall that a <term>linear differential equation</term> looks like this:
				<men xml:id="linear-equation-w-coefficents">
					{\color{blue} \os{\text{coefficient}}{\os{\downarrow}{a_n(x)}}} y^{(n)} + \cdots +
					{\color{blue} \os{\text{coefficient}}{\os{\downarrow}{a_2(x)}}} y''	 	+
					{\color{blue} \os{\text{coefficient}}{\os{\downarrow}{a_1(x)}}} y' 		+
					{\color{blue} \os{\text{coefficient}}{\os{\downarrow}{a_0(x)}}} y 		=
					\os{\text{constant term}}{\os{\downarrow}{f(x).}}
				</men>
				The key idea here is that each term involves either a derivative of <m>y</m> or <m>y</m> itself, all multiplied by coefficients that depend on <m>x</m>.
			</p>
			
			<p>
				Next, the equation is <term>homogeneous</term> if the constant term is zero, meaning <m>f(x) = 0</m>. This ensures the left-hand side of the equation equals zero.
			</p>
			
			<p>
				Finally, if all the coefficients are constants (i.e., numbers, not functions of <m>x</m>), the equation is said to have <term>constant coefficients</term>.
			</p>
			
			<p>
				Combining these ideas, we arrive at the formal definition of an LHCC equation.
			</p>
			
			<definition xml:id="def-lhcc">
				<title>LHCC Equation</title>
				<statement>
				<p>
					A linear homogeneous differential equation with constant coefficients (LHCC) is of the form:
				</p>
				<men xml:id="lhcc-equation">
					a_n\ y^{(n)} + \cdots + a_2\ y'' + a_1\ y' + a_0\ y = 0
				</men>
				<p>
					where <m>a_n,\ a_{n-1},\ \ldots,\ a_2,\ a_1,\ a_0</m> are constants.
				</p>
				</statement>
			</definition>
			
			<p>
				Now that we have the definition, let's practice identifying these equations with a few examples.
			</p>
			
			<example>
				<m>\ \ </m> Identify which of the following are linear homogeneous differential equations.
				<p>
					<me> 
						3y'' - 2ty' + y = 0, \quad 
						\frac{dg}{dx} + 3x^2 = 0, \quad 
						\frac{d^2s}{dt^2} + \frac{ds}{dt} = 4s
					</me>
				</p>
				<solution>
					<p>
						The trick is to check if the constant term is zero and the equation has only <m>y</m> and its derivatives on the left-hand side. Let's rewrite each equation with the constant term isolated:
					</p>
					<p> 
						<me> 
						3y'' - 2ty' + y = {\color{blue} 0}, \quad 
						\frac{dg}{dx} = {\color{blue} -3x^2}, \quad 
						\frac{d^2s}{dt^2} + \frac{ds}{dt} - 4s = {\color{blue} 0} 
						</me>.
					</p>
					<p>
						The first and last equations are homogeneous, but the second is not because the right-hand side is non-zero.
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>
			
			<example>
				<m>\ \ </m> Determine which of the following equations have constant coefficients.
				<p>
					<me> 
						3y'' - 2\tau y' + y = \tau, \quad 
						\sqrt{t} - \frac{dP}{dt} - \frac{P^2}{2} = 1, \quad 
						s''' = \frac{7s}{w}
					</me>
				</p>
				<solution>
					<p>
						Check if each coefficient is constant:
					</p>
					<p>
						<me> 
							\os{\text{yes}}{\os{\downarrow}{\ul{3}}} y'' - 
							\os{\text{no}}{\os{\downarrow}{\ul{2\tau}}}\ y' + 
							\os{\text{yes}}{\os{\downarrow}{\ul{1}}} y = \tau, \quad
							\sqrt{t} - 
							\os{\text{yes}}{\os{\downarrow}{\ul{1}}} \frac{dP}{dt} - 
							\os{\text{yes}}{\os{\downarrow}{\ul{\frac{1}{2}}}} P^2 = 1, \quad
							\os{\text{yes}}{\os{\downarrow}{\ul{1}}} s''' = 
							\os{\text{no}}{\os{\downarrow}{\ul{\frac{7}{w}}}} s
						</me>.
						The second equation has constant coefficients, while the others do not.
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>

		<section xmlns:xi="http://www.w3.org/2001/XInclude"><title> Solutions </title>
			<p>
				Before diving into how to solve <xref ref="def-lhcc" text="custom">LHCC equations</xref>, let's develop some intuition about what the solutions should look like. We begin with the simple first-order LHCC equation,
				<men xml:id="lhcc-intro-de"> 
					y' - 2 y = 0
				</men>
				and note that this equation requires <m>y' - 2y</m> to sum to zero. Recall from algebra that only <xref ref="like-terms" text="custom">like terms</xref> can combine to simplify to zero. 
				So, for the equation to hold, <m>y'</m> and <m>y</m> must be like terms.
			</p>

			<p>
				Let's test a few guesses for <m>y</m>: 
				<m>y = x^2</m>, <m>y = 2 \ln x</m>, and <m>y = e^{2x}</m>.
			</p>

			<sidebyside widths="30% 40% 30%">
				<p>
					<md>
						<mrow> \left[x^2\right]' - 2 \left[x^2\right] = 0 </mrow>
						<mrow> \ub{2x - 2x^2}_{\text{not like terms}} = 0 </mrow>
					</md>
				</p>
				<p>
					<md>
						<mrow> \left[2\ln x\right]' - 4\ln x = 0 </mrow>
						<mrow> \ub{\frac{2}{x} - 4\ln x}_{\text{not like terms}} = 0 </mrow>
					</md>
				</p>
				<p>
					<md>
						<mrow> \left[e^{2x}\right]' - 2 \left[e^{2x}\right] = 0 </mrow>
						<mrow> \ub{2e^{2x} - 2e^{2x}}_{\text{like terms}\, =\, 0} = 0 </mrow>
					</md>
				</p>
			</sidebyside>

			<p>
				As you can see, only <m>y = e^{2x}</m> produces like terms that simplify to zero. This is why exponential functions often work well in LHCC equations. Let's now examine a more complex example to expand on this idea.
			</p>

			<example>
				<m>\ \ </m> Consider the LHCC equation:
				<me>
					y'' + 3y' - 4y = 0.
				</me>
				<p>
					Verify that <m>y = e^{4x}</m> is a solution, but <m>y = x^4</m> and <m>y = \sin(4x)</m> are not.
				</p>
				<solution>
					<p>
						Let's check each function:
					</p>
					<sidebyside>
						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow> 	[e^{4x}]'' + 3\left[e^{4x}\right]' - 4\left[e^{4x}\right]	=\amp\ 0	</mrow>
								<mrow> 	
									\us{\text{like terms simplify to }0}{\ub{
										16e^{4x} + 12e^{4x} - 4e^{4x}	
									}}
									=\amp\ 0	
								</mrow>
								<mrow> 	0	=\amp\ 0	\ \ ✅ </mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow> 	[x^4]'' + 3\left[x^4\right]' - 4\left[x^4\right]	=\amp\ 0	</mrow>
								<mrow> 	
									\us{\text{not like terms, do not combine}}{\ub{
										24x^2 + 12x^3 - 4x^4	
									}}
									=\amp\ 0	\ \ ❌
								</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
					<p>
						<md>
							<mrow> 	[\sin(4x)]'' + 3\left[\sin(4x)\right]' - 4\left[\sin(4x)\right]	=\amp\ 0	</mrow>
							<mrow> 	
								\us{\text{not like terms}}{\ub{
									-16\sin(4x) + 12\cos(4x) - 4\sin(4x)	
								}}
								=\amp\ 0	\ \ ❌
							</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
					<p>
						Of these three, only <m>y = e^{4x}</m> is a solution.
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>

			<p>
				This example further supports the claim that exponential functions, like <m>e^{rx}</m>, are well-suited for LHCC equations because they produce like terms when differentiated. Later, we will see that if <m>e^{rx}</m> is a solution then, in some cases, it is possible for <m>xe^{rx}</m> to be a solution as well.
			</p>

			<p>
				We conclude this section with an important concept that will guide us in the sections to follow.
				<assemblage><title> Terms of the Solution to an LHCC Equation </title>
					<p>
						The general solution to an LHCC equation involves terms of the form:
						<me> e^{rx}, \quad \ub{xe^{rx}, \quad x^2e^{rx}, \quad x^3e^{rx},\  \ldots}_{\text{For Special Cases}} </me> 
						where <m>r</m> is a constant.
					</p>
				</assemblage>
			</p>

		<section xmlns:xi="http://www.w3.org/2001/XInclude"><title> 1st-Order Equations </title>  
			<p>
				In the previous section, we learned that solutions to Linear Homogeneous Constant Coefficient (LHCC) equations often involve terms of the form <m>e^{rx}</m>. However, not every value of <m>r</m> will work. The correct values of <m>r</m> arise from solving an important algebraic equation, obtained by substituting <m>y = e^{rx}</m> into the differential equation. In this section, we will focus on this process for first-order equations before building up to higher-order cases.
			</p>
			
			<p>
				Let's start with a simple first-order equation:
				<me> y' - 5y = 0 </me>
				We want to find the value of <m>r</m> such that <m>y = e^{rx}</m> is a solution. Substituting <m>y = e^{rx}</m> into the differential equation gives:
				<md>
					<mrow> \left[e^{rx}\right]' - 5 \left[e^{rx}\right] =\amp 0 </mrow>
					<mrow> r e^{rx} - 5 e^{rx} 							=\amp 0 </mrow>
					<mrow> (r - 5) e^{rx} 								=\amp 0 </mrow>
				</md>
				Since <m>e^{rx}</m> is never zero, we must have:
				<md>
					<mrow> r - 5 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad r = 5 </mrow>
				</md>
				This tells us that <m>y = e^{5x}</m> is a solution. Therefore, the general solution is:
				<me> y = C e^{5x} </me>
			</p>
			
			<p>
				The equation <m>r - 5 = 0</m>, which gave us the value of <m>r</m>, is called the <term>characteristic equation</term>. Every LHCC equation has one. The characteristic equation gives us the values of <m>r</m> that we need to construct the general solution.
			</p>
			
			<p>
				Applying the same approach to the general first-order LHCC equation gives us:
				<md>
					<mrow> 
						y=e^{rx} \quad\rightarrow\quad
						ay' + by = 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad a \left[e^{rx}\right]' + b \left[e^{rx}\right] 
													=\amp 0 
					</mrow>
					<mrow> a r e^{rx} + b e^{rx}	=\amp 0 </mrow>
					<mrow> (ar + b) e^{rx} 			=\amp 0 </mrow>
				</md>
				This results in the characteristic equation and general solution:
				<me> ar + b = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad r = -\frac{b}{a} \quad \Rightarrow \quad y = C e^{-\frac{b}{a}x} </me>
			</p>
			
			<p>
				Now, let's apply this method to a couple of examples.
			</p>
			
			<example>
				<m>\ \ </m> Find the general solution for each LHCC equation.
				
				<p>
				<me> 3y' - 9y = 0 \hspace{5em} 5y' = \pi y </me>
				</p>
			
				<solution>
				<p>First, rewrite the equations in standard form:</p>
				
				<p><me> y' - 3y = 0 \hspace{5em} 5y' - \pi y = 0 </me></p>
				
				<p>Now solve the characteristic equations:</p>
				
				<md>
					<mrow> 3r - 9 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad r = 3 \hspace{5em} 5r - \pi = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad r = \frac{\pi}{5} </mrow>
				</md>
				
				<p>Thus, the general solutions are:</p>
			
				<me> y = C e^{3x} \hspace{5em} y = C e^{\frac{\pi}{5}x} </me>
				</solution>
			</example>
			
			<p>
				This straightforward method works for any first-order LHCC equation by using the characteristic equation. In the next sections, we'll extend this technique to higher-order equations.
			</p>

		<section xmlns:xi="http://www.w3.org/2001/XInclude"><title> 2nd-Order Equations </title>
			<p>
				Now that we've mastered first-order LHCC equations, it's time to explore second-order LHCC equations. These are slightly more complex but follow a similar approach. We'll see how the characteristic equation helps us find solutions and look at the different cases that arise depending on the nature of the roots.
			</p>

			<p>
				Consider the following second-order equation:
				<men xml:id="second-order-lhcc-intro-de"> y'' - 5y' + 6y = 0 </men>
				Substituting <m>y = e^{rx}</m> into this equation, we get:
				<md>
					<mrow> \left[e^{rx}\right]'' - 5 \left[e^{rx}\right]' + 6 \left[e^{rx}\right] =	\amp\ 0							</mrow>
					<mrow> r^2 e^{rx} - 5r e^{rx} + 6 e^{rx} =										\amp\ 0							</mrow>
					<mrow> (r^2 - 5r + 6) e^{rx} =													\amp\ 0 \qquad (e^{rx} \ne 0)	</mrow>
					<mrow> \text{characteristic equation} \rightarrow \qquad r^2 - 5r + 6 =			\amp\ 0							</mrow>
					<mrow> r_1 = 2, \quad  r_2 =													\amp\ 3							</mrow>
				</md>.
				Therefore, <m>y = e^{2x}</m> and <m>y = e^{3x}</m> are solutions, and the general solution is the combination of these two:
				<me> y = C_1 e^{2x} + C_2 e^{3x} </me>.
			</p>

			<p>
				In general, for any second-order LHCC equation of the form:
				<men xml:id="second-order-lhcc-de"> a y'' + b y' + c y = 0 </men>
				The characteristic equation is:
				<men xml:id="second-order-lhcc-characteristic-equation"> a r^2 + b r + c = 0 </men>
				This quadratic equation will have two solutions, which could be either real or complex. The general solution depends on the nature of these solutions, and we will now explore the different  cases. Before moving on, we highly recommend taking a minute to review some <xref ref="solving-the-quadratic-equation" text="custom">concepts related to solving quadratic equations</xref>.
			</p>

			<p>
				Let <m>r_1</m> and <m>r_2</m> be the solutions to the characteristic equation. When <m>r_1 \ne r_2</m>, the general solution is:
				<men xml:id="second-order-lhcc-solution-r1-notequalto-r2"> y = C_1\, e^{r_1 x} + C_2\, e^{r_2 x} </men>
				However, if <m>r_1 = r_2</m>, the terms combine into like terms, 
				<me> 
					y = C_1\, e^{r_1 x} + C_2\, e^{r_2 x} = \color{blue}\us{ \large \text{like terms}}{\us{\nwarrow}{\ul{C_1\, e^{r_1 x}}} + \us{\nearrow}{\ul{C_2\, e^{r_1 x}}}} = (\us{C}{\ub{C_1 + C_2}})\, e^{r_1 x}
				</me>,
				which is an incomplete solution. To fix this, we multiply the second term by <m>x</m>, resulting in the correct general solution:
				<fn>
					<p>
						See <xref ref="tba"/> for an outline of why the extra <m>x</m> is required in the general solution of a second order LHCC equation when <m>r_1=r_2</m>.
					</p>
				</fn>
				<me> y = C_1\, e^{r_1 x} + C_2\, x e^{r_1 x} </me>
			</p>

			<p>
				Although, the above explanation covers the situation when <m>r_1</m> and <m>r_2</m> are equal or not, we will split the <m>r_1 \ne r_2</m> case into <xref ref="second-order-lhcc-complex-case" text="custom">two cases that treat real or complex values separately</xref>. The three cases are summarized below.
			</p>

			<assemblage><title> Summary of Cases </title>
				<p>
					Let <m>r_1</m> and <m>r_2</m> be the solutions of the characteristic equation associated with the second-order LHCC equation. The general solution is:
					<dl width="wide">
						<li xml:id="lhcc-case-1"><title>Case 1 (real &amp; <m>\ne</m>)</title>
							<m>
								\ds y = C_1 e^{r_1 x} + C_2 e^{r_2 x} \quad (r_1 \ne r_2)
							</m>
						</li>
						<li xml:id="lhcc-case-2"><title>Case 2 (real &amp; <m>=</m>)</title>
							<m>
								\ds y = (C_1 + C_2 x) e^{r_1 x} \quad (r_1 = r_2)
							</m>
						</li>
						<li xml:id="lhcc-case-3"><title>Case 3 (complex)</title>
							<line>
								<m>
									\ds y = e^{\alpha x} (C_1 \cos(\beta x) + C_2 \sin(\beta x))
								</m>
							</line>
							<line>
								<m>
									\ds (r = \alpha \pm i\beta \implies r_1 = \alpha + i\beta, \ r_2 = \alpha - i\beta)
								</m>
							</line>
						</li>
					</dl>
				</p>
			</assemblage>

			<p>
				Let's now practice solving some second-order LHCC equations.
			</p>

			<example>

								</p>

						</p>

								</p>
							</solution>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
				</p>
			</example>

		<section xmlns:xi="http://www.w3.org/2001/XInclude"><title> Higher-Order Equations </title>
			<p>
				Solving higher-order linear homogeneous constant coefficient (LHCC) equations is an extension of the methods used for second-order equations. The basic strategy remains the same: solve the characteristic equation, then construct the general solution. However, for higher-order equations, the characteristic equation is a higher-degree polynomial, which can make finding the solutions more challenging.
			</p>

			<theorem xml:id="def-characteristic-equation">
				<title><em>LHCC Characteristic Equation</em></title>
				<statement>
					<p>
						The <term>characteristic equation</term> of an <m>n</m>-th order LHCC equation
						<me>a_n\ y^{(n)} + a_{n-1}\ y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_1\ y' + a_0\ y = 0</me>
						is the <m>n</m>-th degree polynomial
						<fn>
							<xref ref="characteristic-eqn-details" text="custom">Click here for an explanation.</xref>
						</fn>
						<me>a_n\ r^n + a_{n-1}\ r^{n-1} + \cdots + a_1 r + a_0 = 0</me>.
						The solutions to this polynomial, <m>r_1, r_2, \dots r_n</m>, correspond to the roots that determine the form of the general solution.
					</p>
				</statement>
			</theorem>
			
			<p>
				In general, the characteristic equation will have <m>n</m> roots, which may be real or complex and may also include repeated roots. Each root of the characteristic equation leads to a term in the general solution of the LHCC. Examples in the table below illustrate the process of constructing these general solutions based on the different types of roots.
			</p>

			<table xml:id="higher-order-lhcc-general-solution-examples"><title>Examples of LHCC General Solutions</title>
				<tabular left="minor" right="minor" top="minor" bottom="minor" halign="center">
					<row>
						<cell>
							<line>Characteristic Equation</line>
							<line>Solutions</line>
						</cell>
						<cell>General Solution</cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell>
							<line><m>r = 3, -3, 5.3</m></line>
							<line><em>(3rd order)</em></line>
						</cell>
						<cell><me>c_1e^{3t} + c_2e^{-3t} + c_3e^{5.3t}</me></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell>
							<line><m>r = 6 \pm i\sqrt{7.7}, 0</m></line>
							<line><em>(3rd order)</em></line>
						</cell>
						<cell><me>e^{6t}\left(c_1\sin(\sqrt{7.7}t) + c_2\cos(\sqrt{7.7}t)\right) + c_3</me></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell>
							<line><m>r = -4 \text{ (triple)}, 5.3</m></line>
							<line><em>(4th order)</em></line>
						</cell>
						<cell><me>(c_1t^2 + c_2t + c_3)e^{-4t} + c_4e^{5.3t}</me></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell>
							<line><m>r = \pm \frac{i}{2}, 2 \pm i</m></line>
							<line><em>(4th order)</em></line>
						</cell>
						<cell><me>c_1\sin\left(\frac{t}{2}\right) + c_2\cos\left(\frac{t}{2}\right) + e^{2t}(c_3\sin t + c_4\cos t)</me></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell>
							<line><m>r = 0 \text{ (double)}, 3 \text{ (triple)}</m></line>
							<line><em>(5th order)</em></line>
						</cell>
						<cell><me>c_1t + c_2 + (c_3t^4 + c_4t^3 + c_5t^2 + c_6t + c_7)e^{3t}</me></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell>
							<line><m>r = \pm i, \pi \text{ (double)}, 5</m></line>
							<line><em>(5th order)</em></line>
						</cell>
						<cell><me>c_1\sin t + c_2\cos t + (c_3t + c_4)e^{\pi t} + c_5e^{5t}</me></cell>
					</row>
				</tabular>
			</table>

			<p>
				As the table demonstrates, constructing the general solution of an LHCC equation is systematic once you have the roots of the characteristic equation. However, solving higher-order polynomial equations by hand can be difficult. The next example shows a few algebra techniques you can use to handle higher degree equations.
			</p>

			<example>

				<m>\ \ </m> Solve the following characteristic equations.
				
				<p>
					<ol>
						<li> <m> 4 r^2 - 9 = 0 </m> </li>
						<li> <m> r^2 - 3 = 0 </m> </li>
						<li> <m> r^3 + 3 r^2 - 4 r = 0 </m> </li>
						<li> <m> r^5 + 10 r^4 = 0 </m> </li>
						<li> <m> r^4 - 100 r^2 = 0 </m> </li>
						<li> <m> r^5 - 4 r^3 = 0 </m> </li>
						<li> <m> r^4 - 32 = 0 </m> </li>
						<li> <m> r^4 - 5r^2 + 4 = 0 </m> </li>
					</ol>
				</p>

				<solution>
					<p>
						Note: DoS = Difference of Squares.
						<ol>

				These factoring techniques useful, but they only apply to specific forms of polynomials and it is unlikely that you will be lucky enough to encounter such forms in practice. 					<ol>
						<li> 
							<p>
								First, write down the characteristic equation:
								<md>
									<mrow> r^3 - 6r^2 + 11r - 6 = 0 </mrow>
								</md>.
								Solving for <m>r</m>, we get three distinct real roots:
								<md>
									<mrow> r = 1, \quad r = 2, \quad r = 3 </mrow>
								</md>.
								Therefore, the general solution is:
								<me> y = c_1 e^{x} + c_2 e^{2x} + c_3 e^{3x} </me>.
							</p>
						</li>
						<li> 
							<p>
								The characteristic equation is:
								<me>
									9 r^5 - 4 r^4 + r + 10 = 0
								</me>
								which is challenging to solve by hand. However, typing it into <xref ref="poly-factor-p" text="title"/> shows that it can be factored as
								<me>
									2\left(r+3\right)^2 \left(r-1\right) \left(r-\frac12\right) = 0
								</me>,
								which gives the solutions
								<me>
									r = -3, \quad r = -3, \quad r = 1, \quad r = \frac12
								</me>.
								Therefore, the general solution is:
								<me>
									y = (c_1x + c_2) e^{-3x} + c_3 e^{x} + c_4 e^{\frac12 x}
								</me>.
							</p>
						</li>
					</ol>
					
				</solution>
			</example>

				<m>\ \ </m> Consider the fifth-order LHCC equation:
				<p>
					<me> y^{(5)} - 4y''' + 3y'' - y = 0 </me>.

				<solution>
					<p>
						The characteristic equation is:
						<me> r^5 - 4r^3 + 3r^2 - 1 = 0 </me>.

						<me> r_4 \approx 0.61 + 0.5i, \quad r_5 \approx 0.61 - 0.5i </me>.

							<mrow> y = c_1 e^{-2.29x} + c_2 e^{-0.46x}	+\amp\ c_3 e^{1.52x}								</mrow>

					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>

	<chapter><title> Undetermined Coefficient Method </title>
		<section><title> Nonhomogeneous Equations </title>
			<p>
				In the previous chapter, we solved linear homogeneous constant coefficient equations of the form
				<me>
					a_n\ y^{(n)} + \cdots + a_2\ y'' + a_1\ y' + a_0\ y = 0
				</me>.
				Such equations are called <em>homogeneous</em> because of the zero on the right-hand side. In this chapter, we explore how to solve the more general case of <em>nonhomogeneous</em> equations, which include a non-zero function on the right-hand side, like so
				<me>
					a_n\ y^{(n)} + \cdots + a_2\ y'' + a_1\ y' + a_0\ y = f(x)
				</me>.
				For ease of discussion, will use the shorthand LNCC equations to refer to Linear Nonhomogeneous Constant Coefficient equations.
			</p>

			<p>
				Recall, the solution of a homogeneous equation is made up of <m>e^{r x}</m> terms which have the unique property of being like terms with its derivatives. This is needed so the terms on the left-hand side can cancel out to zero.
			</p>

			<p>
				In contrast, nonhomogeneous equations must have solutions that make the left-hand side simplify to <m>f(x)</m>, rather than zero. For this to happen, the solution should be <q><m>f(x)-</m>like</q> in the sense that it shares terms with <m>f(x)</m>. For example, consider the equation
				<me>
					y'' - 4y' + 3y = 9x
				</me>.
				When the solution, <m>y</m>, is plugged into the equation, the left-side terms must simplify to <m>9x</m>. So, <m>y</m> must be <q><m>9x</m> like</q>, but what is does <q><m>9x</m> like</q> mean? As you will see in the sections that follow, it means <m>y</m> has the form <m>y = Ax+B</m>. For now, let's just verify that <m>y = 3x + 4</m> is the <q><m>9x</m> like</q> solution that corresponds to this equation.
			</p>

			<example>
				<m>\ \ </m>Verify that the solution to the equation
				<p><me>y'' - 4y' + 3y = 9x</me></p>
				is given by <m>y = 3x + 4</m>.
				
				<solution>
					<p>To verify, we substitute <m>y = 3x + 4</m> into the equation:</p>
					<md>
						<mrow> y'' - 4y' + 3y =\amp\ 9x </mrow>
						<mrow> 0 - 4(3) + 3(3x + 4) =\amp\ 9x </mrow>
						<mrow> -12 + 9x + 12 =\amp\ 9x </mrow>
						<mrow> 9x =\amp\ 9x \quad \checkmark </mrow>
					</md>
				</solution>
			</example>

		<section><title> Particular Solutions </title>
			<p>
				As discussed in the previous section, solving non-homogeneous linear differential equations involves finding a solution that satisfies the equation:
				<me>
					a_n y^{(n)} + \cdots + a_2 y'' + a_1 y' + a_0 y = f(x)
				</me>.
				The challenge is that the terms on the left side need to combine in just the right way to match <m>f(x)</m> on the right. This means the form of the particular solution must resemble <m>f(x)</m> closely. When <m>f(x)</m> contains polynomial, exponential, or trigonometric (sine or cosine) terms, the particular solution should involve similar components.
			</p>

			<p>
				The specific function that accounts for <m>f(x)</m> on the right side is known as the <term>particular solution</term>, denoted <m>y_p</m>. The form of this solution depends on the type of function <m>f(x)</m> represents. To help select the correct form of <m>y_p</m>, refer to <xref ref="yp-table"/>, which outlines the most common types of <m>f(x)</m> and their corresponding <m>y_p</m> forms.
			</p>

			<table xml:id="yp-table">
				<title>Selecting the form of <m>y_p</m> based on the form of <m>f(x)</m>.</title>
				<tabular top="medium" left="medium" right="medium" bottom="minor">
					<col halign="center" right="medium"/>
					<col halign="center"/>
					<row bottom="medium">
						<cell><m>f(x)</m></cell>
						<cell><term>Particular Solution Form, </term><m>y_p</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell><m>a</m></cell>
						<cell><m>A</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell><m>ax+b</m></cell>
						<cell><m>Ax+B</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell><m>ax^2+bx+c</m></cell>
						<cell><m>Ax^2+Bx+C</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell><m>ax^3+bx^2+cx+d</m></cell>
						<cell><m>Ax^3+Bx^2+Cx+D</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell><m>a e^{\alpha x}</m></cell>
						<cell><m>A e^{\alpha x}</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell><m>a \sin(\beta x)</m></cell>
						<cell><m>A \sin(\beta x) + B \cos(\beta x)</m></cell>
					</row>
					<row bottom="medium">
						<cell><m>a \cos(\beta x)</m></cell>
						<cell><m>A \sin(\beta x) + B \cos(\beta x)</m></cell>

				</tabular>
			</table>

			<p>
				In this table, the constants <m>a</m>, <m>b</m>, <m>c</m>, <m>d</m>, <m>\alpha</m>, and <m>\beta</m> are assumed to be known, while <m>A</m>, <m>B</m>, <m>C</m>, etc., need to be determined. We will explore how to determine these coefficients in the next section. For now, we are concerned with picking the correct form. Let's work through a few examples to solidify this process.
			</p>

			<example>

				<m>\ \ </m>Find the form of <m>y_p</m> for each of the equations below.

				<p>
					<sidebyside widths="36% 64%">
						<p>
							<m> \ds y'' + y' + 5y = 3e^{6x} </m>
						</p>
						<p>
							<solution>
								Since <m>f(x) = 3e^{6x}</m> has the form <m>a e^{\alpha x}</m>, we set <me>y_p = Ae^{6x}</me>.
							</solution>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
				</p>
				
				<p>
					<sidebyside widths="36% 64%">
						<p>
							<m> \ds y'' + 3y' - y = 2\sin(2x) </m>
						</p>

						<p>
							<solution>
								<m>2\sin(2x)</m> has the form <m>a \sin(\beta x)</m>, so <me>y_p = A\sin(2x) + B\cos(2x)</me>.
							</solution>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
				</p>

				<p>
					<sidebyside widths="36% 64%">
						<p>
							<m> \ds \omega''' + \omega' = 5\theta^2 + \theta + 1 </m>
						</p>
				
						<p>
							<solution>
								<m>5\theta^2 + \theta + 1</m> has the form <m>a\theta^2+b\theta+c</m>, so <me>\omega_p = A\theta^2 + B\theta + C</me>.
							</solution>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
				</p>

				<p>
					<sidebyside widths="36% 64%">
						<p>
							<m> \ds 2\frac{dy}{dx} + 5y = -3 </m>
						</p>
				
						<p>
							<solution>
								Since <m>f(x) = -3</m> is of the form <m>a</m>, we set <me>y_p = A</me>.
							</solution>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
				</p>

				<p>
					<sidebyside widths="36% 64%">
						<p>
							<m> \ds y'' -3y' + 0.5y = 14x^4 </m>
						</p>
				
						<p>
							<solution>
								Even though <m>f(x) = 14x^4</m> only has an <m>x^4</m> term, we consider it to be of the form 
								<me>ax^4+bx^3+cx^2+dx+e</me>, 
								where <m>b = c = d = e = 0</m>. Therefore,
								<me>y_p = Ax^4+Bx^3+Cx^2+Dx+E</me>.
							</solution>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
				</p>
			</example>

			<p>
				When <m>f(x)</m> is a sum of multiple functions, each part of the sum contributes to the form of the particular solution. In these situations, we construct <m>y_p</m> by simply adding together the particular solutions corresponding to each term in <m>f(x)</m>. Let's look at an example to clarify this approach.
			</p>

			<example>

				<m>\ \ </m>Given each of the constant terms, <m>f</m>, of an LNCC equation below, find the form of <m>y_p</m> you would use.

				<p>
					<sidebyside widths="36% 64%">
						<p>
							<m> \ds 2e^{w} - 1 </m>
						</p>
						<p>
							<solution>
								Since <m>f(w)</m> is the sum of an <m>e^{w}</m> term and a constant term, we set 
								<me>K_p = Ae^{w} + B</me>.
							</solution>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
				</p>
				
				<p>
					<sidebyside widths="36% 64%">
						<p>
							<m> \ds 3e^{6x} + 2\sin(2x) </m>
						</p>

						<p>
							<solution>
								Since <m>f(x)</m> is the sum of an <m>e^{6x}</m> term and a <m>\sin(2x)</m> term, we set 
								<me>y_p = Ae^{6x} + B\sin(2x) + C\cos(2x)</me>.
							</solution>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
				</p>

				<p>
					<sidebyside widths="36% 64%">
						<p>
							<m> \ds 2\sin(-2x) - 6\cos(3x) </m>
						</p>
				
						<p>
							<solution>
								Since <m>f(x)</m> is the sum of a <m>\sin(-2x)</m> term and a <m>\cos(3x)</m> term, we set 
								<md>
									<mrow> y_p = A\sin(-2x)	\amp\ + B\cos(-2x)			</mrow>
									<mrow>				+	\amp\ C\sin(3x) + D\cos(3x)	</mrow>
								</md>.
							</solution>
						</p>
					</sidebyside>
				</p>

			</example>

			<p>
				When <m>f(x)</m> involves products of functions, constructing <m>y_p</m> can become more involved. This is because multiplying terms may introduce extra coefficients that turn out to be unnecessary or redundant. In these cases, simplifying the solution to avoid duplication is essential. The following example illustrates this process.
			</p>

			<example>

				<m>\ \ </m>Find the form of <m>y_p</m> for the equation

				<p>
					<me>y'' + y' + 5y = 10x^2 e^{6x}</me>.
				</p>

				<solution>
					<p>
						Since <m>f(x) = 10x^2 e^{6x}</m> is the product of a polynomial and an exponential, we initially set:
						<me>y_p = (Ax^2 + Bx + C)(D e^{6x})</me>.
						However, <m>D</m> is redundant because if we multiply it onto the polynomial, it can be absorbed into <m>A, B, </m> and <m>C</m>, as follows:
						<me>y_p = (Ax^2 + Bx + C)(D e^{6x}) = \left(AD x^2 + BD x + CD\right) e^{6x}</me>.
						So, the form of <m>y_p</m> simplifies to:
						<me>y_p = \left(A x^2 + B x + C\right) e^{6x}</me>.
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>

			<p>
				Here are a few tips to help you avoid redundant coefficients when constructing the form of <m>y_p</m>.
			</p>

			<assemblage><title>Tips to Avoid Redundant Coefficients</title>
				<p>
					<ol>
						<li>
							When <m>f(x)</m> is the product of an exponential and another function, avoid writing a coefficient for the exponential term.
						</li>
						<li>
							For products of polynomials and sine/cosine functions, use:
							<me>(\text{polynomial form}) \sin(\beta x) + (\text{polynomial form}) \cos(\beta x)</me>
							where the polynomial form on each term uses different coefficients. 
						</li>
						<li>
							When working with sums, look for redundant overlapping terms.
						</li>
					</ol>
				</p>
			</assemblage>

			<p>
				Let's put these tips into practice with a couple of examples.
			</p>

			<example>

				<m>\ \ </m>Find the form of <m>y_p</m> for each of the equations below.

				<p>
					<m> \ds y'' + y = e^{-x}\cos(x) </m>
					<solution>
						Since <m>f(x) = e^{-x}\cos(x)</m> is the product of an exponential and a cosine function, we apply tip 1 and set:
						<me>y_p = e^{-x}(A\sin x + B\cos x)</me>.
					</solution>
				</p>
				<p>
					<m> \ds y'' - 4y = e^{6x}e^{-3x} </m>
					<solution>
						Since <m>f(x) = e^{6x}e^{-3x}</m> is the product of two exponential functions, we ignore one of the coefficients and set:
						<me>y_p = Ae^{6x}e^{-3x}</me>.
						Note, <m>e^{6x}e^{-3x}</m> is the same as <m>e^{3x}</m>, so we could also write <m>y_p = Ae^{3x}</m>.
					</solution>
				</p>
				
				<p>
					<m> \ds 9P' - P = t^{3}\cos(t) </m>
					<solution>
						Since <m>f(t) = t^{3}\cos(t)</m> is the product of a polynomial and a cosine function, we apply tip 2 and set:
						<me>P_p = (At^3 + Bt^2 + Ct + D)\sin t + (Et^3 + Ft^2 + Gt + H)\cos t</me>.
					</solution>
				</p>

				<p>
					<m> \ds 2y'' + 3y' - 15y = x^2\cos x + x \sin x </m>
					<solution>
						When setting up the particular solution, it's easy to introduce redundant terms if we aren't careful. For example, if we initially set:
						<md>
							<mrow> y_p = (Ax^2 + \ul{Bx + C) \sin x} + (Dx^2 + \ul{Ex + F)\cos x} </mrow>
						</md>
						and
						<md>
							<mrow> (\ul{Gx + H)\sin x} + (\ul{Ix + J)\cos x}, </mrow>
						</md>
						you may notice that the underlined terms introduce unnecessary repetition. By grouping like terms, we can simplify this to:
						<me>y_p = (Ax^2 + Bx + C)\sin x + (Dx^2 + Ex + F)\cos x</me>.
					</solution>
				</p>

			</example>

						<choice>
