	<subsection xml:id="lt-review-integration-by-parts">
		<title> Review of Integration by Parts (IBP) </title>

		<p>
			Integration by parts is a core calculus technique that allows us to transfer derivatives from one function to another inside an integral. Its most common form is:
			<me>
				\int_{a}^{b} u\ dv = uv\big|_{a}^{b} - \int_{a}^{b} v\ du
			</me>.
		</p>

		<p>
			When <m>u</m> and <m>v</m> are functions of <m>t</m>, we get a version of IBP which makes the transfer of derivatives more clear:
			<me>
				\int_{a}^{b} \os{{\DLBa\large\text{derivative on }v}}{
				\us{\large u}{ \us{\uparrow}{\ul{ 
					u(t)
				}}} \ 
				\us{\large dv}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{
					{\DLBa\os{\downarrow}{v'\vphantom{\Large\Gamma}}(t)} dt
				}}}
				}
				= \ub{u(b)v(b) - u(a)v(a)}_{\large\text{evaluates to a number}}
				- \int_{a}^{b} \os{{\DLBa\large\text{derivative on }u}}{
					\us{\large v}{ \us{\uparrow}{\ul{ v(t) }}} \ 
					\us{\large du}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{ {\DLBa\os{\downarrow}{u'\vphantom{\Large\Gamma}}(t)} dt }}}
				}
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			This simple idea of shifting derivatives is the basis for the Laplace transform method. We'll see it in action when one of the functions is <m>e^{-st}</m>.
		</p>
		
		<task> temporarily omitted </task>
	</subsection>

	<subsection xml:id="lt-using-exponentials-for-derivative-transfer">
		<title> Using Exponentials to Transfer Derivatives </title>

		<task> temporarily omitted </task>

		<p>
			Let's now see why exponentials make this derivative transfer especially useful. Suppose we choose <m>u(t) = e^{-st}</m> and <m>v(t) = y(t)</m>. Differentiating gives <m>u'(t) = -s e^{-st}</m> and <m>v'(t) = y'(t)</m>. Applying integration by parts yields:
			<md>
				<mrow>
					\int_{0}^{b} \os{{\DLBa\large\text{derivative on }y}}{ 
						e^{-st} \ {\DLBa\os{\downarrow}{y'\vphantom{\Large\Gamma}}(t)}\ dt
					} 
					\amp = e^{-st} \ y(t) \Big|_{0}^{b} - \int_{0}^{b} \os{{\DLBa\large\text{derivative on }e^{-st}}}{ 
						{\DLBa\os{\downarrow}{\left(-s e^{-st}\right)\vphantom{\Large\Gamma}}}\ y(t)\ dt
					}
				</mrow>
				<mrow>
					\amp = e^{-sb} y(b) - y(0) + {\DLBa s} \int_{0}^{b} {\DLBa e^{-st}} y(t)\, dt
				</mrow>.
			</md>
		</p>

		<p>
			Notice the key point: the derivative on <m>y</m> has been completely removed. Instead, we get two terms: a boundary term <m>e^{-st} y(t)\Big|_0^b</m> and a new integral without <m>y'(t)</m>.
		</p>

		<p>
			This trade is usually worth it since in the context of Laplace transforms, the term <m>e^{-sb} y(b)</m> will often vanish and <m>y(0)</m> is the given initial condition.
		</p>
	</subsection>

	<objectives label="lt-derivative-transfer-wrapup">
		<ul>
			<li>Integration by parts lets you shift derivatives from one function to another within an integral.</li>
			<li>Choosing <m>u(t) = e^{-st}</m> causes derivatives on <m>y(t)</m> to transfer onto the exponential, producing a simple factor of <m>s</m>.</li>
			<li>Understanding this transfer mechanism explains where the variable <m>s</m> comes from and why it appears in Laplace transform formulas.</li>
  		</ul>
	</objectives>

<section label="lt-definition">
	<title> Defining the Laplace Transform </title>

	<introduction>
		<p>
			We've seen how integration by parts transfers derivatives from one function to another inside an integral. Now we take the next step: defining a transform that systematically applies this principle.
		</p>
		<p>
			In this section, we'll see how this transform is constructed and how it converts functions of <m>t</m> into functions of <m>s</m>. You'll also see how to compute Laplace transforms from the definition.
		</p>
	</introduction>

	<subsection xml:id="lt-motivation">
		<title> Why Use the Laplace Transform? </title>
		<p>
			To see where we're headed, imagine you need to solve the differential equation
			<me>
				y'(t) - 9y(t) = 10 e^{2t}.
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			Suppose you multiply both sides by <m>e^{-st}</m> and integrate with respect to <m>t</m>:
			<me>
				\int \left[y'(t) - 9y(t)\right] e^{-st} \, dt = \int 10 e^{2t} e^{-st} \, dt.
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			The left-hand side contains a derivative inside the integral. From the previous section, you know we can transfer this derivative to the exponential function. Doing so removes the derivative from <m>y(t)</m>, essentially allowing you to use algebra to isolate it.
		</p>

		<p>
			This is the idea behind the Laplace transform and the motivation for its definition.
		</p>	
	</subsection>

	<subsection xml:id="lt-def-basic-definition">
		<title> Definition of the Laplace Transform </title>

		<definition xml:id="laplace-transform-definition"><title>Laplace Transform</title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Given a function <m>f(t)</m> defined for <m>t \geq 0</m>, its Laplace transform, denoted <m>\lap{f(t)}</m>, is defined by
					<me>
						\lap{f(t)} = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} f(t) \, dt,
					</me>
					provided the integral converges. The result is a new function, typically written as <m>F(s)</m>:
					<me>
						F(s) = \lap{f(t)}.
					</me>
				</p>
			</statement>
		</definition>
		
		<task> temporarily omitted </task>

		<paragraphs xml:id="lt-improper-integrals">
			<title> Working with Improper Integrals </title>
			<p>
				Notice that the Laplace transform involves an improper integral: the upper limit is <m>\infty</m>. In calculus, we handle this by replacing <m>\infty</m> with a variable <m>b</m>, computing the definite integral, and then taking the limit as <m>b \to \infty</m>:
				<me>
					\lap{f(t)} = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} f(t)\, dt = \ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} \ob{\int_0^b e^{-st} f(t)\, dt}^{\large\text{(step 1)}}}_{\large\text{(step 2)}}
				</me>.
			</p>

			<p>
				When calculating Laplace transforms, you first compute the integral with respect to <m>t</m>, treating <m>s</m> as a constant. Then you compute the limit of the result as <m>b</m> approaches <m>\infty</m>.
			</p>

			<p>
				The Laplace transform exists if this limit produces a finite value. Often, exponential decay from <m>e^{-st}</m> guarantees convergence when <m>s</m> is large enough.
			</p>

			<task> temporarily omitted </task>
		</paragraphs>
	</subsection>

	<subsection label="constant-function-transform">
		<title> Laplace Transform of a Constant </title>

		<p>
			We begin with the simplest case: the constant function <m>f(t) = 1</m>. By the definition of the Laplace transform,
			<me>
				\lap{1} = \int_0^\infty e^{-st}\, dt.
			</me>
		</p>
		<p>
			This integral converges when <m>s \lt 0</m> and evaluates to
			<me xml:id="lt-L1">
				\text{L}_1:\quad\lap{1} = \frac{1}{s}, \quad s \gt 0
			</me>.
		</p>
		<p>
			The details for this transform mirror the <xref ref="laplace-transform-of-5" text="custom">solution for <m>\lap{5}</m></xref> in the previous section. 
		</p>
	</subsection>

	<subsection label="exponential-function-transform">
		<title> Laplace Transform of Exponentials </title>

		<p>
			To get the transform formula for exponentials of the form <m>f(t) = e^{at}</m>, where <m>a</m> is a constant, we will first find it for <m>e^{7t}</m>:
		</p>

		<proof label="common-exp-7t-details">
			<title> <me>\lap{e^{7t}} = \frac{1}{s - 7}, \quad s \gt 7</me>Click here for details </title>
			<p>
				Using the definition of the Laplace transform we get
				<me>
					\lap{ e^{7t} }
					= \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \cdot e^{7t}\ dt
					= \lim_{b \to \infty}\int_0^b e^{(7-s)t}\ dt
				</me>.
			</p>

			<p>
				Focusing just on the integral and treating <m>7-s</m> as constant, we let <m>u=7-s</m> and apply <m>u</m>-substitution:
				<md>
					<mrow>
						\int_0^b e^{(7-s)t}\, dt
						<xref text="custom" ref="lt-example-2-details"><m>\os{\large ‚ùî}{=}</m></xref> 
						\frac{1}{7-s} e^{(7-s)t} \Bigg|_0^b
						\amp = \frac{1}{7-s} \left( e^{(7-s)b} - e^{0} \right)
					</mrow>
					<mrow>
						\amp = \frac{1}{7-s} \left( e^{(7-s)b} - 1 \right)
					</mrow>
				</md>
			</p>

			<p>
				Now, we compute the limit of this expression as <m>b \to \infty</m>:
				<md>
					<mrow>
						\lim_{b \to \infty} \frac{1}{7-s} \left( e^{(7-s)b} - 1 \right)
						\amp = \frac{1}{7-s} \Bigg[ \ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{(7-s)b}}_{L} - \lim_{b \to \infty} 1 \Bigg]
					</mrow>
				</md>.
			</p>

			<p>
				As before, <m>L</m> goes to <m>0</m> if the <term><xref ref="graph-of-exp-t" text="custom">power of the exponential function</xref></term> goes to <m>-\infty</m>. This only happens if <m>7-s</m> is negative. That is, <m>\ 7-s \lt 0\ </m> or <m>\ 7 \lt s</m>. 
			</p>

			<p>
				So, as long as <m>s \gt 7</m>, then <m>L \to 0</m> and the transform becomes 
				<me>
					\lap{e^{7t}} = \frac{1}{s - 7}, \quad s \gt 7
				</me>.
			</p>
		</proof>

		<p>
			Replacing <m>7</m> with an <m>a</m> in this solution leads to the general formula:
			<me xml:id="lt-L2">
				\text{L}_2:\quad\lap{e^{at}} = \frac{1}{s - a}, \quad s \gt a
			</me>,
			where <m>a</m> is a constant.
		</p>

		<task> temporarily omitted </task>
	</subsection>

	<subsection label="power-function-transform">
		<title> Laplace Transform of Powers of <m>t</m> </title>

		<p>
			Now let's consider power functions, <m>f(t) = t^n</m> where <m>n</m> is a non-negative integer.
			<me>
				\lap{t^n} = \frac{n!}{s^{n+1}}, \quad s > 0.
			</me>
		</p>
		<p>
			We already found the transforms for <m>n = 0</m> and <m>n = 1</m> since
			<ul>
				<li><m>n=0:\qquad\lap{t^0} = \lap{1} = \dfrac{1}{s}</m></li>
				<li><m>n=1:\qquad\lap{t^1} = \lap{t} = \dfrac{1}{s^2}</m></li>
			</ul>
			We could find <m>\lap{t^2}</m> next, but it is more instructive to show following relationship:
		</p>

		<proof label="common-t-sqrd-details">
			<title> <me>\lap{t^2} = \frac{2}{s}\cdot \lap{t}, \quad s \gt 0</me>Click here for details </title>
			<p>
				Applying the definition and writing the integral as a limit, we have
				<me>
					\lap{ t^2 } = \lim_{b \to \infty} \ub{\int_0^b e^{-st} \cdot t^2\ dt}_{I}
				</me>.
			</p>

			<p>
				Applying integration by parts to <m>I</m> once in <m>\lap{ t^2 }</m> shows
				<md>
					<mrow>
						\lap{ t^2 }
						\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{b^2}{s}e^{-sb} + \frac{2}{s}\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot t\ dt\right]
						<xref ref="lt-example-tsqrd-IVP-details" text="custom">üîçHow?</xref>
					</mrow>
					<mrow>
						\amp = -\frac{1}{s} \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{b^2}{e^{sb}}\right] + \frac{2}{s}\lim_{b \to \infty}\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot t\ dt
					</mrow>
					<mrow>
						\amp = -\frac{1}{s}\ub{\lim_{b \to \infty}\frac{b^2}{e^{sb}}}_{L} + \frac{2}{s}\ub{\int_0^\infty e^{-st}\cdot t\ dt}_{\large\lap{t}}
					</mrow>.
				</md>
			</p>

			<p>
				Noticing that the improper integral is just <m>\lap{t}</m>, we have
				<me>
					\lap{ t^2 } = -\frac{1}{s}\cdot L + \frac{2}{s}\cdot \lap{t}
				</me>,
				but <m>L=0</m> if <m>s \gt 0</m> <xref ref="lt-example-tsqrd-LH-details" text="custom">(üîçHow?)</xref>, so we have the relationship
			</p>

			<me>
				\lap{t^2} = \frac{2}{s}\cdot \lap{t}, \quad s \gt 0
			</me>.
		</proof>
		
		<p>
			You can use this work as a template for a general recursive relationship:
		</p>

		<proof label="common-recursive-power-function-details">
			<title> <me>\lap{t^n} = \frac{n}{s}\cdot \lap{t^{n-1}}, \quad s \gt 0</me>Click here for details </title>

			<p>
				Replacing <m>2</m> with <m>n</m> in the previous solution, we have
				<me>
					\lap{ t^n } = \lim_{b \to \infty} \ub{\int_0^b e^{-st} \cdot t^n\ dt}_{I}
				</me>.
			</p>

			<p>
				Applying integration by parts to <m>I</m> once in <m>\lap{ t^{n} }</m> shows
				<md>
					<mrow>
						\lap{ t^{n} }
						\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{b^n}{e^{sb}} + \frac{n}{s}\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot t^{n-1}\ dt\right]
						<xref ref="lt-example-tnth-IVP-details" text="custom">üîçHow?</xref>
					</mrow>
					<mrow>
						\amp = -\frac{1}{s} \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{b^n}{e^{sb}}\right] + \frac{n}{s}\lim_{b \to \infty}\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot t^{n-1}\ dt
					</mrow>
					<mrow>
						\amp = -\frac{1}{s}\ub{\lim_{b \to \infty}\frac{b^n}{e^{sb}}}_{L} + \frac{n}{s}\ub{\int_0^\infty e^{-st}\cdot t^{n-1}\ dt}_{\large\lap{t^{n-1}}}
					</mrow>.
				</md>
			</p>

			<p>
				Noticing that the improper integral is just <m>\lap{t^{n-1}}</m>, we have
				<me>
					\lap{ t^{n} } = -\frac{1}{s}\cdot L + \frac{n}{s}\cdot \lap{t^{n-1}}
				</me>,
				but <m>L=0</m> if <m>s \gt 0</m> <xref ref="lt-example-tnth-LH-details" text="custom">(üîçHow?)</xref>, so we have the relationship
			</p>

			<me>
				\lap{t^{n}} = \frac{n}{s}\cdot \lap{t^{n-1}}, \quad s \gt 0
			</me>.
		</proof>

		<p>
			To get the general formula we apply this relationship repeatedly for increasing <m>n</m>:
			<me> 
				\begin{array}{crcl}
					n=1:	\amp \DLO\lap{t} \amp =	\amp 
						\dfrac{1}{s}\cdot\lap{1} = \dfrac{1}{s}\cdot\dfrac{1}{s} = {\DLO\dfrac{1}{s^2}}\\
					n=2:	\amp \DLBa\lap{t^2}	\amp =	\amp 
						\dfrac{2}{s}\cdot{\DLO\lap{t}} = \dfrac{2}{s}\cdot{\DLO\dfrac{1}{s^2}} = {\DLBa\dfrac{2\cdot 1}{s^3}}\\
					n=3:	\amp \DLGb\lap{t^3}	\amp =	\amp 
						\dfrac{3}{s}\cdot{\DLBa\lap{t^2}} = \dfrac{3}{s}\cdot{\DLBa\dfrac{2\cdot 1}{s^3}} = {\DLGb\dfrac{3\cdot 2\cdot 1}{s^4}}\\
					n=4:	\amp \DLRa\lap{t^4}	\amp =	\amp
						\dfrac{4}{s}\cdot{\DLGb\lap{t^3}} = \dfrac{4}{s}\cdot{\DLGb\dfrac{3\cdot 2\cdot 1}{s^4}} = {\DLRa\dfrac{4\cdot 3\cdot 2\cdot 1}{s^5}}\\
					\vdots	\amp \vdots\quad\amp \amp \qquad\qquad\vdots\\
				\end{array}
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			This pattern suggests that, in general, we have
		</p>

		<me>
			\lap{t^n} =\dfrac{n}{s}\cdot\lap{t^{n-1}} = \dfrac{n}{s}\cdot\dfrac{(n-1)\dotsm 4\cdot 3\cdot 2\cdot 1}{s^{n}}
		</me>,
		
		<p>
			which can be summarized as
		</p>

		<me xml:id="lt-L3">
			\text{L}_3:\quad\lap{t^n} = \frac{n!}{s^{n+1}}, \quad s \gt 0, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots
		</me>.
	</subsection>
	
	<subsection label="sine-cosine-function-transform">

		</p>

		<proof><title> <me>\lap{\cos(3t)} = \frac{s}{s^2 + 3^2}, \quad s \gt 0</me>Click here for details </title>
			<p>
				Applying the definition and writing the integral as a limit, we have
				<me>
					\lap{ \cos(3t)} = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \cdot \cos(3t)\ dt = \lim_{b \to \infty} \ub{\int_0^{b} e^{-st} \cos(3t)\ dt}_{I}
				</me>.
			</p>

			<p>
				Treating <m>s</m> constant, we apply integration by parts to <m>I</m> as follows:
			</p>

			<me>
				I = -\frac{e^{-st}}{s} \cos(3t) \Bigg|_0^b - \frac{3}{s}\int_0^{b} e^{-st} \sin(3t)\ dt
			</me>,

			<aside><title> Integration by Parts </title>
				<p>
					1st Integration by Parts
					<md>
						<mrow>	u	\amp = \cos(3t)				</mrow>
						<mrow>	du	\amp = -3\sin(3t)\ dt		</mrow>
						<mrow>	v	\amp = -\sfrac{e^{-st}}{s}	</mrow>
						<mrow>	dv	\amp = e^{-st}\ dt			</mrow>
					</md>
					2nd Integration by Parts
					<md>
						<mrow>	u	\amp = \sin(3t)				</mrow>
						<mrow>	du	\amp = 3\cos(3t)\ dt		</mrow>
						<mrow>	v	\amp = -\sfrac{e^{-st}}{s}	</mrow>
						<mrow>	dv	\amp = e^{-st}\ dt			</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>
			</aside>

			<p>
				but this requires a second integration by parts:
				<md>
					<mrow>
						I
						\amp = -\frac{e^{-st}}{s} \cos(3t) \Bigg|_0^b - \frac{3}{s} \left[ -\frac{e^{-st}}{s} \sin(3t)\Bigg|_0^b + \frac{3}{s} \int_0^{b} e^{-st} \cos(3t)\ dt \right]
					</mrow>
					<mrow>
						\amp = \left(-\frac{e^{-st}}{s} \cos(3t) + \frac{3e^{-st}}{s^2} \sin(3t)\right)\Bigg|_0^b - \frac{9}{s^2} \ub{\int_0^{b} e^{-st} \cos(3t)\ dt}_{I}
					</mrow>
				</md>.
			</p>

			<p>
				Since the integral <m>I</m> reappeared, we actually have
				<me>
					I = \left(-\frac{e^{-st}}{s} \cos(3t) + \frac{3e^{-st}}{s^2} \sin(3t)\right)\Bigg|_0^b - \frac{9}{s^2} I
				</me>.
				Evaluating the limits of integration and solving for <m>I</m>, we find
				<md>
					<mrow>
						I + \frac{9}{s^2} I  \amp = \left(-\frac{e^{-st}}{s} \cos(3t) + \frac{3e^{-st}}{s^2} \sin(3t)\right)\Bigg|_0^b
					</mrow>
					<mrow>
						I\left(\frac{s^2+9}{s^2}\right) \amp = \left(-\frac{e^{-sb}}{s} \cos(3b) + \frac{3e^{-sb}}{s^2} \sin(3b) + \frac{1}{s} \right)
					</mrow>
					<mrow> 
						I	\amp = \frac{s^2}{s^2+9}\left(-\frac{e^{-sb}}{s} \cos(3b) + \frac{3e^{-sb}}{s^2} \sin(3b) + \frac{1}{s} \right)
					</mrow>
				</md>
			</p>

			<p>
				Finally, the limit of <m>I</m> as <m>b \to \infty</m> is the sum of the limits
				<md>
					<mrow>
						\lim_{b \to \infty} I = \amp
					</mrow>
					<mrow>
						\frac{s^2}{s^2+9} \amp \Bigg(
							- \frac{1}{s}\ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{-sb} \cos(3b)}_{L_1}
							+ \frac{3}{s^2}\ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{-sb} \sin(3b)}_{L_2} 
							+ \frac{1}{s}
						\Bigg)
					</mrow>
				</md>
				Since <xref ref="lt-example-4-details" text="custom"><m>L_1\to0</m> &amp; <m>L_2\to0</m> if <m>s \gt 0</m></xref>, we can conclude that
				<me>
					\lap{\cos(3t)} = \frac{s}{s^2 + 9}, \quad s \gt 0
				</me>.
			</p>
		</proof>

		<p>
			Replacing the constants <m>3</m> and <m>9</m> with <m>b</m> and <m>b^2</m>, respectively, shows that
			<me xml:id="lt-L4">
				\text{L}_4:\quad\lap{\cos(bt)} = \frac{s}{s^2 + b^2}, \quad s \gt 0
			</me>.
		</p>

		<p>
			The derivation of the formula for the Laplace transform of sine, given by
			<me xml:id="lt-L5">
				\text{L}_5:\quad\lap{\sin(bt)} = \frac{b}{s^2 + b^2}, \quad s \gt 0
			</me>,
			follows a similar approach and is left as exercise for you to try on your own.
		</p>
	</subsection>

	<objectives label="common-transforms-wrapup">
		<ul marker="square">
			<li>
				<p>
					We now have the following common Laplace transforms:
					<ul>
						<li><m> \ds\lap{1} = \frac{1}{s}, \quad s \gt 0 </m>,</li>
						<li><m> \ds\lap{e^{at}} = \frac{1}{s - a}, \quad s \gt a </m>,</li>
						<li><m> \ds\lap{t^n} = \frac{n!}{s^{n+1}}, \quad s \gt 0, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots </m>,</li>
						<li><m> \ds\lap{\cos(bt)} = \frac{s}{s^2 + b^2}, \quad s \gt 0 </m>,</li>
						<li><m> \ds\lap{\sin(bt)} = \frac{b}{s^2 + b^2}, \quad s \gt 0 </m>.</li>
					</ul>
				</p>
			</li>
		</ul>
	</objectives>

	<subsection label="lt-linearity-property">

	<subsection label="lt-derivative-transfer-property">
		<title> Derivatives of Functions (Derivative Transfer) </title>

		<p>
			One of the most powerful features of the Laplace transform is its ability to transform differential equations into algebraic equations. This means we need a way to <q>remove</q> the derivatives from the unknown in our differential equation. The following property perfectly encapsulates this idea:
		</p>

		<me>
			\lap{f'(t)} = s \lap{f(t)} - f(0), \qquad s \gt 0
		</me>

		<p>
			In short, this property uses integration by parts to transfer the derivative from <m>f'(t)</m> to <m>e^{-st}</m>. This results in a factor of <m>-s</m> (chain rule on <m>e^{-st}</m>) and <m>f(0)</m> as follows:
		</p>

		<me>
			\lap{f'(t)} = \int_0^\infty e^{-st} f'(t)\ dt \quad \os{\Large\text{IBP}}{\longrightarrow} \quad -(-s)\ub{\int_0^\infty e^{-st} f(t)\ dt}_{\large\lap{f(t)}} - f(0)
		</me>.
	
		<proof xml:id="lt-derivative-transfer-details">
			<title> Click here for more details </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					To show that
					<me>
						\lap{f'(t)} = s\lap{f(t)} - f(0), \quad s \gt 0
					</me>,
					we start with the definition of the Laplace transform:
				</p>

				<p>
					<me>
						\lap{ f'(t) } = \lim_{b \to \infty} \ub{\int_0^b e^{-st} \cdot f'(t)\ dt}_{I}
					</me>.
				</p>

				<p>
					Applying integration by parts to <m>I</m> once in <m>\lap{ f'(t) }</m> with
					<md>
						<mrow> u = e^{-st},			\quad\amp	dv = f'(t)\ dt,	</mrow>
						<mrow> du = -se^{-st}\ dt,	\quad\amp	v = f(t)		</mrow>
					</md>
					integration by parts gives
					<md>
						<mrow>
							\lap{ t^{n} }
							\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[f(t)e^{-sb}\Bigg|_0^b - \int_0^b f(t)\cdot\left(-se^{-st}\right)\ dt\right]
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\left(f(b)e^{-sb} - f(0)e^{0}\right) + s\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot f(t)\ dt\right]
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[f(b)e^{-sb} - f(0) + s\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot f(t)\ dt\right]
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[f(b)e^{-sb}\right] - f(0) +  s\lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot f(t)\ dt\right]
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[f(b)e^{-sb}\right] - f(0) +  s\ub{\int_\infty^b e^{-st}\cdot f(t)\ dt}_{\large\lap{f(t)}}
						</mrow>.
					</md>
				</p>

				<p>
					Recall that a requirement for the Laplace transform of <m>f(t)</m> to exist is that
					<me>
						\lim_{b \to \infty} \left[f(b)e^{-sb}\right] = 0, \quad s \gt 0
					</me>.
				</p>

				<p>
					Thus, setting the limit to zero gives us the desired result
					<me>
						\lap{ f'(t) } = s\lap{f(t)} - f(0)
					</me>.
				</p>
			</statement>
		</proof>

		<task> temporarily omitted </task>

		<p>
			Notice that this property is also recursive, meaning it shows us how to handle higher-order derivatives as well. For example, 
			<md>
				<mrow>
					\lap{f'(t)}
					\amp = \DLGb \ul{s\lap{f(t)} - f(0)}
				</mrow>
				<mrow>
					\lap{ f''(t) }
					\amp = s \lap{f'(t)} - f'(0) = s \Big({\DLGb \ul{s\lap{f(t)} - f(0)}}\Big) - f'(0)
				</mrow>
				<mrow>
					\amp = \DLBb \ul{s^2\lap{f(t)} - sf(0) - f'(0)}
				</mrow>
				<mrow>
					\lap{ f'''(t) }
					\amp = s \lap{f''(t)} - f''(0) = s \Big({\DLBb \ul{s^2\lap{f(t)} - sf(0) - f'(0)}}\Big) - f''(0)
				</mrow>
				<mrow>
					\amp = s^3\lap{f(t)} - s^2f(0) - sf'(0) - f''(0), \text{ and so on}
				</mrow>
			</md>
		</p>

		<p>
			To summarize, we now have the following new properties:
			<md>
				<mrow xml:id="lt-R1">
					\text{R}_{1}:\quad
					\amp \lap{f'(t)} = sF(s) - f(0),
					\amp\amp s \gt 0
				</mrow>
				<mrow xml:id="lt-R2">
					\text{R}_{2}:\quad
					\amp \lap{f''(t)} = s^2F(s) - sf(0) - f'(0),
					\amp\amp s \gt 0
				</mrow>
				<mrow xml:id="lt-R3">
					\text{R}_{3}:\quad
					\amp \lap{f'''(t)} = s^3F(s) - s^2f(0) - sf'(0) - f''(0),
					\amp\amp s \gt 0
				</mrow>
			</md>
		</p>

		<p>
			where <m>F(s) = \lap{f(t)}</m>.
		</p>

	<subsection label="lt-laplace-derivatives">
		<title> Multiplication by <m>t^n</m> (Derivatives in <m>s</m>) </title>

		<p>
			When a function is multiplied by <m>t^n</m>, its Laplace transform becomes the <m>n</m>-th derivative of the original transform (up to a sign):
			<me>
				\lap{t^n f(t)} = (-1)^n \frac{d^n}{ds^n}\Big[\lap{f(t)}\Big]
			</me>.
			<proof xml:id="lt-laplace-derivatives-details">
				<title> Click here for a derivation </title>
				<statement>
					<p>
						To show that
						<me>
							\lap{t^n f(t)} = (-1)^n \frac{d^n}{ds^n}\left[\lap{f(t)}\right]
						</me>,
						we start on the right side and show that it leads to the left side.
					</p>

					<p>
						<md>
							<mrow>
								(-1)^n \frac{d^n}{ds^n}\left[\lap{f(t)}\right]
								\amp = (-1)^n \frac{d^n}{ds^n}\left[\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} f(t)\ dt\right]
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\amp = (-1)^n \int_0^{\infty} \frac{\partial^n}{\partial s^n}\left[e^{-st} f(t)\right]\ dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\amp = (-1)^n \int_0^{\infty} f(t)\frac{\partial^n}{\partial s^n}\left[e^{-st}\right]\ dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\amp = (-1)^n \int_0^{\infty} f(t) \left((-t)^n e^{-st}\right)\ dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\amp = (-1)^n \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} t^n f(t)\ dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\amp = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} t^n f(t)\ dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\amp = \lap{t^n f(t)}
							</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
				</statement>
			</proof>
		</p>

		<p>
			If <m>F(s) = \lap{ f(t) }</m>, then this property becomes
		</p>

		<me xml:id="lt-R5">
			\text{R}_{5}:\quad\lap{t^n f(t)} = (-1)^n \frac{d^n}{ds^n}\Big[F(s)\Big],\quad s &gt; 0,\quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots
		</me>

		<task> temporarily omitted </task>

			<p>

					<p>
						<statement>
							<m>\ds\lap{t \cos(t)}</m>
						</statement>
					</p>
					<p>

					</p>
				</sidebyside>
			</p>

					<p>
						<statement>
							<m>\ds\lap{t^2 \sin(-5t)}</m>
