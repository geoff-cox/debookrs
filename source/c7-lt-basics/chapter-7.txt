	<subsection xml:id="lt-review-integration-by-parts">
		<title> Review of Integration by Parts (IBP) </title>

		<p>
			Integration by parts is a core calculus technique that allows us to transfer derivatives from one function to another inside an integral. Its most common form is:
			<me>
				\int_{a}^{b} u\ dv = uv\big|_{a}^{b} - \int_{a}^{b} v\ du
			</me>.
		</p>

		<p>
			When <m>u</m> and <m>v</m> are functions of <m>t</m>, we get a version of IBP which makes the transfer of derivatives more clear:
			<me>
				\int_{a}^{b} \os{{\DLBa\large\text{derivative on }v}}{
				\us{\large u}{ \us{\uparrow}{\ul{ 
					u(t)
				}}} \ 
				\us{\large dv}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{
					{\DLBa\os{\downarrow}{v'\vphantom{\Large\Gamma}}(t)} dt
				}}}
				}
				= \ub{u(b)v(b) - u(a)v(a)}_{\large\text{evaluates to a number}}
				- \int_{a}^{b} \os{{\DLBa\large\text{derivative on }u}}{
					\us{\large v}{ \us{\uparrow}{\ul{ v(t) }}} \ 
					\us{\large du}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{ {\DLBa\os{\downarrow}{u'\vphantom{\Large\Gamma}}(t)} dt }}}
				}
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			This simple idea of shifting derivatives is the basis for the Laplace transform method. We'll see it in action when one of the functions is <m>e^{-st}</m>.
		</p>

		<p>
			Let's now see why exponentials make this derivative transfer especially useful. Suppose we choose <m>u(t) = e^{-st}</m> and <m>v(t) = y(t)</m>. Differentiating gives <m>u'(t) = -s e^{-st}</m> and <m>v'(t) = y'(t)</m>. Applying integration by parts yields:
			<md>
				<mrow>
					\int_{0}^{b} \os{{\DLBa\large\text{derivative on }y}}{ 
						e^{-st} \ {\DLBa\os{\downarrow}{y'\vphantom{\Large\Gamma}}(t)}\ dt
					} 
					\amp = e^{-st} \ y(t) \Big|_{0}^{b} - \int_{0}^{b} \os{{\DLBa\large\text{derivative on }e^{-st}}}{ 
						{\DLBa\os{\downarrow}{\left(-s e^{-st}\right)\vphantom{\Large\Gamma}}}\ y(t)\ dt
					}
				</mrow>
				<mrow>
					\amp = e^{-sb} y(b) - y(0) + {\DLBa s} \int_{0}^{b} {\DLBa e^{-st}} y(t)\, dt
				</mrow>.
			</md>
		</p>

		<p>
			Notice the key point: the derivative on <m>y</m> has been completely removed. Instead, we get two terms: a boundary term <m>e^{-st} y(t)\Big|_0^b</m> and a new integral without <m>y'(t)</m>.
		</p>

		<p>
			This trade is usually worth it since in the context of Laplace transforms, the term <m>e^{-sb} y(b)</m> will often vanish and <m>y(0)</m> is the given initial condition.
		</p>
	</subsection>

	<objectives label="lt-derivative-transfer-wrapup">

			<li>Choosing <m>u(t) = e^{-st}</m> causes derivatives on <m>y(t)</m> to transfer onto the exponential, producing a simple factor of <m>s</m>.</li>
			<li>Understanding this transfer mechanism explains where the variable <m>s</m> comes from and why it appears in Laplace transform formulas.</li>
  		</ul>
	</objectives>

<section label="lt-definition">
	<title> Defining the Laplace Transform </title>

	<introduction>
		<p>
			We've seen how integration by parts transfers derivatives from one function to another inside an integral. Now we take the next step: defining a transform that systematically applies this principle.
		</p>
		<p>
			In this section, we'll see how this transform is constructed and how it converts functions of <m>t</m> into functions of <m>s</m>. You'll also see how to compute Laplace transforms from the definition.
		</p>
	</introduction>

	<subsection xml:id="lt-motivation">
		<title> Why Use the Laplace Transform? </title>
		<p>
			To see where we're headed, imagine you need to solve the differential equation
			<me>
				y'(t) - 9y(t) = 10 e^{2t}.
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			Suppose you multiply both sides by <m>e^{-st}</m> and integrate with respect to <m>t</m>:
			<me>
				\int \left[y'(t) - 9y(t)\right] e^{-st} \, dt = \int 10 e^{2t} e^{-st} \, dt.
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			The left-hand side contains a derivative inside the integral. From the previous section, you know we can transfer this derivative to the exponential function. Doing so removes the derivative from <m>y(t)</m>, essentially allowing you to use algebra to isolate it.
		</p>

		<p>
			This is the idea behind the Laplace transform and the motivation for its definition.
		</p>	
	</subsection>

	<subsection xml:id="lt-def-basic-definition">
		<title> Definition of the Laplace Transform </title>

		<definition xml:id="laplace-transform-definition"><title>Laplace Transform</title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Given a function <m>f(t)</m> defined for <m>t \geq 0</m>, its Laplace transform, denoted <m>\lap{f(t)}</m>, is defined by
					<me>
						\lap{f(t)} = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} f(t) \, dt,
					</me>
					provided the integral converges. The result is a new function, typically written as <m>F(s)</m>:
					<me>
						F(s) = \lap{f(t)}.
					</me>
				</p>
			</statement>
		</definition>

		<paragraphs xml:id="lt-improper-integrals">
			<title> Working with Improper Integrals </title>
			<p>
				Notice that the Laplace transform involves an improper integral: the upper limit is <m>\infty</m>. In calculus, we handle this by replacing <m>\infty</m> with a variable <m>b</m>, computing the definite integral, and then taking the limit as <m>b \to \infty</m>:
				<me>
					\lap{f(t)} = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} f(t)\, dt = \ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} \ob{\int_0^b e^{-st} f(t)\, dt}^{\large\text{(step 1)}}}_{\large\text{(step 2)}}
				</me>.
			</p>

			<p>
				When calculating Laplace transforms, you first compute the integral with respect to <m>t</m>, treating <m>s</m> as a constant. Then you compute the limit of the result as <m>b</m> approaches <m>\infty</m>.
			</p>

			<p>
				The Laplace transform exists if this limit produces a finite value. Often, exponential decay from <m>e^{-st}</m> guarantees convergence when <m>s</m> is large enough.
			</p>

	<subsection xml:id="lt-def-first-examples">
		<title> First Examples of Laplace Transforms </title>
		<p>
			Let's see how the definition works by computing Laplace transforms for simple functions. We'll start with a constant function and a linear function.
		</p>

		<example xml:id="laplace-first-examples">
			<title> First Look at Laplace Transforms </title>
			<p>
				Find the Laplace transform of 
				<ol marker="(a)">
					<li>
						<statement>
							<p><m>f(t) = 5</m> (constant function)</p>
						</statement>
						<solution xml:id="laplace-transform-of-5">
							<title> <m>\rightarrow \lap{5}</m> </title>
							<p>
								By the definition above, we have the improper integral
								<men xml:id="lt-def-example-eqn-1">
									\lap{5} = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \cdot 5\ dt = \lim_{b \to \infty} \ub{\int_0^b 5e^{-st}dt}_{I}
								</men>.
							</p>

							<p>
								Assuming <m>s</m> and <m>b</m> are constant, we integrate <m>I</m> with respect to <m>t</m>:
								<me>
									I = \int_0^b 5e^{-st}dt = -\frac{5}{s}e^{-st}\Big|_{t=0}^{t=b} = -\frac{5}{s}\left[ e^{-sb} - 1 \right]
								</me>.
							</p>

							<proof xml:id="lt-example-1-details"><title>üîç Next, we compute the limit of <m>I</m> as <m>b \to \infty</m> (with <m>s</m> constant): </title>
								<p>
									üîç Since the limit only controls <m>b</m>, we can think of <m>s</m> as a number (like <m>2</m>). This means that <m>\ds-\frac{5}{s}</m> is also a number that can be pulled out of the limit using the limit property from calculus:
									<me>\ds\lim_{x \to \infty} c \cdot f(x) = c \cdot \lim_{x \to \infty} f(x), \quad c\ \text{is constant}</me>
								</p>
							</proof>

							<md>
								<mrow>
									\lim_{b \to \infty} I = \lim_{b \to \infty} -\frac{5}{s}\left[ e^{-sb} - 1 \right] 
										\amp = -\frac{5}{s} \lim_{b \to \infty} \Big[ e^{-sb} - 1 \Big]
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
										\amp = -\frac{5}{s} \Big[ \ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{-sb}}_{L} - 1 \Big]
								</mrow>
							</md>

							<p> 
								We find the limit using the <xref ref="exponential-end-behavior" text="custom">end behavior of the exponential</xref>. There are two possiblities:<fn> <m>s = 0</m> is excluded since it would lead to <me>\int_0^{\infty}5e^{-0t}\ dt = \int_0^{\infty}5\ dt = \infty</me></fn>
								<ol marker="(1)">
									<li> <m>s</m> is positive: <m>\ds\quad-sb \to -\infty \ \ \Rightarrow\ \ \lim_{b \to \infty}\ e^{-sb} = 0</m> </li>
									<li> <m>s</m> is negative: <m>\ds\quad-sb \to +\infty \ \ \Rightarrow\ \ \lim_{b \to \infty}\ e^{-sb} = \infty</m> </li>
								</ol>
							</p>

							<p>
								Since we only get convergence when <m>s</m> is positive, we must require <m>s \gt 0</m> in the Laplace transform of <m>5</m>:
								<me>
									\lap{5} = -\frac{1}{s} [0 - 1] = \frac{1}{s} \quad \text{for } s \gt 0
								</me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</li>
					<li>
						<statement>
							<p><m>f(t)=t</m></p>
						</statement>
						<solution xml:id="laplace-transform-of-t-power-1"><title> <m>\rightarrow \lap{t}</m> </title>

							<p>
								Applying the definition and writing the integral as a limit, we have
								<me>
									\lap{ t }	= \lim_{b \to \infty} {\ub{\int_0^b e^{-st} \cdot t\ dt}_{I}}
								</me>.
							</p>

							<p>
								<proof xml:id="lt-example-t-IVP-details"><title>üîç Computing <m>I</m> with integration by parts, gives us </title>
									<p>
										üîç Choosing
										<md>
											<mrow> u = t,	\quad\amp	dv = e^{-st}dt, 		</mrow>
											<mrow> du = dt,	\quad\amp	v = -\frac{1}{s}e^{-st}	</mrow>
										</md>
										integration by parts gives
										<md>
											<mrow>
												\int_0^b e^{-st} \cdot t\ dt
													\amp = -\frac{t}{s}e^{-st} - \int_0^b \left( -\frac{1}{s}e^{-st} \right) dt
											</mrow>
											<mrow> 	
													\amp = -\frac{t}{s}e^{-st}+ \frac{1}{s}\int_0^b e^{-st} dt
											</mrow>
											<mrow> 	
													\amp = \left(-\frac{t}{s}e^{-st}- \frac{1}{s^2}e^{-st}\right) \Bigg|_0^b
											</mrow>
											<mrow> 	
													\amp = \left(-\frac{b}{s}e^{-sb} - \frac{1}{s^2}e^{-sb}\right) - \left(0 - \frac{1}{s^2}\right)
											</mrow>
											<mrow> 	
													\amp = -\frac{b}{s}e^{-sb} - \frac{1}{s^2}e^{-sb} + \frac{1}{s^2}
											</mrow>
										</md>
									</p>
								</proof>
								<md>
									<mrow>
										{I}	\amp\ { = -\frac{b}{s}e^{-sb} - \frac{1}{s^2}e^{-sb} + \frac{1}{s^2}}
									</mrow>
								</md>
							</p>

							<p>
								Next, we compute the limit of <m>I</m> as <m>b \to \infty</m>:
								<md>
									<mrow>
										\lim_{b \to \infty} {I} \amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left({ -\frac{b}{s}e^{-sb} - \frac{1}{s^2}e^{-sb} + \frac{1}{s^2}}\right)
									</mrow>
									<mrow>
										\amp = -\frac{1}{s}\lim_{b \to \infty} be^{-sb} - \frac{1}{s^2}\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{-sb} + \lim_{b \to \infty} \frac{1}{s^2}
									</mrow>
									<mrow>
										\amp = -\frac{1}{s}\ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} \frac{b}{e^{sb}}}_{L_1} - \frac{1}{s^2}\ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{-sb}}_{L_2} + \frac{1}{s^2}
									</mrow>
								</md> 
							</p>

							<proof xml:id="lt-example-t-LH-details"><title>üîç As long as <m>s \gt 0</m>, both <m>L_1</m> and <m>L_2</m> go to zero </title>
								<p>
									üîç Assuming <m>s \gt 0</m>, the limits are computed by:
									<ul marker="square">
										<li>
											<p>
												<m>
													\ds L_1 
													= \lim_{b \to \infty}\frac{\os{\infty}{\os{\uparrow}{\boxed{b}}}}{\us{\infty}{\us{\downarrow}{\boxed{e^{sb}}}}}
													\,\us{LH}{=}\, \lim_{b \to \infty}\frac{1}{se^{sb}}
													= \frac{1}{s}\lim_{b \to \infty}\frac{1}{\us{\infty}{\us{\downarrow}{\boxed{e^{sb}}}}} = 0
												</m>
											</p>
										</li>
										<li>
											<p>
												<m>
													\ds L_2
													= \lim_{b \to \infty}\frac{1}{\us{\infty}{\us{\downarrow}{\boxed{e^{sb}}}}} = 0
												</m>
											</p>
										</li>
									</ul>
									where <m>LH</m> denotes L'H√¥pital's Rule was applied to the limit.
								</p>
							</proof>

							<p>
								Therefore, the Laplace transform of <m>t</m> is
								<me>
									\lap{ t } = \frac{1}{s^2}, \quad s &gt; 0
								</me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</li>
				</ol>
			</p>
		</example>

		<p>
			These examples show the typical process: set up the improper integral, compute it as a definite integral, then take the limit as <m>b \to \infty</m>. You'll see how <m>s</m> emerges naturally from this process.
		</p>
		<p>
			In upcoming sections, we'll build a library of Laplace transforms for the most common functions and learn properties that make these computations even faster.
		</p>
	</subsection>

	<objectives label="lt-definition-wrapup">
		<ul marker="square">
			<li>The Laplace transform converts a function of <m>t</m> into a function of <m>s</m> using <me>\lap{f(t)} = \int_0^\infty e^{-st} f(t)\, dt.</me></li>
			<li>The Laplace transform is an improper integral evaluated by rewriting it as <me>\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} f(t)\, dt = \lim_{b \to \infty} \int_0^b e^{-st} f(t)\, dt</me>.</li>
			<li>The result is a function of <m>s</m> and exists only when the integral converges.</li>
		</ul>
	</objectives>

	<subsection label="constant-function-transform">
		<title> Laplace Transform of a Constant </title>

		<p>
			We begin with the simplest case: the constant function <m>f(t) = 1</m>. By the definition of the Laplace transform,
			<me>
				\lap{1} = \int_0^\infty e^{-st}\, dt.
			</me>
		</p>
		<p>
			This integral converges when <m>s \lt 0</m> and leads to our first Laplace transform rule:
			<!-- <md>
				<mrow xml:id="lt-L1">
					\text{L}_1:\quad\lap{1} = \frac{1}{s}, \quad s \gt 0
				</mrow>.
			</md> -->
		</p>

		<assemblage xml:id="lap-L1">
			<title>üìú Laplace Transform of <m>1</m></title>
			<p>
				<md>
					<mrow xml:id="lt-L1">
						\text{L}_1:\quad\lap{1} = \frac{1}{s}, \quad s \gt 0
					</mrow>.
				</md>
				<proof>
					<title>üí° Derivation of L<m>_1</m> </title>
					<p>
						By definition, we have
						<me>
							\lap{1} = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \cdot 1\ dt = \lim_{b \to \infty} \ub{\int_0^b e^{-st}dt}_{I}
						</me>.
					</p>

					<p>
						Assuming <m>s</m> and <m>b</m> are constant, we integrate <m>I</m> with respect to <m>t</m>:
						<me>
							I = \int_0^b e^{-st}dt = -\frac{1}{s}e^{-st}\Big|_{t=0}^{t=b} = -\frac{1}{s}\left[ e^{-sb} - 1 \right]
						</me>.
					</p>

					<p>
						Next, we compute the limit of <m>I</m> as <m>b \to \infty</m> (with <m>s</m> constant):
						<md>
							<mrow>
								\lim_{b \to \infty} I 
								\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} -\frac{1}{s}\left[ e^{-sb} - 1 \right]
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\amp = -\frac{1}{s} \lim_{b \to \infty} \Big[ e^{-sb} - 1 \Big] 
								= -\frac{1}{s} \Big[ \ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{-sb}}_{L} - 1 \Big]
							</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
			
					<p>
						Taking <m>b\to\infty</m>, <m>L</m> must go to <m>0</m> if <m>s</m> is positive, leaving us with the transform of <m>1</m>:
						<me>
							\lap{1} = -\frac{1}{s} [0 - 1] = \frac{1}{s} \quad \text{for } s \gt 0
						</me>.
					</p>
				</proof>
			</p>
		</assemblage>

		<p>
			Although this transform is specific to the contant function <m>1</m>, we will see later that it will serve as the transform formula for all constant functions. 
		</p>
	</subsection>

	<subsection label="exponential-function-transform">
		<title> Laplace Transform of Exponentials </title>

		<p>
			To get the transform formula for exponentials of the form <m>f(t) = e^{at}</m>, where <m>a</m> is a constant, we will first find it for <m>e^{7t}</m>:
		</p>

		<p>
			<me>\lap{e^{7t}} = \frac{1}{s - 7}, \quad s \gt 7</me>
			<solution xml:id="common-exp-7t-details">
				<p>
					Using the definition of the Laplace transform we get
					<me>
						\lap{ e^{7t} }
						= \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \cdot e^{7t}\ dt
						= \lim_{b \to \infty}\int_0^b e^{(7-s)t}\ dt
					</me>.
				</p>

				<p>
					<proof xml:id="lt-example-2-details"><title>üîç Focusing just on the integral, we let <m>u=7-s</m> and apply <m>u</m>-substitution: </title>
						<p>
							üëâ Using <m>u</m>-substitution with
							<md>
								<mrow>
									u	\amp = \ob{(7-s)}^{\text{constant}}t
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									du	\amp = (7-s)dt \quad \Rightarrow \quad dt = \frac{du}{7-s}
								</mrow>
							</md>
							the integral is compute as
							<md>
								<mrow>
									\int_0^b e^{(7-s)t}\ dt	= \int_{t=0}^{t=b} e^{u}\frac{du}{7-s}
									\amp = \frac{1}{7-s}\int_{t=0}^{t=b} e^{u}\ du
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = \frac{1}{7-s} \left[ e^{u} - 1 \right]\Bigg|_{t=0}^{t=b}
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = \frac{1}{7-s} \left[ e^{7-s} - 1 \right]\Bigg|_{0}^{b}
								</mrow>
							</md>.
						</p>
					</proof>
					<md>
						<mrow>
							\int_0^b e^{(7-s)t}\, dt = \frac{1}{7-s} e^{(7-s)t} \Bigg|_0^b
							\amp = \frac{1}{7-s} \left( e^{(7-s)b} - e^{0} \right)
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = \frac{1}{7-s} \left( e^{(7-s)b} - 1 \right)
						</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>

				<p>
					Now, we compute the limit of this expression as <m>b \to \infty</m>:
					<md>
						<mrow>
							\lim_{b \to \infty} \frac{1}{7-s} \left( e^{(7-s)b} - 1 \right)
							\amp = \frac{1}{7-s} \Bigg[ \ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{(7-s)b}}_{L} - \lim_{b \to \infty} 1 \Bigg]
						</mrow>
					</md>.
				</p>

				<p>
					As before, <m>L</m> goes to <m>0</m> if the <term><xref ref="exponential-end-behavior" text="custom">power of the exponential function</xref></term> goes to <m>-\infty</m>. This only happens if <m>7-s</m> is negative. That is, <m>\ 7-s \lt 0\ </m> or <m>\ 7 \lt s</m>. 
				</p>

				<p>
					So, as long as <m>s \gt 7</m>, then <m>L \to 0</m> and the transform becomes 
					<me>
						\lap{e^{7t}} = \frac{1}{s - 7}, \quad s \gt 7
					</me>.
				</p>
			</solution>
		</p>

		<p>
			Replacing <m>7</m> with an <m>a</m> in this solution leads to the general formula:
		</p>

		<p>
			For any constant <m>a</m>, the Laplace transform of <m>e^{at}</m> is given by:
		</p>

		<assemblage xml:id="lap-L2">
			<title>üìú Laplace Transform of <m>e^{at}</m></title>
			<p>
				<md>
					<mrow xml:id="lt-L2">
						\text{L}_2:\quad\lap{e^{at}} = \frac{1}{s - a}, \quad s \gt a
					</mrow>.
				</md>
				<proof>
					<title>üí°Derivation of L<m>_2</m> </title>
					<p>
						By definition,
						<me>
							\lap{ e^{at} }
							= \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \cdot e^{at}\ dt
							= \lim_{b \to \infty}\int_0^b e^{(a-s)t}\ dt
						</me>.
					</p>

					<p>
						For the integral, let <m>u=a-s</m> and apply <m>u</m>-substitution:
						<md>
							<mrow>
								\int_0^b e^{(a-s)t}\, dt
								<xref text="custom" ref="lt-example-2-details"><m>\os{\large ‚ùî}{=}</m></xref> 
								\frac{1}{a-s} e^{(a-s)t} \Bigg|_0^b
								\amp = \frac{1}{a-s} \left( e^{(a-s)b} - e^{0} \right)
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\amp = \frac{1}{a-s} \left( e^{(a-s)b} - 1 \right)
							</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>

					<p>
						Now, we compute the limit of this expression as <m>b \to \infty</m>:
						<md>
							<mrow>
								\lim_{b \to \infty} \frac{1}{a-s} \left( e^{(a-s)b} - 1 \right)
								\amp = \frac{1}{a-s} \Bigg[ \ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{(a-s)b}}_{L} - \lim_{b \to \infty} 1 \Bigg]
							</mrow>
						</md>.
					</p>

					<p>
						As before, <m>L</m> goes to <m>0</m> if <m>a-s</m> is negative. That is, <m>\ a-s \lt 0\ </m> or <m>\ a \lt s</m>. 
					</p>

					<p>
						So, as long as <m>s \gt a</m>, then <m>L \to 0</m> and the transform becomes 
						<me>
							\lap{e^{at}} = \frac{1}{s - a}, \quad s \gt a
						</me>.
					</p>
				</proof>
			</p>
		</assemblage>

	<subsection label="power-function-transform">
		<title> Laplace Transform of Powers of <m>t</m> </title>

		<p>
			Now let's consider power functions, <m>f(t) = t^n</m> where <m>n</m> is a non-negative integer.
			<me>
				\lap{t^n} = \frac{n!}{s^{n+1}}, \quad s > 0.
			</me>
		</p>
		<p>
			We already found the transforms for <m>n = 0</m> and <m>n = 1</m> since
			<ul>
				<li><m>n=0:\qquad\lap{t^0} = \lap{1} = \dfrac{1}{s}</m></li>
				<li><m>n=1:\qquad\lap{t^1} = \lap{t} = \dfrac{1}{s^2}</m></li>
			</ul>
			We could find <m>\lap{t^2}</m> next, but it is more instructive to show following relationship:
		</p>

		<p>
			<me>\lap{t^2} = \frac{2}{s}\cdot \lap{t}, \quad s \gt 0</me>
			<solution label="common-t-sqrd-details">
				<p>
					Applying the definition and writing the integral as a limit, we have
					<me>
						\lap{ t^2 } = \lim_{b \to \infty} \ub{\int_0^b e^{-st} \cdot t^2\ dt}_{I}
					</me>.
				</p>

				<p>
					Applying integration by parts to <m>I</m> once in <m>\lap{ t^2 }</m> shows
					<md>
						<mrow>
							\lap{ t^2 }
							\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{b^2}{s}e^{-sb} + \frac{2}{s}\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot t\ dt\right]
							<xref ref="lt-example-tsqrd-IVP-details" text="custom">üîçHow?</xref>
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = -\frac{1}{s} \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{b^2}{e^{sb}}\right] + \frac{2}{s}\lim_{b \to \infty}\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot t\ dt
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = -\frac{1}{s}\ub{\lim_{b \to \infty}\frac{b^2}{e^{sb}}}_{L} + \frac{2}{s}\ub{\int_0^\infty e^{-st}\cdot t\ dt}_{\large\lap{t}}
						</mrow>.
					</md>
				</p>

				<p>
					Noticing that the improper integral is just <m>\lap{t}</m>, we have
					<me>
						\lap{ t^2 } = -\frac{1}{s}\cdot L + \frac{2}{s}\cdot \lap{t}
					</me>,
					but <m>L=0</m> if <m>s \gt 0</m> <xref ref="lt-example-tsqrd-LH-details" text="custom">(üîçHow?)</xref>, so we have the relationship
				</p>

				<me>
					\lap{t^2} = \frac{2}{s}\cdot \lap{t}, \quad s \gt 0
				</me>.
			</solution>			
		</p>

		
		<p>
			You can use this work as a template for a general recursive relationship:
		</p>

		<p>
			<me>\lap{t^n} = \frac{n}{s}\cdot \lap{t^{n-1}}, \quad s \gt 0</me>
			<solution label="common-recursive-power-function-details">
				<p>
					Replacing <m>2</m> with <m>n</m> in the previous solution, we have
					<me>
						\lap{ t^n } = \lim_{b \to \infty} \ub{\int_0^b e^{-st} \cdot t^n\ dt}_{I}
					</me>.
				</p>

				<p>
					<proof xml:id="lt-example-tnth-IVP-details"><title>üîç Apply integration by parts to <m>I</m> </title>
						<p>
							<md>
								<mrow> u = t^n,				\quad\amp	dv = e^{-st}dt, 		</mrow>
								<mrow> du = nt^{n-1}\ dt,	\quad\amp	v = -\frac{1}{s}e^{-st}	</mrow>
							</md>
							integration by parts gives
							<md>
								<mrow>
									\lap{ t^{n} }
									\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{t^{n}}{s}e^{-sb}\Bigg|_0^b - \int_0^b \left(-\frac{1}{s}e^{-st}\right)\cdot nt^{n-1}\ dt\right]
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\left(-\frac{b^n}{s}e^{-sb} - 0\right) + \frac{n}{s}\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot t^{n-1}\ dt\right]
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{b^n}{e^{sb}} + \frac{n}{s}\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot t^{n-1}\ dt\right]
								</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
					</proof>
					<md>
						<mrow>
							\lap{ t^{n} }
							\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{b^n}{e^{sb}} + \frac{n}{s}\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot t^{n-1}\ dt\right]
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = -\frac{1}{s} \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{b^n}{e^{sb}}\right] + \frac{n}{s}\lim_{b \to \infty}\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot t^{n-1}\ dt
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = -\frac{1}{s}\ub{\lim_{b \to \infty}\frac{b^n}{e^{sb}}}_{L} + \frac{n}{s}\ub{\int_0^\infty e^{-st}\cdot t^{n-1}\ dt}_{\large\lap{t^{n-1}}}
						</mrow>.
					</md>
				</p>

				<p>
					Noticing that the improper integral is just <m>\lap{t^{n-1}}</m>, we have
					<me>
						\lap{ t^{n} } = -\frac{1}{s}\cdot L + \frac{n}{s}\cdot \lap{t^{n-1}}
					</me>.
				</p>

				<p>
					<proof xml:id="lt-example-tnth-LH-details"><title>üîç Since <m>L=0</m> if <m>s \gt 0</m>, we have </title>
						<p>
							The reasoning is the same as in <xref ref="lt-example-tnth-IVP-details" text="custom">previous solution</xref> except we apply L'H√¥pital's rule <em>n</em> times. So we have
							<md>
								<mrow>
									\lim_{b \to \infty}\frac{b^n}{e^{sb}}\
									\amp\os{\large\left(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\right)}{\us{LH}{=}}
									\lim_{b \to \infty}\frac{nb^{n-1}}{se^{sb}}
									= \frac{n}{s}\lim_{b \to \infty}\frac{b^{n-1}}{e^{sb}}
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp\os{\large\left(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\right)}{\us{LH}{=}}
									\frac{n}{s}\lim_{b \to \infty}\frac{(n-1)b^{n-2}}{se^{sb}}
									= \frac{n(n-1)}{s^2}\lim_{b \to \infty}\frac{b^{n-2}}{e^{sb}}
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp \qquad\qquad \vdots \quad LH\ \ n-2 \text{ more times}
								</mrow>
								<mrow>
									\amp\os{\large\left(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\right)}{\us{LH}{=}}
									\frac{n!}{s^n}\lim_{b \to \infty}\frac{1}{se^{sb}}
									= \frac{n!}{s^{n+1}}\lim_{b \to \infty}\frac{1}{\us{\infty}{\us{\downarrow}{\boxed{e^{sb}}}}}
									= 0
								</mrow>
							</md>
						</p>
					</proof>
				</p>

				<me>
					\lap{t^{n}} = \frac{n}{s}\cdot \lap{t^{n-1}}, \quad s \gt 0
				</me>.
			</solution>
		</p>

		<p>
			To get the general formula we apply this relationship repeatedly for increasing <m>n</m>:
			<me> 
				\begin{array}{crcl}
					n=1:	\amp \DLO\lap{t} \amp =	\amp 
						\dfrac{1}{s}\cdot\lap{1} = \dfrac{1}{s}\cdot\dfrac{1}{s} = {\DLO\dfrac{1}{s^2}}\\
					n=2:	\amp \DLBa\lap{t^2}	\amp =	\amp 
						\dfrac{2}{s}\cdot{\DLO\lap{t}} = \dfrac{2}{s}\cdot{\DLO\dfrac{1}{s^2}} = {\DLBa\dfrac{2\cdot 1}{s^3}}\\
					n=3:	\amp \DLGb\lap{t^3}	\amp =	\amp 
						\dfrac{3}{s}\cdot{\DLBa\lap{t^2}} = \dfrac{3}{s}\cdot{\DLBa\dfrac{2\cdot 1}{s^3}} = {\DLGb\dfrac{3\cdot 2\cdot 1}{s^4}}\\
					n=4:	\amp \DLRa\lap{t^4}	\amp =	\amp
						\dfrac{4}{s}\cdot{\DLGb\lap{t^3}} = \dfrac{4}{s}\cdot{\DLGb\dfrac{3\cdot 2\cdot 1}{s^4}} = {\DLRa\dfrac{4\cdot 3\cdot 2\cdot 1}{s^5}}\\
					\vdots	\amp \vdots\quad\amp \amp \qquad\qquad\vdots\\
				\end{array}
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			This pattern suggests that, in general, we have
		</p>

		<me>
			\lap{t^n} =\dfrac{n}{s}\cdot\lap{t^{n-1}} = \dfrac{n}{s}\cdot\dfrac{(n-1)\dotsm 4\cdot 3\cdot 2\cdot 1}{s^{n}}
		</me>,
		
		<p>
			which can be summarized as follows
		</p>

		<assemblage xml:id="lap-L3">
			<title>üìú Laplace Transform of <m>t^n</m></title>
			<p>
				<md>
					<mrow xml:id="lt-L3">
						\text{L}_3:\quad\lap{t^n} = \frac{n!}{s^{n+1}}, \quad s \gt 0, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots
					</mrow>.
				</md>
				<proof>
					<title>üí°Derivation of L<m>_3</m> </title>
					<p>
						Most of the ideas were described above, but the formal derivation follows by induction on <m>n</m>, which we will not cover here.
					</p>
				</proof>
			</p>
		</assemblage>
	</subsection>
	
	<subsection label="sine-cosine-function-transform">
		<title> Laplace Transforms of Sine and Cosine </title>

		<p>
			Finally, we derive the Laplace transforms of <m>\sin(bt)</m> and <m>\cos(bt)</m>.
		</p>

		<p>
			To make the derivation easier, we will find the transform of <m>\cos(3t)</m> first.
		</p>

		<p>
			<me>
				\lap{\cos(3t)} = \frac{s}{s^2 + 3^2}, \quad s \gt 0
			</me>
			<solution>
				<p>
					Applying the definition and writing the integral as a limit, we have
					<me>
						\lap{ \cos(3t)} = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \cdot \cos(3t)\ dt = \lim_{b \to \infty} \ub{\int_0^{b} e^{-st} \cos(3t)\ dt}_{I}
					</me>.
				</p>

				<p>
					Treating <m>s</m> constant, we apply integration by parts to <m>I</m> as follows:
				</p>

				<me>
					I = -\frac{e^{-st}}{s} \cos(3t) \Bigg|_0^b - \frac{3}{s}\int_0^{b} e^{-st} \sin(3t)\ dt
				</me>,

				<aside><title> Integration by Parts </title>
					<p>
						1st Integration by Parts
						<md>
							<mrow>	u	\amp = \cos(3t)				</mrow>
							<mrow>	du	\amp = -3\sin(3t)\ dt		</mrow>
							<mrow>	v	\amp = -\sfrac{e^{-st}}{s}	</mrow>
							<mrow>	dv	\amp = e^{-st}\ dt			</mrow>
						</md>
						2nd Integration by Parts
						<md>
							<mrow>	u	\amp = \sin(3t)				</mrow>
							<mrow>	du	\amp = 3\cos(3t)\ dt		</mrow>
							<mrow>	v	\amp = -\sfrac{e^{-st}}{s}	</mrow>
							<mrow>	dv	\amp = e^{-st}\ dt			</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
				</aside>

				<p>
					but this requires a second integration by parts:
					<md>
						<mrow>
							I
							\amp = -\frac{e^{-st}}{s} \cos(3t) \Bigg|_0^b - \frac{3}{s} \left[ -\frac{e^{-st}}{s} \sin(3t)\Bigg|_0^b + \frac{3}{s} \int_0^{b} e^{-st} \cos(3t)\ dt \right]
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = \left(-\frac{e^{-st}}{s} \cos(3t) + \frac{3e^{-st}}{s^2} \sin(3t)\right)\Bigg|_0^b - \frac{9}{s^2} \ub{\int_0^{b} e^{-st} \cos(3t)\ dt}_{I}
						</mrow>
					</md>.
				</p>

				<p>
					Since the integral <m>I</m> reappeared, we actually have
					<me>
						I = \left(-\frac{e^{-st}}{s} \cos(3t) + \frac{3e^{-st}}{s^2} \sin(3t)\right)\Bigg|_0^b - \frac{9}{s^2} I
					</me>.
					Evaluating the limits of integration and solving for <m>I</m>, we find
					<md>
						<mrow>
							I + \frac{9}{s^2} I  \amp = \left(-\frac{e^{-st}}{s} \cos(3t) + \frac{3e^{-st}}{s^2} \sin(3t)\right)\Bigg|_0^b
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							I\left(\frac{s^2+9}{s^2}\right) \amp = \left(-\frac{e^{-sb}}{s} \cos(3b) + \frac{3e^{-sb}}{s^2} \sin(3b) + \frac{1}{s} \right)
						</mrow>
						<mrow> 
							I	\amp = \frac{s^2}{s^2+9}\left(-\frac{e^{-sb}}{s} \cos(3b) + \frac{3e^{-sb}}{s^2} \sin(3b) + \frac{1}{s} \right)
						</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>

				<p>
					Finally, the limit of <m>I</m> as <m>b \to \infty</m> is the sum of the limits
					<md>
						<mrow>
							\lim_{b \to \infty} I = \amp
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\frac{s^2}{s^2+9} \amp \Bigg(
								- \frac{1}{s}\ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{-sb} \cos(3b)}_{L_1}
								+ \frac{3}{s^2}\ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{-sb} \sin(3b)}_{L_2} 
								+ \frac{1}{s}
							\Bigg)
						</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>

				<p>
					<proof xml:id="lt-example-4-details"><title>üîç Since <m>L_1 \to 0</m> &amp; <m>L_2 \to 0</m> if <m>s \gt 0</m>, we can conclude that </title>
						<p>
							üëâ If <m>s &gt; 0</m>, then we know that <m>e^{-sb} \to 0</m> as <m>b \to \infty</m> and both <m>\cos(3b)</m> and <m>\sin(3b)</m> oscillate between <m>-1</m> and <m>1</m>, Thus, their product must approach zero. That is,
							<me>
								\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{-sb} \cos(3b) = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{b \to \infty} e^{-sb} \sin(3b) = 0
							</me>
						</p>
					</proof>
					
					<me>
						\lap{\cos(3t)} = \frac{s}{s^2 + 9}, \quad s \gt 0
					</me>.
				</p>
			</solution>
		</p>

		<p>
			 leads to the following general formula:
		</p>

		<assemblage xml:id="lap-L4-L5">
			<title>üìú Laplace Transform of <m>t^n</m></title>
			<p>
				<md>
					<mrow xml:id="lt-L4">
						\text{L}_4:\quad\lap{\cos(bt)} = \frac{s}{s^2 + b^2}, \quad s \gt 0
					</mrow>.
				</md>
				<md>
					<mrow xml:id="lt-L5">
						\text{L}_5:\quad\lap{\sin(bt)} = \frac{b}{s^2 + b^2}, \quad s \gt 0
					</mrow>,
				</md>
				<proof>
					<title>üí°Derivation of L<m>_4</m> &amp; L<m>_5</m> </title>
					<p>
						The derivation of L<m>_4</m> mirrors the solution to <m>\lap{\cos(3t)}</m>. Just swap <m>3</m> with <m>b</m> and <m>9</m> with <m>b^2</m>. 
					</p>
					<p>
						The derivation of L<m>_5</m> follows a similar approach to L<m>_4</m> and is left as exercise for you to try on your own.
					</p>
				</proof>
			</p>
		</assemblage>
	</subsection>

	<objectives label="common-transforms-wrapup">
		<ul marker="square">
			<li>
				<p>
					We now have the following common Laplace transforms:
					<ul>
						<li><m> \ds\lap{1} = \frac{1}{s}, \quad s \gt 0 </m>,</li>
						<li><m> \ds\lap{e^{at}} = \frac{1}{s - a}, \quad s \gt a </m>,</li>
						<li><m> \ds\lap{t^n} = \frac{n!}{s^{n+1}}, \quad s \gt 0, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots </m>,</li>
						<li><m> \ds\lap{\cos(bt)} = \frac{s}{s^2 + b^2}, \quad s \gt 0 </m>,</li>
						<li><m> \ds\lap{\sin(bt)} = \frac{b}{s^2 + b^2}, \quad s \gt 0 </m>.</li>
					</ul>
				</p>
			</li>
		</ul>
	</objectives>
</section>

	<subsection label="linearity-of-lts">
		<title> Linearity: Sums and Constant Multiples </title>

		<p>
			Like integrals, the Laplace transform is linear. This means we can take apart sums and pull out constant multiples. In particular, if the Laplace transforms of <m>f(t)</m> and <m>g(t)</m> exist, then we have the following linearity property:
		</p>

		<md>
			<mrow xml:id="lt-P1">
				\text{P}_{1}:\quad\lap{af(t) \pm bg(t)} = a \lap{f(t)} \pm b \lap{g(t)}\quad (a, b\ \text{constants})
			</mrow>.
		</md>

		<p>
			This property allows us to break complex expressions into smaller pieces, each of which can be transformed using our basic table.
		</p>

		<example label="lt-linearity-property-example">
			<title> Transforming a Sum of Functions </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Compute the Laplace transform
					<me> 
						\lap{15 + 6e^{7t} - 11t}
					</me>.
				</p>
			</statement>

			<solution>
				<p>
					We apply the linearity property to break the transform down as follows:
					<md>
						<mrow>
							\lap{15 + 6e^{7t} - 11t}
							\amp = \lap{15} + \lap{6e^{7t}} - \lap{11t}
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = 15\lap{1} + 6\lap{e^{7t}} - 11\lap{t}
						</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>

				<p>
					Then, using the common transforms, <xref ref="lt-L1" text="custom">L<m>_1</m></xref>, <xref ref="lt-L2" text="custom">L<m>_2</m> (<m>a=7</m>)</xref>, and <xref ref="lt-L3" text="custom">L<m>_3</m> (<m>n=1</m>)</xref>, we get 
				</p>

				<md>
					<mrow>
						\lap{15 + 6e^{7t} - 11t}
						\amp = 15\cdot \os{\large s\gt0}{\vphantom{\int^{|}}\frac{1}{s}} + 6\cdot \os{\large s\gt7}{\vphantom{\int^{|}}\frac{1}{s-7}} - 11\cdot \os{\large s\gt0}{\vphantom{\int^{|}}\frac{1}{s^2}}
					</mrow>
					<mrow>
						\amp = \frac{15}{s} + \frac{6}{s-7} - \frac{11}{s^2}, \quad s \gt 7
					</mrow>.
				</md>
				<p>
					We use <m>s \gt 7</m>, because it guarantees that <m>s \gt 0</m> is also true.
				</p>
			</solution>
		</example>

	<subsection label="lt-derivative-transfer-property">
		<title> Derivatives of Functions (Derivative Transfer) </title>

		<p>
			One of the most powerful features of the Laplace transform is its ability to transform differential equations into algebraic equations. This means we need a way to <q>remove</q> the derivatives from the unknown in our differential equation. The following property perfectly encapsulates this idea:
		</p>

		<me>
			\lap{f'(t)} = s \lap{f(t)} - f(0), \qquad s \gt 0
		</me>

		<p>
			In short, this property uses integration by parts to transfer the derivative from <m>f'(t)</m> to <m>e^{-st}</m>. This results in a factor of <m>-s</m> (chain rule on <m>e^{-st}</m>) and <m>f(0)</m> as follows:
		</p>

		<me>
			\lap{f'(t)} = \int_0^\infty e^{-st} f'(t)\ dt \quad \os{\Large\text{IBP}}{\longrightarrow} \quad -(-s)\ub{\int_0^\infty e^{-st} f(t)\ dt}_{\large\lap{f(t)}} - f(0)
		</me>.
	
		<proof xml:id="lt-derivative-transfer-details">
			<title> Click here for more details </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					To show that
					<me>
						\lap{f'(t)} = s\lap{f(t)} - f(0), \quad s \gt 0
					</me>,
					we start with the definition of the Laplace transform:
				</p>

				<p>
					<me>
						\lap{ f'(t) } = \lim_{b \to \infty} \ub{\int_0^b e^{-st} \cdot f'(t)\ dt}_{I}
					</me>.
				</p>

				<p>
					Applying integration by parts to <m>I</m> once in <m>\lap{ f'(t) }</m> with
					<md>
						<mrow> u = e^{-st},			\quad\amp	dv = f'(t)\ dt,	</mrow>
						<mrow> du = -se^{-st}\ dt,	\quad\amp	v = f(t)		</mrow>
					</md>
					integration by parts gives
					<md>
						<mrow>
							\lap{ t^{n} }
							\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[f(t)e^{-sb}\Bigg|_0^b - \int_0^b f(t)\cdot\left(-se^{-st}\right)\ dt\right]
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\left(f(b)e^{-sb} - f(0)e^{0}\right) + s\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot f(t)\ dt\right]
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[f(b)e^{-sb} - f(0) + s\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot f(t)\ dt\right]
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[f(b)e^{-sb}\right] - f(0) +  s\lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot f(t)\ dt\right]
						</mrow>
						<mrow>
							\amp = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[f(b)e^{-sb}\right] - f(0) +  s\ub{\int_\infty^b e^{-st}\cdot f(t)\ dt}_{\large\lap{f(t)}}
						</mrow>.
					</md>
				</p>

				<p>
					Recall that a requirement for the Laplace transform of <m>f(t)</m> to exist is that
					<me>
						\lim_{b \to \infty} \left[f(b)e^{-sb}\right] = 0, \quad s \gt 0
					</me>.
				</p>

				<p>
					Thus, setting the limit to zero gives us the desired result
					<me>
						\lap{ f'(t) } = s\lap{f(t)} - f(0)
					</me>.
				</p>
			</statement>
		</proof>

		<task label="lt-properties-chkpt-3">
			<title> üìñ‚ùì Fill-In the Missing Number </title>
			<statement>
				<p>

					</test>
				</evaluate>
			</evaluation>
		</task>

		<p>
			Notice that this property is also recursive, meaning it shows us how to handle higher-order derivatives as well. For example, 
			<md>
				<mrow>
					\lap{f'(t)}
					\amp = \DLGb \ul{s\lap{f(t)} - f(0)}
				</mrow>
				<mrow>
					\lap{ f''(t) }
					\amp = s \lap{f'(t)} - f'(0) = s \Big({\DLGb \ul{s\lap{f(t)} - f(0)}}\Big) - f'(0)
				</mrow>
				<mrow>
					\amp = \DLBb \ul{s^2\lap{f(t)} - sf(0) - f'(0)}
				</mrow>
				<mrow>
					\lap{ f'''(t) }
					\amp = s \lap{f''(t)} - f''(0) = s \Big({\DLBb \ul{s^2\lap{f(t)} - sf(0) - f'(0)}}\Big) - f''(0)
				</mrow>
				<mrow>
					\amp = s^3\lap{f(t)} - s^2f(0) - sf'(0) - f''(0), \text{ and so on}
				</mrow>
			</md>
		</p>

		<p>
			To summarize, we now have the following new properties:
			<md>
				<mrow xml:id="lt-R1">
					\text{R}_{1}:\quad
					\amp \lap{f'(t)} = sF(s) - f(0),
					\amp\amp s \gt 0
				</mrow>
				<mrow xml:id="lt-R2">
					\text{R}_{2}:\quad
					\amp \lap{f''(t)} = s^2F(s) - sf(0) - f'(0),
					\amp\amp s \gt 0
				</mrow>
				<mrow xml:id="lt-R3">
					\text{R}_{3}:\quad
					\amp \lap{f'''(t)} = s^3F(s) - s^2f(0) - sf'(0) - f''(0),
					\amp\amp s \gt 0
				</mrow>
			</md>
		</p>

		<p>
			where <m>F(s) = \lap{f(t)}</m>.
		</p>

	<subsection label="lt-exp-shifting-property">
		<title> Multiplication by <m>e^{at}</m> (Exponential Shifting) </title>

		<p>
			It turns out that as long as we know the Laplace transform of <m>f(t)</m>,
			<me>F(s) = \lap{f(t)}</me>,
			then we also know the Laplace transform of <m>e^{at}f(t)</m> since
		</p>

		<me xml:id="lt-R4">
			\text{R}_{4}:\quad\lap{e^{at} f(t)} = F(s-a), \quad a \text{ is a constant}
		</me>.

		<p>
			So multiplying a function by <m>e^{at}</m> shifts its Laplace transform horizontally by <m>a</m>. 
		</p>

		<example label="lt-exp-shifting-property-example">
			<title> Laplace Transform of an Exponential Function </title>

			<p>
				Use the exponential shifting property to compute the following
			</p>

			<p>
				<sidebyside widths="25% 73%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
					<p>
						<statement>
							<p><m>\ds\lap{e^{7t}\cos(3t)}</m></p>
						</statement>
					</p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								To apply the <xref ref="lt-R4" text="custom">shifting property</xref>, we identify <m>a=7</m> and <m>f(t)</m> as follows:
								<me>
									\laplacesym \Big\{ \us{\large e^{at}}{\ul{e^{7t}\vphantom{()}}}\ \us{\large f(t)}{\ul{\cos(3t)}} \Big\} = F(s-7), \quad s \gt 7
								</me>
							</p>

							<p>
								By definition, <m>F(s) = \lap{ f(t) }</m>, so 
								<me>
									F(s) = \lap{ \cos(3t) } = \frac{s}{s^2 + 9}
								</me>.
							</p>
							<p>
								Finally, substituting <m>s-7</m> for <m>s</m> in the formula for <m>F(s)</m>, we have
								<me>
									\lap{ e^{7t} \cos(3t) } = F(s-7) = \frac{s-7}{(s-7)^2 + 9}, \quad s \gt 7
								</me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>
			</p>

			<p>
				<sidebyside widths="25% 73%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
					<p>
						<statement>
							<m>\ds\lap{e^{-1.3t}\sin(\pi t)}</m>
						</statement>
					</p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								In this case, <m>a=-1.3</m> and <m>f(t)=\sin(\pi t)</m>. For <m>s \gt -1.3</m>, we have
								<me>
									\laplacesym \Big\{ \us{\large e^{at}}{\ul{e^{-1.3t}\vphantom{()}}}\ \us{\large f(t)}{\ul{\sin(\pi t)}} \Big\} = F(s-(-1.3)) = F(s+1.3) 
								</me>.
							</p>

							<p>
								By definition, <m>F(s) = \lap{ f(t) }</m>, so 
								<me>
									F(s) = \lap{ \sin(\pi t) } = \frac{\pi}{s^2 + \pi^2}
								</me>.
							</p>

							<p>
								Substituting <m>s+1.3</m> for <m>s</m> in <m>F(s)</m>, we have
								<me>
									\lap{ e^{-1.3t} \sin(\pi t) } = F(s+1.3) = \frac{\pi}{(s+1.3)^2 + \pi^2}
								</me>,
								where <m>s \gt -1.3</m>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>
			</p>

			<p>
				<sidebyside widths="25% 73%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
					<p>
						<statement>
							<m>\ds\lap{e^{2t}t^3}</m>
						</statement>
					</p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								In this case, <m>a=2</m> and <m>f(t)=t^3</m>. So
								<me>
									\laplacesym \Big\{ \us{\large e^{at}}{\ul{e^{2t}\vphantom{()}}}\ \us{\large f(t)}{\ul{t^3\vphantom{()}}} \Big\} = F(s-2), \quad s \gt 2
								</me>.
							</p>

							<p>
								By definition, <m>F(s) = \lap{ f(t) }</m>, so 
								<me>
									F(s) = \lap{ t^3 } = \frac{3!}{s^4} = \frac{6}{s^4}
								</me>.
							</p>

							<p>
								Substituting <m>s-2</m> for <m>s</m> in <m>F(s)</m> gives
								<me>
									\lap{ e^{2t} \sin(\pi t) } = F(s-2) = \frac{6}{(s-2)^4}, \quad s \gt 2
								</me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>
			</p>
		</example>

		<p>
			By applying a similar strategy to the functions <m>t^n</m>, <m>\cos(bt)</m>, and <m>\sin(bt)</m>, we can add the following three Laplace transforms to our list of common transforms:
		</p>

		<md label="lt-exp-shifting-property-P6-P8">
			<mrow xml:id="lt-L6">
				\text{L}_{6}:\quad\amp\lap{e^{at}\cos(bt)} = \frac{s-a}{(s-a)^2 + b^2}, \quad s \gt a
			</mrow>

			<mrow xml:id="lt-L7">
				\text{L}_{7}:\quad\amp\lap{e^{at}\sin(bt)} = \frac{b}{(s-a)^2 + b^2}, \quad s \gt a
			</mrow>

	<subsection label="lt-laplace-derivatives">
		<title> Multiplication by <m>t^n</m> (Derivatives in <m>s</m>) </title>

		<p>
			When a function is multiplied by <m>t^n</m>, its Laplace transform becomes the <m>n</m>-th derivative of the original transform (up to a sign):
			<me>
				\lap{t^n f(t)} = (-1)^n \frac{d^n}{ds^n}\Big[\lap{f(t)}\Big]
			</me>.
			<proof xml:id="lt-laplace-derivatives-details">
				<title> Click here for a derivation </title>
				<statement>
					<p>
						To show that
						<me>
							\lap{t^n f(t)} = (-1)^n \frac{d^n}{ds^n}\left[\lap{f(t)}\right]
						</me>,
						we start on the right side and show that it leads to the left side.
					</p>

					<p>
						<md>
							<mrow>
								(-1)^n \frac{d^n}{ds^n}\left[\lap{f(t)}\right]
								\amp = (-1)^n \frac{d^n}{ds^n}\left[\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} f(t)\ dt\right]
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\amp = (-1)^n \int_0^{\infty} \frac{\partial^n}{\partial s^n}\left[e^{-st} f(t)\right]\ dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\amp = (-1)^n \int_0^{\infty} f(t)\frac{\partial^n}{\partial s^n}\left[e^{-st}\right]\ dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\amp = (-1)^n \int_0^{\infty} f(t) \left((-t)^n e^{-st}\right)\ dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\amp = (-1)^n \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} t^n f(t)\ dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\amp = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} t^n f(t)\ dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\amp = \lap{t^n f(t)}
							</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
				</statement>
			</proof>
		</p>

		<p>
			If <m>F(s) = \lap{ f(t) }</m>, then this property becomes
		</p>

		<me xml:id="lt-R5">
			\text{R}_{5}:\quad\lap{t^n f(t)} = (-1)^n \frac{d^n}{ds^n}\Big[F(s)\Big],\quad s &gt; 0,\quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots
		</me>

		<task label="lt-properties-chkpt-10">
			<title> üìñ‚ùì Apply the Laplace Derivative Rule </title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Hypothetically, if <m>\lap{f(t)} = \cos(2s)</m>, then <m>\lap{tf(t)} = </m> <fillin characters="10" />

							<md>

								<mrow>				\amp = -\frac{d}{ds}[\cos(2s)] </mrow>
								<mrow>				\amp = -(-2\sin(2s)) </mrow>

							</md>

		<example label="lt-laplace-derivatives-examples">
			<title> Examples of the Laplace Derivative Property </title>
			<p>
				Use the <xref ref="lt-R5" text="custom">Laplace derivative property</xref> to compute
			</p>

			<p>
				<sidebyside widths="25% 73%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
					<p>
						<statement>
							<m>\ds\lap{t \cos(t)}</m>
						</statement>
					</p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								To apply the <xref ref="lt-R5" text="custom">Laplace derivative property</xref>, we identify <m>n=1</m> and <m>f(t)</m>:
								<me>
									\laplacesym \Big\{ t^1 \ \us{\large f(t)}{\ul{\cos(t)}} \Big\} = (-1)^1 F'(s) = -F'(s), \quad s \gt 0
								</me>
							</p>

							<p>
								By definition, <m>F(s) = \lap{ f(t) }</m>, so 
								<me>
									F(s) = \lap{ \cos(t) } = \frac{s}{s^2 + 1}
								</me>
								and the quotient rule gives us
								<me>
									F'(s) = \frac{(s^2 + 1)(1) - s(2s)}{(s^2 + 1)^2} = \frac{1 - s^2}{(s^2 + 1)^2}
								</me>
							</p>
							<p>
								Therefore,
								<me>
									\lap{t \cos(t)} = -\frac{1 - s^2}{(s^2 + 1)^2} = \frac{s^2 - 1}{(s^2 + 1)^2}, \quad s \gt 0
								</me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>
			</p>

			<p>
				<sidebyside widths="25% 73%" margins="2% 0%" valign="top">
					<p>
						<statement>
							<m>\ds\lap{t^2 \sin(-5t)}</m>
						</statement>
					</p>
					<p>
						<solution>
							<p>
								In this case, <m>n=2</m> and <m>f(t) = \sin(-5t)</m>, so 
								<me>
									\laplacesym \Big\{ t^2 \ \us{\large f(t)}{\ul{\sin(-5t)}} \Big\} = (-1)^2 F''(s) = F''(s), \quad s \gt 0
								</me>
							</p>

							<p>
								By definition, <m>F(s) = \lap{ f(t) }</m>, so 
								<me>
									F(s) = \lap{ \sin(-5t) } = \frac{-5}{s^2 + 25} = -5(s^2 + 25)^{-1}
								</me>
								and two derivatives of this function gives us
								<md>
									<mrow>	F'(s)	\amp = \frac{d}{ds}\left[-5(s^2 + 25)^{-1}\right]				</mrow>
									<mrow>			\amp = -5\left(-1\right)(s^2 + 25)^{-2}(2s)						</mrow>
									<mrow>			\amp = 10s(s^2 + 25)^{-2}										</mrow>
									<mrow>	F''(s)	\amp = \frac{d}{ds}\left[10s(s^2 + 25)^{-2}\right]				</mrow>
									<mrow>			\amp = 10(s^2 + 25)^{-2} + 10s(-2)(s^2 + 25)^{-3}(2s)			</mrow>
									<mrow>			\amp = \frac{10}{(s^2 + 25)^{2}} - \frac{40s^2}{(s^2 + 25)^{3}}	</mrow>
								</md>
							</p>
							<p>
								Therefore,
								<me>
									\lap{t^2 \sin(-5t)} = \frac{10}{(s^2 + 25)^{2}} - \frac{40s^2}{(s^2 + 25)^{3}}, \quad s \gt 0
								</me>.
							</p>
						</solution>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>
			</p>
		</example>

		<p>
			This property lets us handle polynomial factors in <m>t</m> without integrating directly. Instead, we differentiate the Laplace transform with respect to <m>s</m>.
		</p>
	</subsection>

	<objectives label="lt-properties-wrapup">
		<ul>
			<li>Linearity allows us to separate sums and pull out constants.</li>
			<li>Multiplying by <m>e^{at}</m> shifts the <m>s</m> variable in a Laplace transform by <m>a</m>.</li>
			<li>A derivative in <m>t</m> becomes multiplication by <m>s</m> in the transform (minus an initial condition).</li>
			<li>Multiplying by <m>t^n</m> corresponds to differentiating the transform <m>n</m> times with respect to <m>s</m>.</li>
		</ul>
	</objectives>

						</row>
						<row>
							<cell>R<m>_{1}</m></cell>
							<cell><me>f'(t)</me></cell>
							<cell><me>sF(s) - f(0)</me></cell>
							<cell><me>s &gt; 0</me></cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell>R<m>_{2}</m></cell>
							<cell><me>f''(t)</me></cell>
							<cell><me>s^2F(s) - sf(0) - f'(0)</me></cell>
							<cell><me>s &gt; 0</me></cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell>R<m>_{3}</m></cell>
							<cell><me>f'''(t)</me></cell>
							<cell><me>s^3F(s) - s^2f(0) - sf'(0) - f''(0)</me></cell>
							<cell><me>s &gt; 0</me></cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell>R<m>_{4}</m></cell>
							<cell><me>e^{at} f(t)</me></cell>
							<cell><me>F(s-a)</me></cell>
							<cell><me>s &gt; 0</me></cell>
						</row>
						<row bottom="major">
							<cell>R<m>_{5}</m></cell>
							<cell><me>t^n f(t)</me></cell>
							<cell><me>(-1)^n \frac{d^n}{ds^n}\Big[F(s)\Big]</me></cell>
							<cell><me>s &gt; 0</me></cell>
						</row>
					</tabular>
				</table>
			</p>
		</sidebyside>
