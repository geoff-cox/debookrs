<overview>
  <p>The Laplace transform is a bridge between calculus and algebra that turns differential equations into algebraic equations. In this chapter, we build the Laplace transform from the ground up, exploring how integration by parts transfers derivatives, how exponentials make the process elegant, and how the definition evolves into a powerful set of tools for solving differential equations.</p>
</overview>

<objectives>
  <ul>
    <li>Understand how integration by parts underlies the Laplace transform.</li>

    <li>Build a toolkit of common transforms for constants, exponentials, powers of <m>t</m>, sine, and cosine.</li>
    <li>Use Laplace transform properties to combine and manipulate transforms efficiently.</li>

  </ul>
</objectives>

<core-narrative>

  <section label="lt-derivative-transfer">
    <title>Derivative Transfer via Integration by Parts</title>
    <overview>Shows how integration by parts moves derivatives from one function to another inside an integral, the core mechanism behind the Laplace transform.</overview>

    <examples>
      <example role="ibp-review">
        <title>Integration by Parts Refresher</title>
        <statement>Use integration by parts to transfer the derivative from <m>y'</m> to <m>e^{-st}</m>.</statement>
      </example>
      <example role="exp-derivative-transfer">
        <title>Using Exponentials for Derivative Transfer</title>
        <statement>Demonstrate why choosing <m>u(t)=e^{-st}</m> is advantageous for Laplace transforms.</statement>
      </example>
    </examples>

    <section-summary>
      <ul>
        <li>Integration by parts shifts derivatives within an integral.</li>
        <li>Exponentials, when used as one of the functions, produce simple factors of <m>s</m>.</li>
        <li>This mechanism explains the appearance of <m>s</m> in Laplace formulas.</li>
      </ul>
    </section-summary>
  </section>

  <section label="lt-definition">
    <title>Defining the Laplace Transform</title>
    <overview>Introduces the Laplace transform definition and demonstrates how to compute basic transforms from first principles.</overview>

    <examples>
      <example role="lt-def-constant">
        <title>Laplace of a Constant</title>
        <statement>Compute <m>\lap{5}</m> using the definition.</statement>
      </example>
      <example role="lt-def-linear">
        <title>Laplace of a Linear Function</title>
        <statement>Compute <m>\lap{t}</m> step by step using integration by parts.</statement>
      </example>
    </examples>

    <section-summary>
      <ul>
        <li>The Laplace transform converts a time-domain function <m>f(t)</m> into an <m>s</m>-domain function <m>F(s)</m> via <m>\lap{f(t)} = \int_0^\infty e^{-st} f(t) dt</m>.</li>
        <li>The integral is improper; you evaluate it by using limits as <m>b \to \infty</m>.</li>
        <li>Convergence depends on the decay of <m>e^{-st}</m> for suitable values of <m>s</m>.</li>
      </ul>
    </section-summary>
  </section>

  <section label="common-transforms">
    <title>Common Transforms</title>
    <overview>Builds a library of Laplace transforms for common functions, including constants, exponentials, powers of <m>t</m>, and sine/cosine.</overview>

    <examples>
      <example role="lt-exp-transform">
        <title>Laplace of an Exponential</title>
        <statement>Compute <m>\lap{e^{at}}</m> and generalize for any constant <m>a</m>.</statement>
      </example>
      <example role="lt-tpower-transform">
        <title>Laplace of Powers of t</title>
        <statement>Derive <m>\lap{t^n} = \frac{n!}{s^{n+1}}</m> recursively.</statement>
      </example>
      <example role="lt-sine-cosine">
        <title>Laplace of Sine and Cosine</title>
        <statement>Derive transforms for <m>\sin(bt)</m> and <m>\cos(bt)</m>.</statement>
      </example>
    </examples>

    <section-summary>
      <ul>
        <li>Key transforms: <m>\lap{1} = \frac{1}{s}</m>, <m>\lap{e^{at}} = \frac{1}{s-a}</m>, <m>\lap{t^n} = \frac{n!}{s^{n+1}}</m>, and transforms for <m>\sin(bt)</m> and <m>\cos(bt)</m>.</li>
        <li>These formulas eliminate the need to compute integrals from scratch every time.</li>
      </ul>
    </section-summary>
  </section>

  <section label="lt-properties">
    <title>Transform Properties and Rules</title>
    <overview>Explains how Laplace transforms interact with sums, constants, exponentials, derivatives, and powers of <m>t</m>.</overview>

    <examples>
      <example role="lt-linearity">
        <title>Linearity in Action</title>
        <statement>Apply the linearity property to compute <m>\lap{15 + 6e^{7t} - 11t}</m>.</statement>
      </example>
      <example role="lt-derivative-property">
        <title>Transform of a Derivative</title>
        <statement>Show that <m>\lap{f'(t)} = sF(s) - f(0)</m>.</statement>
      </example>
      <example role="lt-shifting">
        <title>Exponential Shifting</title>
        <statement>Use <m>\lap{e^{at}f(t)} = F(s-a)</m> to simplify a transform.</statement>
      </example>
      <example role="lt-tn-derivative">
        <title>Multiplying by t<sup>n</sup></title>
        <statement>Differentiate in <m>s</m> to compute <m>\lap{t^n f(t)}</m>.</statement>
      </example>
    </examples>

    <section-summary>
      <ul>
        <li>Linearity: <m>\lap{af + bg} = a\lap{f} + b\lap{g}</m>.</li>
        <li>Derivatives in <m>t</m> correspond to algebraic terms in <m>s</m> minus initial conditions.</li>
        <li>Multiplying by <m>e^{at}</m> shifts <m>s</m> to <m>s-a</m>.</li>
        <li>Multiplying by <m>t^n</m> means differentiating the transform with respect to <m>s</m>.</li>
      </ul>
    </section-summary>
  </section>
