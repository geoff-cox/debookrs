	<subsection><title> Forward Transforms </title>

		<introduction>

			<p>
				To begin our exploration of the Laplace Transform, we start with the first step in the <xref ref="lt-roadmap" text="custom">Laplace Transform Roadmap</xref>, known as the forward Laplace transform. This step is foundational, as it sets the stage for the subsequent transformations outlined in the roadmap.
			</p>
		
			<p>
				<figure xml:id="lt-roadmap-step1">
					<caption>The Forward Laplace Transform. Converting the <m>y(t)</m>-differential equation into the <m>Y(s)</m>-algebra equation.</caption>
					<image source="figures/LT-Diagram-Focused-Step-1.png" width="100%">
						<shortdescription>The Forward Laplace Transform</shortdescription>
					</image>
				</figure>
			</p>
		
			<p>
				Applying the forward Laplace transform to a differential equation involves converting both the left-hand side (LHS) and right-hand side (RHS) of the equation into their Laplace-transformed counterparts.
			</p>
		
			<p>
				This section investigates how individual functions, like <m>-40e^{3t}</m> in step 1, are transformed into expressions such as <m>-\frac{40}{s-3}</m>. In the next section, we will show where the <m>s^2Y + 3sY + 2Y + 9</m> comes from in the forward transform.
			</p>
			
		</introduction>
		
		<subsection><title> Motivation </title>

			<p>
				The power of the Laplace transform lies in its ability to simplify the process of solving differential equations by leveraging the properties of integration by parts and the behavior of exponential functions. To illustrate this, let's revisit the integration by parts formula:
				<me>
					\int u\ dv = uv - \int v\ du
				</me>.
				Here, if <m>u</m> and <m>v</m> are functions of <m>t</m>, then <m>dv=v'\, dt</m> and <m>du=u'\, dt</m>. The formula can be rewritten as:
				<me>
					\ub{\int u v'\, dt}_{\text{derivative on }v} = u v - \ub{\int v u'\, dt}_{\text{derivative on }u}
				</me>.
				Notice how the derivative initially applied to <m>v</m> on the left-hand side is transferred to <m>u</m> on the right-hand side. This property allows us to move derivatives from one function to another within an integral, a concept that is central to the Laplace transform.
			</p>

			<p>
				To see this in action, consider the integral
				<me>
					\int e^{-st} y' \, dt
				</me>.
				Choosing <m>u = e^{-st}</m> and <m>dv = y' \, dt</m> in the integration by parts formula gives us
				<me>
					\os{\quad (1)}{\int \us{u}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{e^{-st}\vphantom{y'}}}} \ \us{dv}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{y' dt}}}} =
					\os{(2)}{\us{u}{\vphantom{\int}\us{\uparrow}{\ul{e^{-st}\vphantom{y}}}} \ \us{v}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{y}}}} -
					\os{\quad (3)}{\int \us{v}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{y}}} \ \us{du}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{(-s e^{-st} \ dt \vphantom{y})}}}}
				</me>.
				Here, we see that the derivative associated with <m>y'</m>, in integral <m>(1)</m>, was transferred onto <m>e^{-st}</m> and neither <m>(2)</m> nor <m>(3)</m> contain <m>y'</m>.
			</p>

			<p>
				As you will see in the next section, this process of systematically transferring derivatives to exponential functions is at the heart of the Laplace transform. So, it should be no surprise when you encounter both an integral and the exponential function <m>e^{-st}</m> in its definition.
			</p>

			<xi:include href="./p4-c1-s1-interactives.ptx" />

		</subsection>

		<subsection><title> Definition </title>

			<p>
				In the previous section, we mentioned the importance of integration by parts and <m>e^{-st}</m> to the Laplace transform process. In fact, applying a Laplace transform to an equation is equivalent to multiplying both sides by <m>e^{-st}</m> and integrating with respect to <m>t</m>. For example, applying this to our model equation gives:
				<md>
					<mrow>
						e^{-st} \left( y'' + 3y' + 2y \right)
							=\amp\ e^{-st} \left( -40e^{3t} \right)
					</mrow>
					<mrow>
						\int e^{-st} \left( y'' + 3y' + 2y \right) \, dt
							=\amp\ \int e^{-st} \left( -40e^{3t} \right) \, dt
					</mrow>
					<mrow>
						\ub{\int e^{-st} {\color{blue} y''} \, dt}_{\text{transform of } {\color{blue} y''}} +
						3\ub{\int e^{-st} {\color{blue} y'} \, dt}_{\text{transform of } {\color{blue} y'}} +
						2\ub{\int e^{-st} {\color{blue} y} \, dt}_{\text{transform of } {\color{blue} y}}
							=\amp\ \ub{\int e^{-st} \left( {\color{blue} -40e^{3t}} \right) \, dt}_{\text{transform of } {\color{blue} -40e^{3t}}}
					</mrow>
				</md>.
				We formalize this with the following definition.
			</p>

			<definition><title> Laplace Transform </title>
				<statement>
					The <term>Laplace Transform</term> of a given function <m>f(t)</m> is defined as

					<me> \lap{ {\color{blue} f(t)} } = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} {\color{blue} f(t)}\ dt,</me>

					provided that the integral exists (i.e., the improper integral converges).
				</statement>
			</definition>

			<p>
				This definition might seem intimidating at first, but let's break it down. The <m>\infty</m> in the upper limit indicates that the Laplace transform is an improper integral. In calculus, we learn that such an integral can be rewritten as a limit. Specifically,
				<me>
					\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} f(t)\, dt = \ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} \ob{\int_0^b e^{-st} f(t)\, dt}^{\text{step 1: compute the integral}}}_{\text{step 2: compute the limit}}.
				</me>
				Also, note that the integral involves two variables, <m>s</m> and <m>t</m>, but we integrate with respect to <m>t</m>. If you haven't encountered this before, simply treat <m>s</m> as a constant during the integration. Let's dive into an example to clarify this further.
			</p>

			<example>

				<m>\ \ </m>Compute <m>\ds \lap{1}</m>.

				<solution>
					<p>
						By the definition above, we have the improper integral
						<men xml:id="lt-1-example-eqn-1">
							\lap{1} = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \cdot 1\ dt = \lim_{b \to \infty} \ub{\int_0^b e^{-st}dt}_{I}
						</men>.
						To compute <m>I</m>, treat <m>s</m> as a constant and integrate with respect to <m>t</m>.
						<md>

							<mrow>				
								\int_0^b e^{-st}dt
									=\amp\ \int_0^b e^{-st}dt  \qquad (u = -st)	
							</mrow>
							<mrow>				
									=\amp\  -\frac{1}{s} \int_0^b e^{u} dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>				
									=\amp\  -\frac{1}{s} e^{u}\Big|_{t=0}^{t=b}
							</mrow>
							<mrow>			
									=\amp\ -\frac{1}{s}e^{-st}\Big|_{t=0}^{t=b}	
							</mrow>
							<mrow>			
									=\amp\ -\frac{1}{s}\left[ e^{-sb} - e^{-s\cdot 0} \right] = -\frac{1}{s}\left[ e^{-sb} - 1 \right] = I
							</mrow>
						</md>
						Now that we have <m>I</m>, we can plug it into the limit above.
						<md>
							<mrow>
								\lim_{b \to \infty} \int_0^b e^{-st}dt
									=\amp\ \lim_{b \to \infty} -\frac{1}{s}\left[ e^{-sb} - 1 \right]	
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									=\amp\ -\frac{1}{s} \lim_{b \to \infty} \Big[ e^{-sb} - 1 \Big]
										\qquad \leftarrow <xref text="custom" ref="reason-factor-out-s">why?</xref>
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									=\amp\ -\frac{1}{s} \Big[ \ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{-sb}}_{L} - \us{=\ 1}{\ub{\lim_{b \to \infty} 1}} \Big]
							</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>

					<p>
						Let's analyze the remaining limit, <m>L</m>, which depends on the value of <m>s</m>.
						<line>Note: the <m>\frac{1}{s}</m> term in front of the limit implies <m>s \ne 0</m>.</line>
						<ul>
							<li>
								<p>
									<line><m>s &lt; 0:\ </m> Since <m>\ds e^{-(\text{negative})b} = e^{(\text{positive})b} \to \infty</m> as <m>b \to \infty</m></line>
									<line><m>\hspace{4.0em} \implies\ </m> limit diverges (does not exist) when <m>s &lt; 0</m>.</line>
								</p>
							</li>
							<li>
								<p>
									<line><m>s &gt; 0:\ </m> Since <m>\ds e^{-(\text{positive})b} = e^{(\text{negative})b} \to 0</m> as <m>b \to \infty</m></line>
									<line><m>\hspace{4.0em} \implies\ </m> limit converges (exists) when <m>s &gt; 0</m>.</line>
								</p>
							</li>
						</ul>
						To visualize this, here is a <xref ref="compare-exp-graphs" text="custom">graph</xref> comparing <m>s=-1.2\ </m> versus <m>\, +1.2</m>.
					</p>

					<p>
						Therefore, when <m>s &gt; 0</m> the Laplace transform exists and evaluates to
						<me>\lap{1} = -\frac{1}{s} [0 - 1] = \frac{1}{s} \quad \text{for } s \gt 0.</me>
					</p>
				</solution>

			</example>

			<p>
				Now that you've seen an example, let's discuss a few important points:

				<ul marker="square">
					<li>
						<p>
							The integral, <m>I</m>, in <xref ref="lt-1-example-eqn-1"/> was initially a function of both <m>s</m> and <m>b</m>, but after evaluating the limit, it became a function of <m>s</m> alone. In general, every Laplace transform results in a function of <m>s</m>, and it's customary to denote the Laplace transform of a function <m>f(t)</m> as <m>F(s)</m>. For example,
							<me> \lap{f(t)} = F(s) \quad \text{or} \quad \lap{q(t)} = Q(s).</me>
						</p>
					</li>
					<li>
						<p>
							We also identified the values of <m>s</m> for which the integral converges. While these values are important, they generally do not affect the solution of the differential equations in practice. Nonetheless, we will continue to note them as we build our Laplace transform toolbox.
						</p>
					</li>
					<li>
						<p>
							The notation for the Laplace transform involves the symbol <m>\lap.</m> The curly braces that accompany it are not optional; they indicate precisely which function we are transforming.
						</p>
					</li>
				</ul>
			</p>

			<p>
				At this point, we've introduced our first Laplace transform. In the next section, we will build a collection of the most common tranforms used to solve differential equations.
			</p>

			<assemblage xml:id="lt-table-L1"><title> ✳️ Common Laplace Transform <m>L_1</m> </title>
				<p>
					<dl width="narrow">
						<li xml:id="L1"><title> <m>{\LARGE \vphantom{\int}}L_1</m> </title>
							<m>\ds \lap{ 1 } = \frac{1}{s}, \quad s &gt; 0 </m>
						</li>
					</dl>
				</p>
			</assemblage>

		<subsection><title> Common Transforms </title>

			<p>
				In this section, we will explore some of the most commonly encountered Laplace transforms. These functions frequently appear in differential equations, making their transforms especially valuable tools for solving such equations. Let's start with the Laplace transform of the exponential function, <m>e^{at}</m>, and gradually build up to more complex functions like power functions, sine, and cosine.
			</p>

			<paragraphs><title> Laplace Transform of the Exponential Function, <m>e^{at}</m> </title>
				
				The exponential function <m>e^{at}</m> is one of the most important functions in mathematics, especially in the context of differential equations. Let's explore how this works with a specific example.

			</paragraphs>

			<example>

				<m>\ \ </m> Compute <m> \lap{ e^{7t}} </m>.

				<solution>
					<p>
						We begin by applying the definition of the Laplace transform:
						<md>
							<mrow>
								\lap{ e^{7t} }
								=\amp\ \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \cdot e^{7t}\ dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								=\amp\ \lim_{b \to \infty}\int_0^b e^{(-s+7)t}\ dt
							</mrow>
						</md>
						For this improper integral to converge, the exponent <m>-s+7</m> must be negative, meaning:
						<me>
							-s+7 \lt 0 \hspace{0.5cm}\Rightarrow \hspace{0.5cm} -s \lt -7 \hspace{0.5cm}\Rightarrow \hspace{0.5cm} s \gt 7
						</me>.
						Thus, we proceed under the assumption that <m>s \gt 7</m>.
						<md>
							<mrow>
								\lap{ e^{7t} }
									=\amp\ \lim_{b \to \infty}\int_0^b e^{(-s+7)t}\, dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									=\amp\ \lim_{b \to \infty} \frac{1}{7-s} e^{(-s+7)t} \Bigg|_0^b
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									=\amp\ \frac{1}{-s+7} \left[ \lim_{b \to \infty} \left( e^{(-s+7)b} - e^{0} \right) \right]
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									=\amp\ \frac{1}{-s+7} \left[ \lim_{b \to \infty} e^{(-s+7)b} - 1 \right]
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									=\amp\ \frac{1}{-s+7} \left[ 0 - 1 \right] \qquad \Big(e^{(\text{negative})b} \to 0\Big)
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									=\amp\ -\frac{1}{-s+7} = \frac{1}{s-7}. \quad \text{for } s \gt 7
							</mrow>
						</md>
						Thus, the Laplace transform of <m>e^{7t}</m> is:
						<me>\ds \lap{e^{7t}} = \frac{1}{s - 7}, \quad s \gt 7.</me>
					</p>
				</solution>

			</example>

			<p>
				This result can be generalized for any constant <m>a</m>, giving us the Laplace transform of <m>e^{at}</m>. <xref ref="laplace-transform-of-exponential" text="custom">Here are the details.</xref>
			</p>

			<assemblage xml:id="lt-table-L2"><title> ✳️ Common Laplace Transform (Exponential) </title>
				<p>
					<dl width="narrow">
						<li xml:id="L2"><title> <m>L_2.</m> </title>
							<m>\ds \lap{ e^{at} } = \frac{1}{s - a}, \quad s \gt a, \quad <xref ref="laplace-transform-of-exponential" text="custom">\text{details}</xref></m>
						</li>
					</dl>
				</p>
			</assemblage>

			<xi:include href="./p4-c1-s3-1-interactives.ptx" />

			<paragraphs><title> Laplace Transform of the Power Function, <m>t^{n}</m> </title>

				The power function <m>t^n</m> appears frequently in differential equations, particularly in polynomial solutions. We've already encountered the case when <m>n = 0</m>, since <m>t^0 = 1</m>. Now, let's explore the transform for the first and second powers.

			</paragraphs>

			<example>

				<p>
					The Laplace transforms for <m>t</m> and <m>t^2</m> are as follows:
					<ul marker="square">
						<li>	
							<m> \ds \lap{t} 	= \frac{1}{s^2}, \quad s \gt 0 \quad </m> <xref ref="laplace-transform-of-t-power-1" text="custom">Details</xref>
						</li>
						<li>	
							<m> \ds \lap{t^2} = \frac{2}{s^3}, \quad s \gt 0 \quad </m> <xref ref="laplace-transform-of-t-power-2" text="custom">Details</xref>
						</li>
					</ul>
				</p>
			</example>

			<p>
				If you look in each solution, before computing <m>L = 0</m>, you'll notice a relationship between the Laplace transforms of powers that differ by one. Namely,
				<me>
					\lap{t} = \frac{1}{s}\lap{1} \quad \text{and} \quad \lap{t^2} = \frac{2}{s}\lap{t}
				</me>,
				and if you compute the Laplace transform of <m>t^3</m>, you'll find that
				<me>
					\lap{t^3} = \frac{3}{s}\lap{t^2}
				</me>.
				In general, this recursive pattern continues for any power <m>n</m> as
				<me>
					\lap{t^n} = \frac{n}{s}\lap{t^{n-1}}
				</me>.
				So if we wanted the Laplace transform of <m>t^4</m>, we could find it like so
				<md>
					<mrow>
						\lap{t^4}
							=\amp\ \frac{4}{s}\lap{t^3}
					</mrow>
					<mrow> 				
							=\amp\ \frac{4}{s}\left[\frac{3}{s}\lap{t^2}\right]
							= \frac{4\cdot 3}{s^2}\left[\frac{2}{s}\lap{t}\right]
							= \frac{4\cdot 3\cdot 2}{s^3}\cdot\frac{1}{s^2}
							= \ob{\frac{4\cdot 3\cdot 2\cdot 1}{s^5}}^{\text{factorial}}
					</mrow>
				</md>.
				This pattern is true for higher powers of <m>t</m>, leading to the next laplace transform rule which makes use of the <xref ref="factorial" text="title"/>.
			</p>

			<assemblage xml:id="lt-table-L3"><title> ✳️ Common Laplace Transform (Power) </title>
				<p>
					<dl width="narrow">
						<li xml:id="L3"><title> <m>{\LARGE \vphantom{\int}}L_3</m> </title>
							<m>\ds \lap{ t^n } = \frac{n!}{s^{n+1}}, \quad s >0, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots </m>
						</li>
					</dl>
				</p>
			</assemblage>

			<xi:include href="./p4-c1-s3-2-interactives.ptx" />

			<paragraphs><title> Laplace Transform of Sine and Cosine, <m>\sin(bt),\ \cos(bt)</m> </title>

				Finally, let's consider the Laplace transforms of the famous sine and cosine functions.

			</paragraphs>

			<example>

				<m>\ \ </m>Compute <m>\lap{\cos(3t)}</m>.
				
				<solution>
					<p>
						We start by applying the definition of the Laplace transform:
						<me>
							\lap{ \cos(3t)} = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \cdot \cos(3t)\ dt
						</me>.
						Rather than directly integrating, we will use a modified <xref ref="eulers-formula" text="title"></xref> to express cosine in terms of <m>e</m>
						<me>
							\cos(3t) = \frac12\left(e^{3it} + e^{-3it}\right).
						</me>
						Substituting this into the integral gives:
						<md>
							<mrow>
								\lap{ \cos(3t)}
								=\amp\ \frac12 \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \frac12\left(e^{3it} + e^{-3it}\right)\ dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								=\amp\ \frac12\left[\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st}\cdot e^{3it}\ dt + \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st}\cdot e^{-3it}\ dt\right]
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								=\amp\ \frac{1}{2} \left[\lap{e^{3it}} + \lap{e^{-3it}}\right]
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								=\amp\ \frac{1}{2} \left[\frac{1}{s - 3i} + \frac{1}{s + 3i}\right] \qquad (\text{by } <xref ref="L2" text="title"/>)
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								=\amp\ \frac{1}{2} \left[\frac{s + 3i + s - 3i}{(s - 3i)(s + 3i)}\right]
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								=\amp\ \frac{s}{s^2 + 9}.
							</mrow>
						</md>
						Therefore, the Laplace transform of <m>\cos(3t)</m> is:
						<me>
							\lap{\cos(3t)} = \frac{s}{s^2 + 9}.
						</me>
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>

			<example>

				<m>\ \ </m>Compute <m>\lap{\sin(-4t)}</m>.

				<solution>
					<p>
						As with cosine, we begin with the definition of the Laplace transform,
						<me>
							\lap{ \sin(-4t)} = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \cdot \sin(-4t)\ dt
						</me>
						and rewrite sine using Euler's formula,
						<me>
							\sin(-4t) = \frac{e^{-4it} - e^{4it}}{2i}.
						</me>
						Substituting this into the integral, we get:
						<md>
							<mrow>
								\lap{ \sin(-4t)}
								=\amp \frac{1}{2i} \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \left(e^{-4it} - e^{4it}\right)\, dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								=\amp \frac{1}{2i} \left[\int_0^{\infty} e^{-(s + 4i)t}\, dt - \int_0^{\infty} e^{-(s - 4i)t}\, dt\right]
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								=\amp \frac{1}{2i} \left[\lap{e^{-4it}} - \lap{e^{4it}}\right].
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								=\amp \frac{1}{2i} \left[\frac{1}{s + 4i} - \frac{1}{s - 4i}\right] \qquad (\text{by } <xref ref="L2" text="title"/>)
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								=\amp \frac{1}{2i} \left[\frac{s - 4i - (s + 4i)}{(s + 4i)(s - 4i)}\right]
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								=\amp \frac{4}{s^2 + 16}.
							</mrow>
						</md>
						Thus, the Laplace transform of <m>\sin(-4t)</m> is:
						<me>
							\lap{\sin(-4t)} = \frac{4}{s^2 + 16}.
						</me>
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>

			<p>
				A similar approach can be used to show the general formula for the Laplace transforms of sine and cosine are given as follows
			</p>

			<assemblage xml:id="lt-table-L4-L5"><title> ✳️ Common Laplace Transform (Sine, Cosine) </title>
				<p>
					<dl width="narrow">
						<li xml:id="L4"><title> <m>{\LARGE \vphantom{\int}}L_4</m> </title>
							<m>\ds \lap{\sin(bt)} = \frac{b}{s^2+b^2}, \quad s \gt 0 </m>
						</li>
						<li xml:id="L5"><title> <m>{\LARGE \vphantom{\int}}L_5</m> </title>
							<m>\ds \lap{\cos(bt)} = \frac{s}{s^2+b^2}, \quad s \gt 0 </m>
						</li>
					</dl>
				</p>
			</assemblage>

		<subsection><title> Properties of Laplace Transforms </title>

			<p>
				Now that we've explored individual Laplace transforms, its time to delve into some of the key properties that make the Laplace transform such a powerful tool for solving differential equations. These properties allow us to manipulate and combine transforms in ways that simplify complex problems.
			</p>

			<paragraphs><title> Linearity </title>

				<p>
					The first property we'll explore is linearity. This property is foundational because it allows us to break down and combine transforms easily. The linearity of the Laplace transform is similar to the linearity of integrals, where we can distribute the transform across addition and subtraction, and pull out constants.
				</p>

			</paragraphs>

			<example>
				<m>\ \ </m> Use the definition of the Laplace transform to compute:
				
				<me> \lap{15 + 6e^{7t} - 11t}. </me>
				
				<solution>
					<p>
						Let's start by applying the definition of the Laplace transform:
						<md>
							<mrow>
								\lap{15\ +\amp\ 6e^{7t} - 11t}	
							</mrow>
							<mrow>		
									=\amp\ \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \cdot \left(15 + 6e^{7t} - 11t\right)\ dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									=\amp\ \int_0^{\infty} \left( 15e^{-st} + 6e^{-st} \cdot e^{7t} - 11e^{-st} \cdot t \right)\ dt
							</mrow>
						</md>
						Here, we've expanded the expression inside the integral. Notice that each term is something we've already encountered in previous sections. We can now use the linearity property to separate the integral:
						<md>
							<mrow>	
								=\amp\ 15\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st}\cdot 1\ dt + 6\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \cdot e^{7t}\ dt - 11\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} \cdot t\ dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								=\amp\ 15\lap{1} + 6\lap{ e^{7t} } - 11\lap{ t }.
							</mrow>
						</md>
						We already know the Laplace transforms of these individual functions:
						<md>
							<mrow>
								=\amp\ \ub{\frac{15}{s}}_{s \gt 0} + 6\ub{\frac{1}{s - 7}}_{s \gt 7} - 11\ub{\frac{1}{s^2}}_{s \gt 0}.
							</mrow>
						</md>
						To satisfy all the conditions on <m>s</m>, we must have <m>s \gt 7</m> since that ensures all terms are defined. Therefore:
						<me>
							\lap{15 + 6e^{7t} - 11t} = \frac{15}{s} + 6\cdot \frac{1}{s-7} - \frac{11}{s^2}, \quad s \gt 7.
						</me>
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>

			<p>
				This example demonstrates the linearity property in action. The general statement of the linearity property is as follows: if <m>a</m> and <m>b</m> are constants, then:
				<me> \lap{ a f(t) \pm b g(t) } = a \lap{ f(t) } \pm b \lap{ g(t) }, </me>
				or, equivalently:
				<me> \lap{ a f(t) \pm b g(t) } = a F(s) \pm b G(s). </me>
			</p>

			<assemblage xml:id="lt-properties-P1"><title> ✳️ Laplace Transform Property <m>P_1</m> </title>
				<p>
					<dl width="narrow">
						<li xml:id="P1"><title> <m>{\LARGE \vphantom{\int}}P_1</m> </title>
							<m>\ds \lap{ a f(t) \pm b g(t) } = a \lap{f(t)} \pm b \lap{g(t)}</m>
						</li>
					</dl>
				</p>
			</assemblage>

			<xi:include	href="./p4-c1-s4-1-interactives.ptx" />

			<paragraphs><title> Multiplication by <m>e^{at}</m> </title>

				<p>
					The translation property, also known as the first shifting theorem, allows us to handle functions multiplied by an exponential term, <m>e^{at}</m>. This property is particularly useful for simplifying the Laplace transforms of products of exponential functions and other functions, such as sine, cosine, or polynomials.
				</p>

			</paragraphs>

			<example>
				
				<m>\ \ </m> Compute <m> \lap{e^{7t}\cos(3t)} </m>.
				
				<solution>
					<p>
						By the definition of the Laplace transform, we have:
						<md>
							<mrow>
								\lap{e^{7t}\cos(3t)}
									=\amp \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} e^{7t}\cos(3t)\ dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									=\amp \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st+7t} \cos(3t)\ dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									=\amp \int_0^{\infty} e^{-(s-7)t} \cos(3t)\ dt
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									=\amp \ub{\int_0^{\infty} e^{-(s_0)t} \cos(3t)\ dt}_{\lap{\cos(3t)}}, \quad \text{where } s_0 = s-7
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									=\amp\ \frac{s_0}{s_0^2 + 9}  \qquad (s_0 \gt 0)
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									=\amp\ \frac{s-7}{(s-7)^2 + 9} \qquad (s-7 \gt 0 \text{ or } s \gt 7)
							</mrow>
						</md>
						Thus, the Laplace transform of <m>e^{7t}\cos(3t)</m> is:
						<me>\ds \lap{e^{7t}\cos(3t)} = \frac{s-7}{(s-7)^2 + 9}, \quad s \gt 7.</me>
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>

			<p>
				The translation property can be generalized for any function <m>f(t)</m> multiplied by <m>e^{at}</m>. The property is formally stated as:
				<me> \lap{ e^{at} f(t) } = F(s-a), </me>
				where <m>F(s)</m> is the Laplace transform of <m>f(t)</m>.
			</p>

			<assemblage xml:id="lt-properties-P2"><title> ✳️ Laplace Transform Property <m>P_2</m> </title>
				<p>
					Let <m>F(s) = \lap{f(t)}</m>.
				</p>

				<p>
					<dl width="narrow">
						<li xml:id="P2"><title> <m>P_2</m> </title>
							<m>\ds \lap{ e^{at} f(t) } = F(s-a), \quad a </m> is a constant.
						</li>
					</dl>
				</p>
			</assemblage>

			<p>
				By applying this property to the functions <m>t^n</m>, <m>\cos(bt)</m>, and <m>\sin(bt)</m>, we can derive additional common Laplace transforms:
			</p>

			<assemblage xml:id="lt-table-L6-L8"><title> ✳️ Common Laplace Transforms <m>L_6-L_8</m> </title>
				<p>
					<dl width="narrow">
						<li xml:id="L6"><title> <m>{\LARGE \vphantom{\int}}L_6</m> </title>
							<m>\lap{ t^n e^{at} } = \ds\frac{n!}{(s-a)^{n+1}}, \quad s >a </m>
						</li>
						<li xml:id="L7"><title> <m>{\LARGE \vphantom{\int}}L_7</m> </title>
							<m>\ds \lap{ e^{at}\sin(bt) } = \frac{b}{(s-a)^2+b^2}, \quad s >a </m>
						</li>
						<li xml:id="L8"><title> <m>{\LARGE \vphantom{\int}}L_8</m> </title>
							<m>\ds \lap{ e^{at}\cos(bt) } = \frac{s-a}{(s-a)^2+b^2}, \quad s >a</m>
						</li>
					</dl>
				</p>
			</assemblage>

			<xi:include	href="./p4-c1-s4-2-interactives.ptx" />

			<paragraphs><title> Derivative Transform </title>

				<p>
					The derivative property of the Laplace transform is one of its most powerful aspects, especially when solving differential equations. This property allows us to transform differential equations into algebraic equations, making them easier to solve.
				</p>

			</paragraphs>

			<example>
				<m>\ \ </m> Show that <m>\ds \lap{f'(t)} = s\lap{f(t)} - f(0), \quad s \gt 0</m>.

				<solution>
					<p>
				<tabular>
						<col width="20%" halign="right" valign="top"/>
						<col width="80%" halign="left" valign="top"/>
						<row>
							<cell valign="top"><p><m>\ds \lap{ f'(t) } =</m></p></cell>
							<cell>
								<m>\ds \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} f'(t)\ dt </m>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><xref ref="lt-derivative-key-step-1" text="title"/></cell>
							<cell>
								<m>\ds \left[ e^{-st} f(t) \right]_0^{\infty} - \int_0^{\infty} (-s e^{-st}) f(t)\ dt </m>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><xref ref="lt-derivative-key-step-2" text="title"/></cell>
							<cell>
								<m>\ds \left[ e^{-st} f(t) \right]_0^{\infty} + s \ub{\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} f(t)\ dt}{\large \lap{ f(t) }} </m>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><m>=</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<m>\ds \lim_{t \to \infty} \left( e^{-st} f(t) \right) - e^{-s \cdot 0} f(0) + s \lap{ f(t) } </m>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><xref ref="lt-derivative-key-step-3" text="title"/></cell>
							<cell>
								<m>\ds 0 - f(0) + s \lap{ f(t) } </m>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><m>=</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<m>\ds s \lap{ f(t) } - f(0) </m>
							</cell>
						</row>
					</tabular>
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>

			<p>
				This result is crucial for solving differential equations. Essentially, it allows us to eliminate derivatives from an equation, transforming the problem into an algebraic form that's easier to solve. But there's more—this property also works recursively, allowing us to handle higher-order derivatives as well.
			</p>

			<p>
				For example, let's see how this property extends to second and third derivatives:
				<md>
					<mrow>
						\lap{ f'(t) }	
							=\amp\ \us{\swarrow}{\ul{\color{green} s \lap{ f(t)	} - f(0)}}
					</mrow>
					<mrow>
						\lap{ f''(t) }	
							=\amp\ s {\color{green} \lap{ f'(t) }} - f'(0)
					</mrow>
					<mrow>
							=\amp\ s \Big[{\color{green} \lap{f(t)} - f(0)} \Big] - f'(0)
					</mrow>
					<mrow>
							=\amp\ \us{\swarrow \hspace{5em}}{\ul{\color{blue} s \lap{f(t)} - sf(0) - f'(0)}}
					</mrow>
					<mrow>
						\lap{ f'''(t) }	
							=\amp\ s {\color{blue} \lap{ f''(t) }} - f''(0)
					</mrow>
					<mrow>
							=\amp\ s \Big[{\color{blue} s \lap{f(t)} - sf(0) - f'(0)}\Big] - f''(0)
					</mrow>
					<mrow>
							=\amp\ s^3 \lap{f(t)} - s^2 f(0) - s f'(0) - f''(0)
					</mrow>
					<mrow> \vdots </mrow>
				</md>
				And the pattern continues for higher derivatives.
			</p>

			<assemblage xml:id="lt-properties-P3-P5"><title> ✳️ Laplace Transform Properties <m>P_3-P_5</m> </title>
				<p>
					Let <m>F(s) = \lap{f(t)}</m>.
				</p>

				<p>
					<dl width="narrow">
						<li xml:id="P3"><title> <m>P_3</m> </title>
							<m>\ds \lap{ f'(t) } = sF(s) - f(0) </m>
						</li>
						<li xml:id="P4"><title> <m>P_4</m> </title>
							<m>\ds \lap{ f''(t) } = s^2F(s) - sf(0) - f'(0) </m>
						</li>
						<li xml:id="P5"><title> <m>P_5</m> </title>
							<m>\ds \lap{ f'''(t) } = s^3F(s) - s^2f(0) - sf'(0) - f''(0) </m>
						</li>
					</dl>
				</p>

			</assemblage>

			<xi:include	href="./p4-c1-s4-3-interactives.ptx" />

			<paragraphs><title> Multiplication by <m>t^n</m> </title>

				<p>
					The final property we'll explore is how the Laplace transform handles functions multiplied by a power of <m>t</m>. This property is especially useful when dealing with polynomial terms in differential equations.
				</p>

			</paragraphs>

			<example>

				<m>\ \ </m> Show that <m>\ds \lap{tf(t)} = -\frac{d}{ds}\left[\lap{f(t)}\right]</m>.
				
				<solution>
					<p>
						For this problem, it's easier to start on the right side and show that it equals the left:
					</p>
					<tabular>
						<col width="30%" halign="right" valign="top"/>
						<col width="60%" halign="left" valign="top"/>
						<row>
							<cell valign="top"><p><m>\ds -\frac{d}{ds}\left[\lap{f(t)}\right] =</m></p></cell>
							<cell>
								<m>\ds -\frac{d}{ds}\left[\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} f(t)\ dt\right] </m>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><m>=</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<m>\ds -\int_0^{\infty} \frac{\partial}{\partial s}\left[e^{-st} f(t)\right]\ dt </m>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><m>=</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<m>\ds -\int_0^{\infty} f(t)\frac{\partial}{\partial s}\left[e^{-st}\right]\ dt </m>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><m>=</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<m>\ds -\int_0^{\infty} f(t) \left(-te^{-st}\right)\ dt </m>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><m>=</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<m>\ds \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} tf(t)\ dt </m>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><m>=</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<m>\ds \lap{tf(t)} </m>
							</cell>
						</row>
					</tabular>
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>

			<p>
				This shows that multiplying a function by <m>t</m> inside a Laplace transform is equivalent to taking the derivative of the Laplace transform of the same function, multiplied by <m>-1</m>. It turns out that each additional power of <m>t</m> adds another negative sign and derivative.
			</p>

			<example>

				<m>\ \ </m> Show that <m>\ds \lap{t^2f(t)} = \frac{d^2}{ds^2}\left[\lap{f(t)}\right]</m>.
				
				<solution>
					<p>
						Again, it's easier to start on the right side and work our way to the left:
					</p>

					<tabular>
						<col width="30%" halign="right" valign="top"/>
						<col width="60%" halign="left" valign="top"/>
						<row>
							<cell valign="top"><p><m>\ds \frac{d^2}{ds^2}\left[\lap{f(t)}\right] =</m></p></cell>
							<cell>
								<m>\ds \frac{d^2}{ds^2}\left[\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} f(t)\ dt\right] </m>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><m>=</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<m>\ds \int_0^{\infty} \frac{\partial^2}{\partial s^2}\left[e^{-st} f(t)\right]\ dt </m>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><m>=</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<m>\ds \int_0^{\infty} f(t)\frac{\partial^2}{\partial s^2}\left[e^{-st}\right]\ dt </m>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><m>=</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<m>\ds \int_0^{\infty} f(t) \left((-t)^2e^{-st}\right)\ dt </m>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><m>=</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<m>\ds \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st} t^2f(t)\ dt </m>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><m>=</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<m>\ds \lap{t^2f(t)} </m>
							</cell>
						</row>
					</tabular>
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>

			<p>
				A similar process shows that for any power of <m>t</m>, the Laplace transform is the <m>n</m>-th derivative of the Laplace transform of the function, with the sign alternating. The general property is given by:
				<me> \lap{t^n f(t)} = (-1)^n \frac{d^{(n)}}{ds^{(n)}}F(s), \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots </me>.
				The only difference is that you are taking the <m>n</m>-th derivative of <m>e^{-st}f(t)</m> inside the integral.
			</p>

			<assemblage xml:id="lt-properties-P6"><title> ✳️ Laplace Transform Property <m>P_6</m> </title>
				<p>
					Let <m>F(s) = \lap{f(t)}</m>.
				</p>

				<p>
					<dl width="narrow">
						<li xml:id="P6"><title> <m>P_6</m> </title>
							<m>\ds \lap{ t^n f(t) } = (-1)^n \frac{d^{(n)}}{ds^{(n)}}F(s), \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots </m>
						</li>
					</dl>
				</p>
			</assemblage>

			<xi:include	href="./p4-c1-s4-4-interactives.ptx" />

		</subsection>

		<subsection><title> Transforming Equations </title>
				
			<p>
				Throughout this section, we learned how to apply Laplace transforms to individual functions. However, our goal is to solve differential equations, which means we need to understand how to transform an entire differential equation, including its initial conditions. This brings us back to the first step in the Laplace Transform Method roadmap.
			</p>

			<p>
				<image source="figures/LT-Diagram-Focused-Step-1.png" width="100%">
					<shortdescription>Step 1: The Forward Laplace Transform</shortdescription>
				</image>
			</p>

			<p>
				Our goal here is to apply the Laplace transform to both sides of this equation using the common transforms and properties we've discussed. Recall that applying a Laplace transform to a function can be thought of as multiplying the function by <m>e^{-st}</m> and integrating with respect to <m>t</m>. However, instead of explicitly writing out this process for every term, we simply apply the Laplace operator, <m>\laplacesym</m>, to both sides of the equation. Let's break this down with an example.
			</p>

			<example>

				<m>\ \ </m>Apply the Laplace Transform to the initial-value problem
				<me>
					y'' + 3y' + 2y = -40e^{3t}, \quad y(0) = 0, \quad y'(0) = 1,
				</me>
				
				<solution>
					<p>
						First, apply <m>\laplacesym</m> to both sides of the equation
						<me>
							\lap{y'' + 3y' + 2y} = \lap{-40e^{3t}}
						</me>,
						and use the <xref ref="P1" text="custom"> linearity property, <m>P_1</m> </xref>
						<me>
							\ub{\lap{y''}}_{(1)} + 3\ub{\lap{y'}}_{(2)} + 2\ub{\lap{y}}_{(3)} = -40\ub{\lap{e^{3t}}}_{(4)}
						</me>,
						to split this into four separate transforms. For this example, we will look at each one individually.
						<md>
							<mrow>
								(1)\ :\quad \amp \lap{y''} = s^2Y - s\os{\large 0}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y(0)}} - \os{\large 1}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y'(0)}} = s^2 Y - 1 \vphantom{\frac{1}{1}} \quad <xref ref="P4" text="title"/>
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								(2)\ :\quad \amp \lap{y'} = sY - y(0) = sY \vphantom{\frac{1}{1}} \quad <xref ref="P3" text="title"/>
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								(3)\ :\quad \amp \lap{y} = Y \vphantom{\frac{1}{1}}
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								(4)\ :\quad \amp \lap{e^{3t}} = \frac{1}{s - 3} \quad <xref ref="L2" text="title"/>
							</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>

					<p>
						Plugging <m>(1) - (4)</m> back into the equation, we get
						<md>
							<mrow>
								s^2Y - 1 + 3sY + 2Y =\amp\ \frac{-40}{s - 3}, \quad \text{or}
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								s^2Y + 3sY + 2Y - 1 =\amp\ \frac{-40}{s - 3}
							</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>

			<p>
				To further illustrate this process, consider one more example.
			</p>

			<example>

				<m>\ \ </m>Apply the Laplace Transform to the initial-value problem
				<me>
					y'' - 4y' + 6y = t^2, \quad y(0) = 2, \quad y'(0) = 0
				</me>.
				
				<solution>
					<p>
						Applying the Laplace transform to both sides gives:
						<md>
							<mrow>
								\lap{y'' - 4y' + 6y} =\amp\ \lap{t^2}
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								\lap{y''} - 4\lap{y'} + 6\lap{y} =\amp\ \lap{t^2}
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								s^2Y - s\os{\large 2}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y(0)}} - \os{\large 0}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y'(0)}} - 4(sY - \os{\large 2}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y(0)}}) + 6Y =\amp\ \frac{2}{s^3}
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								s^2Y - 2s - 4sY + 8 + 6Y =\amp\ \frac{2}{s^3}	
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
								s^2Y - 4sY + 6Y - 2s + 8 =\amp\ \frac{2}{s^3}
							</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>

			<p>
				In both examples, the differential equations have been transformed into algebraic equations, where solving for <m>Y</m> will be the next step in the Laplace Transform Roadmap.
			</p>

			<xi:include href="./p4-c1-s5-interactives.ptx" />
		
		</subsection>
	
		<subsection><title> Summary &amp; Exercises </title>

			<introduction>

				<p>
					In this section, we introduced the concept of the forward Laplace transform and derived some common Laplace transforms that we will use throughtout this chapter. The following points summarize the essential concepts from the forward Laplace transform section:
				</p>

				<p>
					<assemblage><title> ✳️ Summary of the Key Ideas </title>
						<p>
							<ul>
								<li>
									<term>Differential <m>\to</m> Algebraic Equations</term>. The Laplace transform converts a differential equation into an algebraic equation, simplifying the solution process by eliminating derivatives.
								</li>
								<li>
									<term>Laplace Transform Concept</term>. Applying the Laplace transform to a differential equation involves transforming each term by multiplying by <m>e^{-st}</m> and integrating with respect to <m>t</m> from <m>0</m> to <m>\infty</m>, but is often simplified by directly applying the Laplace operator, <m>\laplacesym</m>.
								</li>
								<li>
									<term>Linearity Property</term>. The Laplace transform is linear, meaning it distributes across addition and subtraction, and allows for constants to be factored out. This property is essential for transforming complex equations.
								</li>
								<li>
									<term>Transforming Initial Conditions</term>. Initial conditions are incorporated directly into the Laplace-transformed equation, modifying the transformed terms to include initial values, making it easier to solve the resulting algebraic equation.
								</li>
								<li>
									<term>Common Function Transforms</term>. The Laplace transforms of common functions, such as exponentials, sines, cosines, and polynomials, are essential tools in transforming differential equations and are summarized in the provided table.
								</li>
								<li>
									<term>Transforming Derivatives</term>. The Laplace transform of a derivative, <m>y'(t)</m> or higher, transfers the derivative onto the Laplace variable <m>s</m>, reducing the order of the equation while introducing initial condition terms.
								</li>
								<li>
									<term>Multiplication by <m>e^{at}</m> and <m>t^n</m></term>. When multiplying a function by an exponential <m>e^{at}</m>, the Laplace transform shifts by <m>a</m> in the <m>s</m>-domain, and multiplying by <m>t^n</m> corresponds to differentiating the transform <m>n</m> times with respect to <m>s</m>, introducing a sign change.
								</li>
								<li>
									<term>Transforming the Entire Equation</term>. The process of applying the Laplace transform to an entire differential equation with initial conditions involves systematically transforming each term and leads to a simplified algebraic equation in the <m>s</m>-domain, ready for solving.
								</li>
							</ul>
						</p>
					</assemblage>
					
				</p>

				<p>
					The table below summarizes the common Laplace transforms that we derived in this section.
				</p>

				<assemblage xml:id="lt-table-L1-L8"><title> ✳️ Common Laplace Transforms <m>L_1-L_8</m> </title>
					<p>
						Let <m>a</m> and <m>b</m> be constant.
						<dl width="narrow">
							<li><title> <m>{\LARGE \vphantom{\int}}L_1</m> </title>
								<m>\ds \lap{ 1 } = \frac{1}{s}, \quad s &gt;0 </m>
							</li>
							<li><title> <m>{\LARGE \vphantom{\int}}L_2</m> </title>
								<m>\ds \lap{ e^{at} } = \frac{1}{s - a}, \quad s &gt;a </m>
							</li>
							<li><title> <m>{\LARGE \vphantom{\int}}L_3</m> </title>
								<m>\ds \lap{ t^n } = \frac{n!}{s^{n+1}}, \quad s &gt;0, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots </m>
							</li>
							<li><title> <m>{\LARGE \vphantom{\int}}L_4</m> </title>
								<m>\ds \lap{ \sin(bt) } = \frac{b}{s^2+b^2}, \quad s &gt;0</m>
							</li>
							<li><title> <m>{\LARGE \vphantom{\int}}L_5</m> </title>
								<m>\ds \lap{ \cos(bt) } = \frac{s}{s^2+b^2}, \quad s &gt;0 </m>
							</li>
							<li><title> <m>{\LARGE \vphantom{\int}}L_6</m> </title>
								<m>\lap{ t^n e^{at} } = \ds\frac{n!}{(s-a)^{n+1}}, \quad s &gt;a </m>
							</li>
							<li><title> <m>{\LARGE \vphantom{\int}}L_7.</m> </title>
								<m>\ds \lap{ e^{at}\sin(bt) } = \frac{b}{(s-a)^2+b^2}, \quad s &gt;a </m>
							</li>
							<li><title> <m>{\LARGE \vphantom{\int}}L_8.</m> </title>
								<m>\ds \lap{ e^{at}\cos(bt) } = \frac{s-a}{(s-a)^2+b^2}, \quad s &gt;a</m>
							</li>
						</dl>
					</p>
				</assemblage>

				<assemblage xml:id="lt-properties-P1-P6"><title> ✳️ Laplace Transform Property <m>P_1</m> </title>
					<p>
						Let <m>F(s) = \lap{f(t)}</m> and <m>G(s) = \lap{g(t)}</m>.
					</p>

					<p>
						<dl width="narrow">
							<li><title> <m>P_1</m> </title>
								<m>\ds \lap{ a f(t) \pm b g(t) } = a F(s) \pm b G(s), \quad a, b </m> are constants
							</li>
							<li><title> <m>P_2.</m> </title>
								<m>\ds \lap{ e^{at} f(t) } = F(s-a), \quad a </m> is a constant
							</li>
							<li><title> <m>P_3.</m> </title>
								<m>\ds \lap{ f'(t) } = sF(s) - f(0) </m>
							</li>
							<li><title> <m>P_4.</m> </title>
								<m>\ds \lap{ f''(t) } = s^2F(s) - sf(0) - f'(0) </m>
							</li>
							<li><title> <m>P_5.</m> </title>
								<m>\ds \lap{ f'''(t) } = s^3F(s) - s^2f(0) - sf'(0) - f''(0) </m>
							</li>
							<li><title> <m>{\LARGE \vphantom{\int}}P_6.</m> </title>
								<m>\ds \lap{ t^n f(t) } = (-1)^n \frac{d^{(n)}}{ds^{(n)}}F(s), \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots </m>
							</li>
						</dl>
					</p>
				</assemblage>

					<exercise>

						<statement>
							<m>\ds \lap{7t}</m>
						</statement>

						<solution>
							<p>
						In order to evaluate this integral, we will need to compute an integration by parts. When that time comes, we will choose <m> \ds u = t </m> an <m> \ds dv = e^{-st}. </m>  Then we take the derivative o <m> \ds u </m> to fin <m> \ds du </m> and the antiderivative o <m> \ds dv </m> to fin <m> \ds v </m> , as follows.
							<md>
								<mrow>  u	 \amp =  t 						\amp dv	=\amp\ e^{-st}dt </mrow>
								<mrow>  \Rightarrow du	 \amp =  dt\hspace{1cm}\mbox{}	\amp \Rightarrow v	=\amp\ -\frac{1}{s}e^{-st}  </mrow>
							</md>
								Note also that we will use L'Hospital's Rule when we have a limit of for <m> \ds \frac{\infty}{\infty}. </m>
							<md>
								<mrow>  H(s)	 \amp =  \lap{ h(t) } </mrow>
								<mrow>   \amp =  \int_0^{\infty}e^{-st}h(t) dt </mrow>
								<mrow>   \amp =  \int_0^{\infty}e^{-st}\cdot 7t dt </mrow>
								<mrow>   \amp =  \lim_{b \to \infty} \int_0^b e^{-st}\cdot 7t dt </mrow>
								<mrow>   \amp =   7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \int_0^b te^{-st} dt </mrow>
								<mrow>   \amp =   7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[t\cdot\left(-\frac{1}{s}e^{-st}\right)\Bigg|_0^b - \int_0^b -\frac{1}{s}e^{-st} dt\right] </mrow>
								<mrow>   \amp =   7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{t}{se^{st}}\Bigg|_0^b + \frac{1}{s}\int_0^b e^{-st} dt\right] </mrow>
								<mrow>   \amp =   7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{t}{se^{st}} + \frac{1}{s} \cdot -\frac{1}{s}e^{-st} \right]_0^b </mrow>
								<mrow>   \amp =   7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{t}{se^{st}} - \frac{1}{s^2 e^{st}} \right]_0^b </mrow>
							</md>
				
							<md>
								<mrow>   \amp =   7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\left( -\frac{b}{se^{sb}} - \frac{1}{s^2 e^{sb}} \right) - \left( -\frac{0}{se^{s\cdot 0}} - \frac{1}{s^2 e^{s\cdot 0}} \right) \right] </mrow>
								<mrow>   \amp =   7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\left( -\frac{b}{se^{sb}} - \frac{1}{s^2 e^{sb}} \right) - \left( 0 - \frac{1}{s^2} \right) \right] </mrow>
								<mrow>   \amp =   7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{b}{se^{sb}} - \frac{1}{s^2 e^{sb}}+ \frac{1}{s^2} \right] </mrow>
								<mrow>   \amp =   7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[-\frac{b}{se^{sb}} \right] - 7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\frac{1}{s^2 e^{sb}} \right] + 7\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\frac{1}{s^2} \right] </mrow>
								<mrow>   \amp =   -7\cdot \frac{1}{s} \cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\frac{b}{e^{sb}} \right] - 7\cdot \frac{1}{s^2}\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\frac{1}{e^{sb}} \right] + 7\cdot \frac{1}{s^2} </mrow>
								<mrow>   \amp =   -\frac{7}{s} \cdot  \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\frac{1}{se^{sb}} \right] - \frac{7}{s^2}\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\frac{1}{e^{sb}} \right] + \frac{7}{s^2} \mbox{ (using L'H Rule)} </mrow>
								<mrow>   \amp =   -\frac{7}{s^2} \cdot  \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\frac{1}{e^{sb}} \right] - \frac{7}{s^2}\cdot \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[\frac{1}{e^{sb}} \right] + \frac{7}{s^2} </mrow>
								<mrow>   \amp =   -\frac{7}{s^2} \cdot  0 - \frac{7}{s^2}\cdot 0 + \frac{7}{s^2} </mrow>
								<mrow>   \amp =   \frac{7}{s^2} </mrow>
							</md>
				
							</p>
						</solution>

						<answer>
							<p>
								<m> \ds \frac{7}{s^2} </m>
							</p>
						</answer>

								</md>

	<subsection><title> Backward Transforms </title>
		<introduction>
			<p>
				Let's review the <xref ref="lt-roadmap" text="custom">Laplace transform diagram</xref> to remember how we will leverage Laplace transforms to solve differential equations.
			</p>

			<p>
				The one last piece we need in order to solve DEs is to be able to take inverse Laplace transforms. We will focus on that piece now, and in the next section we will put all of our new skills together to solve DEs using Laplace transforms.
			</p>

			<p>
				<image source="figures/LT-Diagram-Focused-Step-4.png" width="100%">
					<shortdescription>(for accessibility)</shortdescription>
				</image>
			</p>
		</introduction>

		<subsection><title> Common Forms </title>	
			<p>
				The Laplace Transform Method begins with the <xref ref="lt-roadmap-step1" text="custom">forward transformation</xref> of a differential equation with an unknown function <m>y(t)</m> into an algebraic equation with an unknown function <m>Y(s)</m>. Once <m>Y(s)</m> is determined, the final step is to recover the original function, <m>y(t)</m>, by reversing the transformation using an inverse Laplace transform, denoted as <m>\laplacesym^{-1}</m>.
			</p>

			<figure xml:id="lt-roadmap-step4">
				<caption>
					The Backward Laplace Transform: Reversing the process to recover the solution, <m>y(t)</m>, from the transformed equation.
				</caption>
				<image source="figures/LT-Diagram-Focused-Step-4.png" width="100%">
					<shortdescription>The Backward Laplace Transform</shortdescription>
				</image>
			</figure>

			<p>
				The inverse Laplace transform, like any inverse operation, undoes the effect of the forward transform. For example, applying <m>\cos^{-1}</m> to both sides of
				<md>
					<mrow> \cos(\pi/4) = \sqrt{2}/2 \quad \implies\amp \quad \cos^{-1}(\cos(\pi/4)) = \cos^{-1}(\sqrt{2}/2) </mrow>
					<mrow>						  \quad \implies\amp \quad \pi/4 = \cos^{-1}(\sqrt{2}/2)				  </mrow>
				</md>
				reverses the cosine operation, bringing us back to the angle. Similarly, applying <m>\laplacesym^{-1}</m> to both sides of
				<md>
					<mrow> \lap{e^{-3t}} = \frac{1}{s+3}  \quad \implies\amp \quad \ilap{\lap{e^{-3t}}} = \ilap{\frac{1}{s+3}}  </mrow>
					<mrow>								  \quad \implies\amp \quad e^{-3t} = \ilap{\frac{1}{s+3}}				 </mrow>
				</md>,
				tells us that the inverse Laplace transform of <m>1/(s+3)</m> is <m>e^{-3t}</m>.
			</p>

			<p>
				<table halign="center" xml:id="common-lt-table"><title> <em>Common Laplace Transforms. <m>a, b</m> are constant, <m>n = 1, 2, \ldots</m></em> </title>
					<tabular halign="center">
						<row>
							<cell/>
							<cell><m>t</m>-functions</cell>

		<subsection><title> Matching Forms </title>
			<p>
				When we study Laplace Transforms, we will frequently want to manipulate an algebraic expression to make it match a given form.
			</p>

			<p>
				If you prefer to see a video of this example, <xref ref="video-matching_terms_01" text="custom"> click here </xref>
			</p>

			<p>
				Suppose we have the expression
				<me> \frac{1}{s^2 + 25}, </me>
				and we think it looks much like the form
				<me> \frac{b}{s^2 + b^2}. </me>
				It's not a perfect match, though. Based on the denominator, we might guess <m>b = 5.</m>
			</p>

			<p>
				Notice that this would make the denominator a perfect match,
				<me> \frac{1}{s^2 + 25} = \frac{1}{s^2 + 5^2}. </me>
				However, the numerator still isn't quite right. It would be correct if we would
				multiply the numerator by 5. But if we multiply the numerator by 5, we would change
				the expression. In order to NOT change the expression, we will instead multiply by
				<m>\frac{5}{5},</m> as follows:
				<md>
					<mrow>	\frac{1}{s^2 + 25}	=\amp\ \frac{1}{s^2 + 5^2}						</mrow>
					<mrow>						=\amp\ \frac{5}{5}\cdot \frac{1}{s^2 + 5^2}		</mrow>
					<mrow>						=\amp\ \frac{1}{5}\cdot \frac{5}{s^2 + 5^2}		</mrow>
					<mrow>						=\amp\ \frac{1}{5}\cdot \frac{b}{s^2 + b^2},
																	\text{ where } b=5			</mrow>
				</md>
				Notice that when we multiply by <m>\frac{5}{5}</m>, we are just multiplying by one, so we
				don't change the value of the expression.
			</p>
			
			<p>
				In the previous section, we explored how to directly compute backward Laplace transforms when the function of <m>s</m> matches a form in the <xref ref="common-lt-table" text="custom">common forward transform table</xref>. However, in practice, not all <m>s</m>-functions will align perfectly with these forms. In fact, the most challenging part of the Laplace Transform Method lies in preparing the <m>s</m>-function for the backward step. This process involves modifications of the function before matching the known forms in our table. In this discussion, we will cover some of the minor modifications that will need for the Laplace Transform Method.
			</p>

			<p>
				Many of the forms in <xref ref="common-lt-table"/> can be matched just by observing the denominator of the <m>s</m>-function. The following table outlines the connection between the denominator and it associated transform.
				<table halign="center" xml:id="backward-lt-rules-of-thumb"><title> <em>Matching Guide: <m>s</m>-function <m>\rightarrow</m> Inverse Transform</em> </title>

			<paragraphs><title> Missing Constants </title>

				<p>
					The most common adjustment needed to match one of the <m>s</m>-functions in <xref ref="common-lt-table"/> is to introduce a missing constant in the numerator. This is done by multiplying the numerator and denominator by this constant as shown in the following examples.
				</p>

				<example>

					<m>\ \ </m>Compute the inverse Laplace transforms for each.

					<tabular>
						<col width="20%" halign="center"/>
						<col width="75%"/>
						<row>
							<cell><m>\ds \frac{12}{s}</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<p>
									<m>\hspace{27em}</m>
									<solution>
										<p>
											The <m>s</m> in the denominator tells us that we need <m>L_1</m>. Before we do, let's factor out <m>12</m>:
											<me>\ds \ilap{\frac{12}{s}} = 12 \cdot \ilap{\frac{1}{s}} = 12 \cdot 1 = 12</me>.
										</p>
									</solution>
								</p>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><m>\ds \frac{5}{s^2 + 4}</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<p>
									<m>\hspace{27em}</m>
									<solution>
										<p>
											Since the denominator has the form, <m>s^2 + \text{number}</m>, and there is no <m>s</m> in the numerator, we should apply <m>L_4</m>. As before, it is helpful to first factor out the constant <m>5</m>,
											<me>\ds \ilap{\frac{5}{s^2 + 4}} = 5\ \ilap{\frac{1}{s^2 + 4}} \quad \leftarrow <xref ref="L4" text="custom"><m>L_4</m></xref> (b=2)</me>.
											According to <m>L_4</m>, we are missing <m>2</m> in the numerator. Let's put it there by multiplying by <m>2/2</m>, like so
											<md>
												<mrow> = 5\ \ilap{\frac{2}{2}\frac{1}{s^2 + 4}} =\amp\ 5\ \ilap{\frac{1}{2}\frac{2}{s^2 + 4}}</mrow>
												<mrow>										  =\amp\ \frac{5}{2} \ilap{\frac{2}{s^2 + 4}}</mrow>
												<mrow>										  =\amp\ \frac{5}{2} \sin(2t)</mrow>
											</md>
										</p>
									</solution>
								</p>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><m>\ds \frac{17}{s^4}</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<p>
									<m>\hspace{27em}</m>
									<solution>
										<p>
											This denominator has the form <m>s^{\text{power}}</m>, which matches <m>L_3</m> with <m>n = 3</m>.
											<me>\ds \ilap{\frac{17}{s^4}} = 17\ \ilap{\frac{1}{s^4}} \quad \leftarrow <xref ref="L3" text="custom"><m>L_3</m></xref> (n=3)</me>.
											In this case, the numerator is missing a <m>3!</m>. We can introduce it by multiplying by <m>3!/3!</m>, like so
											<md>
												<mrow> = 17\ \ilap{\frac{3!}{3!}\frac{1}{s^4}} = \frac{17}{3!} \ilap{\frac{3!}{s^4}} = \frac{17}{6} t^3</mrow>
											</md>
										</p>
									</solution>
								</p>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><m>\ds \frac{7s}{s^2 + 25}</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<p>
									<m>\hspace{27em}</m>
									<solution>
										<p>
											The denominator here is <m>s^2 + \text{number}</m>, which matches <m>L_5</m> with <m>b = 5</m>. Therefore,
											<md>
												<mrow> \ilap{\frac{7s}{s^2 + 25}}   =\amp\ 7\ \ilap{\frac{s}{s^2 + 25}} \quad \leftarrow <xref ref="L5" text="custom"><m>L_5</m></xref> (b=5)</mrow>
												<mrow>							  =\amp\ 7\ \cos(5t)</mrow>
											</md>.
										</p>
									</solution>
								</p>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><m>\ds \frac{10}{(s - 3)^2 + 11}</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<p>
									<m>\hspace{27em}</m>
									<solution>
										<p>
											The form of this denominator is <m>\ul{(s \pm \text{shift})^{2} + \text{number}}</m> and has no <m>s</m> in the numerator. This matches <m>L_7</m> with <m>a = 3</m> and <m>b = 5</m>. Therefore,
											<md>
												<mrow> \ilap{\frac{10}{(s - 3)^2 + 11}}</mrow>
												<mrow>	  =\amp\ 10\ \ub{\ilap{\frac{\sqrt{11}}{\sqrt{11}}\frac{1}{(s - 3)^2 + 11}}}_{\large <xref ref="L7" text="custom"><m>L_7</m></xref> (a=3,\ b=5)}</mrow>
												<mrow>	  =\amp\ \frac{10}{\sqrt{11}} \ilap{\frac{\sqrt{11}}{(s - 3)^2 + 11}}</mrow>
												<mrow>	  =\amp\ \frac{10}{\sqrt{11}} e^{3t} \sin(\sqrt{11}t)</mrow>
											</md>

										</p>
									</solution>
								</p>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><m>\ds \frac{2}{(s+7)^5}</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<p>
									<m>\hspace{27em}</m>
									<solution>
										<p>
											The denominator, <m>\ul{(s \pm \text{shift})^{\text{power}}}</m>, matches <m>L_6</m> with <m>a = -7</m> and <m>n = 4</m>. Therefore,
											<md>
												<mrow>
													\ilap{\frac{2}{(s+7)^5}}
														=\amp\ 2\ \ub{\ilap{\frac{4!}{4!}\frac{1}{(s+7)^5}}}_{\large <xref ref="L6" text="custom"><m>L_6</m></xref> (a=-7,\ n=4)}
												</mrow>
												<mrow>
														=\amp\ \frac{2}{4!} \ilap{\frac{4!}{(s+7)^5}}
												</mrow>
												<mrow>
													=\amp\ \frac{1}{12} t^4 e^{-7t}
											</mrow>
											</md>
										</p>
									</solution>
								</p>
							</cell>
						</row>
					</tabular>
				</example>

			</paragraphs>

			<paragraphs><title> Splitting a Fraction </title>

				<p>
					Another common modification is when the <m>s</m>-function contains a sum of different common forms. In these cases, you can usually split the fraction as the sum of these forms.
				</p>

				<example>

					<m>\ \ </m>Compute the inverse Laplace transforms for each.

					<tabular>
						<col width="20%" halign="center"/>
						<col width="75%"/>
						<row>
							<cell><m>\ds \frac{2s - 3}{s^2 + 16}</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<p>
									<m>\hspace{27em}</m>
									<solution>
										<p>
											The key to this problem is to split the fraction into two separate terms:
											<me>\ds \frac{2s - 3}{s^2 + 16} = \frac{2s}{s^2 + 16} - \frac{3}{s^2 + 16}.</me>
											Notice the first term matches with <m>L_5</m> and the second term matches with <m>L_4</m>, so
											<md>
												<mrow>  \amp\ \ilap{\frac{2s-3}{s^2 + 16}}											 </mrow>
												<mrow>  \amp \hspace{3em}= 2\ \ilap{\frac{s}{s^2 + 16}} - 3\ \ilap{\frac{1}{4}\frac{4}{s^2 + 16}}   </mrow>
												<mrow>  \amp \hspace{3em}= 2\ \cos(4t) - \frac{3}{4} \sin(2t)									   </mrow>
											</md>
										</p>
									</solution>
								</p>
							</cell>
						</row>
						<row>
							<cell><m>\ds \frac{s^3 + 2s - 7}{s^7}</m></cell>
							<cell>
								<p>
									<m>\hspace{27em}</m>
									<solution>
										<p>
											We can split the fraction into the terms,
											<me>\ds \frac{s^3 + 2s - 7}{s^7} = \frac{s^3}{s^7} + \frac{2s}{s^7} - \frac{7}{s^7} = \frac{1}{s^4} + \frac{2}{s^6} - \frac{7}{s^7}.</me>
											and apply the inverse Laplace transform to each
											<md>
												<mrow> \ilap{\frac{1}{s^4}} = \frac{1}{3!}\ilap{\frac{3!}{s^4}} = \frac{1}{3!}t^3</mrow>
												<mrow> \ilap{\frac{2}{s^6}} = \frac{2}{5!}\ilap{\frac{5!}{s^6}} = \frac{2}{5!}t^5</mrow>
												<mrow> \ilap{\frac{7}{s^7}} = \frac{7}{6!}\ilap{\frac{6!}{s^7}} = \frac{7}{6!}t^6</mrow>
											</md>
											Therefore, the inverse transform is
											<me>\ds \ilap{\frac{s^3 + 2s - 7}{s^7}} = \frac{1}{3!}t^3 + \frac{2}{5!}t^5 - \frac{7}{6!}t^6.</me>
										</p>
									</solution>
								</p>
							</cell>
						</row>
					</tabular>
				</example>

			</paragraphs>

			<p>
				This section explored two relatively minor modifications needed to match and find the backward Laplace transform. However, some <m>s</m>-functions require more complex techniques to reach the desired form. In the next section, we will discuss how completing the square can help us match the form where the <m>s</m>-variable is shifted.
			</p>

		<subsection><title> Finding Inverse Laplace Transforms by Completing the Square </title>
			<p>
				As you progress through the Laplace Transform Method, you will often encounter <xref ref="rational-function" text="custom">rational functions</xref> that do not immediately match any known form in the table of Laplace transforms. For example, the <m>s</m>-function
				<me>\frac{s-6}{s^2 + 5s + 1}</me>
				does not align with the forms in the <xref ref="common-lt-table" text="custom">common forward transform table</xref>. One common way to address this is by rewriting the denominator in the form <m>(s-a)^2 + b^2</m> to match known transforms like <m>L_7</m> or <m>L_8</m>. This technique, called completing the square, is essential in transforming quadratic expressions to forms that are easier to work with in inverse Laplace computations.
			</p>

			<p>
				This approach is appropriate when the roots of the quadratic expression in the denominator are complex. Recall from <xref ref="solving-the-quadratic-equation" text="custom">solving quadratic equations</xref>, if the discriminant
				<me>b^2 - 4ac</me>
				is negative, then the quadratic function has complex roots. Therefore, a quick compuation of this value provides a good signal that completing the square is the correct approach as the following examples demonstrate.
			</p>

			<example>
				<m>\ \ </m>Compute the inverse Laplace transforms of the following functions:
				<me>H(s) = \frac{s-1}{s^2 - 2s + 5}, \quad K(s) = \frac{11}{s^2 - 6s + 14}, \quad P(s) = \frac{s+3}{s^2 + 2s + 10}.</me>
				
				<solution><title> <m>\ilap{H(s)}</m> </title>
					<p>
						The discriminant of the denominator <m>s^2 - 2s + 5</m> is
						<me>b^2 - 4ac = (-2)^2 - 4(1)(5) = 4 - 20 = -16</me>,
						which is negative. Therefore, completing the square is necessary:
						<md>
							<mrow> s^2 - 2s + 5 = (s^2 - 2s) + 5	=\amp\ (\ob{s^2 - 2s + 1}^{(s - 1)^2} - 1) + 5   </mrow>
							<mrow>								  =\amp\ (s - 1)^2 + 4							 </mrow>
						</md>.
						Rewriting <m>H(s)</m> gives:
						<me>\frac{s-1}{s^2 - 2s + 5} = \frac{s-1}{(s - 1)^2 + 4},</me>
						which matches <xref ref="L8" text="title"/> (<m>a=1, b=2</m>). Therefore,
						<me>h(t) = \ilap{H(s)} = e^{t}\cos(2t).</me>
					</p>
				</solution>

				<solution><title> <m>\ilap{K(s)}</m> </title>
					<p>
						The discriminant of the denominator <m>s^2 - 6s + 14</m> is
						<me>b^2 - 4ac = (-6)^2 - 4(1)(14) = 36 - 56 = -20</me>,
						which is negative. Completing the square gives:
						<md>
							<mrow> s^2 - 6s + 14 = (s^2 - 6s) + 14  =\amp (\ob{s^2 - 6s + 9}^{(s - 3)^2} - 9) + 14  </mrow>
							<mrow>								  =\amp (s - 3)^2 + 5							 </mrow>
						</md>.
						Rewriting <m>K(s)</m> as:
						<me>
							\frac{11}{s^2 - 6s + 14}
								= \ub{\frac{11}{(s - 3)^2 + 5}}_{\large \text{Match with } L_7}
								= \frac{11}{\sqrt{5}}\ub{\frac{\sqrt{5}}{(s - 3)^2 + 5}}_{\large <xref ref="L7" text="custom"><m>L_7</m></xref> (a\,=\,3,\ b\,=\,\sqrt{5})}
						</me>.
						Therefore,
						<md>
							<mrow>
								k(t) = \ilap{K(s)}  =\amp\ \frac{11}{\sqrt{5}}e^{3t}\sin(\sqrt{5}t)
							</mrow>
						</md>.
					</p>
				</solution>

				<solution><title> <m>\ilap{P(s)}</m> </title>
					<p>
						Completing the square for the denominator of <m>P(s)</m> gives:
						<me>
							\frac{s+3}{s^2 + 2s + 10} = \frac{s+3}{(s + 1)^2 + 9}
						</me>,
						and leaves us in a tricky situation where the numerator is not <m>s+1</m>. This situation is addressed by rewriting <m>3</m> as <m>1 + 2</m> and grouping the <m>1</m> with the <m>s</m>. We then split the fraction. Here are all the details:
						<md>
							<mrow>
								\frac{s+3}{s^2 + 2s + 10}
									=\amp\ \frac{s + 1 + 2}{(s + 1)^2 + 9}
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									=\amp\ \frac{s+1}{(s + 1)^2 + 9} + \frac{2}{(s + 1)^2 + 9}
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									=\amp\ \ub{\frac{s+1}{(s + 1)^2 + 9}}_{\large <xref ref="L8" text="title"/>\ (a=-1, b=3)}
										+ \frac{2}{3}\ub{\frac{3}{(s + 1)^2 + 9}}_{\large <xref ref="L7" text="title"/>\ (a=-1, b=3)}
							</mrow>	
						</md>,
						Therefore,
						<md>
							<mrow>
								p(t) =\amp\ \ilap{P(s)}
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									=\amp\ \ilap{ \frac{s+1}{(s + 1)^2 + 9} } + \frac{2}{3}\ilap{ \frac{3}{(s + 1)^2 + 9} }
							</mrow>
							<mrow>
									=\amp\ e^{-t}\cos(3t) + \frac{2}{3}e^{-t}\sin(3t)
							</mrow>	
						</md>
					</p>
				</solution>
			</example>

			<p>
				Completing the square is a crucial technique when working with quadratic expressions in the denominator of Laplace transforms. It allows the backward transformations to be found by rewriting the <m>s</m>-function in a form that aligns with known inverse Laplace transforms. In the next subsection, we will explore another technique: partial fraction decomposition, which helps break down even more complex rational functions into simpler parts that can be easily matched to common inverse transforms.
			</p>

		<subsection><title> Finding Inverse Laplace Transforms Using Partial Fraction Decomposition </title>
			<p>
				When working with the Laplace Transform Method, you will sometimes encounter rational functions where the denominator is a polynomial that can be factored into simpler linear or quadratic factors. In such cases, partial fraction decomposition is a powerful technique that allows us to express the complex rational function as a sum of simpler fractions. These simpler fractions often correspond directly to known forms in the table of Laplace transforms, making it easier to apply the inverse transform.
			</p>

			<p>
				The key idea behind partial fraction decomposition is to break down the original rational function into a sum of fractions, each with a simpler denominator. For example, if the denominator can be factored as <m>(s - a)(s - b)</m>, we can express the rational function as a sum of fractions with these linear factors as denominators. The goal is to match each of these fractions with a form in the Laplace transform table, allowing us to compute the inverse transform.
			</p>

			<p>
				Let's explore this technique through a series of examples, starting with a quadratic denominator and gradually increasing the complexity.
			</p>

			<example>

				<m>\ \ </m>Find the inverse Laplace transforms of each function.
				<me>
					Y(s) = \frac{2s + 5}{(s + 1)(s + 4)}, \quad
					P(s) = \frac{2s + 3}{s^2 + 5s + 6}, \quad
					Q(s) = \frac{3s + 4}{s^2 - 2s + 1}
				</me>
			
				<solution>
					<p>
						First, we decompose <m>Y(s)</m> into partial fractions:
						<me>\frac{2s + 5}{(s + 1)(s + 4)} = \frac{A}{s + 1} + \frac{B}{s + 4}.</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						To find <m>A</m> and <m>B</m>, we multiply both sides by the denominator <m>(s + 1)(s + 4)</m> and solve for the coefficients:
						<me>
							2s + 5 = A(s + 4) + B(s + 1).
						</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						Expanding and collecting like-terms, we get:
						<me>
							2s + 5 = (A + B)s + (4A + B).
						</me>
						Comparing coefficients, we find:
						<me>A + B = 2, \quad 4A + B = 5.</me>
						Solving this system of equations, we obtain:
						<me>A = 1, \quad B = 1.</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						Therefore, the partial fraction decomposition of <m>Y(s)</m> is:
						<me>\frac{1}{s + 1} + \frac{1}{s + 4}.</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						Finally, using the table of Laplace transforms, we find the inverse Laplace transform of each term:
						<me>\ilap{Y(s)} = \ilap{\frac{1}{s + 1}} + \ilap{\frac{1}{s + 4}} = e^{-t} + e^{-4t}.</me>
					</p>
				</solution>

				<solution>
					<p>
						First, factor the quadratic denominator:
						<me>s^2 + 5s + 6 = (s + 2)(s + 3).</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						Next, decompose <m>P(s)</m> into partial fractions:
						<me>\frac{2s + 3}{(s + 2)(s + 3)} = \frac{A}{s + 2} + \frac{B}{s + 3}.</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						To find <m>A</m> and <m>B</m>, multiply both sides by the denominator <m>(s + 2)(s + 3)</m> and solve for the coefficients:
						<me>
							2s + 3 = A(s + 3) + B(s + 2).
						</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						Expanding and collecting like-terms, we get:
						<me>
							2s + 3 = (A + B)s + (3A + 2B).
						</me>
						Comparing coefficients, we find:
						<me>A + B = 2, \quad 3A + 2B = 3.</me>
						Solving this system of equations, we obtain:
						<me>A = 1, \quad B = 1.</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						Therefore, the partial fraction decomposition of <m>P(s)</m> is:
						<me>\frac{1}{s + 2} + \frac{1}{s + 3}.</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						Finally, using the table of Laplace transforms, we find the inverse Laplace transform of each term:
						<me>\ilap{P(s)} = e^{-2t} + e^{-3t}.</me>
					</p>
				</solution>

				<solution>
					<p>
						First, factor the quadratic denominator:
						<me>s^2 - 2s + 1 = (s - 1)^2.</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						Next, decompose <m>Q(s)</m> into partial fractions:
						<me>\frac{3s + 4}{(s - 1)^2} = \frac{A}{s - 1} + \frac{B}{(s - 1)^2}.</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						To find <m>A</m> and <m>B</m>, multiply both sides by the denominator <m>(s - 1)^2</m> and solve for the coefficients:
						<me>
							3s + 4 = A(s - 1) + B.
						</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						Expanding and collecting like-terms, we get:
						<me>
							3s + 4 = As - A + B.
						</me>
						Comparing coefficients, we find:
						<me>A = 3, \quad B - A = 4.</me>
						Solving this system, we find <m>A = 3</m> and <m>B = 7.</m>
					</p>

					<p>
						Therefore, the partial fraction decomposition of <m>Q(s)</m> is:
						<me>\frac{3}{s - 1} + \frac{7}{(s - 1)^2}.</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						Finally, using the table of Laplace transforms, we find the inverse Laplace transform of each term:
						<me>\ilap{Q(s)} = 3e^{t} + 7te^{t}.</me>
					</p>
				</solution>

			</example>

			<p>
				Partial fraction decompostion also works for functions with higher-degree polynomials in the denominator. The key is to factor each denominator into as many linear and/or quadratic factors as possible. Here are a few examples to illustrate this concept.
			</p>

			<example>
				
				<m>\ \ </m>Find the inverse Laplace transforms of each function.
				<me>
					R(s) = \frac{2s^2 + 5s + 1}{s^3 - s}, \quad
					S(s) = \frac{4s^3 + 3s^2 - 2s + 1}{s^4 - 1}
				</me>

				<solution>
					<p>
						First, factor the cubic denominator:
						<me>s^3 - s = s(s - 1)(s + 1).</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						Next, decompose <m>R(s)</m> into partial fractions:
						<me>\frac{2s^2 + 5s + 1}{s(s - 1)(s + 1)} = \frac{A}{s} + \frac{B}{s - 1} + \frac{C}{s + 1}.</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						To find <m>A</m>, <m>B</m>, and <m>C</m>, multiply both sides by the denominator <m>s(s - 1)(s + 1)</m> and solve for the coefficients:
						<me>
							2s^2 + 5s + 1 = A(s - 1)(s + 1) + Bs(s + 1) + Cs(s - 1).
						</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						Expanding and collecting like-terms, we get:
						<me>
							2s^2 + 5s + 1 = (A + B + C)s^2 + (B - C)s + A.
						</me>
						Comparing coefficients, we find:
						<me>A + B + C = 2, \quad B - C = 5, \quad A = 1.</me>
						Solving this system, we find <m>A = 1</m>, <m>B = 2</m>, and <m>C = 3.</m>
					</p>

					<p>
						Therefore, the partial fraction decomposition of <m>R(s)</m> is:
						<me>\frac{1}{s} + \frac{2}{s - 1} + \frac{3}{s + 1}.</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						Finally, using the table of Laplace transforms, we find the inverse Laplace transform of each term:
						<me>\ilap{R(s)} = 1 + 2e^{t} + 3e^{-t}.</me>
					</p>
				</solution>
			
				<solution>
					<p>
						First, factor the quartic denominator:
						<me>s^4 - 1 = (s^2 - 1)(s^2 + 1) = (s - 1)(s + 1)(s^2 + 1).</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						Next, decompose <m>S(s)</m> into partial fractions:
						<me>\frac{4s^3 + 3s^2 - 2s + 1}{(s - 1)(s + 1)(s^2 + 1)} = \frac{A}{s - 1} + \frac{B}{s + 1} + \frac{Cs + D}{s^2 + 1}.</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						To find <m>A</m>, <m>B</m>, <m>C</m>, and <m>D</m>, multiply both sides by the denominator <m>(s - 1)(s + 1)(s^2 + 1)</m> and solve for the coefficients:
						<me>
							4s^3 + 3s^2 - 2s + 1 = A(s + 1)(s^2 + 1) + B(s - 1)(s^2 + 1) + (Cs + D)(s - 1)(s + 1).
						</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						Expanding and collecting like-terms, we find <m>A</m>, <m>B</m>, <m>C</m>, and <m>D</m> by comparing coefficients. Solving the system of equations, we find:
						<me>A = 1, \quad B = -1, \quad C = 4, \quad D = 3.</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						Therefore, the partial fraction decomposition of <m>S(s)</m> is:
						<me>\frac{1}{s - 1} - \frac{1}{s + 1} + \frac{4s + 3}{s^2 + 1}.</me>
					</p>

					<p>
						Finally, using the table of Laplace transforms, we find the inverse Laplace transform of each term:
						<me>\ilap{S(s)} = e^{t} - e^{-t} + 4\cos(t) + 3\sin(t).</me>
					</p>
				</solution>

			</example>

			<p>
				Partial fraction decomposition is a powerful technique when working with rational functions in Laplace transforms. By breaking down complex expressions into simpler fractions, you can match them to known inverse Laplace transforms, making the backward transformation process much easier. In the next subsection, we will explore more advanced techniques for handling cases with repeated roots and higher-order polynomials.
			</p>

			<subsection><title> Summary &amp; Exercises </title>
			<introduction>
				<p>
					This section covers the final step in solving differential equations using the Laplace Transform Method: finding the inverse Laplace transform. The focus is on converting the algebraic equation with unknown function <m>Y(s)</m> back to the original function <m>y(t)</m>. Several methods and techniques are introduced to handle different cases where direct computation isn't possible.
				</p>
				
				<p>
					<assemblage><title> ✳️ Summary of the Key Ideas </title>
						<p>
								<term>Common Forms:</term> A table of common Laplace transforms is provided, which doubles as a reference for inverse transforms. The focus is on recognizing forms that match the table entries for functions like <m>\sin(bt), \cos(bt)</m>, and others.
							</p>

							<p>
								<term>Direct Computation:</term> When the function of <m>s</m> directly matches a form in the common Laplace transform table, the inverse Laplace transform can be easily computed.
							</p>
							
							<p>
								<term>Modifying Functions:</term> When a function doesn't match a known form, minor modifications, such as multiplying by missing constants or splitting fractions, can help.
							</p>
							
							<p>
								<term>Completing the Square:</term> When dealing with quadratic expressions in the denominator, especially when the discriminant is negative, completing the square can transform the expression into a form that matches known inverse Laplace transforms. Several examples demonstrate this technique.
							</p>

							<p>
								<term>Partial Fraction Decomposition:</term> For more complex rational functions, partial fraction decomposition breaks down the function into simpler fractions that match the common transform forms.
							</p>
					</assemblage>
				</p>
			</introduction>

		<introduction>
			<p>
				This section covers the final step in solving differential equations using the Laplace Transform Method: finding the inverse Laplace transform. The focus is on converting the algebraic equation with unknown function <m>Y(s)</m> back to the original function <m>y(t)</m>. Several methods and techniques are introduced to handle different cases where direct computation isn't possible.
			</p>
			
			<p>
				<assemblage><title> ✳️ Summary of the Key Ideas </title>
					<p>
							<term>Common Forms:</term> A table of common Laplace transforms is provided, which doubles as a reference for inverse transforms. The focus is on recognizing forms that match the table entries for functions like <m>\sin(bt), \cos(bt)</m>, and others.
						</p>

						<p>
							<term>Direct Computation:</term> When the function of <m>s</m> directly matches a form in the common Laplace transform table, the inverse Laplace transform can be easily computed.
						</p>
						
						<p>
							<term>Modifying Functions:</term> When a function doesn't match a known form, minor modifications, such as multiplying by missing constants or splitting fractions, can help.
						</p>
						
						<p>
							<term>Completing the Square:</term> When dealing with quadratic expressions in the denominator, especially when the discriminant is negative, completing the square can transform the expression into a form that matches known inverse Laplace transforms. Several examples demonstrate this technique.
						</p>

						<p>
							<term>Partial Fraction Decomposition:</term> For more complex rational functions, partial fraction decomposition breaks down the function into simpler fractions that match the common transform forms.
						</p>
				</assemblage>
			</p>	
		</introduction>

	<subsection><title> Solving Differential Equations Using Laplace Transforms </title>
		<introduction>
			<p>
				The Laplace Transform Method provides a powerful toolset for solving initial-value differential equations, transforming complex calculus problems into simpler algebraic ones. In the previous sections, we explored how to compute the forward Laplace transform of a function and how to apply the inverse Laplace transform to return to the time domain. These two operations are the fundamental components of the Laplace Transform Method.
			</p>

			<p>
				This section brings together the forward and backward transformations to solve entire initial-value differential equations. By applying the forward Laplace transform to both sides of a differential equation, we convert the problem into an algebraic equation in the <m>s</m>-domain. The algebraic equation is typically easier to solve, and once we find the solution in the <m>s</m>-domain, we apply the inverse Laplace transform to recover the solution in the time domain.
			</p>

			<p>
				We will begin with simple examples and progressively tackle more complex problems, incorporating the initial conditions of the differential equations directly into the transformed equations. By mastering these techniques, you will be able to solve a wide range of differential equations using the Laplace Transform Method.
			</p>
		</introduction>
