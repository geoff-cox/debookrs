<section label="whats-a-de">
	<title>What is a Differential Equation?</title>

	<introduction>
		<sidebyside width="90%" margins="0% 10%">
			<p>
				<aside><title>üéß Listen</title><p><audio source="audio/readings/c1/whats-a-de-intro-and-connection-to-algebra.mp3" width="100%" /></p></aside>
				In this section, we'll explore what a differential equation is, starting with an analogy, and then moving to a formal definition.
			</p>
		</sidebyside>
	</introduction>

	<paragraphs label="connection-to-algebra">
		<title>Connection to Algebra and Calculus</title>

		<p>
			To learn something new, it helps to connect it to something you already know. So let's begin by linking differential equations to algebra.
		</p>

		<p>
			Suppose you're asked to solve each of the following equations for <m>y</m>:
		</p>

		<me>
		y + 2 = 11, \qquad y + 2t = 11, \qquad y^3 + 2t = 11
		</me>

		<p>
			The goal of algebra is to figure out what number or function goes in place of <m>y</m> to make both sides equal. Instead of guessing, you learned rules for isolating <m>y</m>. If we apply those rules, we get:
		</p>

		<md>
			<mrow> y = 9, \qquad y = 11 - 2t, \qquad y^3 \amp = 11 - 2t </mrow>
			<mrow> y \amp = \left(11 - 2t\right)^{1/3} </mrow>
		</md>

		<p>
			Now suppose you're asked to solve a new equation:
		</p>

		<me>
			y' + 2t = 11
		</me>

		<p>
			As before, we isolate <m>y'</m>, giving:
		</p>

		<me>
			y' = 11 - 2t
		</me>

		<p>
			So what function has a derivative of <m>11 - 2t</m>? In other words, what goes in place of <m>y</m> to make this equation true?
		</p>

		<p>
			From calculus, you might recognize this as an anti-derivative problem. So the solution is:
		</p>

		<me>
		y = \int (11 - 2t)\, dt = 11t - t^2 + C.
		</me>

		<p>
			Believe it or not, you just solved a differential equation using algebra and calculus! Most problems won't be quite that straightforward, but the goal is the same: find a function that fits in place of <m>y</m> and makes the equation true.
		</p>
	</paragraphs>

	<paragraphs label="definition">
		<title>Definition</title>

		<sidebyside width="90%" margins="0% 10%">
			<p>
				<aside><title>üéß Listen</title><p><audio source="audio/readings/c1/definition.mp3" width="100%" /></p></aside>
				Essentially, a differential equation is any equation where the unknown has a derivative applied to it. The formal definition is as follows:
			</p>
		</sidebyside>

		<definition xml:id="def-diff-eq">
			<title>Differential Equation</title>
			<statement>
				<sidebyside margins="0% 12%">
					<p>
						<aside>
							<title>üìù Note: DE <m>\Rightarrow</m> ODE</title>
							<p>
								üìå Since this text only covers ODEs, any use of <em>DE</em> will mean <em>ODE</em>.
							</p>
						</aside>
						A <term>differential equation (DE)</term> is an equation that involves one or more derivatives of an unknown function. If the function depends on a single variable, the equation is an <term>ordinary differential equation (ODE)</term>. Otherwise, it is called a <term>partial differential equation (PDE)</term>.
					</p>
				</sidebyside>
			</statement>
		</definition>

		<p>
			For an equation to qualify as a differential equation, it must include both a derivative (like <m>f^\prime</m> or <m>dy/dx</m>) and an equals sign. This helps us identify the following as differential equations:
		</p>

		<me>
		\frac{dy}{dx} + 1 = y, \qquad f^{\prime\prime} + x^2 + 3x = 19, \qquad e^t = \tan(y^\prime)
		</me>

				</choice>
				<choice>

				</choice>
				<choice>
					<statement>An equal sign, "="</statement>
					<feedback>Incorrect. An equality sign is required for an equation to be classified as a differential equation.</feedback>
				</choice>
			</choices>
		</task>

		<p>
			In contrast, these are not differential equations, they either lack a derivative or an equals sign:
		</p>

		<me>
		\frac{d^2 y}{dx^2} + 2\frac{dy}{dx}, \qquad x^2 + 3x = 19, \qquad \sin y + e^x = 0
		</me>

	<introduction label="de-components-intro">
		<p>
			Now that you can recognize a differential equation, it's time to look more closely at what it's made of. In this section, we'll break down the core components of a differential equation and explain the role each one plays in shaping it.
		</p>

	<paragraphs xml:id="variables">
		<title>Dependent and Independent Variables</title>
		<p>
			Every differential equation involves dependent and independent variables. The <term>dependent variable</term> is a function that depends on the <term>independent variable</term> and is the unknown function we're solving for. Because of this, the dependent variable always has derivatives applied to it, with respect to the independent variable.
		</p>

		<task label="chkpt-variables-1"><title>üéØ Which Variable has the Derivative</title>

					In a differential equation, you would expect to see a derivative applied to the dependent variable.
				</p>
			</statement>

					<statement>
						<p>
							True
						</p>
					</statement>

						</p>
					</feedback>
				</choice>
				<choice>

						<p>
							False
						</p>
					</statement>
					<feedback>
						<p>
							Incorrect. By definition, a differential equation involves derivatives of the dependent variable.
						</p>
					</feedback>
				</choice>
			</choices>
		</task>

		<p>
			For example, the differential equation:
			<men xml:id="variables-de-1">

			</men>

		</p>

	<paragraphs label="terms-of-a-de" xml:id="terms">
		<title>Terms of a Differential Equation</title>

		<p>
			The word <q>term</q> is commonly used to describe a part of a mathematical expression. In a differential equation, a <term>term</term> refers to any part of the expression separated by plus (<m>+</m>) or minus (<m>-</m>) signs. For example, an equation might be structured like this:
			<me>
				\text{term}\ 1 + \text{term}\ 2 - \text{term}\ 3 = \text{term}\ 4 - \text{term}\ 5.
			</me>
			This equation has three terms on the left-hand side and two on the right.

		</p>

		<p>
			Terms may include the dependent variable, its derivatives, constants, or other functions of the independent variable. If a term does <em>not</em> include the dependent variable or its derivatives, it is called a <term>constant term</term>. For instance, in the differential equation:
		</p>

		<me>
			\frac{3}{x} y^{(6)} + 5.3 y'' + x^2 (y'-4)^3 - y = \frac12\ln(x)
		</me>

		<p>
			the terms break down as follows:
		</p>

	<paragraphs label="coefficients-of-a-de" xml:id="coefficients">
		<title>Coefficients of a Differential Equation</title>

		<p>
			A <term>coefficient</term> in a differential equation is a constant or function (depending only on the independent variable) that multiplies the dependent variable or one of its derivatives. Coefficients help determine the structure of the equation and what solution methods are possible.
		</p>

		<p>
			For example, in the equation:
			<men xml:id="example-coefficients-of-a-de-1">
				y'' - 3y' + 2y = 0,
			</men>

		</p>

		<p>
			Coefficients can also be functions of the independent variable. In the equation:
			<me>
			t^2 y'' + 5t y' + 6y = \sin(t),
			</me>
			the coefficients are the functions of the independent variable <m>t</m>:
		</p>

			<tabular halign="center">

			</row>
			<row>
				<cell><m>t^2</m></cell>
				<cell><m>5t</m></cell>
				<cell><m>6</m></cell>
			</row>
			</tabular>
		</p>

<section label="order-linearity">
	<title>Order &amp; Linearity</title>

	<introduction label="order-linearity-intro">
		<p>
			So far, we've learned how to identify the core parts of a differential equation, its variables, terms, and coefficients. Now we'll turn to how differential equations are classified based on how these components appear. In this section, we'll focus on two of the most important classifications: the equation's <term>order</term>, which tells us the highest derivative involved, and its <term>linearity</term>, which depends on how the dependent variable and its derivatives appear in each term. Both properties play a central role in determining what kinds of solution methods apply.
		</p>
	</introduction>
	
	<paragraphs label="order">
		<title>Order</title>

		<p>
			A differential equation must contain at least one derivative, but there's no limit on how many derivatives can appear. Some equations involve just a first derivative, while others include higher-order derivatives like <m>y''</m>, <m>y^{(3)}</m>, or beyond.
		</p>

		<p>
			To capture this idea, we define the <term>order</term> of a differential equation as the highest derivative of the dependent variable that appears in the equation.
		</p>

		<task label="chkpt-order-1">
			<title>üéØ What is the Order</title>

			<me>\frac{dy}{dx} + y = x</me>

			<me>

		</p>

	<paragraphs label="linear-terms">
		<title>Linear Terms</title>

		<p>
			To decide whether a differential equation is linear, we first need to understand what it means for a <xref ref="summary-terms" text="custom">term</xref> to be linear. In this context, linearity refers to how the dependent variable and its derivatives appear in each term.
		</p>

		<p>
			Let <m>y</m> be the dependent variable. A term is <term>linear</term> if it appears as either:
			<men xml:id="linear-term-forms">
				a(t), \quad a(t)y, \quad \text{or} \quad a(t)y^{(n)},
			</men>
			where the coefficient, <m>a(t)</m>, can be a constant or a function of the independent variable <m>t</m> only, and <m>y^{(n)}</m> is the <m>n</m>-th derivative of <m>y</m>.
		</p>

		<p>
			For example, the following terms are linear:
			<me>
				\us{a(t)}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{23}}}, \quad 
				\us{a(t)}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{2t^3}}}y'', \quad 
				\us{a(t)}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{\frac{1}{t}}}}y^{(5)}, \quad 
				\us{a(t)}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{e^{2t}}}}y, \quad 
				\us{a(t)}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{7\sin(t)}}}y'.
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			The <xref ref="summary-coefficients" text="custom">coefficient</xref> of the term actually has no affect on whether it's linear, regardless of how complicated <m>a(t)</m> appears.
		</p>

	<paragraphs label="nonlinear-terms" xml:id="identifying-nonlinear-terms">
		<title>Nonlinear Terms</title>

		<p>
			A term is <term>nonlinear</term> if it not in one of the forms in <xref ref="linear-term-forms"/>. Assuming <m>y</m> is the dependent variable, some telltale signs that a term is nonlinear include:
			<sidebyside widths="60% 5% 35%" valign="bottom">
				<p>
					<ul marker="square">
						<li><p><m>y</m> or <m>y^{(n)}</m> are raised to a power other than 1</p></li>
					</ul>
				</p>
				<p/>
				<p>e.g., <m>\ y^2,\ (y')^{-4}</m></p>
			</sidebyside>
			<sidebyside widths="60% 5% 35%" valign="bottom">
				<p>
					<ul marker="square">
						<li><p><m>y</m> or <m>y^{(n)}</m> appear inside a nonlinear function</p></li>
					</ul>
				</p>
				<p/>
				<p>e.g., <m>\ \ln(y),\ \sin(y),\ e^{y'}</m>
				</p>
			</sidebyside>
			<sidebyside widths="60% 5% 35%" valign="bottom">
				<p>
					<ul marker="square">
						<li><p><m>y</m> or <m>y^{(n)}</m> are multiplied or divided by each other</p></li>
					</ul>
				</p>
				<p/>
				<p>e.g., <m>\ y y''',\ \sfrac{y'}{y}</m></p>
			</sidebyside>
		</p>

		<p>
			Let's apply these strategies by breaking down the linearity of each term in the following differential equation:
			<me>
			y\frac{d^5y}{dt^5} + 2t^3\ \frac{d^2y}{dt^2} + \ln\left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right) + y^2 = t^3
			</me>
		</p>

		<p>
			The terms are:
		</p>

		<dl>
			<li>
			<title><m>\ds y\frac{d^5y}{dt^5}</m></title>
			<p>
				Nonlinear. This term involves a product of the dependent variable and its derivative, violating the rule that only one can appear in a term.
			</p>
			</li>
			<li>
			<title><m>\ds 2t^3\frac{d^2y}{dt^2}</m></title>
			<p>
				Linear. The derivative <m>y''</m> appears by itself and to the first power. The coefficient <m>2t^3</m> does not affect linearity.
			</p>
			</li>
			<li>
			<title><m>\ds \ln\left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)</m></title>
			<p>
				Nonlinear. The derivative <m>y'</m> appears inside a nonlinear function, the natural logarithm.
			</p>
			</li>
			<li>
			<title><m>\ds y^2</m></title>
			<p>
				Nonlinear. The dependent variable appears raised to a power other than one.
			</p>
			</li>
			<li>
			<title><m>\ds t^3</m></title>
			<p>
				Linear. This term doesn't involve the dependent variable or any of its derivatives.
			</p>
			</li>
		</dl>

	<paragraphs label="linearity">
		<title>Linearity</title>

		<p>
			Now that we've defined what it means for a <xref ref="summary-terms" text="custom">term</xref> to be linear, we can describe what it means for an entire differential equation to be linear.
		</p>

		<p>
			Essentially, a differential equation is <term>linear</term> if <em>every term in the equation is linear</em>. If just one term is nonlinear, the entire equation is <term>nonlinear</term>. We formally define linear differential equations as follows:
		</p>

		<task label="chkpt-linearity-1">
			<title>üéØ What Makes an Equation Linear of Nonlinear</title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Select the true statement.
				</p>
			</statement>
			<choices randomize="yes">
				<choice correct="yes">
					<statement>An equation is nonlinear if at least one of its terms are nonlinear.</statement>
					<feedback>Correct! A differential equation is nonlinear if <em>any</em> of its terms are nonlinear.</feedback>
				</choice>
				<choice>
					<statement>An equation is linear if at least one of its terms are linear.</statement>
					<feedback>Incorrect. A differential equation needs <em>all</em> of its terms to be linear.</feedback>
				</choice>
				<choice>
					<statement>As long as there are more linear terms than nonlinear terms, the equation is linear.</statement>
					<feedback>Incorrect.</feedback>
				</choice>
			</choices>
		</task>

		<definition xml:id="def-linear">
			<title>Linear Differential Equations</title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					A differential equation is <term>linear</term> if it can be written as a sum of linear terms involving the dependent variable and its derivatives:
					<fn>
					<p>
						This structure is known as a <term>linear combination</term> of the dependent variable and its derivatives.
					</p>
					</fn>
					<men xml:id="linear-equation">
					\underset{\text{linear term}}{\underbrace{a_n(t)\ y^{(n)}}} + \cdots +
					\underset{\text{linear term}}{\underbrace{a_2(t)\ y''}} +
					\underset{\text{linear term}}{\underbrace{a_1(t)\ y'}} +
					\underset{\text{linear term}}{\underbrace{a_0(t)\ y}} = f(t)
					</men>
					where each <m>a_k(t)</m> is a function of the independent variable only, and <m>f(t)</m> is the input or forcing term.
				</p>
			</statement>
		</definition>

		<p>
			For example, the following differential equations are linear:
		</p>

		<men xml:id="linear-equation-examples">
			3\frac{dy}{dx} + 2y = 0, \qquad y''' - 2y' - \frac{7}{x}y = e^x, \qquad w'' - \tan(t) = 3t.
		</men>

		<p>
			Each term involving the dependent variable or its derivatives is linear, none appear raised to a power, inside a function, or multiplied together.
		</p>

		<p>
			In contrast, the following equations are nonlinear because they include at least one nonlinear term (‚ú∑):
		</p>

		<men xml:id="nonlinear-equation-examples">
			y' + \us{\large ‚ú∑}{\ul{ t y^2 }} = t, \qquad 
			t \frac{dP}{dx} + \us{\large ‚ú∑}{\ul{ \sin(P) }} = t^2, \qquad 
			\us{\large ‚ú∑}{\ul{\omega\omega^{(5)}}} + s = \us{\large ‚ú∑}{\ul{ e^\omega }}
		</men>

		<p>
			In each case, it only took one term to violate the definition of a linear differential equation.
		</p>

		<p>
			Let's practice identifying the linearty of entire equations with a few examples.
		</p>

		<example>
			<title>Identify the Linearity of the Equation</title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Determine whether each of the following differential equations is linear:
					<me>
					y^{(5)} + \frac{y'}{x^2} + 5y = \ln x, \qquad P^{(6)} + \frac{m P'}{P} = (m - 1)^2
					</me>
				</p>
			</statement>
			<solution>
				<p>
					In the first equation, all terms involving <m>y</m> or its derivatives appear linearly:
					<md>
					<mrow>
						\underset{\overline{\text{linear}}}{\vphantom{\frac11}\color{BurntOrange} y^{(5)}} +
						\underset{\overline{\text{linear}}}{\frac{1}{x^2} \color{BurntOrange} y'} +
						\underset{\overline{\text{linear}}}{5 \color{BurntOrange} y} =\ 
						\underset{\overline{\text{linear}}}{\ln x}
					</mrow>
					</md>
					So the equation is linear.
				</p>

				<p>
					In the second equation, the term <m>\frac{m P'}{P}</m> is nonlinear, since <m>P</m> appears in both the numerator and denominator:
					<md>
					<mrow>
						\underset{\text{linear}}{\underline{\color{BurntOrange} P^{(6)}}} +
						\underset{\overline{\text{nonlinear}}}{\frac{m \color{BurntOrange} P'}{\color{BurntOrange} P}} =\ 
						\underset{\text{linear}}{(m - 1)^2}
					</mrow>
					</md>
					This makes the entire equation nonlinear.
				</p>
			</solution>
		</example>

	<paragraphs label="order-linearity-wrapup">
		<title>üóùÔ∏è Key Takeaways</title>
		<p>
			<aside><title>üéß Listen</title><p><audio source="audio/readings/c1/initial-conditions-wrapup.mp3" width="100%" /></p></aside>
			<ul marker="square">
				<li>
					The <term>order</term> of a differential equation is the highest derivative of the dependent variable that appears. Only derivatives, not exponents, affect the order.
				</li>
				<li>
					A <term>linear term</term> includes exactly one occurrence of the dependent variable or one of its derivatives, raised to the first power and not inside another function. Coefficients do not affect linearity.
				</li>
				<li>
					A <term>nonlinear term</term> involves a product, quotient, exponent, or function of the dependent variable or its derivatives.
				</li>
				<li>
					A differential equation is <term>linear</term> if all terms involving the dependent variable are linear. If even one is nonlinear, the entire equation is classified as <term>nonlinear</term>.
				</li>
			</ul>
