<introduction>
	<p>
		Imagine turning a complex differential equation into an algebra problem instead. That is the magic of the <em>Laplace transform method</em>. It works by transforming a differential equation into one where derivatives become powers of <m>s</m> and the initial conditions drop in automatically as constants.
	</p>

	<p>
		The method unfolds in three main moves:
	</p>

	<ol marker="(i)">
		<li><term>Forward transform.</term> Apply the Laplace transform (term-by-term) to a differential equation and get an algebraic equation in <m>Y(s)</m>.</li>
		<li><term>Algebra in the Laplace domain.</term> Isolate <m>Y(s)</m> and prepare it for next step.</li>
		<li><term>Backward transform.</term> Apply the inverse Laplace transform to the prepared <m>Y(s)</m> to turn it back into <m>y(t)</m> as the solution to the origianl differential equation.</li>
	</ol>

	<p>
		Throughout the chapter, we will zoom in on each step with the same guiding equation,
		<men xml:id="lt-model-eqn-w-ics">
			y'' - 9y = 10e^{2t}, \quad y(0)=1, \quad y'(0)=-7
		</men>,
		and the roadmap, below. The details of each step will be revealed as we go. For now, take a moment to preview what's to come.
	</p>

<section label="lt-method-step1">
	<title>Step 1 ‚Äî Into the Laplace Domain</title>

	<introduction>
		<p>
			The first step of the Laplace transform method is to convert a differential equation‚Äîwhere the unknown appears through derivatives‚Äîinto an algebraic equation in the Laplace domain. This transformation makes it easier to isolate the unknown and move toward a solution.
		</p>
	</introduction>

    <subsection label="lt-method-step1-overview">
        <title>Crossing Into the Laplace Domain</title>

        <p>
            In terms of the roadmap, we can visualize this first step as follows:
        </p>

		<assemblage xml:id="lt-roadmap-step-1"><title>Step 1Ô∏è‚É£: Into the Laplace Domain (Forward Transform)</title>
			<p>
				<sidebyside widths="32% 18% 42%" valign="middle">
					<p>
						<me>\ul{\qquad\textbf{Original Domain}\qquad}</me>
					</p>
					<p/>
					<p>
						<me>\DLBa\textbf{Laplace Domain}</me>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>
				<sidebyside widths="32% 18% 42%" valign="top">
					<p>
						<md>
							<mrow>\os{\vphantom{m}}{y'' - 9y = 10e^{2t}}</mrow>
							<mrow>y(0) = 1,\quad y'(0) = -7</mrow>
							<mrow>\os{\large üî∫ üî∫ üî∫ üî∫ üî∫}{\text{Differential Equation}}</mrow>
						</md>
					</p>
					<p>
						<md>
							<mrow>\underrightarrow{\text{1Ô∏è‚É£ Forward}}</mrow>
							<mrow>\text{Apply}\ \laplacesym</mrow>
							<mrow/>
						</md>
					</p>
					<p>
						<assemblage>
							<p>
								<md>
									<mrow>\DLBa s^2Y - 9Y - s + 7 = \frac{10}{s - 2}</mrow>
									<mrow>\DLBa\dotsm</mrow>
								</md>
							</p>
						</assemblage>
					</p>
				</sidebyside>
			</p>
		</assemblage>

		<p>
			Simply put, forward transforming a differential equation into the Laplace domain means applying the Laplace transform to both sides of the equation. The result is an algebraic equation where the derivatives have been converted into powers of <m>s</m> and the new unknown is <m>Y</m>.
		</p>
    </subsection>

	<subsection xml:id="lt-forward-mechanics">
		<title>Basic Mechanics of the Forward Transform</title>
        <p>
            Symbolically, applying the forward Laplace transform to the differential equation
			<me>
				y'' - 9y = 10e^{2t}, \quad y(0) = 1,\quad y'(0) = -7
			</me>
			amounts to applying it to both sides of the equation as follows:
			<me>
				\lap{y'' - 9y} = \lap{10e^{2t}}
			</me>.
        </p>

		<p>
			Using the <xref ref="lt-P1" text="custom">linearity property</xref> we can see that the transform applies to each term:
		</p>

		<me>
			\lap{y''} - 9\lap{y} = 10\lap{e^{2t}}
		</me>.

		<p>
			Letting <m>Y(s) = \lap{y(t)}</m> and applying rule <xref ref="lt-R2" text="custom">R<m>_2</m></xref> for <m>\lap{y''}</m> and common transform <xref ref="lt-L2" text="custom">L<m>_2</m></xref>, we get:
		</p>

		<me>
			s^2 Y(s) - s y(0) - y'(0) - 9 Y(s) = 10 \left(\frac{1}{s-2}\right)
		</me>.
		
		<p>
			This is where the initial conditions come into play. Substituting the initial conditions and simplifying gives the desired Laplace domain equation:
		</p>

		<me>
			s^2Y - 9Y - s + 7 = \frac{10}{s - 2}
		</me>.

		<p>
			Our new unknown is <m>Y</m> and since the derivatives have been eliminated, we can use algebra to solve for it. In fact, the solution to our differential equation is actually hiding inside <m>Y</m>. So, by solving for <m>Y</m> you are also solving for <m>y(t)</m> at the same time!
		</p>

		<p>
			Let's practice this with a one more example.
		</p>
		
		<example xml:id="lt-forward-lt-example">
			<title>Forward Transforming Equations</title>
			<statement>
				<p>
					Forward transform the following equations into the Laplace domain:
				</p>
				<me>
					\begin{array}{l}
						y'' - 4y' + 6y = t^2,\\
						y(0) = 2, \quad y'(0) = 0
					\end{array}
				</me>
			</statement>

			<solution> temporarily omitted </solution>
		</example>

        <p>
            Now that you've seen the mechanical steps involved with applying the Laplace transform to a differential equation, it is important to take a moment to see what's happening behind the scenes during this process. 
        </p>
	</subsection>

	<subsection label="lt-forward-intro">
		<title>What is Going on Behind the Scenes?</title>

		<task> temporarily omitted </task>

		<p>
            Since the Laplace transform only applies to functions, applying a <q>forward transform to a differential equation</q>, really means we are taking the Laplace transform to both sides of that equation. So, applying a forward transformation to the initial-value problem
			<me>y' - 9 y= 10 e^{7t}, \quad y(0) = 1</me>
			implies that we multiply both sides of the equation by <m>e^{-st}</m> and integrate from <m>0</m> to <m>\infty</m>:
		</p>

		<sbsgroup widths="35% 65%" valign="middle">
			<sidebyside>
				<p><me>\ul{\qquad\text{Symbolically}\qquad}</me></p>
				<p><me>\ul{\qquad\text{Behind the Scenes Math}\qquad}</me></p>
			</sidebyside>
			<sidebyside>
				<p><me>\small\lap{y' - 9y} = \lap{10 e^{7t}}</me></p>
				<p>
					<md>
						<mrow>\small\left(y' - 9y\right) e^{-st} \amp = (10 e^{7t}) e^{-st}</mrow>
						<mrow>\small\int_0^{\infty} \left(y' - 9y\right) e^{-st} dt \amp = \int_0^{\infty} (10 e^{7t}) e^{-st} dt</mrow>
					</md>
				</p>
			</sidebyside>
		</sbsgroup>

		<p>
			Next, we use the linearity of the integral:
		</p>

		<sidebyside widths="35% 65%" valign="middle">
			<p><me>\small\lap{y'} - 9\lap{y} = 10\lap{e^{7t}}</me></p>
			<p><me>\small\us{\large I_1}{\ul{\int_0^{\infty} (y') e^{-st} dt}} - 9 \int_0^{\infty} (y) e^{-st} dt = 10 \us{\large I_2}{\ul{\int_0^{\infty} (e^{7t}) e^{-st} dt}}</me></p>
		</sidebyside>

		<p>
			After applying integration by parts to <m>I_1</m>, the derivative is transferred from <m>y(t)</m> to <m>e^{-st}</m>, resulting in a factor of <m>s</m> and an evaluation of <m>y(t)</m> at <m>t=0</m> and <m>t=b</m>:
		</p>

		<sidebyside widths="42% 58%" valign="middle">
			<p>
				<me>\small
					-y(0) + sY(s) - 9 Y(s) = 10 \left(\frac{1}{s-7}\right)
				</me>
			</p>
			<p><me>\leftarrow\ \text{Symbolically}</me></p>
		</sidebyside>

		<sidebyside widths="36% 64%" valign="middle">
			<p><me>\text{Mathematically}\ \rightarrow </me></p>
			<p>
				<md>
					<mrow>\small
						\us{\large\text{as}\ b\ \to\ \infty}{\ul{\cancelto{\large 0}{e^{-sb}y(b)}}}<fn>assuming <m>y(t)</m> grows no faster than the exponential decays.</fn> - y(0) 
						+ s\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st}y\, dt - 9\int_0^{\infty} e^{-st}y\, dt = \frac{10}{s-7}
					</mrow>
				</md>
				
			</p>
		</sidebyside>
		
        <p>
            The details for computing <m>I_2 = \lap{e^{7t}}</m> are given <xref ref="common-exp-7t-details" text="custom">here</xref>.
        </p>

		<p>
			Since the two integrals are <m>\lap{y} = Y(s)</m>, the symbolic and mathematical paths come back together as  
			<me>
				sY(s) - 9 Y(s) - y(0) = \frac{10}{s-7}
			</me>.
		</p>

		<p>
			By transforming our equation into this new form, we have momentarily stored <m>y(t)</m> inside the function <m>Y</m>. Therefore, solving for <m>Y</m> indirectly also solves for <m>y(t)</m>.
		</p>
	</subsection>
	
	<assemblage>
		<title>Step 1 ‚Äî Into the Laplace Domain Summary</title>
		<p>
			Apply <m>\laplacesym</m> to each term of the differential equation; derivatives become powers of <m>s</m> and initial conditions drop in as constants, leaving an algebraic equation in <m>Y(s)</m>.
		</p>
	</assemblage>

	<objectives label="lt-method-step1-wrapup">
		<ul>
			<li>The Laplace transform method begins by converting a differential equation into an algebraic equation in the Laplace domain.</li>
			<li>Applying a Laplace transform to a differential equation means applying it to both sides, then term by term, using linearity.</li>
			<li>Derivatives of <m>y(t)</m> become powers of <m>s</m> multiplied by <m>Y(s)</m>, along with terms involving initial conditions.</li>
			<li>The right-hand side of the equation is transformed using the known transforms from the <xref ref="lt-table-common" text="custom">table of common Laplace transforms</xref>.</li>
			<li>Once in the Laplace domain, the function <m>y(t)</m> is stored inside <m>Y(s)</m>, setting the stage for solving algebraically.</li>
        </ul>
	</objectives>
</section>

<section label="lt-method-step2">
	<title>Step 2 ‚Äî Solving in the Laplace Domain</title>

	<introduction>
		<p>
			Once we've applied the Laplace transform to a differential equation, we're left with a new algebraic equation involving <m>Y(s)</m>. Solving this equation is usually easier than solving the original differential equation, but we still need to work carefully. In this section, you'll learn how to isolate <m>Y(s)</m>, clean it up using algebra, and get it into a form that matches the inverse transform table.
		</p>
	</introduction>

	<subsection label="lt-laplace-solving-overview">
		<title>What It Means to Solve in the Laplace Domain</title>
		<p>
			After transforming a differential equation into the Laplace domain, you're left with an algebraic equation involving <m>Y(s)</m>. We will call this the Laplace-domain equation. In this setting, your new unknown is now represented by its Laplace transform, <m>Y(s)</m>.
		</p>
		
		<p>
			Your goal is twofold: first, solve for <m>Y(s)</m> using algebra, and second, rewrite <m>Y(s)</m> so it matches known inverse transform forms. That prepares it for the final step of returning to the original domain.
		</p>

		<assemblage xml:id="lt-roadmap-step-2"><title>Step 2Ô∏è‚É£: Solve for <m>Y</m> &amp; Prepare it for an Inverse Transform</title>
			<p>
				<sidebyside widths="66%" valign="middle" margins="17% 17%">
					<p><me>\DLBa\textbf{2Ô∏è‚É£  Laplace Domain}</me></p>
				</sidebyside>
				<sidebyside widths="66%" valign="middle" margins="17% 17%">
					<p>
						<assemblage>
							<p>
								<md>
									<mrow>\amp \DLBa s^2Y - 9Y - s + 7 = \frac{10}{s - 2}
										\amp\amp\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\DLBa 
										‚óÄ\ \ \begin{array}{l}\text{Laplace Domain}\\ \text{Equation}\end{array}</mrow>
									<mrow>\amp\qquad\qquad {\Big\downarrow}\quad\text{Solve for}\ Y</mrow>
									<mrow>\amp \DLBa Y = \frac{1}{s^2 - 9}\left( \frac{10}{s - 2} + s - 7 \right)
										\amp\amp\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\DLBa 
										‚óÄ\ \ \text{Isolated}\ Y </mrow>
									<mrow>\amp\qquad\qquad {\Big\downarrow}\ \ \text{Prepare for Inverse}</mrow>
									<mrow>\amp \DLBa Y = \frac{1}{s - 3} + \frac{2}{s + 3} - \frac{2}{s - 2}
										\amp\amp\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\DLBa

	<subsection label="lt-laplace-preparing-for-inverse">
		<title>Preparing for the Inverse Transform</title>

		<introduction>
			<p>
				Once you've solved for <m>Y(s)</m>, the next step is to prepare it for returning to the original domain. This means rewriting <m>Y(s)</m> as a combination of expressions that match known Laplace transforms, so we can use the inverse transform to find <m>y(t)</m>.
			</p>

			<p>
				The inverse Laplace transform works by reversing the process of the forward transform. This can be done using the same <xref ref="lt-table-common" text="custom">table of transforms</xref>‚Äîbut in reverse. Our goal is to express <m>Y(s)</m> as a sum of terms that match entries in the <m>s</m>-domain column.
			</p>
		</introduction>

		<paragraphs>
			<title>Preparation Guidelines</title>
			<p>
				After isolating <m>Y</m> from the transformed equation, you typically are left with a messy combination of <xref ref="rational-function" text="custom">rational functions</xref> in the variable <m>s</m>.
			</p>

			<p>
				Since we can only invert functions in the <m>s</m>-domain column of the <xref ref="lt-table-common" text="custom">table of common Laplace transforms</xref>, it helps to list them out to guide us on how to prepare <m>Y</m>.
			</p>

			<sidebyside width="50%">
				<tabular top="major" bottom="minor" left="major" right="minor" halign="center" width="100%">
					<col/>
					<col/>
					<col/>
					<col right="major"/>
					<row>
						<cell><me>\frac{1}{s}</me></cell>
						<cell><me>\frac{n!}{s^{n+1}}</me></cell>
						<cell><me>\frac{s}{s^2+b^2}</me></cell>
						<cell><me>\frac{b}{s^2+b^2}</me></cell>
					</row>
					<row>
						<cell><me>\frac{1}{s - a}</me></cell>
						<cell><me>\frac{n!}{(s-a)^{n+1}}</me></cell>
						<cell><me>\frac{s-a}{(s-a)^2+b^2}</me></cell>
						<cell><me>\frac{b}{(s-a)^2+b^2}</me></cell>
					</row>
				</tabular>				
			</sidebyside>

			<p>
				To invert <m>Y</m>, it must first be written as the sum of one or more of these terms. However, since the first row is a special case of the second when <m>a=0</m>, we can reduce the list down to the following function types:
			</p>

			<men xml:id="lt-pfd-forms">
				\frac{1}{s - a},\quad \frac{1}{(s - a)^{n+1}},\quad \frac{b}{(s-a)^2+b^2},\quad \frac{s-a}{(s-a)^2+b^2},
			</men>

			<p>
				Fortunately, there is a technique that was designed for this exact task. Recall that <term>partial fraction decomposition (PFD)</term> guarantees that any rational function can be written as the sum of rational terms with linear or quadratic factors in the denominator.
			</p>

			<p>
				Therefore, if the denominator of <m>Y</m> has degree three or higher, PFD will always be your first step toward preparing <m>Y</m> for a backward transform out of the Laplace domain.
			</p>
		</paragraphs>

		<paragraphs>
			<title>Dealing with Quadratic Denominators</title>

			<p>
				After partial fraction decomposition, <m>Y</m> will be the sum of rational terms with either a linear or quadratic factor in the denominator. If they are all linear then you are done and you can move on to inverting <m>Y</m>. In contrast, if at least one term has a quadratic denominator then it will be of the form
				<me>
					\frac{\text{*}}{As^2 + Bs + C}
				</me>.
				where we have omitted the numerator since will be not be important to this discussion.
			</p>

			<p>
				Notice that the denominator is not nicely factored like you see in the transform table. To achieve this, we need to <xref ref="completing-the-square" text="custom">complete the square</xref>.
			</p>

			<p>
				Note that if the quadratic denominator term was the result of partial fraction decomposition, then it cannot be reduced further and you should skip directly to completing the square of the denominator. However, if <m>Y</m> started with this form, then you should first check to see if the denominator is factorable. If so, then use PFD. Otherwise, complete the square of the denominator so that it resembles the form in the table:
			</p>

			<me>
				\frac{*}{As^2 + Bs + C}\quad\rightarrow\quad\frac{*}{(s - a)^2 + b^2},
			</me>

			<task> temporarily omitted </task>
		</paragraphs>
	</subsection>

<section label="lt-method-step3">
	<title>Step 3 ‚Äî Leaving the Laplace Domain</title>

	<introduction>
		<p>
			Up to this point, we've been working inside the Laplace domain ‚Äî a mathematical space where differential equations are replaced by algebraic ones. We used forward Laplace transforms to move from <m>y(t)</m> to <m>Y(s)</m>, solved for <m>Y(s)</m>, and simplified it to match known inverse forms.
		</p>

		<p>
			Now, it's time to take the final step: returning to the original domain where the solution is a function of time. To do this, we apply the <term>inverse Laplace transform</term>, denoted <m>\laplacesym^{-1}</m>, which undoes the effect of the forward transform.
		</p>
	</introduction>

	<subsection label="lt-inverse-concept">
		<title>What Is the Inverse Laplace Transform?</title>

		<p>
			Symbolically, the inverse Laplace transform works much like the inverse of a standard function. For example, we can release <m>y(t)</m> from inside the cosine by applying the inverse cosine:  
		</p>

		<md>
			<mrow> \cos(y(t)) = t^2	\amp\quad\Rightarrow\quad \cos^{-1}(\cos(y(t))) = \cos^{-1}(t^2) </mrow>
			<mrow>					\amp\quad\Rightarrow\quad y(t) = \cos^{-1}(t^2) </mrow>
		</md>
		
		<p>
			Similarly, we can release <m>y(t)</m> from inside a Laplace transform by applying its inverse:
			<md>
				<mrow> \lap{y(t)} = {\DLBb\frac1{s}}	\amp\quad\Rightarrow\quad \ilap{\lap{y(t)}} = \ilap{{\DLBb\frac1{s}}} </mrow>

			</md>
		</p>

		<p>
			This is the idea behind the final step of the Laplace transform method as it takes us back into the original domain by releasing the <m>y(t)</m> contained inside <m>Y = \lap{y(t)}</m>. Visually, this looks like:
		</p>

		<assemblage xml:id="lt-roadmap-step-30"><title>Step 3Ô∏è‚É£: Out of the Laplace Domain (Backward Transform)</title>

	<subsection label="lt-inverse-table-usage">
		<title>Using the Table to Invert <m>Y(s)</m></title>

		<p>
			During the forward step, we used the <xref ref="lt-table-common" text="custom">table</xref> to map functions of <m>t</m> in the <m>t</m>-domain column, like <m>e^{at}</m> or <m>\sin(bt)</m>, into functions of <m>s</m> in the <m>s</m>-domain column. Now, we'll do the opposite: match terms in <m>Y(s)</m> to the forms in the <m>s</m>-domain column and map them back to functions in the <m>t</m>-domain column.
		</p>

		<p>
			For example, suppose we have the following <m>Y(s)</m> in the Laplace domain:
		</p>

		<me>Y(s) = \frac{1}{s - 5}</me>

		<p>
			To return back this to the original domain we apply the inverse to both sides:
		</p>

		<me>
			\ilap{Y(s)} = \ilap{\frac{1}{s - 5}} \quad\Rightarrow\quad y(t) = \ilap{\frac{1}{s - 5}}
		</me>

		<p>
			We have <m>y(t)</m>, but it needs to be a function of <m>t</m>. For that, we match <m>\frac{1}{s - 5}</m> to one of the forms in the <m>s</m>-domain column of the <xref ref="lt-table-common" text="custom">table</xref>. 
		</p>
		
		<p>
			We see the best match is <xref ref="lt-L2-table" text="custom">L<m>_2</m></xref> with <m>a = 5</m>, so we get:

		<me>y(t) = e^{5t}</me>

	<subsection label="lt-inverse-linearity">
		<title>Linearity of the Inverse Laplace Transform</title>

		<p>
			Most Laplace-domain solutions are not single terms. Instead, they're sums of several expressions. Fortunately, like the forward transform, the inverse Laplace transform is also linear. This means that inverse transforms of sums and constant multiples of functions can be broken down into smaller, more manageable steps.
		</p>

		<p>
			Assuming <m>f(s)</m> and <m>g(s)</m> are functions in the Laplace domain, then we have:
		</p>

		<md>
			<mrow xml:id="lt-P2">
				\text{P}_{2}:\quad\ilap{af(s) \pm bg(s)} = a\ilap{f(s)} \pm b\ilap{g(s)}\quad (a, b\ \text{constants})
			</mrow>.
		</md>

		<p>

		</p>

			</p>

		</example>

		<p>
			Linearity makes inverting multi-term expressions much more manageable. Sometimes you'll run into expressions that don't quite match anything in the table. They're close‚Äîbut not perfect. 	<subsection label="lt-inverse-minor-adjustments">
		<title>Minor Adjustments for a Perfect Match</title>
		<introduction>
			<p>
				Sometimes, the Laplace-domain function <m>Y(s)</m> is close to a known table form‚Äîbut not quite there. In these cases, a few algebraic adjustments will help reveal the match. These are not major rewrites, just small nudges: splitting a numerator, factoring out constants, or rewriting terms to expose a recognizable pattern.
			</p>
		</introduction>

		<paragraphs>
			<title>Separate Fractions to Match Multiple Forms</title>

			<p>
				Suppose your function has a sum in the numerator, like
				<me>Y(s) = \frac{2s + 5}{s^2 + 4}</me>.
				The denominator matches both sine and cosine forms in the table, but the numerator doesn't match any of the entries. The fix? Separate the addition in the numerator:
			</p>

			<me>
			Y(s) = \frac{2s}{s^2 + 4} + \frac{5}{s^2 + 4}
			</me>.

			<p>
				Then factor constants and attempt to match with the table again:
			</p>

			<me>
				Y(s) 
				= 2\ \us{\ds<xref ref="lt-L5-table" text="custom">\text{L}_5</xref>\text{‚úîÔ∏è}}{\ul{\frac{s}{s^2 + 4}}}
				+ 5\ \us{\ds\text{almost}\ <xref ref="lt-L4-table" text="custom">\text{L}_4</xref>}{\ul{\frac{1}{s^2 + 4}}}\!
			</me>.

			<p> 
				The first term perfectly matches, the second term needs <m>b=2</m> in the numerator. Missing numbers in the numerator is common with inverse transforms, but there is an easy fix. We multiply numerator and denominator by the missing number:
			</p>

			<aside><title>üö© Multiplying by <m>1</m></title>
				<p>
					<m>\sfrac22</m> is just a special form of <m>1</m>; multiplying by it doesn't change the fraction.
				</p>
			</aside>

			<me>	
				Y(s) 
				= 2\frac{s}{s^2 + 4} + 5\frac{1}{s^2 + 4} \cdot \us{\ds üö©}{\boxed{\frac{2}{2}}} 
				= 2\ \us{\ds<xref ref="lt-L5-table" text="custom">\text{L}_5</xref>\text{‚úîÔ∏è}}{\ul{\frac{s}{s^2 + 4}}} 
				+ \frac{5}{2}\ \us{\ds<xref ref="lt-L4-table" text="custom">\text{L}_4</xref>\text{‚úîÔ∏è}}{\ul{\frac{2}{s^2 + 4}}}
			</me>.

			<p>
				With these minor adjustments, the inverse transform becomes straightforward:
				<me>	
					y(t) = 2\cos(2t) + \frac{5}{2}\sin(2t)
				</me>.
			</p>

			<task> temporarily omitted </task>

		</paragraphs>

		<paragraphs>
			<title>Adding and Subtracting to Match Shifts</title>

			<p>
				Some forms contain a shift, like <m>(s - a)</m>. For example, the form
				<me>Y(s) = \frac{s + 1}{(s - 2)^2 + 9}</me>
				looks similar to <xref ref="lt-L8-table" text="custom">L<m>_8</m></xref>, but the numerator should be <m>s-2</m>. This can be fixed by subtracting <m>2</m> and adding <m>2</m>, like so:
			</p>

			<aside><title>üö© Adding <m>0</m></title>
				<p>
					<m>-2 + 2</m> is just a special form of <m>0</m>; adding it to the numerator doesn't change the fraction.
				</p>
			</aside>

			<me>
				Y(s) = \frac{s - \os{\dsüö©}{\overline{2 + 2}} + 1}{(s - 2)^2 + 9} = \frac{s - 2 + 3}{(s - 2)^2 + 9}
			</me>

			<p>
				Now you can separate the fraction as before:
			</p>

			<me>
				Y(s)
				= \us{\ds<xref ref="lt-L8-table" text="custom">\text{L}_8</xref>\text{‚úîÔ∏è}}{\ul{\frac{s - 2}{(s - 2)^2 + 9}}}
				+ \us{\ds<xref ref="lt-L7-table" text="custom">\text{L}_7</xref>\text{‚úîÔ∏è}}{\ul{\frac{3}{(s - 2)^2 + 9}}}
			</me>

			<p>
				These can be a little tricky so here's a few additional examples to prepare you for what you may encounter.
			</p>

			<example><title>Handling Coefficients on <m>s</m></title>

					<p>

		</p>
	</subsection>

			<li>If <m>Y(s)</m> is close but doesn't match a table entry, try splitting the numerator, factoring constants, or completing the square.</li>
