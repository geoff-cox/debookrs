    Additional Examples.

    Example 22.

    Determine if the integrating factor method can be applied to the differential equation
    \begin{equation*} y' = y^2 + x. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    As always, we start by trying to classify the equation to determine which method applies. A quick observation shows us that we are dealing with a first order, nonlinear differential equation.
    The \(y^2\) term makes the DE nonlinear.
    This leaves separation of variables as the remaining possibility, but it is also easy to see that the \(x\) and \(y\) terms cannot be separated by multiplication.
    Since the classification falls outside the requirements of any of our known methods, we are not equipped to solve it at this time.

    Example 23.

    Given that \(y\) is some unknown function of \(x\text{,}\) compute \(\left[y\sin x\right]'\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    \begin{gather*} \left[ y\sin x \right]' = y \cdot \left[ \sin x \right]'+\left[ y \right]'\cdot \sin x = y\cos x + y'\cdot \sin x \end{gather*}

    Example 24.

    Suppose some product rule produced the following derivative
    \begin{equation} 2e^{2x} \cdot \sin x + e^{2x}\cdot \cos x.\tag{28} \end{equation}
    Write down the product rule that this came from. That is, rewrite it in the form
    \begin{equation*} [f \cdot g]^{\prime}. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Our goal is to find \(f\) and \(g\) so that the product rule gives us (28). This requires that we match the terms of \(f' \cdot g + f \cdot g'\) and (28).
    Observing that \([e^{2x}]^{\prime} = 2e^{2x}\text{,}\) and \([\sin x]^{\prime} = \cos x,\) then it makes sense to match the terms like so
    \begin{equation*} \underset{f^{'}}{\underline{2e^{2x}}} \cdot \underset{g}{\underline{\sin x}} + \underset{f}{\underline{e^{2x}}} \cdot \underset{g^\prime}{\underline{\cos x}}. \end{equation*}
    Therefore, we can rewrite (28) like so
    \begin{equation*} 2e^{2x} \cdot \sin x + e^{2x}\cdot \cos x = [e^{2x} \cdot \sin x]^{\prime}, \end{equation*}
    and we have "reversed the product rule".

    Example 25.

    Suppose the following expression is the derivative of a product of two functions, where \(z\) is a function of \(t\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} 6ze^{6t} + e^{6t} z' \end{equation*}
    Find the two functions and write down the derivative of their product, explicitly showing the steps involving the product rule.
    Solution.
    We are still trying to identify two functions, \(f\) and \(g\text{,}\) such that we see each function and its derivative as in the product rule. We may need to rearrange a little bit, but we do in fact have such functions, as shown below.
    \begin{equation*} \us{\large f'}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{6e^{6t}}}} \cdot \us{\large g}{\us{\uparrow}{z}} + \us{\large f}{\us{\uparrow}{\ul{e^{6t}}}} \cdot \us{\large g'}{\us{\uparrow}{z'}} \end{equation*}
    Hence, \(f=e^{6t}\) and \(g=z\) and the derivative, using the product rule, is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dt}\Big( e^{6t}\cdot z \Big) = 6ze^{6t} + e^{6t} z' \end{equation*}
    Warning: You’ve been looking at a very special class of expressions! Not every sum of terms comes from the product rule. For example, we cannot un-do the product rule in the expression
    \begin{equation*} x^3\cdot y' + x^2\cdot y, \end{equation*}
    because the derivative of \(x^3\) is not \(x^2\text{.}\) You should pay close attention to whether you can or cannot un-do the product rule; this can help confirm if you have or have not made a mistake in solving a DE. We’ll discuss this further later.

    Example 26.

    Rewrite the expression
    \begin{equation} e^{h(x)} \cdot y' + 3x^2 e^{h(x)}\cdot y\tag{29} \end{equation}
    as a "reversed product rule" in the form
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}\left[y \cdot e^{h(x)}\right]. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    We will approach this as before and attempt to match the appropriate terms.
    The product rule tells us that \(\frac{d}{dx}\left[y \cdot e^{h(x)}\right]\) is the same as
    \begin{equation*} y' \cdot e^{h(x)} + y \cdot e^{h(x)} \cdot h'. \end{equation*}
    We used the chain rule to compute \(\frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{h(x)}\right].\)
    After arranging the terms, we match (29) with
    \begin{equation*} e^{h(x)} \cdot y' + \underline{h'(x)} e^{h(x)} \cdot y. \end{equation*}
    and see that \(h'(x)\) can only be matched to \(3x^2\text{.}\) So,
    \begin{equation*} h'(x) = 3x^2, \end{equation*}
    and this means that \(h\) is the anti-derivative of \(3x^2\text{,}\) or
    \begin{equation*} h(x) = x^3 + C. \end{equation*}
    This shows that there are many possible answers (one for each value of \(C\)), but only one is required. So we pick \(C=0\) which makes \(h(x) = x^3\) and conclude that
    \begin{equation*} e^{h(x)} \cdot y' + 3x^2 e^{h(x)}\cdot y = \frac{d}{dx}\left[y \cdot e^{x^3}\right]. \end{equation*}

    Example 27.

    Find a function, \(\mu\text{,}\) such that tbe product rule applied to \(\frac{d}{dx}\left[y \cdot \mu\right]\) gives
    \begin{equation} \mu \cdot y' + 6 \mu \cdot y.\tag{30} \end{equation}
    Solution.
    As we did before, we need to identify functions such that we see each function and its derivative as in the product rule. We might label them as follows.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{ccccccc} z \!\!\!\! \amp \cdot \amp \!\!\!\! y' \!\!\!\! \amp + \amp \!\!\!\! 6z \!\!\!\! \amp \cdot \amp \!\!\!\! y \\ \underset{\large f }{\uparrow} \!\!\!\! \amp \amp \!\!\!\! \underset{\large g'}{\uparrow} \!\!\!\! \amp \amp \!\!\!\! \underset{\large f'}{\uparrow} \!\!\!\! \amp \amp \!\!\!\! \underset{\large g }{\uparrow} \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Based on the labeling in the question above, if we are going to be able to un-do the product rule, we need the function \(z\) to be such that its derivative is \(6z\text{.}\) We can translate that into a mathematical statement:
    \begin{equation*} \text{"the derivative of } z \text{ is } 6z \text{"}\hspace{0.1cm} \Rightarrow \hspace{0.1cm}\frac{dz}{dx} = 6z. \end{equation*}
    In order to determine the unknown function \(z\text{,}\) we need to solve a differential equation! It turns out that this DE is first order and separable, so we can solve it using separation of variables.
    \begin{align*} \frac{dz}{dx} \amp = 6z \\ \frac{dz}{dx} \cdot dx \amp = 6z \cdot dx \\ \frac 1z\ dz \amp = 6 \cdot dx \\ \int\frac1z\ dz \amp = \int 6\ dx \\ \ln|z| \amp = 6x+C \\ |z| \amp = e^{6x+C} \\ z \amp = \pm e^C e^{6x} \\ z \amp = A e^{6x} \end{align*}
    While we could choose any value for \(A\) that we like, we are looking for just one function \(z\text{,}\) so we will simplify things by choosing \(A = 1\text{.}\) Thus, the function we found is \(z = e^{6x}\text{.}\) Now let’s confirm that it works. If we substitute our function in for \(z\) in (30), then we can un-do the product rule as follows:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{cccccccccccc} e^{6x} \!\!\!\! \amp \cdot \amp \!\!\!\! y' \!\!\!\! \amp + \amp \!\!\!\! 6e^{6x} \!\!\!\! \amp \cdot \amp \!\!\!\! y \!\!\!\! \amp \ds = \frac{d}{dx}\Big( \amp \!\!\!\! e^{6x} \!\!\!\! \amp \cdot \amp \!\!\!\! y \!\!\!\!\! \amp \Big). \\ \underset{\large f }{\uparrow} \!\!\!\! \amp \amp \!\!\!\! \underset{\large g'}{\uparrow} \!\!\!\! \amp \amp \!\!\!\! \underset{\large f'}{\uparrow} \!\!\!\! \amp \amp \!\!\!\! \underset{\large g }{\uparrow} \!\!\!\! \amp \amp \!\!\!\! \underset{\large f }{\uparrow} \!\!\!\! \amp \amp \!\!\!\! \underset{\large g }{\uparrow} \!\!\!\!\! \amp \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Notice that if we identify \(e^{6x}\) as the function \(f\text{,}\) then we do indeed see its derivative, \(6e^{6x}\) in the other term. If we made a mistake in finding the function \(z\text{,}\) this would be the point where we could catch our mistake.

    Example 28.

    Suppose \(y\) is a function of \(x\text{.}\) Find one function \(z(x)\) such that the derivative of \(z(x)\cdot y\) has the form
    \begin{equation} z\cdot y' + z\sec^2 x\cdot y\tag{31} \end{equation}
    Solution.
    It may be easier to sort this out if we label parts of the equation to align with the product rule, as below.
    \begin{equation*} \ub{z}_{\scriptsize f}\cdot \ub{y'}_{\scriptsize g'} + \ub{z\sec^2 x}_{\scriptsize f'}\cdot \ub{y}_{\scriptsize g} \end{equation*}
    If we’re going to be able to undo the product rule, we will need to find a function \(z\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dz}{dx} = z\sec^2 x. \end{equation*}
    This is a first order, separable DE, and we solve it accordingly.
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{z}dz \amp = \sec^2 x dx \\ \int \frac{1}{z}dz \amp = \int \sec^2 x dx \\ \ln|z| \amp = \tan x + C \\ |z| \amp =e^{\tan x + C} \\ z \amp = \pm e^C\cdot e^{\tan x} \\ z \amp = A e^{\tan x} \end{align*}
    Since we need only one function \(z\text{,}\) we can choose any value we like for \(A\text{.}\) Here we will choose \(A = 1\text{,}\) and therefore the function \(z\) is \(z = e^{\tan x}\text{.}\) We can check by un-doing the product rule.
    \begin{align*} \ub{e^{\tan x}}_{\scriptsize f}\cdot \ub{y'}_{\scriptsize g'} + \ub{e^{\tan x}\sec^2 x}_{\scriptsize f'}\cdot \ub{y}_{\scriptsize g} \amp = \frac{d}{dx}\Big( \ub{e^{\tan x}}_{\scriptsize f}\cdot \ub{y}_{\scriptsize g} \Big) \end{align*}

    Example 29.

    Suppose \(P\) and \(y\) are functions of \(x\text{.}\) Find one function \(z(x)\) such that the derivative of \(z(x)\cdot y\) has the form
    \begin{equation} z\cdot y' + z P(x)\cdot y \tag{32} \end{equation}
    Solution.
    This is a generic version of the previous example. If we can draw a conclusion using this generic version, we might be able to use it in future examples.
    As before, it may be easier to sort this out if we label parts of the equation to align with the product rule, as below.
    \begin{equation*} \ub{z}_{\scriptsize f}\cdot \ub{y'}_{\scriptsize g'} + \ub{zP(x)}_{\scriptsize f'}\cdot \ub{y}_{\scriptsize g} \end{equation*}
    If we’re going to be able to undo the product rule, we will need to find a function \(z\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dz}{dx} = zP(x). \end{equation*}
    This is a first order, separable DE, and we solve it accordingly.
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{z}dz \amp = P(x) dx \\ \int \frac{1}{z}dz \amp = \int P(x) dx \\ \ln|z| \amp = \int P(x) dx \\ |z| \amp =e^{\int P(x) dx} \\ z \amp = \pm e^{\int P(x) dx} \end{align*}
    Since we need only one function \(z\text{,}\) we usually choose the positive one and therefore we write \(z = e^{\int P(x) dx}\text{.}\)
    This may not look like much, but let’s see if it would work for the previous examples already did. Recall that we were trying to un-do the product rule in the expression
    \begin{equation} z\cdot y' + z\sec^2 x\cdot y \tag{33} \end{equation}
    In this example, we would identify the function \(P(x)\) as \(P(x) = \sec^2 x\text{.}\) Then the function \(z\) we desire would be given by
    \begin{equation*} \ds z = e^{\int P(x) dx} = e^{\int \sec^2 x dx} = e^{\tan x + C} = e^{\tan x}. \end{equation*}
    (Notice that we chose \(C = 0\) because we need only one function \(z\text{,}\) and this is the simplest choice for \(C\)).
    So our formula does give the same function \(z\) we found previously. Let’s practice one more.

    Example 30.

    Suppose \(y\) is a function of \(x\text{.}\) Find one function \(z(x)\) such that the derivative of \(z(x)\cdot y\) has the form
    \begin{equation*} z\cdot y' + z\cdot\frac{1}{x}\cdot y \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    In this example, the \(\ds \frac{1}{x}\) plays the same role as \(P(x)\) (in the previous EXAMPLE). Thus we can find \(z\) in a similar way.
    \begin{equation*} \ds z = e^{\int 1/x\ dx} = e^{\ln|x| + C}. \end{equation*}
    As in the previous example, we need only one function \(z\text{,}\) so we can simplify this expression. In this case, we can choose \(C = 0\text{,}\) and we can also eliminate the absolute value. This lead us to \(z = e^{\ln x}\text{,}\) which further simplifies to \(z = x\text{.}\) As in the previous examples, we can replace \(z\) in the original expression and then actually un-do the product rule.
    \begin{align*} z\cdot y' + z\cdot\frac{1}{x}\cdot y \amp = x\cdot y' + x\cdot\frac{1}{x}\cdot y \\ \amp = x\cdot y' + y \\ \amp = \frac{d}{dx}\Big( xy \Big) \end{align*}

    Additional Narrative.

    We know that \(\ds \frac{d}{dx}\left[y \cdot \mu\right]\) can be written as
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx} \cdot \mu + y \cdot \frac{d\mu}{dx}. \end{equation*}
    So we must match these terms with the terms in (16). Writing out these terms side-by-side helps determine the matching, as shown here
    From the figure we see that \(\mu = e^{5x^2}\) since it is the term next to \(\frac{dy}{dx}\text{.}\) Moreover, a quick check also shows that \(\frac{d\mu}{dx} = 10xe^{5x^2},\) completing the matching.
    In this chapter, we will study a solution technique that will help us solve differential equations that are first order and linear. Consider this (carefully selected) example.
    \begin{equation} x^7 \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} + 7x^{6}\cdot y = e^x\tag{34} \end{equation}
    Notice that the dependent variable is \(y\) and the independent variable is \(x\text{,}\) so we seek to find a formula for \(y\) in terms of \(x\text{.}\) The DE is also first order and linear.
    What makes this DE special is that the left hand side can be rewritten as follows.
    \begin{equation} \frac{d}{dx}\Big(x^7 \cdot y \Big) = e^x\tag{35} \end{equation}
    We can verify this by actually using the product rule to take the derivative on the left hand side:
    \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}\Big(x^7 \cdot y \Big)\\ =\amp\ \frac{d}{dx}\Big(\underbrace{x^7}_{f(x)} \cdot \underbrace{y}_{g(x)} \Big)\\ =\amp\ f(x) \cdot g'(x) + f'(x) \cdot g(x)\\ =\amp\ x^7 \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} + 7x^{6}\cdot y \end{align*}
    Now that we have confirmed that Equation (35) is equivalent to Equation (34), we can proceed in solving the DE.
    The unknown function we want to solve for is \(y\text{,}\) and it is part of the product we are taking the derivative of in Equation (35). So we begin by undoing that differentiation by integrating both sides of Equation (35) with respect to \(x\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} \int \frac{d}{dx}\Big(x^7 \cdot y \Big) dx =\amp\ \int e^x dx\\ x^7 \cdot y =\amp\ e^x + C \end{align*}
    We can now just divide both sides of Equation by the coefficient on \(y\) to obtain the general solution.
    \begin{equation*} y = \frac{e^x}{x^7} + \frac{C}{x^7} \end{equation*}
    Recall that we said this was a carefully selected DE. This very special DE had the property that we could undo the product rule on its left hand side. This is not, in general, the case when we encounter a first order linear DE. For example, consider this DE.
    (use one of the first examples we do in the book??)
    We cannot immediately undo the product rule here, but there is some hope. We do see the unknown function \(y\) and its derivative, so we have some of the stuff we need to undo the product rule. What we’ll see in this chapter is that we can manipulate the DE so that we will be able to undo the product rule and proceed to solve as we did in the example above, and so we begin this chapter by focusing on the details around making that possible.
    The integration factor technique relies heavily on our understanding of the product rule for differentiation, so let’s quickly recall what this looks like and go over some examples.
    \(\left[f(x) \cdot g(x)\right]' = f(x) \cdot g'(x) + f'(x) \cdot g(x)\)
    or
    \(\ds \frac{d}{dx}\left[f(x) \cdot g(x)\right] = f(x) \cdot \frac{d}{dx}\left[g(x)\right] + \frac{d}{dx}\left[f(x)\right] \cdot g(x)\)
    DO WE WANT A SUMMARY?
    Let’s summarize the procedure we have been working on.
    Solving First Order Linear DEs with an Integrating Factor
    • Verify that the DE is first order and linear. (Don’t overlook this step!! Nothing that follows here makes any sense if the DE isn’t both first order and linear.)
    • Rearrange the DE to put it in standard form for this solution technique.
      \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = f(x) \end{equation*}
      In standard form, the dependent variable and the derivative are on the left hand side of the equation and the coefficient on the derivative is 1. (Here we assume that \(x\) is the independent variable and \(y\) is the dependent variable; this will look different if your DE has different independent and dependent variables).
    • Identify the function \(P(x)\) and then find the integrating factor \(z = e^{\int P(x) dx}.\) (Note that we integrate with respect to the independent variable. We also need only one integrating factor, so we typically omit the \(+C\) that is ordinarily associated with an indefinite integral.)
    • Multiply both sides of the DE by the integrating factor.
    • Rewrite the left hand side of the DE by "un-doing" the product rule. This is the crucial step where you should check to make sure that you really can un-do the product rule. If you can’t then check to make sure you correctly determined determined the integrating factor.
    • Integrate both sides of the equation (with respect to the independent variable). (Here you should have a constant of integration.)
    • Solve for the dependent variable to get the general solution.
    • If an initial condition is provided, apply it last to determine the value of the constant of integration.
    The idea in the previous examples will be at the center of helping us solve the DEs of this chapter, but we need to expand on this a bit to fully prepare you for the discussion in the next section. Let’s now dive into the following examples to see what we mean.

    Taken From Integrating Factor.

    \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}\left[ y \cdot e^{-2x} \right] \amp = 5 e^{-2x} \quad \leftarrow \text{Easy to solve!} \end{align*}
    and then we integrate both sides with respect to \(x\) to free the unknown \(y\) from inside the \(\frac{d}{dx}\text{,}\) so that we can solve for it. However, we have not talked about how the integrating factor gets the differential equation into this form. Luckily, the product rule for deriviatives holds the answer. Therefore, let’s start this discussing by reminding ourselves that the product rule is given by
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}[\ f \cdot g\ ] = \frac{d}{dx}[\ f\ ] \cdot g + f \cdot \frac{d}{dx}[\ g\ ]. \end{equation*}
    Ok, the claim is that after a some math the differential equation
    \begin{equation} \frac{dy}{dx} - 2y = 5 \tag{36} \end{equation}
    can be rewritten as
    \begin{equation} \frac{d}{dx}\left[ y \cdot e^{-2x} \right] = 5 e^{-2x}. \tag{37} \end{equation}
    To explain this, it may be easier to work backwards. That is, let’s start with (37) and see how it may connect back to (36). We start by applying the product rule on the left side,
    \begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}[\ y \cdot e^{-2x}\ ] \amp = 5 e^{-2x} \tag{38}\\ \frac{d}{dx}[\ y\ ] \cdot e^{-2x} + y \cdot \frac{d}{dx}[\ e^{-2x}\ ] \amp = 5 e^{-2x} \tag{39}\\ \frac{dy}{dx} \cdot e^{-2x} + y \cdot \left(-2 e^{-2x}\right) \amp = 5 e^{-2x} \tag{40}\\ \frac{dy}{dx} \cdot e^{-2x} - 2 y \cdot e^{-2x} \amp = 5 e^{-2x}. \tag{41} \end{align}
    At this point stop and compare equation (36) with the last equation (41). Do you see how the two equations are connected?
    You should see that multiplying both sides of (36) by \(e^{-2x}\) gives you (41).
    In a nutshell, the integrating factor technique attempts to solve
    \begin{equation*} y' + P(x)y = Q(x) \end{equation*}
    by multiplying both sides by an unknown function, \(\mu\)
    \begin{equation*} \mu y' + \mu P(x)y = \mu Q(x) \end{equation*}
    then find a \(\mu\) so that the left-hand side can be written as the product rule of \(\mu\) and \(y\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} \us{\large \text{product rule}}{\ub{\mu y' + \mu P(x)y}} = \mu Q(x) \end{equation*}
    To clarify, let’s look at an example where we know \(\mu\text{.}\)
    The previous example shows how to solve the equation when you know what \(\mu\) is. However, in a real problem, you will have to find \(\mu\) first!
    Let’s build on the previous example to try and understand why \(\mu = e^{6x}\) worked. It is important to try and think of \(\mu(x)\) as the missing part needed to to be consistent with the product rule. That is, the left-side of the equation
    \begin{equation*} \us{\large \text{product rule}}{\us{\large \text{incomplete}}{\ub{y' + 6y}}} = 1 \end{equation*}
    is missing a second function needed to be consistent with the product rule. So let’s fill-in this missing function by multiplying both sides by \(\mu\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation} \mu y' + 6 \mu y = \mu.\tag{42} \end{equation}
    Is the left-side, \(\mu y' + 6 \mu y\text{,}\) equal to \(\left[\mu y\right]'\text{?}\) Well, not exactly, since taking this derivative shows the difference between these two, namely
    \(\ds\left[\mu y\right]'\) \(=\) \(\mu y'\) \(+\) \(\mu'\) \(y\)
    \(\uparrow\)
    difference
    \(\downarrow\)
    left-side \(\rightarrow\) \(\mu y'\) \(+\) \(6\mu\) \(y\)
    However, what if \(\mu\) was chosen so that \(\mu' = 6\mu\text{?}\) It turns out that this is exactly how we chose \(\mu\text{.}\) Finding \(\mu\) such that \(\mu' = 6\mu\) is itself a differential equation which can be solved using separation of variables. (You may try it yourself first for practice, then look below to see how to solve this DE.)
    Using separation of variables, solve
    \begin{align*} \mu' =\amp\ 6\mu. \end{align*}
    to get the solution
     5 
    \begin{align*} \frac{d\mu}{dx} =\amp\ 6\mu\\ \frac{1}{\mu}d\mu =\amp\ 6\ dx\\ \int\frac{1}{\mu}d\mu =\amp\ \int 6\ dx\\ \ln|\mu| =\amp\ 6x + C\\ |\mu| =\amp\ e^{6x + C}\\ \mu =\amp\ \pm e^C e^{6x}\\ \mu =\amp\ A e^{6x} \end{align*}
    \begin{equation*} \mu = A e^{6x}. \end{equation*}
    Since, we only need one \(\mu\) to work, we choose \(A=1\) for convenience, hence
    \begin{equation*} \mu = e^{6x}. \end{equation*}
    Now you see where the \(\mu\) came from in part 1 of Example 18. In fact, this example outlines how \(\mu\) is found for any first order linear differential equation of the form
    \begin{equation} y' + P(x)y = Q(x).\tag{43} \end{equation}
