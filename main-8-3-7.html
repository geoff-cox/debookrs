    Section 9.5 Transforming Equations

    Throughout this section, we learned how to apply Laplace transforms to individual functions. However, our goal is to solve differential equations, which means we need to understand how to transform an entire differential equation, including its initial conditions. This brings us back to the first step in the Laplace Transform Method roadmap.
    Our goal here is to apply the Laplace transform to both sides of this equation using the common transforms and properties weâ€™ve discussed. Recall that applying a Laplace transform to a function can be thought of as multiplying the function by \(e^{-st}\) and integrating with respect to \(t\text{.}\) However, instead of explicitly writing out this process for every term, we simply apply the Laplace operator, \(\laplacesym\text{,}\) to both sides of the equation. Letâ€™s break this down with an example.

    Example 13.

    \(\ \ \)Apply the Laplace Transform to the initial-value problem
    \begin{equation*} y'' + 3y' + 2y = -40e^{3t}, \quad y(0) = 0, \quad y'(0) = 1, \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    First, apply \(\laplacesym\) to both sides of the equation
    \begin{equation*} \lap{y'' + 3y' + 2y} = \lap{-40e^{3t}}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and use the linearity property, \(P_1\)
    \begin{equation*} \ub{\lap{y''}}_{(1)} + 3\ub{\lap{y'}}_{(2)} + 2\ub{\lap{y}}_{(3)} = -40\ub{\lap{e^{3t}}}_{(4)}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    to split this into four separate transforms. For this example, we will look at each one individually.
    \begin{align*} (1)\ :\quad \amp \lap{y''} = s^2Y - s\os{\large 0}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y(0)}} - \os{\large 1}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y'(0)}} = s^2 Y - 1 \vphantom{\frac{1}{1}} \quad \knowl{./knowl/xref/P4.html}{\text{\(P_4\)}}\\ (2)\ :\quad \amp \lap{y'} = sY - y(0) = sY \vphantom{\frac{1}{1}} \quad \knowl{./knowl/xref/P3.html}{\text{\(P_3\)}}\\ (3)\ :\quad \amp \lap{y} = Y \vphantom{\frac{1}{1}}\\ (4)\ :\quad \amp \lap{e^{3t}} = \frac{1}{s - 3} \quad \knowl{./knowl/xref/L2.html}{\text{\(L_2.\)}} \end{align*}
    Plugging \((1) - (4)\) back into the equation, we get
    \begin{align*} s^2Y - 1 + 3sY + 2Y =\amp\ \frac{-40}{s - 3}, \quad \text{or}\\ s^2Y + 3sY + 2Y - 1 =\amp\ \frac{-40}{s - 3} \end{align*}
    To further illustrate this process, consider one more example.

    Example 14.

    \(\ \ \)Apply the Laplace Transform to the initial-value problem
    \begin{equation*} y'' - 4y' + 6y = t^2, \quad y(0) = 2, \quad y'(0) = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Applying the Laplace transform to both sides gives:
    \begin{align*} \lap{y'' - 4y' + 6y} =\amp\ \lap{t^2}\\ \lap{y''} - 4\lap{y'} + 6\lap{y} =\amp\ \lap{t^2}\\ s^2Y - s\os{\large 2}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y(0)}} - \os{\large 0}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y'(0)}} - 4(sY - \os{\large 2}{\os{\Large \shortparallel}{y(0)}}) + 6Y =\amp\ \frac{2}{s^3}\\ s^2Y - 2s - 4sY + 8 + 6Y =\amp\ \frac{2}{s^3} \\ s^2Y - 4sY + 6Y - 2s + 8 =\amp\ \frac{2}{s^3} \end{align*}
    In both examples, the differential equations have been transformed into algebraic equations, where solving for \(Y\) will be the next step in the Laplace Transform Roadmap.

    1. True-False: Applying the Laplace transform to a differential equation transforms the equation into an algebraic equation in the s-domain..

    • True.

    • True. The Laplace transform converts a differential equation in the time domain into an algebraic equation in the s-domain.

    • False.

    • True. The Laplace transform converts a differential equation in the time domain into an algebraic equation in the s-domain.

    2. Which of the following represents the correct Laplace transform of the equation \(y'' + 3y' + 2y = -40e^{3t}\text{?}\)

    • \(s^2Y + 3sY + 2Y + 1 = \frac{-40}{s - 3}\)
    • No, this is incorrect. Check the constant term on the left-hand side after applying initial conditions.
    • \(s^2Y + 3sY + 2Y - 1 = \frac{-40}{s - 3}\)
    • Correct! This is the correct transformation using the Laplace transform and applying initial conditions.
    • \(s^2Y + 2Y - 3sY = \frac{-40}{s - 3}\)
    • No, this is incorrect. Double-check the coefficients and terms on the left-hand side.
    • \(Y(s) = \frac{-40}{s^2 - 3}\)
    • No, this is incorrect. This does not represent the correct transformation of the given differential equation.

    • s

    • -1

    • No, this is incorrect. There should be no constant term for \(y'(t)\) when \(y(0) = 0\text{.}\)

    • 0
    • No, this is incorrect. The correct value is \(3\text{,}\) not \(0\text{.}\)
    • -3
    • No, this is incorrect. The value should be positive \(3\text{,}\) not negative.

    7. True-False: The linearity property of the Laplace transform allows us to split an equation into separate transforms for each term on the left-hand side..

    • True.

    • True. The linearity property lets us apply the Laplace transform to each term individually.

    • False.

    • True. The linearity property lets us apply the Laplace transform to each term individually.

    8. What is the Laplace transform of the initial-value problem \(y'' - 4y' + 6y = t^2\) with initial conditions \(y(0) = 2\) and \(y'(0) = 0\text{?}\)

    • \(s^2Y + 4sY + 6Y = \frac{2}{s^3}\)
    • No, this is incorrect. Double-check the signs and the initial condition terms.
    • \(s^2Y - 4sY + 6Y - 2s + 8 = \frac{2}{s^3}\)
    • Correct! This is the correct transformation of the initial-value problem.
    • \(s^2Y - 4sY + 6Y = \frac{2}{s^3}\)
    • No, this is incorrect. You are missing the initial condition terms on the left-hand side.
    • \(Y(s) = \frac{2}{s^3}\)
    • No, this is incorrect. This only represents the transform of \(t^2\) and does not account for the left-hand side of the equation.
